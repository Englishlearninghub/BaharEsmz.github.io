What went wrong?

Before you read: think about a disaster response

Think about any natural disaster you've read about or heard of — a flood, earthquake, hurricane, or wildfire. Before you look at this week's grammar, consider:

1. What are the first three things a government or emergency service should do in the immediate hours after a disaster?

2. What kinds of mistakes do disaster responses typically involve — too slow, poor communication, lack of resources, wrong priorities?

3. When disaster investigators write their reports afterwards, what kind of language do they use to describe what went wrong? Think: are they describing facts, or are they making judgements about what was right or wrong?

Keep these questions in mind as you read the commission report below.

CONTEXT

Read first: the Veldoria Commission Report

Before any grammar: What were the three main failures identified by the commission? Where does the report place most responsibility — on the government, on infrastructure, or on communication systems? Do you think the tone is fair?

UN Disaster Response Review Commission · Veldoria · Final Report, March 2025
The 2024 Meridian River Floods:
Failures of Preparedness, Response & Coordination
Prepared by: Dr. Amara Diallo (Emergency Management, former ICRC) & Nikos Papadakis (Urban Infrastructure Analysis) · Confidential draft

Executive Summary

The flooding of the Meridian River basin in October 2024 resulted in 312 confirmed deaths, the displacement of an estimated 84,000 residents, and infrastructure losses as severe as those recorded in the country's worst flood year of 1998. This commission finds that the human cost was not as low as it could have been, and that a series of avoidable failures contributed to the loss of life. The government should have issued formal evacuation orders at least 48 hours before the levee breach. Early warning systems could have saved an estimated 80–120 lives had they been operational.

1. Failures of Preparedness

Not only were the levees inadequately maintained, but the flood modelling data was also eighteen months out of date. The Meridian Basin Flood Risk Assessment, last updated in April 2023, projected maximum water levels as high as 6.2 metres — a figure that proved catastrophically optimistic when levels reached 8.7 metres. The regional authority should have commissioned a revised assessment prior to the high-risk season.

Both the primary evacuation routes and the backup road network were compromised by ongoing construction work that had not been suspended despite known flood risk. This construction should not have been permitted to continue within the designated flood plain. Local engineers might have anticipated the compound effect of simultaneous road closures and rising water — but no formal risk assessment was completed.

2. Failures of Response

The emergency response on the night of 14–15 October was as chaotic as any this commission has reviewed. Neither the national emergency coordination centre nor the regional civil defence authority had a functioning communication protocol at the time of the breach. The breakdown of coordination meant that search and rescue teams were dispatched to the same districts twice while other areas received no assistance for up to eleven hours.

The Ministry of Infrastructure could have pre-positioned emergency response equipment in the high-risk districts, as recommended in the 2022 preparedness audit. Had this been done, response times might have been reduced by forty to sixty percent. Not only did the delay cost lives, but it also eroded public trust in emergency institutions in a way that will affect future compliance with evacuation orders.

3. Failures of Communication

Public warnings were not as clear as international standards require. The national broadcaster issued an alert at 23:14 — as late as possible given the rate of water rise — but the alert used technical language that many residents did not understand. A plain-language alert should have been issued six hours earlier. Both the failure to communicate clearly and the failure to act early reflect a systemic problem with how Veldoria's emergency management system prioritises expert accuracy over public accessibility.

This commission recommends that the Veldorian government treat this disaster as seriously as the 1998 floods when allocating reconstruction and preparedness funding. The findings of this report are as damning as any previously produced by this office.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the red phrases — what are they expressing?

Perfect modals — three different shades of meaning

Compare these three sentences from the report:
"The government should have issued evacuation orders 48 hours earlier."
"Early warning systems could have saved 80–120 lives."
"Local engineers might have anticipated the compound effect."
All three are past modal + have + past participle. But do they express the same thing? What is the difference in force between should, could, and might?

"Should have" = strong moral judgement / critical advice after the fact — it didn't happen, and it was wrong that it didn't. "Could have" = unrealised possibility — it was capable of happening but didn't. "Might have" = weaker, more speculative — it's possible it would have happened, but we're less certain. The strength: should have > could have > might have.

Look at: "This construction should not have been permitted to continue."
This is a negative perfect modal — and it uses the passive. What are the two things being expressed simultaneously?

First: the passive ("should not have been permitted") removes the agent — we don't say who permitted it. This is appropriate in a formal report. Second: the negative perfect modal expresses that something happened that should NOT have — a criticism of an action that occurred. So: something happened (construction continued) + it was wrong that it happened = should not have + been + past participle.

Look at: "Had this been done, response times might have been reduced by forty to sixty percent."
What is unusual about "had this been done"? What full conditional sentence does this come from?

This is an inverted third conditional — formal written English sometimes inverts the if-clause: "If this had been done" → "Had this been done." The full version: "If the equipment had been pre-positioned, response times might have been reduced." The inversion (had + subject + past participle) is used in formal writing to give extra weight. You've seen this from Grade 9 Week 1 (mixed conditionals) — now it reappears in the context of perfect modals.

STUDY

Perfect Modals — Advice & Regret

Perfect Modals: should have / could have / might have

Modal + have + past participle. Used to talk about past situations that did not happen — expressing what was possible, advisable, or wrong. A key feature of formal reports, retrospective analysis, and expressing regret.

Form — all three:

FormStructureExample
Affirmativemodal + have + past participle"The government should have issued warnings earlier."
Negativemodal + not + have + past participle"Construction should not have been permitted in the flood plain."
QuestionModal + subject + have + past participle?"Could the levees have been reinforced in time?"
With passivemodal + have + been + past participle"Response times might have been reduced by 50%."
Inverted conditionalHad + subject + past participle, … modal + have…"Had the alert been issued earlier, lives could have been saved."

Modal Should have Could have Might have
Core meaning It was the right thing to do — but it didn't happen. Strong regret or criticism. It was possible — but it didn't happen. Unrealised ability or opportunity. It was possible it would happen — but we're less certain. Speculation about the past.
Tone Critical, moral, advisory — implies a clear failure of duty Neutral — possibility without strong judgement Tentative — hedged, softened
Negative "Shouldn't have done it" = it was wrong to do it (and they did) "Couldn't have done it" = it wasn't possible (so we don't blame them) "Mightn't have happened" = it's possible it wouldn't have occurred

Common C1 errors:

✗The government should have issue the evacuation order.WRONG — needs past participle: "should have issued." Never base form after "have."
✗They should issued the warnings earlier.WRONG — missing "have": "should have issued." The full structure is: modal + HAVE + past participle.
✗The engineers could have anticipate the risk.WRONG — past participle needed: "could have anticipated."
✓The regional authority should have commissioned a revised flood assessment six months earlier.CORRECT — should + have + past participle (commissioned). Strong past advice/criticism.
✓Had the equipment been pre-positioned, response times might have been reduced significantly.CORRECT — inverted conditional + might have + been + past participle (passive).

Exercise 2 · Choose the right modal

Choose the most appropriate perfect modal (should have / could have / might have) for each context. Justify your choice.

In some cases more than one is possible — choose the one that fits the context most precisely and explain why.

1.The commission's tone is strongly critical of the government. Which modal for: "The government ___ issued a formal alert 48 hours earlier"?
2.The report is speculating about what the engineers noticed. Which modal for: "Engineers ___ anticipated the compound effect of simultaneous road closures"?
3.The report says better equipment placement was entirely feasible. Which modal for: "Pre-positioned equipment ___ reduced response times by 40–60%"?
4.A resident describes feeling they had no choice: "I didn't know the evacuation route was closed — I ___ planned differently if I had known." (personal regret)
1.should haveStrong moral criticism — the government had a duty to issue the alert and failed. "Should have" is the only appropriate modal for strong, clear-cut criticism of a failure of duty.
2.might have / could haveBoth acceptable. "Might have anticipated" = speculative — we're not sure they could have. "Could have anticipated" = it was within their capacity to notice. The report uses "might have" because it's hedging the claim about the engineers' foresight.
3.could haveUnrealised possibility stated as feasible fact — the report says "as recommended" which confirms the pre-positioning was achievable. "Could have" = it was possible to achieve this. "Should have" would also work if the report is being critical of the decision not to do it.
4.could have / should have"Could have planned differently" = I had the capacity/opportunity. "Should have planned differently" = it was the right thing to do and I regret not doing it. Both grammatically correct — the emotional weight differs.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the amber phrases — what is being compared?

As…as — more than a simple equality comparison

Find all the amber phrases in the report. For each one, is the comparison saying the two things are equal? Or is it doing something more nuanced?

Not all as…as phrases express simple equality. "As severe as" = equal severity (comparing 2024 to 1998). "Not as low as it could have been" = less than expected — a negative comparison meaning it was higher than it should have been. "As late as possible" = at the extreme end of lateness. "As seriously as" = to the same degree. "As damning as any" = a superlative-style comparison. So as…as extends well beyond "X = Y in this quality."

Compare: "The damage was as severe as 1998" vs "The human cost was not as low as it could have been."
What is the difference between "as severe as" (positive) and "not as low as" (negative)?

Positive as…as = equality (same level). Negative not as…as = below the standard/level mentioned. "Not as low as it could have been" = the death toll was higher than it should have been. The negative construction is often used to express that something fell short: "not as effective as expected" = less effective than expected.

STUDY

As…as — Extended Uses at C1

As…as — Beyond Basic Equality

At B1/B2, as…as is taught as a simple equality comparison: "as tall as." At C1, the structure is used in five more sophisticated patterns — all common in formal writing, analysis, and critical commentary.

1. Basic equality comparison (review)

StructureExampleNote
as + adj/adv + as"The 2024 floods were as severe as those of 1998."Same degree; both sides compared on the same quality.
as much/many … as"Twice as many districts were affected as in 2018."Quantity comparison: as many / as much / as few / as little.

2. Negative comparison — not as…as

StructureExampleMeaning
not as + adj + as"The human cost was not as low as it could have been."Less than expected / below the stated standard. = the cost was higher than it should have been.
not as + adj + as + expected/required"Public warnings were not as clear as international standards require."Failed to meet a standard or expectation.

3. Intensifying uses — as…as possible / as…as ever

StructureExampleMeaning
as + adj/adv + as possible"The alert was issued as late as possible given the rate of water rise."At the maximum possible degree — often carries a critical implication.
as + adj + as any / as ever"The response was as chaotic as any this commission has reviewed."Superlative-style: at least as bad/good as the worst/best known case.
as…as ever"The need for preparedness funding is as urgent as ever."Continues to be at the same high degree — implies it hasn't improved.

4. As…as with modal verbs — counterfactual comparisons

StructureExampleUse
not as + adj + as + it could/should have been"The response was not as coordinated as it should have been."Criticism: it fell short of what was possible or required.
as + adj + as + it would have been (if…)"The damage would have been as bad as 1998 had the levees not held."Counterfactual comparison in hypothetical context.

5. Fixed expressions with as…as (corpus-frequent)

as soon as possible (ASAP) as long as (= provided that / on the condition that) as far as I know / as far as we can tell as well as (= in addition to) as much as X, Y is also… (concession) treat X as seriously as Y

⚠ Common errors:

✗The response was as faster as we expected.WRONG — use base adjective/adverb, not comparative: "as fast as."
✗The damage was not so severe as 1998.Technically possible (not so…as is archaic/formal BrE) but "not as severe as" is strongly preferred in modern English.
✓The government should treat this disaster as seriously as the 1998 floods when allocating funding.CORRECT — as seriously as = to the same degree of seriousness. Note: adverb "seriously" used (not adjective) because it modifies the verb "treat."

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the teal phrases — two halves, one idea

Correlative conjunctions — find the two parts

Look at: "Not only were the levees inadequately maintained, but the flood modelling data was also eighteen months out of date."
Can you find the two halves of this sentence? What connects them — and how would you write this as two separate sentences?

Two halves: (1) "Not only were the levees inadequately maintained" + (2) "but the flood modelling data was also eighteen months out of date." As two sentences: "The levees were inadequately maintained. In addition, the flood modelling data was eighteen months out of date." The correlative conjunction connects two pieces of information that reinforce each other — but does so more emphatically and formally than "and also." Note also the inversion after "not only": "were the levees" not "the levees were."

Compare these three teal phrases from the report:
"Not only did the delay cost lives, but it also eroded public trust."
"Both the communication breakdown and the under-resourcing of first responders contributed to the death toll."
"Neither the national coordination centre nor the regional civil defence authority had a functioning protocol."
What is different about each one — in meaning and in structure?

Not only…but also = adds something further, often surprising or more serious than the first item. Implies: "and what's more." / Both…and = includes two things equally. Both of them. / Neither…nor = excludes two things equally. Neither of them. Each has a different logical meaning: addition (not only), inclusion of both (both…and), or exclusion of both (neither…nor).

STUDY

Correlative Conjunctions

Not only…but also · Both…and · Neither…nor

Correlative conjunctions work in pairs — the first half sets up an expectation, the second half completes it. All three are high-frequency in formal written English and in persuasive speech. They make connections between two parallel ideas more emphatic and elegant than "and" alone.

not only … but also
/nɒt ˈəʊnli … bʌt ˈɔːlsəʊ/
Addition — "and what's more / furthermore"
Emphasises that the second item is additional — often more surprising or more serious than the first. The "also" can be omitted in informal use, but is standard in formal writing.
"Not only did the delay cost lives, but it also eroded public trust in emergency institutions."
both … and
/bəʊθ … ænd/
Inclusion — "the two of them / each equally"
Connects two items and includes both equally. Used to emphasise that two things share a quality or that two people/entities are jointly responsible. Takes plural agreement when connecting two subjects.
"Both the communication breakdown and the under-resourcing of first responders contributed to the death toll."
neither … nor
/ˈnaɪðə … nɔː/
Exclusion — "not the one and not the other"
Negative counterpart of both…and. Excludes both items. The verb agrees with the subject closest to it (proximity rule). Never use "not…nor" — this is a double negative error.
"Neither the coordination centre nor the regional authority had a functioning protocol."

Critical grammar: inversion after "not only"

When "not only" appears at the start of a clause, the subject and auxiliary verb invert — like in a question. This is the most common error students make with this structure.

Without inversion (mid-sentence)With inversion (sentence-initial)
"The response was not only slow but also poorly coordinated." — no inversion when "not only" is mid-clause"Not only was the response slow, but it was also poorly coordinated." — inversion when "not only" opens the clause
"The agency not only failed to communicate but also failed to act.""Not only did the agency fail to communicate, but it also failed to act."

Inversion rule: auxiliary verb (is/was/did/had/will) + subject. If no auxiliary, use "do/does/did": "Not only did they fail…" (not "Not only they failed…")

Agreement rules:

StructureAgreementExample
Both…and (two subjects)Plural verb"Both the levees and the road network were compromised."
Neither…nor (two subjects)Verb agrees with nearer subject (proximity)"Neither the minister nor the officials were available." / "Neither the officials nor the minister was available."
Not only…but alsoVerb agrees with its own subject normally"Not only were the levees damaged, but the roads were also blocked."

Parallel structure — the key requirement:

Both halves of a correlative conjunction must be grammatically parallel — the same part of speech on each side.

✗Not only the levees failed but also flooding the roads.WRONG — not parallel: noun phrase (the levees failed) ≠ gerund (flooding). Correct: "Not only did the levees fail, but the roads also flooded."
✗The report criticises both the government's slow response and that agencies didn't communicate.WRONG — not parallel: noun phrase ≠ that-clause. Correct: "both the government's slow response and the failure of agencies to communicate."
✓Neither the evacuation plan nor the communication protocol was adequate for a flood of this scale.CORRECT — parallel: noun phrase (the evacuation plan) + noun phrase (the communication protocol). Verb "was" agrees with the nearer subject "the communication protocol" (singular).

VOCABULARY

Natural Disasters & Safety

Core vocabulary from the report

leveenoun
/ˈlevi/
an embankment built alongside a river to prevent flooding — either natural (formed by sediment deposits) or artificial (built by engineers). A levee breach occurs when water breaks through or overtops it.
"Not only were the levees inadequately maintained, but the modelling data used to assess them was eighteen months out of date."
evacuationnoun
/ɪˌvækjuˈeɪʃən/
the organised movement of people away from a dangerous area. An evacuation order is an official instruction to leave. Voluntary evacuation = recommended but not mandatory. Mandatory evacuation = legally required to leave.
"The government should have issued formal evacuation orders at least 48 hours before the levee breach."
displacementnoun
/dɪsˈpleɪsmənt/
the forced movement of people from their homes due to disaster, conflict, or environmental change. Internally displaced persons (IDPs) remain within their country; refugees cross international borders.
"The flooding resulted in the displacement of an estimated 84,000 residents, many of whom could not return for three months."
flood plainnoun
/ˈflʌd pleɪn/
the flat area of land adjacent to a river, formed by sediment deposited during floods. Flood plains are highly fertile but inherently at risk — building on them is controversial because it increases both the frequency and severity of flood damage.
"Construction should not have been permitted to continue within the designated flood plain during high-risk months."
early warning systemnoun phrase
/ˈɜːli ˈwɔːnɪŋ ˈsɪstəm/
a network of sensors, monitoring stations, and communication infrastructure designed to detect hazards and alert populations before they cause harm. Effective early warning systems require both technical accuracy and accessible communication.
"Early warning systems could have saved an estimated 80–120 lives had they been operational on the night of 14 October."
search and rescue (SAR)noun phrase
/sɜːtʃ ænd ˈreskjuː/
emergency operations to locate and help people in danger. Urban search and rescue (USAR) involves collapsed buildings. Water rescue is specific to flood and maritime contexts. SAR teams are typically the first responders in disaster settings.
"Search and rescue teams were dispatched to the same districts twice while other areas received no assistance for eleven hours."
first respondernoun
/fɜːst rɪˈspɒndə/
emergency services personnel — firefighters, paramedics, police, and specialised rescue teams — who are the first to arrive at the scene of a disaster or emergency. Their effectiveness depends heavily on pre-positioning of equipment and training.
"Both the communication breakdown and the under-resourcing of first responders contributed to the scale of the disaster."
catastrophicadjective
/ˌkætəˈstrɒfɪk/
involving or causing sudden great damage or suffering — far beyond normal or manageable. Degrees: minor → significant → severe → catastrophic. In disaster management, catastrophic typically means all response resources are overwhelmed.
"Water levels reached 8.7 metres — a figure that proved catastrophically optimistic against the 6.2m projection."

Extended vocabulary: disaster management & risk

mitigationnoun
/ˌmɪtɪˈɡeɪʃən/
action taken to reduce the severity, impact, or likelihood of a disaster. Distinct from preparedness (getting ready for a specific event) and response (acting during an event). Mitigation is long-term: building levees, planting flood-absorbing forests, relocating communities from flood plains.
"In terms of long-term mitigation, the commission recommends a twenty-year investment programme in Meridian Basin infrastructure."
resiliencenoun
/rɪˈzɪliəns/
the ability of a system, community, or individual to absorb disruption and recover. In disaster management: community resilience = how well communities can cope with and recover from disasters without external aid. Distinguished from mere resistance (not being damaged) — resilient systems bend but bounce back.
"Both the infrastructure failures and the coordination gaps revealed a resilience deficit that years of underfunding had created."
risk assessmentnoun phrase
/rɪsk əˈsesmənt/
a systematic process of identifying, analysing, and evaluating potential hazards and their consequences. In disaster contexts: identifies likelihood × impact for each hazard type. The 2023 Meridian Basin risk assessment was a key failure in the Veldoria floods — it was outdated.
"The regional authority should have commissioned a revised risk assessment prior to the high-risk season."
infrastructurenoun
/ˈɪnfrəˌstrʌktʃə/
the fundamental physical systems of a society — roads, bridges, levees, power grids, water treatment, communication networks. Critical infrastructure = systems whose failure would have severe consequences. Infrastructure resilience is a key focus of modern disaster management.
"Infrastructure losses in 2024 were as severe as those recorded in Veldoria's worst flood year of 1998."
aftershocknoun
/ˈɑːftəʃɒk/
a smaller earthquake that follows the main shock in the same region. More broadly used metaphorically: the aftershock of a disaster = the secondary effects that continue after the immediate event (economic disruption, psychological trauma, political fallout).
"Neither the government nor the health system was prepared for the aftershock of the disaster — a wave of displacement-related illness."
hazardnoun
/ˈhæzəd/
a condition or event with the potential to cause harm. In disaster risk science, a hazard only becomes a disaster when it intersects with a vulnerable population. Types: geophysical (earthquake, volcano), hydrological (flood, tsunami), climatological (drought, wildfire), meteorological (hurricane, storm).
"Both the flooding hazard and the compound road closure hazard should have been identified in a comprehensive risk register."
contingency plannoun phrase
/kənˈtɪndʒənsi plæn/
a plan prepared for a specific possible future event — especially a negative or unexpected one. A contingency plan pre-defines what to do if a particular scenario occurs. The absence of a contingency plan for simultaneous road closures and flooding was a key failure in Veldoria.
"Not only was there no contingency plan for simultaneous road closures, but the one that existed for flooding alone had not been reviewed since 2019."
triagenoun / verb
/ˈtriːɑːʒ/
the process of sorting injured people (or competing priorities) according to urgency and available resources. From French — originally a medical sorting process. In disaster contexts: deciding which areas or casualties to address first when resources are insufficient for all.
"As a result of poor triage during the first twelve hours, resources were misallocated and the most vulnerable communities received help last."

SPEAKING PRACTICE

Regret, contrast — spoken

Commissions present their findings out loud; survivors retell what happened; officials answer for what should have been done. These tasks put this week's perfect modals, as…as structures, and disaster vocabulary under mild spoken pressure. Use your phone's voice memo app.

Retell task

Present the Commission's findings

Re-read the Veldoria Commission report in the Context section. Then close it and, from memory, present its key findings aloud in 90 seconds — as if you were the Commission chair at a press conference. Build your presentation around perfect modals: what should have been done, what could have been prevented, what needn't have happened.

How: Record without looking at the text. When you listen back, count: your perfect modals — and check each one has the full form (should have been issued, not should been issued). Weak forms are fine (should've); missing pieces are not.
Timed monologue

The near miss

Set a timer for 2 minutes. Speak without stopping about a time when something went wrong — or nearly went wrong — for you, your city, or your country, and what should have been done differently. A storm, a missed flight, a public failure — any scale works.

Try to use at least three items from this week's word set: evacuation, early warning system, mitigation, contingency plan, risk assessment, resilience.

How: Record it; don't stop to self-correct. When you listen back, note: where you paused or searched for a word, and whether your regret language was varied — or whether every sentence began with "They should have…". Re-record once, deliberately varying the openings.
Describe & compare

Two responses, one storm

Imagine two neighbouring towns hit by the same flood. Town A had a contingency plan, drills, and a working early warning system; Town B had none of these. Speak for 90 seconds comparing the two responses using this week's as…as structures:

  • "Town B's response was nowhere near as fast as…"
  • "The damage was twice as severe as…"
  • "Not so much a failure of resources as a failure of planning…"
How: Record it. When you listen back, check: Did each comparison keep both halves of the structure (as + adjective + as)? Did you also manage one correlative pair — not only… but also… or neither… nor…?

Technique: the pause is not the enemy

  • Under time pressure, learners rush and drop modal pieces ("should have been" → "should be"). A one-second pause before a complex form is invisible to listeners — and saves the grammar.
  • Perfect modals carry stress on the main verb: should have WARNED, could have been preVENted. Say the recording back with exaggerated stress once.
  • Retelling from memory always loses details — that's the point. Retell what the report argued, not every number it contained.

Before Session 1, make sure you can…

  • Form should have / could have / might have — active and passive — and explain the difference in meaning and force between them
  • Use the inverted conditional: "Had + subject + past participle, … modal + have + past participle"
  • Use negative perfect modals correctly: "should not have been permitted" (not "should not have permit")
  • Use as…as in at least four ways: equality, negative (not as…as), intensifying (as…as possible), and counterfactual
