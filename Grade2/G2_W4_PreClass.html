3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Countable & Uncountable Nouns

Countable nouns can be counted (one, two, three) and have a plural form. Uncountable nouns cannot be counted directly and have no plural form.

Countable

an egg → two eggs
a tomato → three tomatoes
an apple → four apples
a potato → five potatoes

Uncountable

rice (NOT "a rice", NOT "two rices")
bread
water
pasta
TypeRuleExample
Countablea/an + singular; number + plural (-s)an egg, three eggs
Uncountableno a/an; no plural; use "some" for a quantitysome rice, some water

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Can we say "two rices"? No — rice is uncountable, it has no plural.
Can we say "two eggs"? Yes — egg is countable.
Do we say "a water" or "some water"? Some water — water is uncountable.

3.2 — Some / Any

Some or any? Follow the sentence type
Some = positive sentences. Any = negative sentences and questions. But offers with "would you like...?" use "some", not "any".

⚠️ Watch the Exception!

❌ Sounds strange✅ Correct
Would you like any water?Would you like some water?

When we offer something politely, we use "some" even in a question — it's a friendly exception to the rule.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we usually use "some" or "any" in negative sentences? Any.
Do we usually use "some" or "any" in questions? Any.
What word do we use in a polite offer, like "Would you like ___ coffee?" Some (the exception).

4Like vs. Would Like — and Ordering

These two look similar, but they mean different things.

ExpressionMeaningExample
I like + nounA general preference (always true)I like pasta. (in general, all the time)
I'd like (I would like) + nounWhat you want right now (an order/request)I'd like the spaghetti, please. (right now, at the restaurant)

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Does "I like pasta" mean right now, or always? Always — a general preference.
Does "I'd like the spaghetti" mean right now, or always? Right now — an order.

Useful Restaurant Phrases

7Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "I'd like some rices, please."
    Incorrect — "rice" is uncountable, no plural: "I'd like some rice, please."
  2. "I have some eggs."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Eggs" is countable and correctly plural after "some". Resist the urge to change anything.
  3. "I don't have some bread."
    Incorrect — negatives need "any": "I don't have any bread."
  4. "Would you like any tea?"
    Incorrect — offers use "some", even in a question: "Would you like some tea?"
  5. "I really like pasta."
    Also correct. This is a general preference, correctly using "like" (not "would like"). A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.
