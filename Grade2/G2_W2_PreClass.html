Notice: Sofia and Jack ask each other lots of questions with "do" and "does". They also use words like "often", "rarely" and "hardly ever" to say how often they do things, and "can" to ask for things politely.

3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Present Simple Questions

To make a question in the present simple, we put do or does at the very beginning — and the main verb loses its "-s".

Flip the sentence: statement → question
For he/she/it: "She likes hobbies" → "Does she like hobbies?" — the "-s" moves from the verb onto "does", so the verb goes back to its base form.
SubjectQuestion formShort answer
I / you / we / theyDo + subject + verb?Yes, I do. / No, I don't.
he / she / itDoes + subject + verb?Yes, she does. / No, she doesn't.
Wh- questionWhat / How often + do/does + subject + verb?I play the guitar. / She practises every day.

⚠️ Don't Add "-s" Twice!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
Does she likes music?Does she like music?
Do she like painting?Does she like painting?

"Does" already carries the "-s" for he/she/it, so the main verb after it goes back to its base form — never "does she likes".

🔎 Check Your Understanding

In a question, does "do/does" go at the start or the end? At the start.
Do we use "do" or "does" for "she"? Does.
Is "Does she likes music?" correct? No — it should be "Does she like music?" (no -s on "like").

3.2 — Adverbs of Frequency (Extended)

Last week you learned always, usually, sometimes and never. Here are more frequency words, from most to least often.

The extended frequency scale
New this week: often (between usually and sometimes), rarely and hardly ever (between sometimes and never).

We also use these words to answer "How often...?" questions: "How often do you practise?" — "I practise every day." / "I rarely practise."

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Is "often" more or less frequent than "sometimes"? More frequent.
Do "rarely" and "hardly ever" mean almost the same thing? Yes — both are close to "never".
What question do we ask to find out how frequently someone does something? "How often...?"

3.3 — Can for Requests (Review)

We use can to ask for something politely, or to ask permission. This is a review from before — now we use it to talk about hobbies.

FormExample
Can I + verb...?Can I come with you?
Can you + verb...?Can you bring your guitar?
Positive answerSure! / Of course! / No problem!
Negative answerSorry, I can't. / I'm afraid not.

⚠️ No "To" After Can!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
Can I to borrow your guitar?Can I borrow your guitar?

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Is "Can I borrow your guitar?" asking about ability, or making a request? Making a request.
Do we add "to" after "can"? No — "Can I borrow", not "Can I to borrow".

4.3 — Likes & Dislikes

I love... I like... I don't mind... I don't really like... I hate...

After love, like and hate, you can use either the "-ing" form OR "to + verb" — both are correct and mean almost the same thing:

"-ing" form"to + verb" form
I love painting.I love to paint.
I like cooking.I like to cook.
He hates waiting.He hates to wait.

But after don't mind and dislike, we normally only use the "-ing" form: "I don't mind cooking." "She dislikes waiting." (NOT "dislikes to wait").

6Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "Does she likes music?"
    Incorrect — no "-s" on the verb after "does": "Does she like music?"
  2. "Do she like painting?"
    Incorrect — "she" needs "does": "Does she like painting?"
  3. "Can I borrow your guitar, please?"
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Can" is correctly followed by the base verb, with no "to". Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Can you to help me with this?"
    Incorrect — no "to" after "can": "Can you help me with this?"
  5. "How often do you practise?"
    Also correct. "Do" correctly matches "you", and the question word order is right. A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

8Explore More: Build Your Own Independence
