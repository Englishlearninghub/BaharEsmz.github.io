Meet Sofia: Her Day, Her Family

Present simple · possessive adjectives · adverbs of frequency

📍 This week's story: Sofia just moved into a new apartment. Her neighbour, Jack, says hello and asks about her day and her family. Read what Sofia says. Don't worry about grammar names yet — just try to understand her life.

3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Present Simple: Form

SubjectFormExample
I / you / we / theybase verbI wake up at 6:30.
he / she / itverb + sShe walks to school.
negative (I/you/we/they)don't + verbWe don't skip breakfast.
negative (he/she/it)doesn't + verbHe doesn't finish early.
questionDo/Does + subject + verb?Does she walk to school?

We use the present simple for routines, habits, and things that are always or usually true — exactly what Sofia uses to describe her normal day.

⚠️ Don't Forget the "-s"!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
She wake up at 6:30.She wakes up at 6:30.
He don't like coffee.He doesn't like coffee.

Only he / she / it need the extra "-s" on the verb — and in negatives, "doesn't" already has the "-s", so the main verb goes back to normal: "He doesn't like" (NOT "doesn't likes").

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Does Sofia wake up at 6:30 just today, or every day? Every day — it's her routine.
Do we add "-s" for "I"? No.
Do we add "-s" for "she"? Yes — "she wakes up".
Is "He doesn't likes coffee" correct? No — after "doesn't", the verb has no "-s": "He doesn't like coffee".

3.2 — Possessive Adjectives

SubjectPossessive adjectiveExample
ImyMy husband is called Mark.
youyourIs this your family?
hehisLeo goes with his grandmother.
sheherEmma walks with her friend.
ititsThe building has its own garden.
weourOur daughter is 8 years old.
theytheirEmma and Leo love their grandmother.

Remember: "his" is for a man, "her" is for a woman — this doesn't match the noun after it, only the person the word belongs to.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Is "his" for a man or a woman? A man.
Emma has a friend. Do we say "her friend" or "his friend"? Her friend — Emma is a girl.
Sofia and Mark have a daughter. Do we say "my daughter" or "their daughter"? Their daughter — it belongs to both of them.

3.3 — Adverbs of Frequency

These words say how often something happens. They go in a special position — before the main verb, but after the verb "be".

The frequency scale: 100% → 0%
Real English uses "usually" and "sometimes" much more often than learners expect.
PositionExample
Before the main verbI always wake up at 6:30. She never skips breakfast.
After the verb "be"He is usually tired in the morning.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Does "always" mean 100% or 50%? 100%.
Does "never" mean 100% or 0%? 0%.
Which happens more often: "usually" or "sometimes"? Usually.
In "He is always tired", does "always" go before or after "is"? After — "is always".

6Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "She wake up at 6:30."
    Incorrect — "she" needs "-s": "She wakes up at 6:30."
  2. "He don't like coffee."
    Incorrect — "he" needs "doesn't": "He doesn't like coffee."
  3. "My brother visits every Sunday."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Visits" correctly has "-s" for "brother" (he). Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "He always is tired in the morning."
    Incorrect position — with "be", the adverb goes after: "He is always tired in the morning."
  5. "Her name is Emma, and him is my daughter."
    Incorrect — for a girl, we use "she": "Her name is Emma, and she is my daughter."

🗣Speaking Practice

Sofia talks about her day and her family. Now you talk about yours — out loud. You only need your phone's voice memo app.

Timed monologue

One minute: my day

Set a timer for 1 minute. Talk about your normal day, from morning to night: "I wake up at seven. I have breakfast. I go to work at…" Add how often: always, usually, sometimes, never.

How: Record it. Don't stop for mistakes. When you listen back, check: the frequency word sits before the verb — "I usually have coffee," not "I have usually coffee."
Roleplay scenario

Tell me about your family

Role A: Ask three simple questions: "Do you have brothers or sisters? What does your mother do? What time does your family have dinner?"

Role B: Answer with full little sentences: "I have one brother. He works in a shop. We have dinner at seven." Then change roles.

How: With a partner, or play both people yourself; record 2 minutes. When you listen back, check the -s: he workS, she getS up. It's small but important!
Sentence challenge

Six routine verbs, one sentence each

Say one TRUE sentence with each phrase — with a time or a frequency word. Up to 60 seconds each:

  • wake up
  • get up
  • have breakfast
  • go to work / go to class
  • have dinner
  • go to bed
How: One recording, timer on. When you listen back, check: AT seven, IN the morning, ON Mondays.

Tips: talk about your day every day

  • Say your morning aloud while you live it: "Now I'm making coffee…" One minute a day is real practice.
  • Learn routines as pairs: wake up / get up, go to work / come home.
  • The word usually sounds like "YOO-zhuh-lee" — three beats. Say it five times!
