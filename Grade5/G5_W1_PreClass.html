NOTICE & DISCOVER

What did you notice in the chat?

Work through these questions before you look at the grammar

Try to answer from the context of the chat. Reveal the answer only after you have thought about it.

Tom says: "I'm going to book the tickets now." Has he booked them yet? How do you know?

No — he hasn't booked them yet when he says this. Going to here expresses an intention or plan he has decided at that moment (or just before). Shortly after, he says "Booked!" — that's the completed action.

Rasha says "I'm going to be a bit late." and also "That film's going to be sold out by Sunday." Are both uses the same? What is different?

The first is a personal plan / warning based on what she knows (her meeting). The second is a prediction based on evidence (everyone at work is talking about it). Both use going to but for slightly different reasons. This is the key to understanding the grammar — it can express both plans AND evidence-based predictions.

Look at the blue phrases: "Shall we meet…", "Let's meet…", "What if we go…", "Why don't we…" What are they all doing? What do they have in common?

They are all making suggestions — inviting the others to agree on a shared plan. None of them use going to. Each one is a different structure for the same function. You will study all of these in the Suggestions section.

Look at how going to is written and try saying it out loud: "I'm going to book." How does it sound when you say it quickly in a natural sentence?

In natural, informal speech, going to is almost always reduced to "gonna": /ˈɡɒnə/. The g stays, but the -ing to collapses. You will practise this in the Pronunciation section. It is not a mistake — it is how native speakers actually talk.

STUDY

Going to — form, meaning & use

1. The form

Going to is built from three pieces: the verb be + going to + a base verb. Because it uses be, it must agree with the subject in every tense.

Positive
Subject + am / is / are + going to + base verb
I am going to book the tickets.  ·  She is going to be late.  ·  They are going to watch a film.
Negative
Subject + am not / isn't / aren't + going to + base verb
I'm not going to be late.  ·  He isn't going to come.  ·  We aren't going to book online.
Question
Am / Is / Are + subject + going to + base verb?
Are you going to get the 7:15?  ·  Is she going to be late?  ·  What are they going to watch?

2. Full conjugation table

In everyday speech, contractions are standard: I'm / you're / she's / we're / they're going to…

3. The two main meanings

This is where most learners get confused. Going to has two closely related but distinct uses. The context usually makes it clear which one you mean.

Meaning A — Plans & intentions

You have decided something before the moment you speak. It is already in your head as a plan or intention.

✓ "I'm going to book the tickets now."Tom decided to do this moments ago. It's his intention.
✓ "We're going to meet at the café."The group has agreed. This is their plan.
✓ "I'm not going to go — I have work."She has already made this decision.

Meaning B — Predictions with evidence

You can see, feel, or know something now that tells you what is going to happen. There is visible or logical evidence.

✓ "It's going to be cold tonight."Yuna can see the weather forecast / feel the temperature.
✓ "That film's going to be sold out."Everyone at work is talking about it — evidence.
✓ "Look at those clouds — it's going to rain!"The evidence (clouds) is visible right now.
📌 Corpus note (COCA / BNC) In spoken English, going to for plans is far more frequent than will for the same situations. When people talk about what they have already decided, they almost always use going to, not will. Will is preferred for in-the-moment decisions and promises — you will study that contrast in Week 3.

Going to vs Will — a first look

Why this comparison matters

This is the most common confusion at B1 level. You do not need to master the full contrast yet — that comes in Week 3. But you need to understand the core difference now so you don't confuse them in your plans and predictions.

FeatureGoing toWill
Plans & intentions ✓ Pre-decided plans
"I'm going to see that film tonight."
(I decided this before now)		 ✗ Not standard for pre-decided plans
Spontaneous decisions ✗ Not standard ✓ Decision made at this moment
"I'll come too!"
(you decide as you speak)
Predictions with evidence ✓ Evidence-based predictions
"Look at the queue — it's going to take forever."		 Possible for general predictions
"That film will win an Oscar."
(no specific evidence, just opinion)
Promises & offers ✗ Unusual ✓ Natural for promises/offers
"I'll help you."  "I'll get the tickets."

The simplest test

Ask yourself: Was this decision already made?

✓ Going to: "I'm going to study tonight." → You decided this earlier. It's already in your plan.
✓ Will: "Oh, the café is closed. I'll find another one." → You just found this out. You decide now.
⚠ Common error at B1 ✗ "I will meet my friends tomorrow evening." (sounds like a promise or announcement)
✓ "I'm going to meet my friends tomorrow evening." (you've already arranged it — this is natural)

When talking about plans you have already made, use going to. Reserve will for the moment of decision.

Making suggestions & arranging plans

The language of suggestions

When you arrange plans with other people, you need more than going to. You need the language of suggestions, agreement, and counter-proposals. All of these appeared in the group chat.

StructureExample from the chatRegisterGrammar note
Let's + base verb "Let's meet at the café on King Street." Informal Short for Let us. Very common in spoken English. Includes the speaker in the plan.
Shall we + base verb? "Shall we meet at the cinema first?" Neutral Invites agreement from the group. Common in British English. Question form only.
Why don't we + base verb? "Why don't we order food at the café?" Informal Softer suggestion; implies a reason the listener may not have considered.
What if we + past simple? "What if we go to the late showing?" Informal Introduces a possibility more tentatively; useful when you're not sure the others will agree.
How about + -ing? "How about getting coffee first?" Informal Always followed by a gerund (-ing form). How about to get is wrong.
What about + -ing? "What about booking the 9:30?" Informal Same structure as How about. Gerund (-ing) required after both.
We could + base verb "We could meet at 7 instead." Neutral Softest form — presents a possibility without committing. Polite in any context.
Why not + base verb? "Why not take the bus?" Informal Quick, casual. Base verb (not -ing) after Why not.

Look more carefully at these phrases

"It's going to rain all day." Has Marco checked a forecast or made a plan to rain? What is he doing?

He is making a prediction based on visible evidence (the sky). He can see the evidence right now. There is no plan here — you cannot plan rain. This is going to for evidence-based predictions.

"I'm going to pack light." and "I'm going to look up train times tonight." — Which use is this?

These are plans / intentions. Rasha and Marco have already decided to do these things. They are reporting a decision they have made (or are making right now, in this conversation).

"The early train's going to be packed." — Marco says this with confidence. What is his evidence?

Past experience: "it always is on weekends." He is predicting based on what he knows from experience — which counts as evidence. This is still the evidence-based use of going to, just with a different kind of evidence (past pattern, not visible sign).

Going to for predictions — reading the signs

When the evidence is speaking

English grammar distinguishes between two types of predictions. The type you use depends on whether you have evidence right now, or whether you are just expressing a general opinion about the future.

Going to — evidence is present

Use when you can point to a specific reason, sign, or piece of evidence right now.

✓ "Look at those clouds — it's going to rain."Evidence: the clouds you can see.
✓ "He's driving too fast — he's going to crash."Evidence: his speed right now.
✓ "The queue is huge — we're going to miss the start."Evidence: visible queue length.

Will — general opinion / guess

Use when you believe something will happen but you have no specific evidence at this moment.

✓ "I think that film will win an Oscar."Opinion, not evidence. You don't know.
✓ "I think technology will change everything."General future opinion.
✓ "It'll probably rain at some point this week."Vague future; no specific evidence right now.

Types of evidence for going to predictions

Type of evidenceExampleWhat you're seeing/knowing
Visual sign"Look at those clouds — it's going to rain."You can see the clouds right now
Physical sensation"I feel terrible — I'm going to be sick."You feel it in your body now
Current situation"We're late — we're going to miss the bus."The time and the situation are clear
Known pattern / data"The early train's going to be packed — it always is."Past experience counts as evidence
Something you've read/seen"The forecast says it's going to snow."You have just read the information
📌 Key rule to remember The simplest test for predictions: Can I point to the evidence right now? Yes → going to. No, just an opinion or guess → will / think + will.

PRONUNCIATION

Gonna — how going to really sounds

The weak form in real speech

In formal written English, you write going to. But in natural spoken English — in conversations, films, podcasts, and real-life interactions — almost everyone says gonna. This is not a mistake. It is how the language actually sounds.

Full form → Natural spoken form going to → gonna  /ˈɡɒnə/
The g sound stays. The -ing to collapses into a quick, unstressed /ə/ sound.

Listen for these reductions

Written formSpoken formPhoneticExample sentence (spoken)
I am going toI'm gonna/aɪm ˈɡɒnə/"I'm gonna book the tickets."
She is going toShe's gonna/ʃiːz ˈɡɒnə/"She's gonna be late."
We are going toWe're gonna/wɪə ˈɡɒnə/"We're gonna miss the start."
It is going toIt's gonna/ɪts ˈɡɒnə/"It's gonna rain all day."
Are you going toYou gonna?/juː ˈɡɒnə/"You gonna get the late showing?"

When to use each form

Write: going to

  • Essays and formal writing
  • Emails to colleagues or teachers
  • Professional messages
  • Formal presentations

Say: gonna

  • Everyday conversation
  • Text messages with friends
  • Informal emails
  • Any relaxed spoken context
📌 Important Gonna only replaces going to + base verb (the future structure). It does not replace going to when it means physical movement: "I'm going to the cinema" (I am going there) ≠ "I'm going to see a film" (future plan). The first does NOT reduce to gonna; the second does.

Practise out loud

Say these sentences naturally, reducing going to to gonna:

I'm gonna watch something tonight. She's gonna love this film. We're gonna be late if we don't leave now. It's gonna be a great weekend. Are you gonna come with us? He's not gonna like that ending.

Movies & entertainment — the complete set

Why vocabulary matters here

You cannot talk about film plans convincingly if you only know the word "film". This section gives you the vocabulary to discuss films in detail: genres, opinions, the cinema experience, and how to recommend (or warn against) a film. These words come up constantly in real B1 conversations.

SUMMARY

Quick-reference summary

Going to — complete reference
Form (+) Subject + am/is/are going to + base verb
I'm going to see it.   She's going to be late.   We're going to book.
Form (−) Subject + am not / isn't / aren't going to + base verb
He isn't going to come.   I'm not going to be ready.
Form (?) Am/Is/Are + subject + going to + base verb?
Are you going to join us?   Is it going to rain?
Use 1 Plans & intentions — already decided before the moment of speaking
"I'm going to book the tickets now." (Tom has already decided)
Use 2 Evidence-based predictions — visible or known evidence points to a future event
"It's going to rain." (you can see the clouds)   "The train's going to be packed." (you know from experience)
vs Will Going to for pre-decided plans and evidence-based predictions.
Will for spontaneous decisions, general opinions, promises, and offers.
Pronunciation In natural speech: going to → gonna /ˈɡɒnə/
Only in writing and formal spoken contexts: full form going to.
Suggestions — complete reference
Let's + base verb"Let's meet at 7."Informal; speaker included; both agree to do it
Shall we + base verb?"Shall we go together?"Neutral; invitation to group; question form
Why don't we + base verb?"Why don't we try the late showing?"Implies a reason; informal
How about / What about + -ing?"How about getting coffee first?"Informal; always -ing after about
We could + base verb"We could take the earlier train."Softest; no commitment; polite
What if we + past simple?"What if we went on Sunday?"Tentative; useful when uncertain
