At the doctor’s — Marco’s appointment

Dialogue 👔 City Medical Centre — Saturday 10:00

Dr. Okafor: Good morning. Come in, please. Take a seat. What seems to be the problem?

Marco: Good morning, doctor. I haven’t felt well since Thursday evening. I have had a high fever, a sore throat and a really bad headache.

Dr. Okafor: I see. Have you taken your temperature?

Marco: Yes — it was 38.9 this morning. I have already taken some paracetamol but it hasn’t helped much.

Dr. Okafor: And have you had any other symptoms? A cough, difficulty breathing, a rash?

Marco: I have had a dry cough since yesterday. No rash. No breathing problems.

Dr. Okafor: Have you ever had tonsillitis or flu that required antibiotics?

Marco: I have had tonsillitis once, when I was sixteen. And I ’ve had flu a couple of times, but I have never needed antibiotics for it.

Dr. Okafor: Good. Have you travelled abroad recently?

Marco: No. I haven’t left the city in three months.

Dr. Okafor: Right. Let me examine you. (examines Marco) Your throat is inflamed and your lymph nodes are slightly swollen. This looks like a bacterial throat infection. I’m going to prescribe antibiotics. You need enough rest to recover — I would say at least five days. You are working too much for your body to fight this properly.

Marco: How long will it take to feel better?

Dr. Okafor: Most patients feel significantly better within 48 to 72 hours of starting the antibiotics. I’d recommend avoiding cold drinks and finishing the full course — don’t stop taking the medication early, even if you feel better.

Marco: I don’t mind resting if it means I get better faster. Thank you, doctor.

Dr. Okafor: You’re welcome. Here is your prescription. And please — stop trying to do everything at once. Your body needs a break.

NOTICE

What did you notice?

👀 Notice & discover

1. Look at the orange highlights in the chat. Marco says: “I have had a fever since yesterday evening.” When did the fever start? When did it end? Is it still happening?
It started yesterday evening and is still continuing now. The present perfect links a past event to the present moment. The fever has not ended.
2. Yuna says: “Have you ever been this ill before?” Is she asking about a specific time (last week, in 2019)? Or is she asking about Marco’s whole life experience up to now?
She is asking about his whole life experience — at any point in his life, has he been this ill? “Ever” signals a life-experience question. No specific time is mentioned.
3. Tom says: “I have already booked the tickets.” Marco says: “I haven’t been to the doctor yet.” What is the difference between already and yet?
Already = the action happened sooner than expected (positive surprise). Yet = we expected the action to have happened by now, but it hasn’t (used in negatives and questions).
4. Rasha says: “You’ve been working too hard” and “not getting enough sleep.” What is the difference between too and enough in meaning?
Too means more than is good/desirable (negative excess). Enough means the right amount. Not enough means less than needed. “Too hard” = more work than is healthy. “Not enough sleep” = less sleep than the body needs.
5. The doctor says: “I’d recommend avoiding cold drinks and finishing the full course.” What grammar structure follows recommend? What about avoid?
Both recommend and avoid are followed by a gerund (verb + -ing): avoiding, finishing. These verbs cannot be followed by a bare infinitive in standard English: ✗ recommend to avoid, ✗ avoid to finish.

STUDY 1

The Present Perfect — form & meaning

What is the present perfect?

Corpus note: The present perfect is one of the most frequent tenses in spoken British English (BNC). It connects a past action or state to the present moment.

Form
Subject + have / has + past participle
► I / You / We / They + have     He / She / It + has
I have eaten.  |  She has arrived.  |  They have finished.  |  He hasn’t called.
SubjectPositiveNegativeQuestion
I / You / We / Theyhave workedhaven’t workedHave you worked?
He / She / Ithas workedhasn’t workedHas she worked?

Four meanings of the present perfect

The present perfect has one form but four distinct meanings. The context (and key time words) tells you which meaning is being used.

1 📋 Life experience
Has this ever happened at any point in your life? No specific time mentioned.
“Have you ever had tonsillitis?”
“I have never been to Japan.”
everneverbefore
2 🕑 Unfinished situation
Something started in the past and continues right now. Still true at the moment of speaking.
“I have had a fever since Thursday.”
“She has worked here for ten years.”
forsince
3 ⏱ Recent news / relevance now
A recent action with a visible result or impact on the present.
“I have just had a bad cold.”
“She has broken her arm.” (she can’t play tennis now)
justrecentlylately
4 ✓ Completion before now
Has it happened yet? Did it happen before expected? Was it done already?
“I have already booked the tickets.”
“I haven’t been to the doctor yet.”
alreadyyetstill

The present perfect always connects PAST to NOW

e.g. Thursday

NOW
when you speak
Future

Compare: “I had a fever on Thursday.” (Past simple — finished, Thursday is over) vs “I have had a fever since Thursday.” (Present perfect — still now)

STUDY 2

For & since — already, yet & just

For vs Since

Both are used with the present perfect to describe unfinished situations, but they answer different questions.

FOR — a length of time

  • for two days
  • for three hours
  • for a week
  • for ten years
  • for a long time

Use FOR with a period / duration of time.

SINCE — a starting point

  • since Thursday
  • since 9 o’clock
  • since last week
  • since I was a child
  • since we met

Use SINCE with a point in time (when it started).

Already, yet & just — the three companions

These three adverbs are almost always used with the present perfect. Their position in the sentence matters.

AdverbMeaningUsed inPositionExample from the story
ALREADY Happened sooner than expected (positive) Positives & questions Between have and past participle “I have already taken some paracetamol.”
YET Expected but not happened (so far) Negatives & questions End of sentence “I haven’t been to the doctor yet.”
JUST Very recently — moments ago Positives Between have and past participle “I have just had a really bad cold.”
Common error ⚠ ✗ “Have you eaten already?” (possible but sounds surprised)  |  ✓ “Have you eaten yet?” (neutral question)
✗ “I haven’t eaten already.”  |  ✓ “I haven’t eaten yet.”
↗ The contrast that causes the most B1 errors. You know the present perfect. But when do you use the past simple instead? This contrast is the number one grammar difficulty at B1 according to learner corpus data. Study it carefully.
STUDY 3

Present Perfect vs Past Simple — the key contrast

The critical rule

Learner corpora (LINDSEI/ICLE) consistently show this is the most frequent B1 error. One rule decides almost everything:

🕐 Present Perfect
Use when there is NO specific finished time in the sentence. The connection to the present is what matters.

✓ “I have seen that film.”
✓ “Have you ever been ill?”
✓ “She has just arrived.”
📆 Past Simple
Use when there IS a specific finished time (last week, in 2019, yesterday, when I was young).

✓ “I saw that film last week.”
✓ “I was ill when I was sixteen.”
✓ “She arrived at 3pm.”
Present Perfect ✓Past Simple ✓Why
I have visited Paris. I visited Paris in 2018. 2018 = specific finished time → past simple
She has already taken the medicine. She took the medicine at 8am. 8am = specific finished time → past simple
Have you ever eaten sushi? Did you eat sushi last night? last night = specific finished time → past simple
I have seen him yesterday. ✗ I saw him yesterday. ✓ Yesterday = finished specific time → MUST use past simple
Time words that FORCE past simple (never use with present perfect) yesterday last week / month / year in 2019 / in March ago when I was a child at that time
Time words that signal present perfect ever / never already / yet / just for / since recently / lately so far / up to now

STUDY 4

Too & Enough — degree modifiers

What do too and enough mean?

Both express degree — how much of something there is. They always communicate something in relation to what is needed or desirable.

Position rules — where do too and enough go?

This is where most learners make errors. The position is fixed and different for each word.

StructurePositionPatternExample
TOO + adjective TOO comes before the adjective too + adj (+ to-infinitive) He is too tired to work. / You are working too hard.
TOO + adverb TOO comes before the adverb too + adv She speaks too quickly. / It ended too soon.
adjective/adverb + ENOUGH ENOUGH comes after the adj/adv adj + enough (+ to-infinitive) Is he well enough to travel? / She doesn’t sleep enough.
ENOUGH + noun ENOUGH comes before the noun enough + noun You need enough rest. / Do you have enough water?
NOT ENOUGH + noun Negative not + enough + noun He isn’t getting enough sleep. / There isn’t enough time.
⚠ Common error: position of ENOUGH with adjectives ✗ “He is enough tired.”  |  ✓ “He is tired enough.”
✗ “She has enough big room.”  |  ✓ “She has a big enough room.”

Too / enough + to-infinitive

Common patterns
too + adj + to + verb  →  He is too ill to go to work.
adj + enough + to + verb  →  She is well enough to travel.
The to-infinitive explains the purpose or result: too tired TO sleep / well enough TO fly.
↗ Last grammar point — and perhaps the trickiest. The doctor said: “I recommend avoiding cold drinks and finishing the full course.” Some verbs must be followed by an -ing form (gerund). Others take a to-infinitive. And a few change meaning depending on which you use. This is verb + gerund.
STUDY 5

Verb + Gerund — enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest

What is a gerund?

A gerund is the -ing form of a verb used as a noun. It follows certain verbs as their object. You cannot use a to-infinitive after these verbs — it is a fixed grammatical rule in English.

Structure
Verb + gerund (-ing)
I enjoy working.  |  She avoids eating sugar.  |  Have you finished reading?
enjoy + -ing
To get pleasure from doing something.
“I enjoy working, but I need balance.”
“She enjoys swimming in the sea.”
“Do you enjoy cooking?”
✗ I enjoy to work.  |  ✓ I enjoy working.
avoid + -ing
To deliberately not do something; to stay away from.
“The doctor said to avoid drinking cold things.”
“I avoid eating late at night.”
“He avoids going to the doctor.”
✗ Avoid to drink cold things.  |  ✓ Avoid drinking cold things.
finish + -ing
To complete something you were doing.
“Finish taking the full course of antibiotics.”
“Have you finished reading that report?”
“I need to finish writing this email.”
✗ Finish to take the medicine.  |  ✓ Finish taking the medicine.
mind + -ing
To object to / be bothered by. Used mainly in questions and negatives.
“I don’t mind resting if it helps.”
“Do you mind waiting a moment?”
“She doesn’t mind working at weekends.”
✗ I don’t mind to rest.  |  ✓ I don’t mind resting.
suggest + -ing (or + that-clause)
To put forward an idea for someone to consider. Two structures are possible; gerund is most common in spoken B1.
suggest + gerund:
“The doctor suggested resting for five days.”
“I suggest making an appointment today.”
suggest + that-clause:
“The doctor suggested that he rest for five days.”
“I suggest that you see a specialist.”
✗ She suggested to rest.  |  ✓ She suggested resting.  /  ✓ She suggested that he rest.

Quick reference — verb + gerund

These verbs are ALWAYS followed by gerund (-ing), never infinitive:
enjoy avoid finish mind suggest keep miss practise consider admit deny risk

VOCABULARY 1

Illnesses & symptoms — the essential set

Why this vocabulary matters

If you get sick in an English-speaking country — or visit a friend who is ill — you need precise language. Saying “my body hurts” is not enough. The words below are the most frequent in real doctor-patient conversations in English (BNC medical register data).

Symptoms — what you feel or notice

feverˈfiːvərn
A body temperature above normal (above ~37.5°C / 99.5°F), usually caused by infection.
“I’ve had a fever since Thursday evening.”
Collocates: high fever, mild fever, run a fever, temperature
sore throatsɔː θrəʊtn
Pain or irritation in the throat, especially when swallowing.
“I have a really bad sore throat — it hurts to swallow.”
Collocates: scratchy throat, raw throat, throat infection
headacheˈhedeɪkn
Pain in the head, ranging from mild to severe.
“I’ve had a splitting headache all day.”
Collocates: splitting headache, migraine, pounding, throbbing
coughkɒfn / v
A sudden, forceful expulsion of air from the lungs. Can be dry (no mucus) or chesty.
“I’ve had a dry cough since yesterday.”
Collocates: dry cough, chesty cough, persistent cough, cough up
nausea / nauseousˈnɔːziə / ˈnɔːziəs
The feeling that you are about to vomit; feeling sick to your stomach.
“I’ve been feeling nauseous since I woke up.”
Collocates: feel sick, feel nauseous, wave of nausea
rashræʃn
Red or irritated marks on the skin, usually caused by an allergic reaction or infection.
“Have you noticed any rash or skin irritation?”
Collocates: skin rash, allergic rash, hives, itchy
swollenˈswəʊlənadj
Larger than normal due to fluid or inflammation; a common sign of infection.
“Your lymph nodes are slightly swollen.”
Collocates: swollen glands, swollen lymph nodes, inflamed, puffy
dizziness / dizzyˈdɪzinəs / ˈdɪzi
A feeling of spinning or unsteadiness; feeling lightheaded.
“I’ve been feeling dizzy when I stand up quickly.”
Collocates: feel dizzy, lightheaded, loss of balance
fatiguefəˈtiːgn
Extreme tiredness, often as a symptom of illness. More formal than tiredness.
“I’ve been experiencing severe fatigue for the past week.”
Collocates: chronic fatigue, exhaustion, lethargy, worn out
pain / achepeɪn / eɪkn
Pain = general physical discomfort. Ache = a dull, continuous pain (backache, stomach ache, toothache).
“I’ve had a dull ache in my lower back all week.”
Collocates: sharp pain, dull ache, aching muscles, stomach ache

Illnesses — the names you need

tonsillitisˌtɒnsɪˈlaɪtɪsn
Inflammation of the tonsils (tissue at the back of the throat), usually caused by bacteria.
“I had tonsillitis once when I was sixteen.”
flu / influenzafluː / ˌɪnfluˈenzən
A viral infection with fever, aches, and fatigue. More serious than a common cold.
“I’ve had flu a couple of times, but I’ve never needed antibiotics.”
Collocates: come down with flu, flu jab, flu season
infectionɪnˈfekʃənn
Illness caused by bacteria, viruses, or fungi entering the body. Can be treated with antibiotics (bacterial only).
“This looks like a bacterial throat infection.”
Collocates: bacterial infection, viral infection, treat an infection
allergy / allergicˈælədʒi / əˈlɜːdʒɪkn / adj
An abnormal reaction by the immune system to a normally harmless substance.
“I’m allergic to penicillin. Have you got anything else?”
Collocates: allergic reaction, seasonal allergy, hay fever, intolerance

Life experience phrases — “Have you ever…?”

The present perfect is the natural grammar for talking about life experiences. These are the most frequent conversation starters in real English (COCA spoken corpus).

QuestionPossible answer (positive)Possible answer (negative)

💡 Notice the grammar switch “Have you ever been to hospital?” → Present perfect (life experience, no specific time)
“Yes, I went when I was twelve.” → Past simple (specific time given in the follow-up)

Exercise 1

True or False — grammar knowledge check

Decide if each statement is true or false, then click to reveal the explanation.

The present perfect is used with specific finished time words like “yesterday” and “last year”.

False. Specific finished time words (yesterday, last year, in 2020) force the past simple. “I saw him yesterday.” ✓ “I have seen him yesterday.” ✗

Yet is used in negative sentences and questions; already is used in positive statements and surprise questions.

True. “I haven’t finished yet.” / “Have you called yet?” — yet in negatives & questions. “I’ve already called.” — already in positives. Both are correct.

You use since with a period of time (e.g. since three hours) and for with a starting point (e.g. for Monday).

False. It is the opposite: FOR a period of time (for three hours) and SINCE a starting point (since Monday). A very common B1 confusion.

“Enough” comes after an adjective but before a noun.

True. “She is well enough.” (adjective + enough) / “She needs enough rest.” (enough + noun). This is the position rule for enough.

All five verbs studied this week (enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest) are followed by the gerund (-ing form), not the infinitive.

True. All five require gerund: enjoy working, avoid eating, finish taking, mind waiting, suggest resting. ✗ enjoy to work, ✗ avoid to eat, etc.

“She’s gone to the pharmacy” and “She’s been to the pharmacy” mean exactly the same thing.

False. Gone = she is still there (has not come back). Been = she went and has already returned. This is a classic B1 distinction that often appears in exams.

SUMMARY

Week 2 at a glance

🕐

Present Perfect

have / has + past participle — four meanings: experience, unfinished, recent news, completion. Key words: ever, never, for, since, already, yet, just.

📆

PP vs Past Simple

Specific finished time (yesterday, last year, in 2020) → past simple. No specific time / connection to now → present perfect. This is the critical rule.

📈

Too & Enough

Too + adj/adv (before). Adj/adv + enough (after). Enough + noun (before). Not enough + noun. Too/enough + to-infinitive for result.

⚙

Verb + Gerund

enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest → always followed by -ing. Never followed by to-infinitive. Suggest can also take a that-clause.

🎨

Illnesses vocab

fever, sore throat, headache, cough, nausea, rash, swollen, fatigue, tonsillitis, infection, allergy, antibiotics, prescription, recovery.

💬

Life experiences

“Have you ever…?” → present perfect. “Yes, I have — I [did] when I was…” → follow-up with past simple. gone vs been distinction essential.
