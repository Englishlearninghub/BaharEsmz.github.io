NOTICE

What did you notice?

👀 Notice & discover

1. Rasha says: “I’m going to call them tomorrow morning.” — and Tom says: “I’ll drive.” Both are talking about the future. What is different between these two?
Rasha already decided before she spoke — this is a pre-made plan. Tom decided at the moment of speaking (spontaneously, in response to what Rasha said). Going to = pre-made plan. Will = spontaneous decision made now.
2. Marco says: “The doctor said I’m going to be fully recovered in a few days.” Yuna says: “It’ll definitely be sunny on Saturday!” Both are predictions. What makes them different?
Marco’s prediction is based on evidence (the doctor’s diagnosis). Yuna’s prediction is based on the weather forecast she checked. Going to is often used when there is visible evidence for a prediction. Will is used for general opinions about the future (think, believe, expect, probably).
3. Tom says: “I’ll pay for the first round of drinks on the beach!” What kind of speech act is this — a plan, a prediction, or something else?
This is a promise — Tom is committing to do something for the group. Will is always used for promises, offers and spontaneous commitments. Not going to.
4. Look at the purple highlights: well, actually, I mean, you know. None of these add factual information to the sentence. Why do speakers use them? What purpose do they serve?
These are discourse markers — words that manage conversation rather than carry meaning. They give the speaker thinking time, soften a message, signal a correction or addition, or make the conversation feel more natural and collaborative. They are extremely common in real spoken English but almost never taught in EFL courses.
5. The hotel email uses both structures. Find one example of each. What decides which one is used?
“We are going to send a digital key” = a specific plan the hotel has already arranged. “The restaurant will be open until 22:30” = a fact about a future state (more like a schedule / general prediction). Context and speaker intention decide which form is more natural.

STUDY 1

Going to — plans & evidence-based predictions

Form: be + going to + infinitive

Corpus note: Going to is one of the most frequent future forms in spoken English (BNC/COCA). It was previewed in Grade 3 and taught in detail in Week 1 of this course. This study section reviews and deepens that knowledge in a new context.

Structure
Subject + am / is / are + going to + bare infinitive
Negative: am / is / are + not + going to + verb
Question: Am / Is / Are + subject + going to + verb?
I’m going to rest.  |  She isn’t going to come.  |  Are you going to book a room?  |  gonna /ˈɡɒnə/ in fast speech
SubjectPositiveNegativeQuestion
II’m going to driveI’m not going to driveAm I going to drive?
You / We / TheyYou’re going to driveYou aren’t going to driveAre you going to drive?
He / She / ItShe’s going to driveShe isn’t going to driveIs she going to drive?

Two uses of going to

📋 Use 1: Pre-made plans & intentions

A decision was made before the moment of speaking. The plan already exists.

  • “I’m going to call the hotel tomorrow morning.”
    (decided earlier, not just now)
  • “We’re going to spend the weekend at the coast.”
  • “I’m going to look up some options tonight.”
planintentiondecided before

👁 Use 2: Evidence-based predictions

You can see something happening now that makes a future outcome almost certain.

  • “The doctor said I’m going to be fully recovered.”
    (medical evidence)
  • “Look at those clouds — it’s going to rain.”
    (visible evidence now)
  • “Be careful — you’re going to drop that!”
visible evidencealmost certain
⚠ Spoken English: gonna /ˈɡɒnə/ In natural, fast speech, going to is often reduced to gonna: “I’m gonna call them.” This is completely normal and not “incorrect” in spoken English. In formal writing, always use the full form: going to. Never write gonna in emails or essays.
↗ Now the other half of the picture. You know what going to does. Now meet will — which covers several different jobs that going to cannot do: promises, spontaneous decisions, general predictions, offers and refusals.
STUDY 2

Will — promises, spontaneous decisions & predictions

Form: will + bare infinitive

Corpus note: Will is the most frequent future marker in both spoken and written English (COCA). It has multiple functions that learners at B1 must distinguish carefully.

Structure
Subject + will + bare infinitive
Contraction: I’ll, you’ll, he’ll, she’ll, it’ll, we’ll, they’ll
Negative: won’t (= will not)  |  Question: Will + subject + verb?
I’ll drive.  |  She won’t be late.  |  Will you help?  |  It’ll be sunny.

The five uses of will

Each use below is illustrated with an example from this week’s contexts. Knowing which use applies helps you choose will vs going to accurately.

UseMeaningSignal words / situationExample from the story
1. Spontaneous decision Made at the exact moment of speaking, not planned before Responding to a new situation / offer “I’ll drive if someone else books the hotel.” (Tom just decided now)
2. Promise A commitment to do something for someone else Often I’ll / we’ll — a personal commitment “I’ll pay for the first round of drinks!”
3. Offer Offering to do something to help Shall I? / I’ll + action “I’ll get your room keys sorted right now.”
4. General prediction What we think or expect will happen — no specific evidence visible now I think, I expect, probably, definitely, I’m sure “It’ll definitely be sunny on Saturday.” (checked the forecast)
5. Future fact / schedule A neutral statement about what will happen (events, rules, schedules) The restaurant / hotel / train… “The restaurant will be open until 22:30.”
⚠ Won’t = refusal or strong negative prediction “The car won’t start.” (refusal / failure)  |  “I won’t be late — I promise.” (strong negative prediction). Won’t is also used for refusals: the car is “refusing” to start. Very common in spoken English.
↗ The contrast that causes the most B1 errors. You now have both forms separately. The crucial skill at B1 is knowing which to choose, and why, in any given context. This is where most learners struggle — because both forms are about the future.

Going to vs Will — the contrast

The decision-time test

This single question resolves almost every going to vs will choice: Was the decision made before this moment, or at this moment?

📋 Going to
Decision made BEFORE now  |  Evidence visible NOW

“Look at those clouds — it’s going to rain.” (can see the evidence)
⚡ Will
Decision made NOW  |  Promise / Offer  |  General prediction
“I’ll drive!” (spontaneous — just decided)
“I’ll pay for drinks.” (promise)
“It’ll probably be fine.” (opinion)

Common errors at B1

Errors to avoid ✗ “I will call the hotel tomorrow.” (if you already decided)  →  ✓ “I’m going to call the hotel tomorrow.”
✗ “I’m going to pay for this” (spontaneous offer at checkout)  →  ✓ “I’ll pay for this.”
✗ “Look — he will fall!” (you can see it now)  →  ✓ “He’s going to fall!”

Present continuous as future — a note

A third future form that native speakers use constantly is the present continuous for future: “We’re arriving at 3pm.” This is used for fixed, arranged plans — especially where a time is specified. Very close to going to in meaning.

STUDY 4

Discourse markers — well, actually, I mean, you know

What are discourse markers?

Corpus note: COCA spoken data consistently shows these four items are among the most frequent words in natural spoken English. Removing them makes speech sound robotic and unnaturally formal. EFL learners who do not use them sound mechanical; learners who use them well sound natural and fluent.

Definition Discourse markers are words or phrases that manage the conversation rather than carry factual meaning. They signal transitions, soften statements, buy thinking time, correct misunderstandings, and build connection with the listener. They almost never affect the truth value of a sentence — but they dramatically affect how natural the speaker sounds.

The four discourse markers this week

well

Functions: thinking time · softening a disagreement or unexpected response · introducing hesitation
“Well, I’m feeling much better already.” (softens — slightly unexpected good news)
“Well, the restaurant won’t be serving until 18:00…” (softens disappointing news)
“Well, I’m not sure — can I think about it?” (hesitation — buying time)
“Did you enjoy the trip?” — “Well, it was interesting.” (hedged / qualified answer)
⚠ Well as an adverb = “She speaks English well.” This is completely different from well as a discourse marker at the start of a turn.

actually

Functions: introducing a correction or contrast · adding surprising or unexpected information · softening a disagreement
“Actually, I know a really nice place — my cousin stayed there.” (adds information)
“We were wondering — is it possible to get an early dinner? Actually, we’re quite hungry.”
“I thought it was expensive, but actually the prices are quite reasonable.” (correction / contrast)
“Do you like jazz?” — “Actually, I love it.” (contrast with possible expectation)
⚠ Actually does NOT mean “at the present time” (that is “currently” or “at the moment”). This is a very common false friend error, especially for Spanish, French and Turkish speakers.

I mean

Functions: clarifying or rephrasing something just said · correcting yourself · adding detail to avoid misunderstanding
“I mean, I’m still a bit tired, but the doctor said I’ll be fine.” (clarification)
“It was great — I mean, not perfect, but really enjoyable.” (qualification)
“We should leave early. I mean, around 7 at the latest.” (adds precision)
“She was brilliant. I mean, genuinely one of the best speakers I’ve heard.” (emphasis)
⚠ I mean as a discourse marker is informal. In formal writing, use that is to say or in other words. Do not start a formal essay with “I mean.”

you know

Functions: appealing for shared understanding · softening an assumption · buying thinking time · building rapport with the listener
“You know, actually we’ll probably want a 9 o’clock table.” (casual, building connection)
“It’s just one of those days, you know?” (appealing for empathy)
“I was nervous, you know, it was my first time presenting.” (softens personal admission)
“You know that feeling when everything just clicks? That was today.”
⚠ You know can be overused — it is a very frequent speech marker, but using it in every sentence sounds unnatural. In spoken English: a few times naturally. Never in formal writing.

Talking about the weather on a trip

sunny / overcast / cloudy
Sunny = bright sun. Overcast = completely covered by clouds (dull, grey). Cloudy = some clouds but not complete cover.
“It’s been cloudy all week, but it’ll be sunny on Saturday.”
mild / chilly / breezy
Mild = pleasantly warm, not hot. Chilly = noticeably cold. Breezy = pleasantly windy (coastal term).
“It’s going to be mild and breezy — perfect for the beach.”
clear up / brighten up
When clouds disappear and it becomes sunny after being cloudy.
“Don’t worry — I think it’ll clear up by the afternoon.”
Collocates: the weather will clear up, it should brighten up later
pour down / drizzle / shower
Pour down = rain very heavily. Drizzle = very light fine rain. Shower = short period of rain.
“It’s going to pour down this afternoon — I checked the forecast.”

1. “Look — those children will fall off that wall!”
✗ Error: visible evidence right now → use going to. ✓ Correct: “Those children are going to fall off that wall!”

2. “Who wants tea? — I’m going to make it.” (spontaneous response)
✗ Error: spontaneous offer made at this moment → use will. ✓ Correct: “I’ll make it.”

3. “We’re going to arrive at the hotel around 3pm — I booked the rooms last week.”
✓ Already correct. The rooms were booked last week = pre-made plan → going to is exactly right here. Do not change it.
?
4. “I know a great restaurant. Actually I went there last month.” (said as a contrast)
✓ The sentence is grammatically fine. But this sentence is actually correct! Actually is being used appropriately to add/contrast information. However, the comma is missing after Actually in spoken transcription, and the sentence-internal position can vary. Accept as correct.
?
5. “I’ll not be late — I promise.”
✗ Error: the negative of will in natural English is won’t, not I’ll not. The form I’ll not sounds unnatural in standard British or American English. ✓ Correct: “I won’t be late — I promise.”

SUMMARY

Week 3 at a glance

📋

Going to

Pre-made plans (decided before) + evidence-based predictions (can see it now). Form: am/is/are + going to + infinitive.

⚡

Will

Spontaneous decisions + promises + offers + general predictions (I think…). Contraction: ’ll. Negative: won’t.

⚖

The contrast

Key question: was the decision made BEFORE or AT this moment? Before → going to. At this moment → will.

💬

Discourse markers

well (soften / hesitate) · actually (contrast / correct) · I mean (clarify) · you know (build rapport). Spoken only.

✈

Travel vocabulary

trip, journey, destination, itinerary, depart/arrive, accommodation, forecast, book, catch, check in/out.

🏢

Hotel vocabulary

double room, en suite, reservation, check-in, reception, amenities, room service, availability, self-catering.
