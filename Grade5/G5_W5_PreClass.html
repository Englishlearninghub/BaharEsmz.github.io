Frau Huber: Good morning, Ms Berger, Mr Fischer. Please, sit down. So, you're looking for a renovation loan for the guesthouse — congratulations on the progress so far.
Nora: Thank you. We've almost finished the renovation, but we need around €30,000 more to complete the bathroom and buy furniture.
Frau Huber: That's very manageable. Let me show you two options. Loan A has a fixed interest rate of 3.4%; Loan B has a variable rate starting at 2.9%.

Frau Huber: That's a very common preference for a new business. Over five years, Loan A is almost as expensive as Loan B, but it's far less risky, because the payment never changes.
Nora: What would you recommend for a business like ours?
Frau Huber: Honestly, I'd recommend the fixed rate. You should choose stability while you're still new. You could also consider borrowing a little less and finishing part of the work yourselves.
Lukas: That's actually what I'd rather do — borrow less and finish the bathroom ourselves.
Frau Huber: That's a sensible plan. Now, in terms of paperwork: you must submit your business registration by Friday, and I ought to mention you'll also need your renovation invoices.
Nora: We have most of them already. Is there anything else we have to bring?
Frau Huber: Just proof of income, and a form of ID if you haven't already provided one. You don't have to decide on the loan today, but the current offer expires in fourteen days.
Lukas: And if we have more questions before then?
Frau Huber: You could call me directly. I'll send you the final offer by email tomorrow morning.
Nora: Perfect. Thank you so much for your time.
Frau Huber: My pleasure. Good luck with the guesthouse — I think you're going to do very well.
23 March, 11:20 — right after the meeting

Lukas
Good — that's what I'd rather have anyway. Did she say anything about the paperwork?

Yes — she told us that we had to submit the business registration by Friday. She also said she would send the final offer by email tomorrow.

Notice the Pattern

Go back to the documents. Look closely before reading on.

A. Preferences. Find "I'd rather have a fixed rate" and "I prefer fixed rates." Both express a preference, but one is followed directly by a noun, and one needs a different structure to compare two things ("I'd rather borrow less than borrow the full amount"). Can you see the difference in what follows each expression?

B. Advice. Find "you should choose stability," "you must submit your business registration," and "you ought to keep a reserve fund." All give advice or obligation — but are they equally strong? Which one sounds like a rule, and which ones sound like recommendations?

C. Equal comparison. Find "Loan A is almost as expensive as Loan B." What two things are being compared, and are they equal, or is one bigger?

D. Reported speech. Now compare Frau Huber's exact words in the meeting notes with what Nora says afterwards on WhatsApp. "I'd recommend..." becomes "she said she'd recommend..." "You must submit..." becomes "she told us that we had to submit..." "I'll send..." becomes "she said she would send..." What happens to the verbs when we report what someone said?

A. "Would rather" is followed by a base-form verb (rather have, rather borrow) and uses "than" for comparison. "Prefer" is often followed directly by a noun or by "-ing," and uses "to" for comparison (prefer X to Y).

B. "Must" = a strict obligation/rule (no choice). "Should / ought to" = a recommendation or advice (there is a choice, but it's a good idea).

C. Loan A and Loan B are being compared, and "almost as expensive as" shows they are nearly equal in cost, with Loan A very slightly less than fully equal.

D. When we report what someone said, the verb usually moves one step back into the past: am/is → was, will → would, must → had to. This is called "backshift," and it's the seed of what you'll study fully as reported speech in Grade 6.

You've just noticed four real grammar patterns in one short conversation. Sections 3–6 now name and explain each one properly.

3

Grammar Focus — Prefer / Would Rather

Expressing preferences

ExpressionFollowed byComparing two thingsExample
prefernoun / -ing formprefer X to YI prefer fixed rates to variable ones.
would preferto + infinitivewould prefer to X rather than YI'd prefer to wait rather than rush the decision.
would ratherbase form (no "to")would rather X than YI'd rather have a fixed rate than a variable one.

The most common learner error

Learners often add "to" after would rather by mistake, copying the pattern of would prefer. Remember: would rather + base verb, never "would rather to."

✗ I'd rather to borrow less.

✓ I'd rather borrow less.

3.1 Talking about someone else's preference

"Would rather" can also take a second subject, followed by the simple past (not present!) even though the meaning is present or future:

  • I'd rather you called the bank, not me. (present meaning, past form)
  • Nora would rather Lukas dealt with the paperwork.

1. I prefer saving money to spending it immediately.
2. We'd rather talk to the advisor in person than by phone.
3. Lukas would prefer to sign the papers this week rather than wait.
4. I'd rather you didn't tell the whole family about the loan yet.

Grammar Focus — Modal Verbs for Advice

should · ought to · must · have to · could (extended)

ModalStrengthMeaningExample
should / ought toRecommendationa good idea, but not obligatoryYou should keep a reserve fund.
mustStrong obligationa rule, often from the speaker's authority or a real requirementYou must submit the registration by Friday.
have toStrong obligationa requirement, often from an outside rule or system (not the speaker personally)New businesses have to provide two years of projected income.
couldA suggestion / possibilityone option among several — softer than shouldYou could also consider a smaller loan.

Must vs. have to — a subtle but useful distinction

Must often expresses the speaker's own authority or strong personal opinion ("I, the advisor, insist..."). Have to often reports an external rule or system ("the bank's policy requires..."). In everyday spoken English the two frequently overlap, but the distinction matters most in the negative:

mustn't = it is forbidden. don't have to = it is not necessary (but you may if you wish).

✓ You don't have to decide today. (no obligation)

✓ You mustn't sign anything without reading it first. (forbidden)

4.1 Giving advice politely

In real spoken English, advice is often softened. Compare the directness:

  • Direct: You should choose the fixed rate.
  • Softer: I think you should probably choose the fixed rate.
  • Very soft: If I were you, I'd choose the fixed rate.

1. New applicants have to provide two forms of ID — it's bank policy, not my personal opinion.
2. You mustn't miss the Friday deadline — the offer will expire.
3. You could also ask about a smaller loan if €30,000 feels like too much.
4. I really think you should talk to an accountant before deciding.

5

Grammar Focus — As + Adjective + As

Comparing two things that are equal, or nearly equal

Form

as + adjective + as (equal)  ·  not as + adjective + as (unequal, first thing is less)

PatternMeaningExample
as X asequal / the same degreeLoan A is almost as expensive as Loan B.
not as X asthe first thing is less X than the secondThe variable rate isn't as safe as the fixed rate.
just as X asemphasises true equalityA small loan can be just as useful as a large one.

Quick note: "not as...as" is far more common in real spoken English than its logical opposite "less...than," which sounds more formal. Corpus data shows native speakers prefer "not as safe as" over "less safe than" in everyday conversation.

1. Frau Huber's advice was as clear as we'd hoped.
2. A fixed-rate loan isn't always as cheap as a variable one at first.
3. Finishing the bathroom themselves would be just as satisfying as hiring someone.

Grammar Focus — Reported Speech (Preview)

say / tell + that-clause — a first look, full rules coming in Grade 6

When we report what someone said, instead of quoting their exact words, the verb tense usually moves one step back into the past. This is called backshift.

"I'd recommend the fixed-rate loan."
(would — stays the same)
→
She said she'd recommend the fixed-rate loan.
"You must submit your registration."
→
She told us that we had to submit our registration.
"I'll send you the offer tomorrow."
→
She said she would send us the offer the next day.
Direct speechReported speech
am / is / arewas / were
willwould
musthad to
cancould
today / tomorrowthat day / the next day

Say vs. Tell

Say — no personal object needed: She said (that) she was busy. If you name the listener, you need "to": She said to me that... (less common).

Tell — always needs a personal object: She told us that... ✗ She told that... is incorrect.

This is only a preview — you'll meet the full system of reported questions, commands, and time-expression changes in Grade 6. For now, focus on recognising the pattern and using say/tell + that for simple statements.

1. Lukas: "I'm worried about the interest rate." → Lukas said (that) he was worried about the interest rate.
2. Frau Huber: "You can apply online." → She told us (that) we could apply online.
3. Nora: "We'll decide by Friday." → Nora said (that) they would decide by Friday.

loanləʊnn

A sum of money that a bank lends you, which you pay back over time, usually with interest.
Collocates: apply for a loan, take out a loan, repay a loan, loan application
interest raten phrase
"Loan A has a fixed interest rate of 3.4%."
The percentage a bank charges you (or pays you) for borrowing (or saving) money, usually calculated per year.

Collocates: a fixed-rate mortgage, a variable-rate loan, switch to a fixed rate
collateralkəˈlætərəln, uncountable

Vocabulary — Reporting Verbs

The verbs we use to report what someone said — each has its own grammar pattern, so learn the pattern together with the word.

sayseɪv
"Frau Huber said she'd recommend the fixed-rate loan."
To report someone's words. Pattern: say + (that) + clause. No personal object needed.
Pattern: say (that) + clause — NOT "say to me that..." in reported statements.
telltelv
"She told us that we had to submit our registration."
To report someone's words, always naming who was told. Pattern: tell + person + (that) + clause.
Pattern: tell someone (that) + clause — never "tell that..." without a person.
explainɪkˈspleɪnv
Frau Huber explained that the fixed rate was safer because the payment never changes.
To give reasons or make something clear by giving more detail than a simple statement.
Pattern: explain (that) + clause / explain something to someone
mentionˈmenʃənv
"I ought to mention you'll also need your renovation invoices."
To say something briefly, often as an extra or secondary piece of information.
Pattern: mention (that) + clause / mention something (to someone)
promiseˈprɒmɪsv
Frau Huber didn't just say she might send the offer — she made it a firm commitment: "I'll send you the final offer tomorrow" → she promised to send it.
To give a firm, reliable commitment to do something in the future.
Pattern: promise to + infinitive / promise (that) + clause
warnwɔːnv
A financial advisor might warn a client not to miss the fourteen-day deadline.
To tell someone about a possible danger, problem, or unpleasant consequence in advance.
Pattern: warn someone (not) to do something / warn someone that + clause
adviseədˈvaɪzv
Frau Huber advised Lukas and Nora to keep a reserve fund.
To give a professional or personal recommendation about what someone should do.
Pattern: advise someone to + infinitive (note: the noun is "advice," spelled with a c)
recommendˌrekəˈmendv
"I'd recommend the fixed-rate loan."
To suggest that something is good, suitable, or the best choice.
Pattern: recommend + noun / recommend -ing / recommend that someone do something

Practice — Fix the Errors

Five sentences below. One of them is already correct — don't "fix" a sentence that has nothing wrong with it.

1. I'd rather to choose the fixed rate.

2. She told that we had to submit the form by Friday.

3. Loan A isn't as cheap as Loan B in year one.

4. Nora said she will send the papers tomorrow.

5. You not have to decide today, but you mustn't miss next month's deadline.

1. I'd rather choose the fixed rate. (no "to" after would rather)
2. She told us that we had to submit the form by Friday. (tell always needs a personal object)
3. ✅ Correct as written.
4. Nora said she would send the papers the next day. (backshift: will → would; tomorrow → the next day, when reported later)
5. You don't have to decide today, but you mustn't miss next month's deadline. (missing auxiliary "don't")

Skills — Listening: The Bank's Voicemail

Transcript-based listening task — read aloud with a partner or record yourselves, then answer.

From: Frau Huber (Alpen Bank Wien) → To: Nora — 24 March, 09:40

"Good morning, Ms Berger. I'm calling to follow up on yesterday's meeting. I said I'd send the final offer today, and I've just emailed it to you — please check your inbox. I'd recommend reading the terms carefully before you sign; you don't have to decide immediately, but the offer expires in fourteen days. If you'd rather discuss anything by phone first, you could call me directly. Also, a small reminder: you must still send the missing invoice from February. Speak soon."

Listening check 1: What did Frau Huber say she had already done?

She said she'd send the final offer that day, and she had just emailed it.

Listening check 2: What is Nora NOT obliged to do immediately, and what is she still required to do?

She doesn't have to decide immediately (though the offer expires in 14 days), but she must still send the missing invoice from February.

Listening check 3: What alternative does Frau Huber offer if Nora would rather not decide by reading alone?

Nora could call Frau Huber directly to discuss anything by phone first.
