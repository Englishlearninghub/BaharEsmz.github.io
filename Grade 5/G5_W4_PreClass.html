Notice the Pattern

Go back to the diary. Look closely at the yellow and green highlights before reading on.

A. Yellow highlights — all use a verb form with have/has + past participle. Some are followed by for, some by since, and some contain the words already, yet, or just.

Look at "for six weeks" and "since we started." One tells you a length of time. One tells you a starting point. Which is which?

Now look at "already finished," "haven't started yet," and "has just left." One means "before now, earlier than expected." One means "not now, but we expect it soon." One means "a very short time ago." Can you match them?

B. Green highlights — words like himself, herself, themselves. In each case, who is doing the action, and who receives it? Are they the same person, or different people?

for + a length of time (for six weeks). since + a starting point (since we started, since the 1980s).

already = before now / sooner than expected. yet = not now, used in negatives/questions, expecting it soon. just = a very short time ago.

In every green example, the person doing the action and the person affected by it are the same person — that's exactly what a reflexive pronoun signals.

You have just done what linguists call noticing — spotting a pattern in real language before anyone gives you a rule. Section 3 and 4 now give that pattern a name.

3

Grammar Focus — Present Perfect

for · since · already · yet · just

Form

have / has + past participle

We've renovated the kitchen.  ·  Lukas has cut himself.  ·  They haven't started the plumbing.

3.1 For vs. Since — talking about duration

We use the present perfect to connect the past to the present: an action or state that started before now and is still relevant, true, or continuing.

WordUsed withExample from the diary
fora period of time (how long)We've been renovating this flat for six weeks.
sincea point in time (when it started)The kitchen hasn't been touched since the 1980s.

Quick test: if you can answer the question "How long?" with your time expression, use for. If it answers "Since when?", use since.

1 Feb (start) ————— since here —————> 20 March (now)

for six weeks = the whole distance between the two points.

3.2 Already, Yet, Just — talking about recent or expected events

WordMeaningPositionSentence type
alreadysooner than expected; before nowmid-position, between have/has and the participleaffirmative — We've already finished the electrics.
yetnot now, but expected soonend of the sentencenegatives and questions — We haven't chosen the tiles yet. Have you finished yet?
justa very short time agomid-position, between have/has and the participleaffirmative — The electrician has just left.

Corpus note (EGP frequency order)

Learners usually meet these three words in the order for → since → already → yet → just because that's roughly how often they appear in real spoken and written English at B1. If you can use "already" and "yet" comfortably, "just" tends to follow quickly — the three share the same grammatical slot.

3.3 A quick contrast: Present Perfect vs. Simple Past

You met the simple past in Grade 4. The present perfect is not about finishing a story — it's about the connection to now.

Simple PastPresent Perfect
Focusa finished moment in the past, often with a specific timethe result now, or an unfinished period up to now
ExampleLukas cut himself on 10 February.Lukas has cut himself — his hand is still bandaged.

1. We haven't slept properly since the renovation began.
2. Marek has worked with us for three months.
3. Nobody has used that staircase since the bathtub incident.
4. The paint has been drying for two days.

Grammar Focus — Reflexive Pronouns

myself · yourself · himself · herself (singular first)

Form

Subject pronoun + -self (singular) or -selves (plural)

SubjectReflexive pronoun
Imyself
you (singular)yourself
hehimself
sheherself
ititself
weourselves
you (plural)yourselves
theythemselves

We focus on the singular forms this week — myself, yourself, himself, herself — because learner data shows these cause the most confusion when speakers default to object pronouns instead ("he hurt him" instead of "he hurt himself"). The plural forms follow the same pattern.

4.1 When the subject and object are the same person

Use a reflexive pronoun when the person doing the action and the person affected by it are the same:

  • Lukas cut himself. (Lukas cut Lukas — same person.)
  • Nora burned herself on the blowtorch.
  • Did you hurt yourself carrying that tile?

Compare: Lukas cut himself. (his own hand)  vs.  Lukas cut him. (someone else's hand — a different person entirely.) Mixing these up is one of the most common B1 errors in learner corpora, especially with verbs like hurt, cut, burn, injure.

4.2 Reflexive pronouns for emphasis

A second, very different use: adding a reflexive pronoun to emphasise that someone did something alone, without help, or that they, personally, did it (not somebody else).

  • Lukas taught himself how to lay tiles. (no teacher — he learned alone)
  • I painted the whole room myself. (nobody helped me)
  • The architect designed the plan herself. (emphasis: she, personally)

Notice the difference in position: the true reflexive sits right after the verb (cut himself); the emphatic reflexive often moves to the end of the clause and can usually be removed without changing the core meaning.

4.3 Common expressions with reflexive pronouns

ExpressionMeaning
enjoy yourself / myself / himself...have a good time
help yourselfserve yourself (food, tools, etc.)
behave yourselfact politely / correctly
by myself / by himselfalone, without company or help
talk to yourselfspeak aloud with no listener

1. Marek warned Nora, but she didn't listen and burned herself anyway.
2. The workers hurt themselves lifting the bathtub, but nobody hurt them on purpose.
3. Lukas is proud that he fixed the wiring himself — he didn't hire an electrician for that part.
4. Please, help yourself to a coffee while you wait.

Vocabulary — Renovation & Construction

Every word below appeared in Section 1. Read the example from the story before the definition — figure out the meaning from context first.

staircaseˈsteəkeɪsn
"Two workers hurt themselves carrying the bathtub up a narrow staircase."
A set of stairs, usually indoors, connecting one floor of a building to another.
Collocates: narrow/spiral staircase, go up/down the staircase, at the top of the staircase
wiringˈwaɪərɪŋn, uncountable
"The electrician has just left — he says the wiring is finally safe."
The system of electrical wires installed inside the walls of a building.
Collocates: faulty/exposed wiring, rewire a flat, check the wiring
plumbingˈplʌmɪŋn, uncountable
"We haven't started the plumbing yet, though."
The system of pipes that carries water and gas through a building.
Collocates: plumbing problem, call a plumber, connect the plumbing
blowtorchˈbləʊtɔːtʃn
"Nora burned herself on the blowtorch while soldering a pipe."
A small tool that produces a very hot, controlled flame, used for joining metal or removing old paint.
Collocates: light/use a blowtorch, burn yourself on the blowtorch
solderˈsɒldərv
"...while soldering a pipe" — joining two pieces of metal together using melted metal.
To join metal parts (like pipes or wires) by melting a soft metal between them.
Collocates: solder a pipe/joint, solder together, a soldering iron
demolish / demolitiondɪˈmɒlɪʃ / ˌdeməˈlɪʃənv / n
"Demolition day. Lukas cut himself on a piece of broken tile while pulling up the old kitchen floor."
To pull down or destroy a structure completely, usually before building something new.
Collocates: demolish a wall, demolition work, demolition day
renovate / renovationˈrenəveɪt / ˌrenəˈveɪʃənv / n
"We've been renovating this flat for six weeks now, and it finally looks like a home."
To repair and improve an old building so it's in good condition again.
Collocates: a major/minor renovation, renovate a property, mid-renovation
contractorkənˈtræktərn
"I've just spoken to the plumber," writes Marek, the contractor, in the group chat.
A person or company hired to carry out building or repair work, usually managing a team of workers.
Collocates: hire a contractor, the site contractor, contractor's team
cast-ironˌkɑːst ˈaɪənadj
"...while carrying a cast-iron bathtub up a narrow staircase."
Made of a very hard, heavy type of iron — old bathtubs were traditionally made from it.
Collocates: cast-iron bathtub, cast-iron pipe, heavy as cast iron
non-slip matn phrase
"The site has already installed non-slip mats on the stairs since the accident."
A mat with a rough or textured surface designed to prevent people from slipping.
Collocates: install non-slip mats, a non-slip surface

Vocabulary — Injuries & Safety

Read the story example first, then the definition. Notice which verbs are almost always followed by a reflexive pronoun.

cut yourselfv phrase
"Lukas cut himself on a piece of broken tile while pulling up the old kitchen floor."
To injure your skin with something sharp. Almost always used with a reflexive pronoun when you injure your own body.
Collocates: cut yourself badly/slightly, cut yourself on something
burn yourselfv phrase
"Nora burned herself on the blowtorch while soldering a pipe."
To injure your skin with heat or fire.
Collocates: burn yourself on something, get burned
twist an ankletwɪstv phrase
"One employee lost his footing and twisted his ankle."
To injure a joint (usually the ankle or wrist) by turning it suddenly and awkwardly.
Collocates: twist an ankle/wrist/knee, a twisted ankle
scrapeskreɪpv
"...his colleague, trying to steady the tub, scraped his arm against the wall."
To injure the surface of your skin by rubbing it against something rough or hard.
Collocates: scrape your arm/knee/elbow, a scraped knee
lose your footingidiom
"One employee lost his footing and twisted his ankle."
To suddenly lose your balance and start to slip or fall.
Collocates: lose your footing on the stairs/ice
first-aid kitn phrase
Lukas jokes that they should buy a first-aid kit "in bulk" after two accidents.
A small box or bag containing basic supplies (bandages, plasters) for treating minor injuries.
Collocates: keep a first-aid kit, a fully-stocked first-aid kit
incident reportn phrase
"I've just finished the incident report," Marek tells Nora and Lukas.
An official written record describing exactly what happened during an accident at work.
Collocates: submit/file an incident report, according to the incident report
risk assessmentn phrase
Not directly quoted, but implied by the site's new rules: a risk assessment would identify the staircase as dangerous.
A formal process of identifying possible dangers before work begins, so they can be prevented.
Collocates: carry out a risk assessment, a thorough risk assessment
touch woodidiom, spoken
"Not yet, touch wood. We've had enough drama for one renovation."
Said after mentioning good luck, to (superstitiously) avoid tempting bad luck to happen.
Used alone, usually at the end of a sentence about something going well so far.
More Safety & Injury Words to Know

bruise (n/v) · bandage (n/v) · need hospital treatment (v phrase) · see a doctor (v phrase) · recover (v) · wear protective gear (v phrase) · lift in pairs (v phrase) · a minor/serious injury (adj phrase) · submit a report (v phrase)

Practice — Fix the Errors

Five sentences below. One of them is already correct — don't "fix" a sentence that has nothing wrong with it. Click reveal to check your work.

1. Nora has burn herself on the blowtorch.

2. We haven't chosen the tiles already.

3. Lukas taught himself how to lay tiles.

4. Marek has worked with us since three months.

5. The workers hurt them moving the bathtub.

1. Nora has burned herself on the blowtorch. (past participle needed)
2. We haven't chosen the tiles yet. (negative sentences use yet, not already)
3. ✅ Correct as written. "Taught himself" is a genuine reflexive — no change needed.
4. Marek has worked with us for three months. (a period of time takes for, not since)
5. The workers hurt themselves moving the bathtub. (same person acting and affected → reflexive pronoun)

Speaking Practice

Lukas and Nora's renovation is full of things to say out loud: what they've done so far, who got hurt and how, and what still isn't finished. These tasks put this week's grammar in your voice. All you need is your phone's voice memo app.

Retell task

The diary, spoken

Re-read the Renovation Diary. Close it. Now tell the story of the week in 60–90 seconds, as if you were Lukas leaving a voice message for a friend: what you've already finished, what you haven't done yet, and what went wrong. The Present Perfect carries the progress report: "We've painted the hallway… we haven't tiled the bathroom yet… Nora's hurt herself again…"

How: Record it like a real voice message — one take, natural speed. When you listen back, check: progress up to now = Present Perfect (we've finished); finished-time details = Past Simple (she cut her hand on Tuesday).
Sentence challenge

Six words, sixty seconds each

Use each of these items from this week's vocabulary in a spoken sentence about a real (or invented) home project. 60 seconds per word — and notice how many need a reflexive pronoun:

  • to renovate
  • scaffolding
  • to hurt yourself
  • a bruise
  • a first-aid kit
  • to do it yourself
How: One continuous recording, timer running. When you listen back, check the reflexives: I hurt myself, she cut herself, we did it ourselves — the pronoun must match the subject.
Discussion questions

For a partner — or the microphone

Discuss with a partner, or record spoken answers (about 60 seconds each). Choose two:

  • Have you ever renovated, repaired, or built something yourself? How did it go — honestly?
  • DIY or professionals: which would you choose for your own home, and why?
  • Have you ever hurt yourself doing something at home? What happened?
  • Lukas and Nora are renovating a guesthouse together. Could you work on a big project with your partner or best friend — or would it end the relationship?
How: One question per recording. When you listen back, check: every "Have you ever…?" answer should open in the Present Perfect and switch to Past Simple for the story.

Technique: the progress report shape

  • All progress reports have the same three-part shape: done (we've finished…), not done (we haven't… yet), problem (and unfortunately…). Learn the shape once; fill it forever.
  • Reflexives are rhythm words: hurt-my-SELF, by-them-SELVES. Stress the self.
  • Yet lives at the end of the sentence: "We haven't finished the kitchen yet." Park it there every time.

Skills — Listening: The Voicemail

Transcript-based listening task — read the transcript aloud with a partner, or record yourselves reading it, then answer.

From: Marek (contractor) → To: Nora — 21 March, 08:15

"Hi Nora, it's Marek. I've just arrived at the flat and I wanted to update you. The plumber has already connected the new bathtub, and it's working perfectly — no leaks so far, touch wood. We haven't finished the bathroom tiling yet, though; we're still waiting for the tiles you ordered. They said they've had the order since Monday, but it hasn't been delivered yet. Also — small thing — one of the new guys scraped himself on some old wiring in the hallway this morning. He's fine, just a small scratch, nothing like the bathtub incident. Give me a call when you've got a minute. Bye for now."

Listening check 1: What has already happened, according to Marek?

The plumber has connected the new bathtub, and it is working with no leaks so far.

Listening check 2: What hasn't happened yet, and why?

The bathroom tiling hasn't been finished yet because the ordered tiles haven't been delivered yet, even though the shop has had the order since Monday.

Listening check 3: What happened to the new worker, and how serious was it?

He scraped himself on old wiring in the hallway. It was minor — just a small scratch, much less serious than the bathtub incident.

Explore More — Independent Learning

Grammar and vocabulary are bigger than one worksheet. Choose at least two of these before next class and bring back one thing you discovered.

Watch

Search YouTube for a short "home renovation fail" or DIY-disaster clip in English. Note down every present perfect sentence you hear in the commentary or captions.

Investigate

Talk

Reflect
