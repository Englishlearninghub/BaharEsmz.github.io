THE SCENE

Vienna Climate Conference — Thursday morning

Chen’s first major assignment as Third Secretary.

Three weeks into her new posting, A. Chen is presenting climate data to the Vienna International Panel on Environmental Policy. She has prepared three documents: her presenter’s notes, a written briefing paper, and a short diplomatic response to a colleague’s challenge. Read all three before any grammar is named. Find every highlighted phrase and ask yourself: what does it do?

Presenter’s Notes A. Chen — Climate Panel Opening Remarks

Good morning. I would like to begin by thanking the panel for their time. Presenting this data today is both a privilege and a responsibility.

Slide one shows global mean temperature between 1980 and 2024. As you can see, temperatures rose sharply through the 1990s before levelling off briefly around 2000. Since then, they have climbed steadily, reaching a new peak in 2023. The trend shows no signs of reversing.

Slide two concerns biodiversity. The panel will note that species populations declined dramatically between 1990 and 2010 before stabilising — though at a level far below historical baselines. The rate of recovering has been insufficient.

I look forward to discussing these findings with the panel and to hearing your responses. I also appreciate the delegate’s raising this issue in advance — her doing so has allowed us to prepare a more detailed briefing.

Briefing Paper Environmental Policy Unit — Key Trends Summary

Purpose: This briefing aims at providing the panel with a concise summary of the three datasets presented. It avoids oversimplifying the evidence while acknowledging the difficulty of communicating complex statistical trends to a non-specialist audience.

Temperature data: Global mean temperature increased sharply by 0.8°C between 1980 and 2005, then plateaued for approximately five years before rising steeply again. The 2020s have seen temperatures fluctuate within a narrow band while maintaining an overall upward trajectory.

Renewable energy: Solar and wind capacity grew exponentially from 2010 onwards, surpassing coal for the first time in 2021 before continuing to climb. The panel’s approving additional funding is essential to maintaining this trajectory.

Note: We recommend reviewing the data methodology section prior to discussion. The risk of misinterpreting outlier years is significant.

Diplomatic Response Response to Delegate Hartmann’s Challenge — Session Notes

I appreciate Delegate Hartmann’s questioning the methodology — his challenging our approach is entirely appropriate and I welcome it. However, I must respectfully disagree with the suggestion that the data is inconclusive.

I agree that interpreting long-term trends requires caution, and I concede that isolating individual variables is methodologically complex. That said, the evidence strongly supports concluding that anthropogenic warming is both measurable and accelerating.

I would resist characterising the uncertainty as reason for inaction. On the contrary, I insist on treating the precautionary principle as the appropriate framework here. We cannot afford to delay implementing the recommended measures.

Before reading any explanations — three questions

1. The gold phrases are all verb forms ending in -ing. But they’re not present participles (actions happening now). What grammatical role are they playing in their sentences? (Hint: try replacing them with a noun.)

2. The rose phrases also use -ing, but with a word before them: “the delegate’s raising”, “her doing so”, “his challenging”. Why is there a possessive before the -ing form?

3. The teal phrases describe graph movements: rose sharply, levelled off, climbed steadily, peaked. How many different verbs can you find for “going up”? How many for “going down”? What do the adverbs add to the meaning?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

The gold phrases — verbs acting like nouns

Work it out before reading the explanation

“Presenting this data today is both a privilege and a responsibility.” — What is the subject of the verb is? Can you replace Presenting this data with a single noun and keep the same meaning?

The subject of is is Presenting this data — the whole -ing phrase. You could replace it with “This presentation” and the sentence works. This is a gerund: an -ing form functioning as a noun — here, as the subject of the sentence.

“I look forward to discussing these findings.” — Why is discussing in the -ing form? What comes before it?

It follows the preposition to (part of the fixed phrase look forward to). After any preposition, English requires the gerund (-ing form), not the infinitive. This is one of the most common C1 errors: writing “look forward to discuss” instead of “look forward to discussing.”

“It avoids oversimplifying the evidence.” and “We recommend reviewing the data.” — Could you replace oversimplifying with to oversimplify? What about recommend — can it take an infinitive?

No — avoid and recommend always take the gerund, never the infinitive. These are verb pattern verbs: they are followed by -ing as a fixed grammatical rule, not a matter of choice. This week you will learn the complete list.

Now look at the rose phrase: “the delegate’s raising this issue”. Why is there an apostrophe-s before raising? What would change if we wrote “the delegate raising this issue” without the possessive?

With the possessive (the delegate’s raising), raising is a gerund — a noun — and the delegate’s is its possessive determiner, just as in “the delegate’s speech.” Without the possessive (the delegate raising), raising becomes a participle modifying delegate. Both are used in English, but formal written English prefers the possessive form. This is the gerund + possessive structure — a key C1 feature.

STUDY

Gerunds & verb patterns (-ing)

What is a gerund?

A gerund is the -ing form of a verb used as a noun. It can do everything a noun can do: be a subject, an object, a complement, or the object of a preposition. It looks like a present participle but functions completely differently.

Three core uses
Subject: Studying the data was essential.
Object: We recommend reviewing the methodology.
After preposition: She is responsible for presenting the findings.

Verb + gerund (not infinitive)

These verbs always take the gerund. Using the infinitive after them is a grammar error at C1 level.

Group 1
Completion / avoidance
finish · stop · avoid · quit · give up · put off · delay · postpone
“We cannot afford to delay implementing the measures.”
“I would resist characterising uncertainty as inaction.”
All mean: the action of the gerund does not happen (or stops/is prevented).
Group 2
Mental activity / stance
consider · suggest · recommend · propose · deny · admit · acknowledge
“We recommend reviewing the data methodology.”
“I concede that isolating variables is complex.”
These verbs describe thinking about, positioning toward, or judging an action.
Group 3
Enjoyment / preference / dislike
enjoy · dislike · love · hate · prefer · can’t stand · mind · resent
“I don’t mind presenting to large audiences.”
“She resents being asked to simplify complex data.”
These express attitude toward an activity.
Group 4
Fixed phrases & expressions
look forward to · be used to · get used to · insist on · aim at · result in · be worth · it’s no use
“I look forward to hearing your responses.”
“I insist on treating the precautionary principle as the framework.”
⚠️ ‘look forward to’ takes gerund, NOT infinitive.
Group 5
Meaning-change verbs
stop · try · remember · forget · regret · go on · mean · need
stop + gerund: “She stopped reviewing the data.” (ceased the activity)
stop + infinitive: “She stopped to review the data.” (paused in order to do it)
These verbs change meaning entirely depending on gerund or infinitive.
Group 6
After prepositions
Any preposition: by · without · before · after · instead of · despite · in addition to · for
“Before presenting the slides, she reviewed the data.”
“This was achieved without oversimplifying the evidence.”
After ALL prepositions, English uses the gerund, not the infinitive.

Gerund as subject — formal preference

✓ Gerund subject (formal)

“Interpreting long-term trends requires caution.”

The gerund phrase is the subject. Preferred in formal writing.

Also correct: it + infinitive

“It requires caution to interpret long-term trends.”

The ‘it’ version is also formal, slightly less dense. Both are C1.
⚠️ Gerund vs. present participle — they look the same but function differently “Presenting this data is a responsibility.” → gerund (noun — subject of ‘is’)
“She is presenting the data now.” → present participle (part of continuous verb form)
“Presenting the data clearly, she impressed the panel.” → participle clause (modifies ‘she’)
Same word, three different grammatical roles. Context always makes it clear.
STUDY

Gerunds with possessives — the formal construction

Why does formal English add a possessive before the gerund?

In formal written English, when a gerund has its own logical subject, that subject appears as a possessive determiner before the gerund. This is called the fused participle (informal) vs. the gerund construction (formal). At C1, the possessive form is expected in writing.

Formal: possessive + gerund

“I appreciate the delegate’s raising this issue.”

The gerund ‘raising’ is a noun; ‘the delegate’s’ is its possessive. The delegate’s action (raising) is the object of ‘appreciate’.

Informal: noun + participle

“I appreciate the delegate raising this issue.”

Acceptable in informal speech. Here ‘delegate’ is the object and ‘raising’ is a participle modifying it. Ambiguous and less precise.

The pattern with different determiners

Formal patternExample from the documentsInformal equivalent
Noun’s + gerund“The delegate’s questioning the methodology is appropriate.”The delegate questioning…
Pronoun possessive + gerund“Her doing so allowed us to prepare a better briefing.”Her doing so / She doing…
His / their / my + gerund“His challenging our approach is entirely appropriate.”Him challenging…
The panel’s + gerund“The panel’s approving additional funding is essential.”The panel approving…
✓ Rule summary Formal: possessive (the panel’s / her / his / their) + gerund
Informal: object form (the panel / her / him / them) + participle

Both are grammatically defensible — linguists debate the distinction — but at C1 level and in formal writing tasks (IELTS, essays, formal letters), the possessive + gerund construction is expected.

Common C1 error: using the infinitive

✗ Error

“I look forward to hear your responses.”

To here is a preposition (part of ‘look forward to’), not a to-infinitive marker. It requires a gerund.

✓ Correct

“I look forward to hearing your responses.”

After the preposition to, always use the gerund.

Look back at the teal phrases in the three documents

How many different verbs for going up can you find across the three documents? List them.

At least: rose, climbed, increased, grew, surpassed, continued to climb. Each implies a slightly different speed, scale, or quality of increase. This variation is exactly what formal graph description requires — you must not write “went up” repeatedly.

The documents use adverbs to describe how something changed: sharply, steadily, briefly, dramatically, exponentially, steeply. What do these adverbs add that the verb alone cannot?

The adverb describes the rate and extent of the change. “Temperatures rose” tells you the direction. “Temperatures rose sharply” tells you the speed and severity. At C1, pairing precise verbs with precise adverbs is the hallmark of strong academic and professional writing.

Find the phrase “levelled off briefly”. This uses a phrasal verb. Can you find other phrasal verbs for graph movements in the text?

levelled off (= became stable), plateaued (= reached a flat period). You might also use: bottomed out (reached the lowest point), picked up (started increasing again), tailed off (gradually decreased), levelled out. These are more vivid than single-word equivalents.

Adverbs of rate and extent — the precision layer

Pairing a verb with a precise adverb is what separates B2 from C1 graph description. The adverb answers: how fast? and by how much?

▲ Speed / rate
  • sharply — fast, steep
  • rapidly — fast, continuous
  • steeply — very steep angle
  • gradually — slowly, over time
  • steadily — at a consistent pace
  • slowly — minimal rate
▲ Size / scale
  • dramatically — large and striking
  • significantly — notably, importantly
  • substantially — to a large degree
  • marginally — by a small amount
  • slightly — by a little
  • exponentially — doubling/compounding
▲ Qualifying
  • overall — looking at the whole period
  • broadly — in general terms
  • relatively — compared to other data
  • temporarily — for a limited time
  • consistently — without interruption
  • briefly — for a short period

STUDY

Expressing (dis)agreement — formal & diplomatic

From the diplomatic response — Chen’s careful language

Notice how Chen’s response neither fully agrees nor flatly contradicts. This is the diplomatic register: it acknowledges, concedes, then redirects. At C1, expressing nuanced positions — especially in writing — is a core skill.

FunctionFormal expressionRegister note
Full agreementI fully concur with… / I am in complete agreement that… / This is entirely consistent with…Formal; used in written statements
Partial agreementI agree to some extent that… / While I accept that X, I would argue Y… / There is some merit in this view, however…Essential for nuanced academic writing
ConcessionI concede that… / I acknowledge that… / I accept that X, though I would note that Y…Shows intellectual honesty while maintaining position
Polite disagreementI must respectfully disagree… / With respect, I would suggest… / I am not entirely persuaded that…Diplomatic; formal contexts
Strong disagreementI strongly disagree with the suggestion that… / I would challenge the claim that… / The evidence does not support the view that…Formal debate; academic essays
RedirectingThat said, the evidence suggests… / On the contrary… / Nevertheless, it should be noted that…After concession, redirect to your position
📚 Corpus note (COCA / BNC) In formal academic and diplomatic English, concede → redirect is the most powerful pattern for expressing disagreement without sounding confrontational. Notice how Chen uses: “I agree that… I concede that… That said, the evidence strongly supports concluding that…” — acknowledge, concede, then assert. This three-move structure appears consistently in high-scoring IELTS and formal writing.

True or False — gerund instincts

Before any drilling — test what you already know. Click True or False.

“I look forward to present the data.” is grammatically correct.

False. After ‘look forward to’, ‘to’ is a preposition, not an infinitive marker. The gerund is required: ‘look forward to presenting’.

“Studying climate data requires patience.” — here Studying is a gerund (subject of the sentence).

True. Studying climate data is the subject of the verb requires. It’s a gerund phrase functioning as a noun.

“She stopped to review the data” and “She stopped reviewing the data” mean the same thing.

False. Stopped to review = she paused her current activity IN ORDER TO review. Stopped reviewing = she was reviewing and then ceased doing so. Completely different meanings.

True. In formal written English, the possessive + gerund construction is standard. The ‘object + participle’ form is more informal and somewhat ambiguous.

Exercise 6

Error correction — gerunds & graph language

Each sentence has one error. Find it, correct it, and state the rule.

1.“The panel looks forward to hear the full findings next week.”
2.“We recommend to review the data before the vote.”
3.“I appreciate him raising the concern.” (rewrite formally)
4.“Temperature went up very much between 2005 and 2020.” (improve the language)
5.“After stopping to rise, the figures remained stable for five years.” (is this right?)
6.“Renewable capacity grown exponentially since 2010.”

1. to hear → hearing. After ‘look forward to’, ‘to’ is a preposition → gerund required.

2. to review → reviewing. Recommend always takes the gerund, not the infinitive.

3. him raising → his raising. Formal possessive + gerund required.

4. went up very much → e.g. “Temperature rose significantly” / “climbed sharply” / “increased substantially.”

5. ‘Stopped to rise’ means “stopped in order to rise” (nonsensical). Should be “stopped rising” = ceased the rising movement.

6. grown → has grown. Missing auxiliary ‘has’ — present perfect is required here.

-ing as noun verb + gerund prep + gerund

  • Gerund = -ing form used as a noun (subject, object, after preposition)
  • Groups: completion/avoidance · mental stance · enjoyment · fixed phrases · meaning-change
  • After ALL prepositions: gerund (not infinitive)
  • look forward to doing — NOT to do
Grammar 2
Gerunds with Possessives
  • Formal: possessive + gerund → the delegate’s raising / her doing
  • Informal: object + participle → the delegate raising / her doing
  • Pronouns: my/your/his/her/our/their + gerund
  • Nouns: noun + ’s + gerund (e.g. the panel’s approving)
