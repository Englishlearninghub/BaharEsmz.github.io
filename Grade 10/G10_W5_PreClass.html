THE SCENE

Vienna. A. Chen’s final week as Third Secretary.

The final dispatch.

Five weeks ago, Chen arrived in Vienna as a newly posted Third Secretary. Tonight, the posting ends. Before handing over to the incoming diplomat, Chen must produce three documents: a formal letter of thanks to the panel chair from last week’s climate conference; a formal proposal for a new Digital Diplomacy Initiative; and a brief final report to Ambassador Davies.

All three draw on everything we have studied: the full tense system, formal writing conventions, AWL vocabulary, and the collocations we have been building since Grade 6. Read them before turning to the grammar. Find the highlighted phrases — they are your entry point.

Formal LetterThird Secretary A. Chen to Dr. Hartmann

Embassy of the United Kingdom
Metternichgasse 13, 1030 Vienna
2 July 2026

Dr. Ingrid Hartmann
Chair, Vienna Climate Conference Panel

Dear Dr. Hartmann,

Re: Expression of Gratitude following the Vienna Climate Conference

I am writing to express my sincere gratitude for the opportunity to present at the Vienna Climate Conference last week. The panel had been reviewing submissions for several months before we met, and I have since reflected extensively on the questions you raised.

The data we had gathered over the preceding year clearly indicated a trajectory that demands urgent attention. By the time the next conference convenes, we will have implemented the recommendations outlined in the attached proposal. I am also preparing a digital briefing pack, which will be circulated to all signatories before the autumn session.

I hope that our collaboration will continue to prove fruitful. Should you require any further information, please do not hesitate to contact my office.

Yours sincerely,
A. Chen
Third Secretary, British Embassy Vienna

Enc: Digital Diplomacy Initiative — Proposal (5pp.)

Policy ProposalDigital Diplomacy Initiative — British Embassy Vienna

1. Executive Summary

This proposal outlines a plan to establish a Digital Diplomacy Hub at the Vienna Embassy. Advances in artificial intelligence and data analytics have fundamentally altered the diplomatic landscape. Investing in digital infrastructure is no longer optional; it is a strategic requirement.

2. Background & Context

Since Chen joined the embassy five weeks ago, it has become apparent that the embassy’s current data systems are outdated. The climate conference demonstrated that real-time data analysis significantly enhances the quality of diplomatic engagement. Our partners in Berlin and Paris have already adopted comparable systems.

3. Proposed Initiative

We propose the allocation of €120,000 over two years to fund a secure digital platform, staff training, and a dedicated data analyst post. By December 2027, the hub will have processed over 200 briefing requests using AI-assisted research tools.

4. Benefits

The initiative will significantly contribute to the embassy’s ability to respond to fast-moving diplomatic situations. Evidence from comparable embassies indicates a 40% reduction in briefing preparation time. Furthermore, our commitment to digital transformation will strengthen our position as a leading embassy in the region.

5. Conclusion & Call to Action

We strongly recommend that the Foreign Office approve this initiative at the earliest opportunity. Should further information be required, a detailed budget breakdown is available upon request.

Final ReportA. Chen to H.E. Ambassador R. Davies

NOTICE & DISCOVER

The tense choices in Chen’s documents

Work through these before reading the Study section

In the letter: “The panel had been reviewing submissions for several months before we met.” Two things happened in the past. Which happened first? What tense signals that sequence?

The reviewing happened first (and continued until the meeting). The meeting happened second. The Past Perfect Continuous (had been + -ing) signals the earlier, ongoing action. The Simple Past (we met) gives the later event. The past perfect always signals: this happened before the main past event.

In the proposal: “Our partners in Berlin and Paris have already adopted comparable systems.” Why is the Present Perfect used rather than Past Simple? What would change if it said “adopted”?

The Present Perfect connects a past action to the present situation — it implies: they adopted these systems, and they are currently using them — this is relevant NOW. The Past Simple would simply report a past event without that present relevance. In formal writing, this distinction is critical: you use Present Perfect when the past event informs the current argument.

In the final report: “The posting began with the Ambassador’s Gala” (Past Simple) but “I have developed a deeper understanding” (Present Perfect) in the same paragraph. Why the switch?

“The posting began” refers to a specific past event at a specific time (five weeks ago, now over) → Past Simple. “I have developed” describes a result or change that is relevant NOW — Chen is about to move on and is framing their current state → Present Perfect. The tense encodes the relationship to now, not just the time of the event.

The letter contains: “I am also preparing a briefing pack, which will be circulated…” Two different future forms. What does each signal that the other cannot?

“Am preparing” (Present Continuous for future) signals an arrangement already in progress — it is planned and happening now. “Will be circulated” (future passive with will) is a prediction or commitment about a future event, not yet in motion. At C1, choosing the right future form depends on how certain, how arranged, and how soon the event is.

STUDY

The complete C1 tense system — all forms in one view

Eight tenses that matter at C1

The most common C1 error is not ignorance of a tense but choosing the wrong one in context. Every tense choice encodes meaning: the timing, the relevance, the speaker’s relationship to the event.

Present Perfect

have/has + past participle

“I have worked on three principal areas.”

“Partners have already adopted this system.”

Past action with present relevance. Result or experience. Use with: just, already, yet, recently, since, for, ever, never. NOT with specific past times.

Past Simple

verb + -ed / irregular

“The posting began with the gala.”

“She submitted the documents on Monday.”

Completed past action at a specific time. No present connection. Use with: yesterday, last week, in 2022, at 9am, when + clause.

Past Perfect

had + past participle

“The panel had reviewed submissions before we met.”

“I had not anticipated the pace.”

Past-before-past. Always refers to an earlier past event. Use with: before, after, by the time, already, when (in past narrative).

Past Continuous

was/were + -ing

“While Chen was preparing the brief, the panel was meeting.”

Background past action in progress when another past event occurred. Two simultaneous past actions. Interrupted past activity.

Past Perfect Continuous

had been + -ing

“The panel had been reviewing submissions for months before we met.”

Ongoing activity before a past point. Emphasises duration leading up to that moment. Use with: for, since + past reference.

Future Perfect

will have + past participle

“By December 2027, we will have processed 200 requests.”

Action completed before a specific future point. Use with: by (the time), before, when + future clause.

Future: will

will + infinitive

“Our collaboration will continue.”

“The pack will be circulated.”

Prediction, promise, offer, spontaneous decision, future passive. NOT for pre-arranged personal events (use going to / present continuous).

Future: Present Continuous

am/is/are + -ing

“I am also preparing a digital briefing pack.”

“She is meeting the Ambassador tomorrow.”

Arranged, fixed future event. Already in progress or organised. NOT for general predictions.

📌 C1 note: tense choice encodes meaning, not just time At C1, the error is rarely forming a tense incorrectly — it is choosing the wrong tense for the situation. Ask yourself: does this action have present relevance? Is it completed or ongoing? Is it before or after the reference point? Is it arranged or merely predicted?

2. Four future forms — choosing deliberately

FormWhen to useExample
will + infinitivePrediction, promise, spontaneous decision, future facts“Our collaboration will continue to prove fruitful.”
going to + infinitiveIntention already decided; evidence-based prediction“We are going to launch the hub in January.”
Present ContinuousFixed, arranged, booked event“I am meeting the Ambassador tomorrow at 10.”
Present SimpleScheduled, timetabled event“The conference closes at 18:00 on Friday.”
📌 C1 tip: will does NOT replace all future forms “I’m meeting the Ambassador tomorrow” (arranged) is more natural than “I will meet the Ambassador tomorrow.” “I’m going to submit the report on Friday” (decision already made) is preferred over “I will submit.” At C1, choose deliberately.

3. Present Perfect vs. Past Perfect — the sequence trap

TenseReference pointExample
Present PerfectNow — past action with present relevance“I have reflected on your questions.” (since the conference, relevant now)
Past PerfectA past moment — an even earlier past action“The panel had reviewed submissions before we met.” (before the meeting)

Think of it this way: Present Perfect looks back from now. Past Perfect looks back from a past moment. If your narrative is in the past, use Past Perfect for anything earlier within that narrative.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

The two genres — what makes them different?

Compare the letter and the proposal before reading the Study section

The letter opens: “I am writing to express my sincere gratitude…” The proposal opens: “This proposal outlines a plan to establish…” What is the fundamental difference in how each genre positions itself?

The letter opening centres on the writer — who is writing, why, and to whom. It is personal and relational. The proposal opening centres on the document itself — what it contains and what its purpose is. It is impersonal and functional. Letters begin with the writer; proposals begin with the content.

The proposal uses section headings (Executive Summary, Background, etc.). The letter uses none. Why?

A proposal is a reference document — the reader may return to specific sections. Headings enable navigation. A formal letter is linear — read once, beginning to end. Using headings in a letter would make it feel like a report and break the relational register.

Find the call to action in the proposal. How does it differ from the closing of the letter?

The proposal’s call to action is direct and decision-demanding: “We strongly recommend that the Foreign Office approve this initiative.” The writer wants a clear decision. The letter’s close is relational and open: “Should you require any further information, please do not hesitate to contact my office.” The writer wants to maintain the relationship. Proposals demand action; letters maintain connection.

Exercises — tenses, genre & collocation

Exercise 1 — True or False: Tense knowledge check

Read each statement about English tenses. Mark it True or False, then reveal the explanation.

"The present perfect is used for actions completed at a specific time in the past." (e.g. I have seen that film in 2019.)

False. The present perfect cannot be used with a finished time marker (in 2019, last year, yesterday). Use the past simple: I saw that film in 2019. Present perfect = unspecified time / connection to now.

The past perfect is often used in formal writing to show that one past event happened before another. (e.g. By the time she arrived, the meeting had already concluded.)

True. The past perfect signals a “backstory” — something completed before the main past narrative. It is especially common in formal reports and diplomatic writing to sequence events.

"Will + infinitive is always the correct way to talk about the future in formal writing." (e.g. The hub will process all requests.)

False. Will is one future form, but formal writing also uses is expected to, is anticipated that, is set to, and will have + past participle (future perfect) for projected completions. Over-reliance on bare will can read as informal.

In Chen’s proposal, the phrase “By December 2027, the hub will have processed…” uses the future perfect to express a completed future achievement.

True. Future perfect (will have + past participle) looks back from a future deadline and states what will be completed by that point. It is the standard tense for proposal milestones and KPIs in formal writing.

2
By the end of next year, the Digital Diplomacy Hub ____________ over 500 briefing requests. [future perfect — milestone]

will have processed — Future perfect: a completed achievement at a specified future point.

3
Global temperatures ____________ gradually since 1980 and the trend continues. [present perfect continuous — ongoing process]

have been rising — Present perfect continuous: an ongoing process that started in the past and is still in progress. The continuous form emphasises the duration.

4
At the time of the conference, scientists ____________ on the problem for several decades. [past perfect continuous — ongoing at a past point]

had been working — Past perfect continuous: an activity that had been in progress for a duration up to a specific past moment.

5
We recommend that the proposal ____________ to all senior officials immediately. [subjunctive — after formal recommendation]

be circulated — Formal subjunctive after recommend that: base form of the verb (no -s, no auxiliary). This is the same pattern from Week 2.

6
Dr. Hartmann ____________ three papers on climate policy since 2020. [present perfect — relevance to now]

has published — Present perfect: the papers are relevant now (they form part of his current expertise). No specific time adverb makes this present perfect, not past simple.

7
The ambassador ____________ Chen’s proposal at last month’s strategy meeting. [past simple — specific finished time]

approved — Past simple: last month’s meeting is a specific finished time, requiring the past simple, not the present perfect.

8
Were the funding ____________, the hub could be operational within eighteen months. [inverted conditional — hypothetical]

to be approved — Were + subject + to + infinitive: formal inverted conditional (from Week 1). It signals an unlikely or hypothetical future condition with formal register.

Exercise 4 — Error clinic — formal writing correction

Six sentences from student draft proposals and letters. Some have errors. Find and fix each one — or write “Correct” if the sentence is already fine. One sentence is a trap: it is completely correct.

1

“By June 2028, the programme will process 1,000 applications.”

Error The future perfect is required for a completed projection at a deadline: “By June 2028, the programme will have processed 1,000 applications.” Bare will process sounds like an ongoing action, not a completed milestone.

2

“Dear Sir or Madam, … Yours sincerely, J. Chen.”

Error Dear Sir or Madam (unnamed) must close with Yours faithfully, not Yours sincerely. The rule: named salutation → sincerely; unnamed → faithfully.

3

“The committee recommends that the proposal is approved without delay.”

Error After recommend / suggest / propose that, the formal subjunctive requires the base form: “…that the proposal be approved without delay.” Adding is breaks the subjunctive construction.

4

“In light of these findings, we strongly recommend that the Foreign Office allocate additional resources to this initiative.”

✓ Correct This is entirely correct. Subjunctive base form (allocate, not allocates) after recommend that is the right formal construction. The collocation allocate resources is also accurate. This was the trap sentence — did you try to “fix” it?

5

“I am writing to enquire about the possibility of an extend contract for our partnership.”

Error extend is a verb used as an adjective here — it must be the adjective form: “…an extended contract…” or alternatively “the possibility of extending our contract.”

6

“The hub will operate since January 2027.”

Error Since is used with the present or past perfect, not bare future: “The hub will have been operating since January 2027 [by the time we review it].” Alternatively: “The hub will begin operating in January 2027.”

Exercise 5 — AWL word families in context

Complete each sentence by choosing the correct form of the AWL word. Write the full word, then reveal.

1
The proposal aims to ____________ a new Digital Diplomacy Hub within eighteen months. [establish / establishment]

establish — verb form after to (infinitive). Establishment is the noun: the establishment of a new hub.

2
A ____________ review of diplomatic procedures will be conducted annually. [rigorous / rigorously]

rigorous — adjective modifying the noun review. Rigorously is an adverb, used with verbs: tested rigorously.

3
The ____________ of digital tools across all embassies is the initiative’s primary goal. [implement / implementation]

implementation — noun required after The as subject. Implement is the verb: to implement digital tools.

4
The findings are ____________ to any organisation facing these challenges. [relevance / relevant]

relevant — adjective after linking verb are. Relevance is the noun: the relevance of the findings.

5
The report provides a ____________ assessment of the current situation. [comprehensive / comprehensively]

comprehensive — adjective before noun assessment. Comprehensively is an adverb: the situation was comprehensively analysed.

6
These projections ____________ that significant investment will be required. [indicate / indication]

indicate — verb form (subject = projections, third person plural). Indication is the noun: an indication that…
