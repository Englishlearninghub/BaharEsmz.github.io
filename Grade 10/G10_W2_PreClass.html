THE SCENE

9:00 a.m. — Two letters on two desks

The gala is over. Two very different mornings begin.

Ambassador Davies is at his desk reviewing an immigration case. His attaché, still tired from last night, has just opened a wedding invitation. Read both documents carefully. Before reading any explanations, try to find every verb phrase that refers to the future.

Internal Memo Embassy of the United Kingdom, Vienna — Immigration Case

To: H.E. Ambassador R. Davies    Re: Ms. Fatima Al-Rashid — Residency Application

By the time the review committee convenes on Friday, Ms. Al-Rashid will have resided in Austria for seven years. She will have fulfilled all residency requirements and will have submitted the necessary documentation to the relevant authorities.

We hope the committee will identify the significance of her contribution to the local community. We wish to suggest that the committee consider her application with the urgency it warrants.

Legal Attaché Office

Wedding Invitation Elena Marchetti & Daniel Kowalski — Vienna City Hall

Together with their families,

Elena Marchetti & Daniel Kowalski

request the pleasure of your company at their wedding ceremony

“By the time you read this, we will have been planning this day for nearly two years. We hope you can join us to celebrate.”

Saturday, 14 September · Vienna City Hall · RSVP by 31 August

Quick meaning check — before any grammar terms

1. Both documents use the structure will have + past participle. Find all examples in both texts.

2. The memo says “will have resided for seven years.” She hasn’t finished yet — so why is the verb in a completed form?

3. The invitation says “will have been planning” (different from “will have planned”). What do you think the difference might be?

4. Circle every purple word in the memo. These all belong to a specific vocabulary list. Can you guess what they have in common?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

What do those teal verb phrases have in common?

Work it out before reading the explanation

Look at the three teal phrases: “will have resided,” “will have fulfilled,” “will have submitted.” What three words appear in every single one?

Will + have + past participle. Always in that order. That is the Future Perfect.

The memo says these things will happen before Friday. Friday hasn’t come yet — but the actions are described as already complete. Where in time is the speaker standing when they say this?

The speaker is mentally standing at Friday and looking back. From that future viewpoint, these actions are complete. That’s the key idea: the Future Perfect describes a future moment’s past.

The invitation says “will have been planning” — not “will have planned.” What do you think the “been” and the “-ing” add to the meaning?

The continuous form emphasises the duration and ongoing effort of the activity — “nearly two years of continuous planning.” Without it, “will have planned” just states that planning is complete.

Find the phrase “by the time the committee convenes.” The word “convenes” is present simple — not future. Is that an error?

No — it’s a rule. After by the time (and other time conjunctions like when, before, after), we use present simple to refer to a future event, not “will.” This is one of the most common errors learners make with the Future Perfect.

STUDY

The Future Perfect: looking back from ahead

The core idea: a future moment’s past

The Future Perfect does not describe what will happen. It describes what will already be finished by the time a future point arrives.

will have + past participle   won’t have + past participle   Will… have + past participle?

The three-point timeline

Based on the memo: “By Friday, she will have resided here for seven years.”

NOW — Present
Ms. Al-Rashid is living in Austria. She has been here for nearly seven years.
BETWEEN NOW AND FRIDAY — Action continues
She continues to reside here and to submit documentation. The action is ongoing.
FRIDAY — Future deadline (the committee meets)
“By Friday, she will have resided here for seven years.”
Looking back from Friday, the seven years are complete. We see it as finished from that future vantage point.

Four uses of the Future Perfect

1 Completed action before a future point “By Friday, she will have submitted all documents.” — Complete before the deadline.
2 Duration up to a future point (with FOR) “By next year, they will have been married for a decade.” — Ten years complete at that future point.
3 Confident inference about a present/future state “Don’t call — he’ll have gone to bed by now.” — We assume this is already done.
4 Formal: conditions met before a deadline “By the date of the hearing, the applicant will have fulfilled all requirements.” — Legal / official writing.

Time markers that signal the Future Perfect

Time markerExample sentence
by (the time)“By the time the ceremony begins, the guests will have arrived.”
by + date“By Friday, she will have submitted all the documents.”
before“Before the committee meets, she will have fulfilled the requirements.”
when“When you read this, we will have been planning for two years.”
in (time period)“In five years, they will have built a new life together.”
⚠ Three critical errors to avoid

✗ Wrong

“By the time she will arrive, we will have left.”

Never use future in a time clause after by the time / when / before.

✓ Correct

“By the time she arrives, we will have left.”

Use present simple in the time clause.

✗ Wrong

“By December, she will submitted her documents.”

✓ Correct

“By December, she will have submitted her documents.”

Negative, question, and passive forms

FormExample
Negative“By Friday, she won’t have received a response yet.”
Yes/No question“Will she have submitted everything by then?”
Wh- question“How long will she have lived here by the time the case is decided?”
Passive (very formal)“By Monday, the documents will have been reviewed by the committee.”
STUDY

Future Perfect vs. Future Perfect Continuous

The same moment — two different emphases

Both forms look back from a future point. The difference is what they emphasise.

Future Perfect

will have + past participle

Emphasises completion of an action.

“By Friday, she will have submitted the application.”

“By 2030, they will have built the new embassy.”

Future Perfect Continuous

will have been + -ing

Emphasises duration / ongoing effort up to that point.

“By the wedding, we will have been planning for two years.”

“By next year, she will have been living here for a decade.”

💡 Quick test Ask: does the sentence focus on HOW LONG something has been happening? → Use Future Perfect Continuous.
Does it focus on WHAT WILL BE COMPLETE? → Use Future Perfect (simple).

The Academic Word List: words that open doors

What is the AWL?

The AWL was compiled by Averil Coxhead (2000) from a corpus of 3.5 million words across 28 academic disciplines. It contains 570 word families — not just individual words, but complete families (analyse, analysis, analyst, analytical all count as one family). These 570 families make up approximately 10% of all words in academic texts.

The memo above uses six AWL words. You highlighted them in purple. Mastering the AWL transforms your academic writing — these words appear in almost every academic or professional text you will ever read.

📚 Why word families? Learning one word gives you one word. Learning the family gives you four or five. When you know analyse, you also know analysis, analyst, analytical, analytically, unanalysed. At C1, you need to switch between these forms fluidly and without hesitation.

VOCABULARY

Immigration vocabulary — the formal register

From Ms. Al-Rashid’s case file

Immigration is a real-world context where formal English, the Future Perfect, and AWL vocabulary all converge. It is also ethically rich and globally relevant. Notice how the Future Perfect fits naturally: “By Friday, she will have fulfilled all residency requirements.”

Key immigration vocabulary

visaresidence permitpermanent residency naturalisationcitizenshipasylum seeker refugeedeportationleave to remain eligibilitylawful residenceintegration legal representativeapplicantwork permit undocumenteddisplaced personright of return
TermDefinitionFuture Perfect example
residence permitOfficial document allowing a foreign national to live in a country for a defined period.“By March, she will have held a residence permit for five years.”
permanent residencyThe right to live indefinitely in a country without holding citizenship.“By Friday, she will have applied for permanent residency.”
naturalisationThe legal process by which a foreign national becomes a citizen.“By next year, she will have begun the naturalisation process.”
asylumProtection granted to someone who has left their country as a political refugee.“By the hearing, he will have been seeking asylum for two years.”
integrationThe process of becoming accepted into and participating in a new society.“By the review, she will have integrated into Austrian society for seven years.”
⚠ Register sensitivity The words we choose when discussing immigration carry moral weight. At C1 level, precision is essential:

“illegal immigrant” → informal, politically loaded
“undocumented person” → neutral
“person without legal status” → formal, legally precise

Similarly: a refugee has been granted protected status (1951 Convention). An asylum seeker has applied but a decision is still pending. These are not interchangeable.

STUDY

Wishes, hopes & giving suggestions

Both documents express wishes and suggestions

The memo says “We hope the committee will recognise…” and “We wish to suggest that the committee consider…” The invitation says “We hope you can join us.” These are the key functions this week.

Wish vs. Hope — the essential distinction

HOPE — something possible

Use with normal (indicative) tenses.

“I hope she gets the residency permit.”

(possible future)

WISH — something unreal or regretted

Use with past tense (subjunctive).

“I wish the process were faster.” (unreal present)

“I wish I had applied earlier.” (past regret)

StructureRegisterExample
I/We hope (that) + clauseNeutral → formal“We hope the committee will consider this carefully.”
It is hoped that…Formal / written“It is hoped that a decision will have been reached by Friday.”
I am hopeful that…Formal“I am hopeful that the committee will take a sympathetic view.”
We wish to + infinitiveFormal“We wish to inform you of the committee’s decision.”
May + subject + verbCeremonial / formal“May your marriage bring you lasting happiness.”

Giving suggestions — the subjunctive rule

StructureRegisterExample
I suggest + gerundNeutral“I suggest reviewing the file again.”
I suggest that + subjunctiveFormal“We suggest that the committee consider this urgently.”
I would suggest / recommend that…Formal / polite“I would recommend that the matter be resolved without delay.”
It would be advisable to…Formal“It would be advisable to seek legal counsel.”
✓ The formal subjunctive rule After suggest/recommend/propose that in formal writing, use the base form of the verb (no third-person -s, no past tense):

“We suggest that the committee consider” — NOT considers
“We recommend that this matter be resolved” — NOT is

This is one of the clearest markers distinguishing C1 from B2 formal writing.

Speaking Practice

This week's grammar — future perfect, wishes and hopes — is made for talking about weddings, plans, and immigration timelines. Use it actively.

Timed monologue

Five years from now

Set a timer for 2 minutes. Speak about where you imagine your life will have gone in five years — use future perfect at least twice (e.g. By then, I will have finished...). Don't stop, even if you search for a word.

How to do this: record yourself using your phone's voice memo app (or Vocaroo.com if you prefer typing on a laptop). Save or send the recording to your teacher if asked.
When you listen back, check: where you paused or searched for a word — those are the vocabulary gaps to review next.

Weddings & migration

How to do this: record yourself using your phone's voice memo app (or Vocaroo.com if you prefer typing on a laptop). Save or send the recording to your teacher if asked.

Sentence challenge

AWL under pressure

Use each of these 5 words from this week's Academic Word List in a spoken sentence: anticipate, provisional, undergo, subsequent, resolve. You have 60 seconds per word — time yourself.

How to do this: record yourself using your phone's voice memo app (or Vocaroo.com if you prefer typing on a laptop). Save or send the recording to your teacher if asked.

Exercise 5

Error correction — find and fix the mistake

Each sentence contains one error. Identify it and write the correct version.

1.“By the time the committee will meet, she will have fulfilled all requirements.”
2.“I wish I attend the ceremony last year, but I was abroad.”
3.“We suggest that the committee considers the application without delay.”
4.“By December, she will submitted her final documentation.”
5.“We hope that the committee would take a sympathetic view of this case.”
6.“By next year, he will have been live here for a decade.”

1. will meet → meets — present simple in time clause (after “by the time”)

2. attend → had attended — wish + past perfect for past regret

3. considers → consider — subjunctive after “suggest that” (no third-person -s)

4. will submitted → will have submitted — Future Perfect needs “have”

5. would → will — “hope” takes indicative, not conditional

6. live → living — Future Perfect Continuous = will have been + -ing

The Future Perfect

will have + past participle will have been + -ing

  • Describes an action complete before a future point

  • Continuous form: emphasises duration, not just completion
  • Passive: will have been + past participle (common in legal/official writing)

Function 1
Wishes & Hopes
  • Hope = something possible → indicative tense: “I hope she gets the permit.”
  • Wish = unreal/regret → past tense: “I wish the process were faster.”
  • We wish to inform you… (formal fixed phrase)
  • May your marriage bring you happiness. (ceremonial)
  • It is hoped that… / I am hopeful that… (formal written)
Function 2
Giving Suggestions
  • I suggest + gerund (neutral): “I suggest reviewing the file.”
  • I suggest that + subjunctive (formal): “We suggest that the committee consider” — no -s!
  • I would recommend that + base form: “that the matter be resolved”
  • It would be advisable to… (formal)

The subjunctive (base form, no -s) after suggest/recommend/propose that is a key C1 marker.
