THE SCENE

8:00 p.m. — British Embassy, Vienna

Two guests. The same evening. Two completely different languages.

It is 8:00 p.m. at the British Embassy, Vienna. Two people are communicating about the same evening — in completely different ways. Read both. Notice everything before reading a single explanation.

✉ Formal letter — H.E. Ambassador Davies to a colleague

Dear Minister Weiss,

I am writing to express my sincere gratitude for your attendance at this evening’s reception. Should you require any further information regarding the agenda, please do not hesitate to contact my office.

Whatever concerns remain unresolved, I am confident they can be addressed at tomorrow’s meeting, provided that all relevant parties are present.

Yours sincerely,

H.E. Ambassador R. Davies

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Before any explanations — what do you see?

Look at both texts again. Can you answer these?

Find one word in the letter that means need but sounds completely different. What is it?
require — “Should you require any further information”. The everyday word is need; the formal word is require.
The letter starts with “Should you require…” rather than “If you need…”. Why do you think the Ambassador chose this structure?
It is a formal inverted conditional — the subject and auxiliary are swapped, and ‘if’ is removed. This is a hallmark of formal written English at C1 level.
The attaché texts “whatever tho”. The Ambassador writes “Whatever concerns remain…”. Same word — completely different. What is whatever doing in the letter that it isn’t doing in the text?
In the text, whatever is a casual dismissal. In the letter, whatever introduces a clause meaning “it doesn’t matter what the concern is.” This is the grammatical ‘ever clause’ — one of this week’s main grammar targets.
The letter says “provided that all relevant parties are present.” What everyday word does this replace?
if — but in a more formal register. Provided that is a formal condition marker. You’ll learn the full set in Study section 4.

True or False? — Test your first impressions

Before we study anything, test what you already instinctively know about formal and informal English.

Contractions like “I’m” and “can’t” are acceptable in formal business emails.

False. Formal English requires full forms: I am, cannot, will not. Contractions signal an informal register.

The greeting “Dear Sir or Madam,” is used when you do NOT know the recipient’s name.

True. And in British English, this closing requires Yours faithfully — not Yours sincerely.

“Unless you don’t confirm by Friday” is a grammatically correct formal condition.

False. Unless already contains a negative meaning. Adding ‘don’t’ creates a double negative that reverses the intended meaning. Correct: “Unless you confirm by Friday…”

“Should you need assistance” means the same thing as “If you need assistance.”

True — same meaning, different register. Should you need is a formal inverted conditional. The grammar is different; the meaning is identical.
STUDY

What is register?

Register — the full picture

Register is the variety of language used in a specific social situation. It is shaped by: who is speaking, who they are addressing, the purpose, and the context.

The register spectrum — think of this not as two boxes (formal/informal) but as a continuous scale. Tonight’s gala has all five happening simultaneously:

FrozenLegal statutes
FormalThe Ambassador’s letter
ConsultativeProfessional talk
CasualFriends at the bar
IntimateThe attaché’s texts

At C1, operate across the middle three — and recognise all five.

Yours sincerely vs. Yours faithfully (British English)
Use Yours sincerely when you know the recipient’s name (opened “Dear Mr. Brown”). Use Yours faithfully when you don’t (opened “Dear Sir or Madam”).

Nominalisation — the ambassador’s secret weapon

Converting verbs and adjectives into nouns is a hallmark of C1 formal writing.

Costs increased rapidly.
→
There was a rapid increase in costs.
Unemployment grew last year.
→
The growth in unemployment was notable last year.
The results were significant.
→
The significance of the results was clear.
She discovered the problem early.
→
Her early discovery of the problem proved crucial.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

The ambassador said whatever. What did he mean?

Go back to the letter. Find the -ever word.

His letter says: “Whatever concerns remain unresolved, I am confident they can be addressed.” Is he talking about one specific concern, or all concerns regardless of what they are?
All concerns, regardless of what they are. Whatever means “it doesn’t matter what the concern is — all will be addressed.” That’s the job of an -ever clause.
The attaché texts: “whenever I’m at these things I’m like... why am I here”. What does whenever mean here — a specific time, or any time?
Any time — it doesn’t matter when. Whenever = “at any time that…” or “every time that…”
STUDY

Ever clauses: whatever, whenever, wherever…

The pattern

WH- word + ever = “it doesn’t matter which / who / where / when / how”

Formula
WH- word + ever  →  “regardless of which / who / where / when / how”
The outcome does not depend on the variable. Can open a sentence or appear in the middle.
✓ Whatever concerns you raise, they will be addressed formally. = It doesn’t matter what the concern is — all will be dealt with.
✓ Whenever you are ready to begin the proceedings, we shall proceed. = The timing doesn’t matter — we start at any point they are ready.
✓ Wherever the next summit is held, our delegation will attend. = The location is irrelevant — we will be there.
✓ Whoever chairs the opening session should begin no later than nine. = The identity of the chairperson doesn’t matter — they begin at nine.
✓ However complex the political situation becomes, dialogue must continue. = Regardless of how complex things get — dialogue continues.
✓ Whichever proposal the committee endorses, it will require additional funding. = The choice of proposal is irrelevant — both need more money.
✗ Whoever arrives first they should open the conference hall. Wrong — don’t add a second subject pronoun. Whoever already functions as the subject: Whoever arrives first should open the hall.
Two uses of ‘however’ — don’t confuse them
“However hard she tries, she can’t pass.” — ever clause (= regardless of how hard)
“She tried hard. However, she couldn’t pass.” — linking adverb (= but/nevertheless)
STUDY

He wrote provided that. Why not just say if?

Unless — the rule that trips everyone

unless = if…not / except if. It already contains a negative. Never write “unless…not.”

✓ Correct

“Unless you confirm your attendance, your place will be given away.”

= If you don’t confirm → your place goes to someone else.

✗ Common error

“Unless you don’t confirm, your place will be given away.”

The double negative reverses the meaning entirely.

Formal condition markers — the full set

ExpressionExampleNotes
provided (that)“The summit will proceed provided that all parties ratify the terms.”Very common in contracts, formal agreements
providing (that)“You may attend providing you register in advance.”Slightly less formal than ‘provided that’
on condition that“The offer stands on condition that payment is received within 30 days.”Very formal; legal and business texts
as long as“You may use the facility as long as you book in advance.”Neutral to semi-formal
in the event that“In the event that the conference is cancelled, fees will be refunded.”Formal notices, official communication
unless“Unless all delegations approve, the resolution cannot pass.”Formal and neutral — no double negative!
STUDY

The ambassador begins with Should. That’s deliberate.

Inverted conditionals — the three forms

In formal English, we can form conditional clauses by swapping subject and auxiliary, removing if entirely. Corpus data (COCA) confirms these appear almost exclusively in formal written English.

Form 1 — Should + subject + bare infinitive

Replaces “If + present simple” — use for polite requests, formal offers, professional correspondence

“Should you require assistance, our team is available around the clock.”

= If you require assistance…

“Should there be any objections, please raise them before we proceed.”

Form 2 — Were + subject + to + infinitive

Replaces the 2nd conditional if-clause — hypothetical situations, formal business and legal writing

“Were the negotiations to break down, both economies would face severe consequences.”

= If the negotiations were to break down…

“Were she to be appointed Director, significant reforms would follow.”

Form 3 — Had + subject + past participle

Replaces the 3rd conditional if-clause — past hypotheticals, formal regret, academic prose

“Had the ambassador been informed earlier, the misunderstanding could have been avoided.”

= If the ambassador had been informed earlier…

“Had I known about the reception, I would have prepared accordingly.”

Standard formFormal inverted form
If you need assistance, contact us.Should you need assistance, please contact us.
If she were to resign, the board would be concerned.Were she to resign, the board would be deeply concerned.
If I had prepared better, I wouldn’t have made that error.Had I prepared more thoroughly, that error would have been avoided.
If there are any problems, let us know.Should any problems arise, do not hesitate to notify us.

Academic & formal collocations

Academic collocations — Grade 10 focus

Collocation errors are more damaging to C1 writing than grammar errors. A native speaker doesn’t “make a research” — they “conduct research.” The wrong verb for that noun is a collocation error.

conduct research
“The team conducted extensive research into the matter.”
draw conclusions
“It is difficult to draw firm conclusions from limited data.”
raise concerns
“Several members raised serious concerns about the timeline.”
significant impact
“The policy had a significant impact on employment.”
forge a relationship
“The programme helped participants forge lasting relationships.”
mutual understanding
“Both sides reached a mutual understanding of the terms.”
address an issue
“The report fails to adequately address the core issue.”
sustained effort
“Success requires sustained effort over many years.”

SPEAKING PRACTICE

Speaking Practice

Register-switching has to become automatic in speech, not just in writing. These tasks push you to move between formal and informal on demand.

Roleplay scenario

The gala, twice

With a partner (or playing both parts yourself), act out this exchange twice. Scene: a diplomat asks a colleague for a favour regarding tomorrow's schedule. Version A (informal): as if texting a friend. Version B (formal): as if speaking to the Ambassador. Use at least one -ever clause and one piece of formal vocabulary from this week's toolkit in Version B.

How to do this: record yourself using your phone's voice memo app (or Vocaroo.com if you prefer typing on a laptop). Save or send the recording to your teacher if asked.
When you listen back, check: how clearly the register shifted between versions — vocabulary, contractions, and tone.
💡 Tip: Record Version A first, then immediately record Version B without pausing to plan — the quick switch is the real skill.
Opinion card

Agree or disagree?

“Formal language is becoming less important because most communication today happens by text and email.” Speak for 90 seconds giving your view. Use at least 3 words from this week's formal vocabulary toolkit (e.g. facilitate, ascertain, utilise) and one inverted conditional.

How to do this: record yourself using your phone's voice memo app (or Vocaroo.com if you prefer typing on a laptop). Save or send the recording to your teacher if asked.
When you listen back, check: whether you used natural pausing rather than long silences, and whether your 3 vocabulary items sounded natural rather than forced.
Shadowing drill

The Ambassador's letter

Take three sentences from the Ambassador's letter in this week's opening scene. Read them aloud three times: once slowly and clearly, once at normal pace, once as naturally and fluently as you can, copying the rhythm of formal written English read aloud.

How to do this: record yourself using your phone's voice memo app (or Vocaroo.com if you prefer typing on a laptop). Save or send the recording to your teacher if asked.
When you listen back, check: whether your third read sounds like natural spoken delivery of formal text, not a robotic word-by-word read.

LISTENING & WATCHING

Listening & Watching

Register is easier to hear than to explain. These three sources sit at very different points on the spectrum — notice how each one signals formality through voice, not just words.

TED Talks

ted.com / YouTube

TED speakers use a consultative-to-formal register — polished but not stiff. It's the closest real-world match to the “professional talk” segment of this week's spectrum diagram.

Your task: Pick any talk under 10 minutes. Listen once with no subtitles, then again with. Note 3 formal vocabulary items or nominalisations (e.g. significance, growth) the speaker uses.

In Our Time

BBC Radio 4 / podcast

This is about as formal and “frozen” as spoken English gets outside of a courtroom — academics discussing a topic with precise, dense language. Useful for hearing the far formal end of the spectrum.

Your task: Listen to the first 5 minutes of any episode. Note one inverted conditional or one example of nominalisation if you hear one, and one moment where the register clearly shifts more formal than everyday speech.

The Guardian Audio Long Reads

podcast

Journalism sits in the consultative-to-formal range — more accessible than academic radio but still carefully written. Good practice for the middle of the spectrum you're expected to operate in at C1.

Your task: Choose one article under 15 minutes. After listening, summarise it in 2–3 sentences using at least one word from this week's formal vocabulary toolkit.

Formal written English only — rare in natural speech (COCA).
