1Context First: Read Before Any Rule

Read the class conversation. Just try to understand. We look at the grammar after.

Class Conversation
Sam
OK, everyone! What's your job?
Elena
I'm a nurse. It's hard work, but I love it.
Diego
I'm a chef! I'm and... wait — I'm tired and happy today, all at once!
Sam
Ha! And you, Yuki?
Yuki
Hi! I'm an engineer. I'm a hard-working engineer!
Elena
Nice! Diego, are you a chef or a waiter?
Diego
I'm a chef! I'm a happy chef, but I'm a busy chef too.
Sam
Great! Now, turn to your partner and ask about their job.

Notice: Elena, Diego and Yuki use "a" and "an" before job words. They also put describing words like "hard-working", "happy" and "busy" before the job word. Small words like "and", "but" and "or" connect their ideas.

2Notice the Language (Before Any Rule)

You've just read how Elena, Diego and Yuki talked about their jobs. Before we name any grammar rules, let's look closely at the exact words they used — this will make the rules much easier to understand.

Look again at the highlighted words. Talk to a partner about these questions.

Q1. Find "a nurse", "a chef" and "an engineer". Why does "engineer" use "an" instead of "a"? Listen to the first sound.
Q2. Look at "a hard-working engineer" and "a happy chef". Does the describing word (hard-working, happy) come before or after the job word?
Q3. Find "tired and happy", "hard work, but I love it", and "a chef or a waiter". Which word joins two similar ideas? Which word shows a difference? Which word shows a choice?

Talk about your answers before you go to Section 3.

3Grammar Focus

You noticed that Yuki said "an engineer" while Elena said "a nurse" — and that describing words like "happy" and "hard-working" came right before the job. Now let's turn those patterns into clear rules you can use for any job, not just Elena's, Diego's or Yuki's.

3.1 — A or An?

We use "a" before a consonant sound, and "an" before a vowel sound (a, e, i, o, u).

A or an? Listen to the first sound
"An" is easier to say before a vowel sound — try saying "a engineer" out loud, it's difficult!

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we say "a engineer" or "an engineer"? An engineer.
Do we say "a nurse" or "an nurse"? A nurse.

3.2 — Adjectives with Nouns

Yuki didn't just say "an engineer" — she said "a hard-working engineer." A describing word (adjective) goes before the noun it describes.

Adjective + NounExample
happy + chefa happy chef
hard-working + engineera hard-working engineer
busy + nursea busy nurse

⚠️ Word Order Matters!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
a chef happya happy chef
an engineer hard-workinga hard-working engineer

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Does the adjective go before or after the noun? Before.

3.3 — And, But, Or

Diego said he was "tired and happy," Elena said her job was hard, "but" she loves it, and she asked Diego if he was a chef "or" a waiter. These three small words connect ideas in very different ways.

WordJobExample
andadds two similar ideasI'm tired and happy.
butshows a difference/contrastIt's hard work, but I love it.
orshows a choiceAre you a chef or a waiter?

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Which word shows a choice between two things? Or.
Which word shows a difference or a surprise? But.

6Error Clinic

Even careful learners mix up "a/an" and word order when they're new to English. Here are five sentences a student might write — read each one. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "I'm a engineer."
    Incorrect — "engineer" starts with a vowel sound: "I'm an engineer."
  2. "She's a chef happy."
    Incorrect word order — the adjective goes first: "She's a happy chef."
  3. "I'm a hard-working nurse."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. The adjective correctly comes before the noun. Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Are you a chef and a waiter?"
    This should probably be a choice, not an addition: "Are you a chef or a waiter?"
  5. "I'm an artist."
    Also correct. "Artist" starts with a vowel sound, so "an" is right. A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

🗣Speaking Practice

Jobs and feelings — two things people ask about every day: "What do you do?" "How are you?" This week you answer with your voice. Voice memo app ready!

Roleplay: jobs

What do you do?

Person A asks: "What do you do?"

Person B answers: "I'm a… / I'm an…" and one more sentence: "I work in a hospital." Then B asks about A's family: "What does your brother do?" — "He's an engineer."

How: With a partner, or both people yourself. Record 1–2 minutes. Check the little word: a nurse, a teacher — but an engineer, an artist (before A, E, I, O, U sounds).
Sentence challenge

Six jobs, one sentence each

Say one sentence for each job — about a real person you know, or a famous person. Up to 60 seconds each:

  • a nurse
  • a teacher
  • a driver
  • a chef
  • an engineer
  • an artist
How: One recording, timer on. Check: "My friend IS a chef. She ISN'T a driver." The verb be is always there!
Feelings mirror

How are you — really?

Ask yourself aloud: "How are you?" Answer with a feeling word and a face to match: "I'm happy! / I'm tired. / I'm hungry!" Do it five times with five different feelings.

How: In front of the mirror, or record video on your phone. Check: the feeling word carries the music — "I'm TIRED" sounds tired, "I'm HAPPY!" sounds happy.

Tips: jobs and feelings

  • Learn YOUR job sentence perfectly: "I'm a… I work in…" You will say it a hundred times in your life.
  • "How are you?" — "I'm fine, thanks. And you?" Learn the whole ping-pong, not just one side.
  • One new feeling word per day. Today: how are you, really?
