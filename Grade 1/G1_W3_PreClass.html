2Notice the Language (Before Any Rule)

You've just read how Diego and Yuki asked Elena about the people in her photo. Before we name any grammar rules, let's look closely at the exact words they used — this will make the rules much easier to understand.

Look again at the highlighted words. Talk to a partner about these questions.

Q1. Find "Is this your husband?" and "Are these your children?" One is about one person, the other about more than one. Which is which?
Q2. Elena says "This is a photo" (close, in her hand) but "That's my grandmother" (a person further away in the photo, or in her memory). Do you think "this" and "that" both mean "near"?
Q3. Find "Is this...?", "Are these...?" and "Are you...?" Where does "is/are" go in a question — before or after the subject?
Q4. Find "tall", "young", "small", "funny", "old", "strong". What job do these words do?

Talk about your answers before you go to Section 3.

3Grammar Focus

You noticed that Diego asked "Is this...?" for one person and Yuki asked "Are these...?" for many. Now let's turn those patterns into clear rules you can use to ask about anyone, not just Elena's family.

3.1 — To Be: Questions

To make a question with "to be", we put am/is/are before the subject.

StatementQuestionShort answer
You are Diego.Are you Diego?Yes, I am. / No, I'm not.
He is your husband.Is he your husband?Yes, he is. / No, he isn't.
They are your children.Are they your children?Yes, they are. / No, they aren't.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

In a question, does "is/are" go before or after the subject? Before.
What's the short answer to "Is he tall?" if yes? Yes, he is.

3.2 — This / That / These / Those

Elena didn't only answer questions — she also pointed at people in the photo herself: "This is a photo of my family," and "That's my grandmother, over there." These words point at people or things. They change depending on distance and number.

Near or far? One or many?
"This is my husband" (near, one) — "Those are my parents" (far, many)

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Is "this" for one thing or many things? One thing.
Is "those" for near or far? Far.
Which word means "many things, near"? These.

6Error Clinic

Even careful learners mix up "is/are" and "this/these" when they're new to English. Here are five sentences a student might write — read each one. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "This your husband?"
    Incorrect — questions need "is": "Is this your husband?"
  2. "This are my children."
    Incorrect — plural, near, needs "these": "These are my children."
  3. "Is he tall?"
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Is" correctly comes before "he" in the question. Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Those is my parents."
    Incorrect — plural needs "are": "Those are my parents."
  5. "Are you happy in this photo?"
    Also correct. The question word order is right. A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

🗣Speaking Practice

Who's this? This week you describe people — family, friends, photos — with your voice. Voice memo app ready!

Describe a person

45 seconds: someone I love

Open a photo of a family member or friend on your phone. Describe the person in about 45 seconds:

  • Who is it? "This is my sister."
  • What is she like? "She's tall. She's funny."
  • One more thing: "She's 25. She's a teacher."
How: Look at the photo and record. Check: He's / She's = "he is / she is". One smooth word.
Sentence challenge

Six words and their opposites

Say one sentence with each pair — about real people (no names needed!). Up to 60 seconds each:

  • tall / short
  • young / old
  • funny
  • strong
  • beautiful / handsome
  • thin / big
How: One recording, timer on. Check: the adjective does NOT change — "They are tall," never "talls."
Roleplay: who's this?

The photo game

Person A shows a photo (family, friends, or famous people) and asks: "Who's this?"

Person B answers or guesses: "Is it your brother? He's very tall!" Then change roles.

How: Best with a partner — alone, describe three photos and record. Check: Who's = "who is". Same music as he's, she's, it's.

Tips: describing people kindly

  • Start with who, then two adjectives, then one fact. Small recipe, complete description.
  • Describe people in photos on your phone — one photo a day, three sentences. Free practice!
  • Kind words first: funny, friendly, smart. They make every description warmer.

8Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

You've learned a lot this week — from Elena's family photo to writing about your own! Great learners keep noticing English outside of class too. Try one or more of these tasks before your next lesson.

  • Photo practice: Find a photo of your family or friends. Point at 3 people and say "This is..." or "That's..." out loud.
  • Describe a celebrity: Choose a famous person. Write 2 sentences describing them with adjectives.
  • Question practice: Ask a friend or classmate 3 yes/no questions with "is/are".
  • Listening hunt: Watch a short video where someone describes their family. Listen for this/that/these/those.
