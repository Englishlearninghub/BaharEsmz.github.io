Posted Sunday morning

Last Saturday, the four of us finally explored Vienna together. We arrived punctually at Stephansplatz, and Julia guided us confidently through the narrow streets of the old town. We walked slowly along the canal and admired the view. Near the old bridge, we got a little lost — but Daniel asked politely for directions, and a kind local pointed us the right way. We crossed the street carefully at the traffic lights, and after a short walk, we finally spotted the hidden café, exactly opposite the small park. We arrived hungrily and ordered cake immediately! Mei photographed the view beautifully, and we chatted happily for almost two hours. It certainly wasn't a perfect walk — but it was a perfect Saturday.

Notice the Pattern

Before we give these patterns a grammar name, let's find them ourselves. This helps you remember the rule much better than just reading it.

Step 1: Scroll back up to Section 1 and re-read Sofia's blog post. Step 2: Come back here and read questions A and B. Step 3: Try to answer them yourself before clicking "Check your noticing."

A. Simple Past. Find these words in Sofia's blog: "arrived," "guided," "walked," "asked," "crossed," "photographed," "chatted." What do almost all of these words end in? What's different about "got" and "found"?

B. Adverbs of Manner. Find: "arrived punctually," "guided us confidently," "asked politely," "crossed the street carefully," "photographed the view beautifully," "chatted happily." What do all of these words end in? What do they tell us — WHAT happened, or HOW it happened?

A. Most of these words end in -ed — this is the Simple Past of regular verbs. "Got" and "found" don't end in -ed because they are irregular verbs (you'll study these fully in Grade 5) — for now, just notice that most verbs are regular and follow the -ed pattern.

B. All of these words end in -ly. They describe HOW the action happened, not what happened. "Arrived punctually" doesn't just tell us Julia arrived — it tells us she arrived on time. These words are called adverbs of manner.

Well done — you've just found two real grammar patterns by yourself, with no rules given to you first. Sections 3 and 4 now explain each pattern fully, using the exact same examples you just looked at.

↗ Next: name the first pattern. You noticed the -ed pattern. Now let's name it properly: the Simple Past of regular verbs.
3

Grammar Focus — Simple Past (Regular Verbs)

Talking about finished actions in the past — this week, only regular (-ed) verbs

You noticed this pattern yourself in Section 2. Now let's look at it properly, rule by rule, with clear examples.

3.0 Form — the three patterns

FormStructureExample
Positivesubject + verb + -edWe walked along the canal.
Negativesubject + didn't + base verbWe didn't stop until we found the café.
QuestionDid + subject + base verb?Did you enjoy the walk?

3.1 Spelling rules for -ed

Read the table below slowly. For each row, cover the "Example" column and try to guess it first.

RuleExample
Most verbs: add -edwalk → walked, ask → asked
Verb ends in -e: add -d onlyarrive → arrived, guide → guided, live → lived
Consonant + y: change y → iedstudy → studied, carry → carried
Vowel + y: just add -edplay → played, stay → stayed
Short verb, one vowel + one consonant: double the consonantstop → stopped, chat → chatted

3.2 Pronouncing -ed — three sounds

This is the part most learners never get taught directly — but it makes a huge difference to how natural you sound.

SoundAfter these endingsExamples
/t/voiceless sounds: k, p, s, ch, sh, fwalked /wɔːkt/, asked /ɑːskt/
/d/most voiced sounds: b, g, v, l, m, n, r, and vowelsarrived /əˈraɪvd/, played /pleɪd/
/ɪd/verbs ending in -t or -dwanted /ˈwɒntɪd/, needed /ˈniːdɪd/

3.3 A common learner error

Don't double the past

✗ We didn't walked to the café.

✓ We didn't walk to the café. (the -ed moves to "didn't"; the main verb stays base form)

✗ Did you enjoyed the walk?

✓ Did you enjoy the walk?

Grammar Focus — Adverbs of Manner

Describing HOW an action happened

4.0 Form — adjective to adverb

RuleExample
Most adjectives: add -lyquiet → quietly, quick → quickly, polite → politely
Adjective ends in -y: change y → ilyhappy → happily, easy → easily, hungry → hungrily
Adjective ends in -le: change e → ygentle → gently, simple → simply
Adjective ends in -ic: add -allybasic → basically, dramatic → dramatically
Irregular — no -ly at allgood → well, fast → fast, hard → hard

4.1 Where do adverbs of manner go?

PositionExample
After the verb (most common)Julia guided us confidently.
After the object, if there is oneMei photographed the view beautifully.
Not between the verb and its object✗ Mei photographed beautifully the view.

Adjective or adverb? Don't mix them up

✗ She sang beautiful.

✓ She sang beautifully. (describes the verb "sang" — needs an adverb)

✗ He is a good singer, and he sings good too.

✓ He is a good singer (adjective + noun), and he sings well too (adverb + verb).

1. Daniel asked the local politely. (adverb — describes "asked")
2. It was a polite question. (adjective — describes "question")
3. We crossed the street carefully at the lights.
4. Julia is a confident guide, and she always guides us confidently.

Vocabulary — Around the City

Every word below appeared in Section 1. Read the example from the story before the definition — try to guess the meaning first.

squareskweərn
"Let's meet at Stephansplatz" — Platz is German for square, an open public space in a city.
A large open space in a town or city, usually surrounded by buildings, where people meet or gather.
Collocates: the main square, a town square, meet in the square
canalkəˈnæln
"We walked slowly along the canal and admired the view."
A man-made waterway, similar to a river, often running through a city.
Collocates: along the canal, a canal boat, cross the canal
bridgebrɪdʒn
"Turn right and walk along the canal, past the old bridge."
A structure built over a river, canal, or road so people can cross it.
Collocates: cross a bridge, an old/new bridge, under the bridge
traffic lightsn, plural
"We crossed the street carefully at the traffic lights."
The red, yellow (amber), and green lights that control traffic and tell people when to cross the street.
Collocates: at the traffic lights, wait for the lights, the lights turn green
cornerˈkɔːnərn
"The café is on the corner, just opposite the small park."
The point where two streets meet, or the edge where two sides of something join.
Collocates: on the corner, around the corner, turn the corner
old townn phrase
"Julia guided us confidently through the narrow streets of the old town."
The oldest, usually historic, central part of a city, often with narrow streets and old buildings.
Collocates: the historic old town, wander through the old town

Vocabulary — Giving Directions

These exact phrases came from the local's directions and Julia's note in Section 1.

go straight aheadv phrase
"From Stephansplatz, walk straight ahead down Rotenturmstraße."
Continue forward, without turning left or right.
Collocates: go/walk straight ahead, straight ahead until you reach...
turn left / rightv phrase
"Turn right and walk along the canal." "Then turn left."
Change direction to the left or right side.
Collocates: turn left at the corner, take the next left/right
pastpɑːstpreposition
"Walk along the canal, past the old bridge."
Beyond or further than a place, continuing in the same direction.
Collocates: walk past, go past, past the church/bridge/shop
oppositeˈɒpəzɪtpreposition
"The café is on the corner, just opposite the small park."
Directly facing something, on the other side.
Collocates: opposite the park, directly opposite, sit opposite someone
next topreposition phrase
"It's next to a bright red door."
Right beside something, with nothing in between.
Collocates: right next to, next to the entrance
crosskrɒsv
"We crossed the street carefully at the traffic lights."
To go from one side of a street, road, or bridge to the other.
Collocates: cross the street/road, cross at the lights, a pedestrian crossing

Language for Giving Directions — What to Say

SituationUseful phrases
Asking for directionsExcuse me, are you looking for something? / We're trying to find... / Do you know where... is?
Giving directionsGo straight ahead until... / Turn left/right at... / Walk past the... / It's on the corner, opposite the...
Checking distanceIt's only a five-minute walk. / It's not far from here. / It's quite close.
Confirming understandingDid you understand the directions? / We followed them carefully. / We found it easily / with a little difficulty.

Speaking Practice

Last Saturday the four friends walked through Vienna, got lost, and asked a stranger for help. Getting lost is one of the most useful speaking lessons there is. Voice memo app ready!

Roleplay scenario

Excuse me, where is…?

Role A — Lost visitor: You are looking for the old town square. Stop a stranger politely: "Excuse me, how do I get to…?" Repeat the directions back to check you understood.

Role B — Local person: Give clear directions with this week's phrases: "Go straight ahead… turn left at the traffic lights… it's opposite the church, next to the café."

How: With a partner, or both roles yourself; record 2 minutes. When you listen back, check: did Role A repeat the directions? Repeating ("So… left at the lights, then straight?") is what real people do — it saves you every time.
Retell task

Tell the Saturday story

Read the walk story again. Close it. Now tell it from memory in about 60 seconds, in the past: where they walked, how they got lost, who they asked, and what they found. Regular past verbs everywhere: walked, crossed, turned, asked, discovered…

How: Record once, listen, record again a little smoother. When you listen back, check the -ed sounds: walked = /t/, discovered = /d/, waited = /ɪd/. Only verbs ending in t or d get the extra syllable!
Shadowing drill

Copy the dialogue

Choose 4 lines from this week's dialogue — two questions and two answers with directions. Read them aloud three times:

  • Slowly and clearly;
  • at normal speed;
  • as naturally as you can, like a real conversation.
How: Record the third time. When you listen back, check: direction words carry the stress — turn LEFT, go STRAIGHT, it's OPPOSITE the church.

Tips: never really lost

  • Learn one polite opener by heart: "Excuse me, could you help me? I'm looking for…" It opens every door.
  • Directions come in pieces. Repeat each piece back — locals will happily wait.
  • The -ed ending is quiet in fast speech. Train your ear for it now; it pays forever.
