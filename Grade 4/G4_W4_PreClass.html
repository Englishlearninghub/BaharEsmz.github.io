Making Comparisons: FitCity vs. Iron Works

Comparative & superlative adjectives · "more" vs. "‑er/‑est" · Sport & fitness vocabulary

📍 This week's story: Two friends, Alex and Priya, are trying to decide which gym to join before the Riverside Half‑Marathon. Along the way we'll also meet three amateur runners battling it out for the fastest 10K time in the city. Their texts, reviews and results are full of the language you need this week — read them closely before you look at any grammar rules.

1Context First: Read Before You Study Any Rule

Blog Review

Downtown Fitness Face-Off: FitCity vs. Iron Works

Posted by CityLife Reviews · Fitness & Wellness section

If you're choosing a gym in the downtown area this year, it really comes down to two names: FitCity Fitness and Iron Works Gym. We visited both at peak hours to see which one actually deserves your membership fee.

FitCity is cheaper than Iron Works — $45 a month compared to $65 — and it's also closer to the train station, which matters if you're commuting straight from work. However, Iron Works is bigger, with 90 machines instead of 60, and it's open 24 hours, which makes it more convenient for shift workers and night owls.

In terms of atmosphere, FitCity felt busier and honestly a bit more crowded in the evening — you might wait for a treadmill. Iron Works felt quieter, but some readers have told us it's also less friendly for total beginners, since the machines are aimed at serious lifters.

Our verdict? If price and location matter most to you, FitCity is the more practical choice. But if you want the most modern equipment and don't mind the price, Iron Works is worth the extra money. Of every gym we've reviewed downtown this year, Iron Works remains the most expensive — but also, according to member surveys, the best equipped.

3Grammar Focus: Comparatives & Superlatives

Now that you've noticed the patterns yourself, here is the full reference. This is exactly what governs every highlighted word in Section 1.

3.1 — Forming the Comparative (comparing two things)

Adjective typeRuleExamples
1 syllableadd -ercheap → cheaper, fast → faster, near → nearer
1 syllable ending consonant-vowel-consonantdouble final consonant + -erbig → bigger, fit → fitter
2 syllables ending in -yy → i + -erbusy → busier, easy → easier, friendly → friendlier
2+ syllables (most others)more / less + adjectivemore convenient, more crowded, less friendly, more expensive
irregularcompletely different wordgood → better, bad → worse, far → further/farther

All comparatives are followed by than when we mention both things being compared: "FitCity is cheaper than Iron Works." "Than" can be dropped only when the second item is obvious from context (as Priya does in speech).

3.2 — Forming the Superlative (comparing three or more things — the extreme)

Adjective typeRuleExamples
1 syllablethe + adjective + -estthe cheapest, the fastest
1 syllable, consonant-vowel-consonantdouble consonant + -estthe biggest, the hottest
2 syllables ending in -yy → i + -estthe busiest, the easiest
2+ syllablesthe most / least + adjectivethe most expensive, the most modern, the least friendly
irregularcompletely different wordgood → the best, bad → the worst, far → the furthest

Superlatives almost always take the article the — this is the single word that answers Notice Question 3 above. We also often add a group for comparison: "the best gym in the city", "the fastest runner of the three", "the most expensive gym we've reviewed this year."

⚠️ The #1 Corpus-Confirmed Error at This Level

Learner corpus data (LINDSEI/ICLE) shows that B1 learners massively overuse more + adjective, even with short adjectives that should take -er. This is the single most common comparative error at this level.

❌ Incorrect (overused)✅ Correct
more happyhappier
more fastfaster
more bigbigger
more nearnearer

Rule of thumb: if the adjective has only one syllable — or two syllables ending in -y — do NOT use "more". Add the ending instead. Save "more/most" for longer adjectives (convenient, expensive, comfortable, popular, exciting…).

4Sport & Fitness Vocabulary

These words appear throughout this week's texts and tasks. Learn them in the context of gyms, running and competitive sport.

4.1 — Collocations: play / do / go

One of the most common vocabulary errors at this level is mixing up these three verbs with sports. Corpus data shows a clear pattern:

play + ball sports/games → football, tennis, basketball, chess
do + individual/martial/fitness activities → yoga, athletics, karate, weights, exercise
go + activities ending in -ing → running, swimming, cycling, jogging, climbing

4.2 — Gym & Training Vocabulary

membership feethe amount you pay, usually monthly, to use a gym
personal best (PB)your own fastest time or best result ever recorded
pacethe speed at which you run, expressed as time per kilometre/mile
stamina / endurancethe ability to keep doing something physically demanding for a long time
to warm up / to stretchto prepare your muscles before exercise
treadmilla machine for running or walking indoors
to break/beat a recordto achieve a better result than the best one recorded so far
to burn outto become exhausted from overtraining

4.3 — Adjectives for Comparing Performance & Places

competitivestrongly wanting to win or do better than others
intenseinvolving a lot of effort, energy or concentration
convenienteasy to use, reach, or fit into your schedule
crowdedfull of people
exhaustingmaking you extremely tired
consistentperforming at the same (usually high) level every time

4.4 — More Related Vocabulary to Recognise

6Error Clinic

Amateur runner forum posts often contain exactly the kind of errors corpus research predicts. Read each sentence below. Some are wrong — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "My pace today was more slow than last week's."
    Incorrect. "Slow" is one syllable → slower. "My pace today was slower than last week's."
  2. "Iron Works is definitely more expensive, but it's also the more modern gym downtown."
    Incorrect — this compares against ALL gyms downtown (more than two), so it needs the superlative: "...it's also the most modern gym downtown."
  3. "This is the more difficult route I've ever run."
    Incorrect. "Ever run" signals a superlative meaning (comparing this route to every route ever run): "This is the most difficult route I've ever run."
  4. "Sam trains harder than Marcus, but Marcus is still faster."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Harder" and "faster" are both correctly formed one-syllable comparatives, both compare exactly two people, and "than" is used correctly. Resist the urge to change anything.
  5. "Of the three runners, Elena is the most consistent one."
    Also correct. "Consistent" has three syllables, so "the most consistent" is the right superlative form — this is a second, harder trap: don't assume every sentence in an error clinic must contain a mistake.

🗣Speaking Practice

FitCity or Iron Works? Cheaper or better? Comparing out loud is one of the most useful speaking skills at this level — you do it every time you choose anything. Voice memo app ready!

Describe & compare

Two places you know

Choose two real places you know well — two gyms, two cafés, two supermarkets, two parks. Compare them aloud for 60–90 seconds: "X is cheaper than Y, but Y is more convenient… X is the most crowded place in my area…" Finish with your choice: which one wins, and why?

How: Record it in one take. When you listen back, check: short adjectives take -er/-est (cheaper), long ones take more/most (more convenient) — never both ("more cheaper" is the one to hunt!).
Opinion card

Agree or disagree?

"Exercising with other people is better than exercising alone."

Speak for 60 seconds. Say your opinion, give one example, and use at least three words from this week's vocabulary (stamina, pace, to warm up, competitive, membership fee, personal best…).

How: One take, recorded. When you listen back, check: at least two comparisons — an opinion about "better" needs a than somewhere!
Sentence challenge

Six words, one sentence each

Say one sentence with each item — about sport in your own life. Up to 60 seconds per item:

  • to warm up
  • pace
  • stamina
  • competitive
  • crowded
  • a personal best
How: One recording, timer on. When you listen back, check: word stress — comPETitive, conVENient, STAmina. Say any wrong one five times correctly.

Tips: comparing like a native

  • Than is tiny in real speech — it sounds like /ðən/: "cheaper th'n mine." Don't give it a full vowel.
  • Soften comparisons with a bit / much / far: "a bit cheaper, much more convenient." Instant upgrade.
  • One superlative is a strong finish: "…and that's the best reason of all."

8Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

Corpus-informed learning doesn't stop with this page. Use these tasks to keep noticing comparatives and superlatives in real English this week — no answer key needed, this is about building your own research habits.

  • Corpus check: Visit the free English-Corpora.org COCA interface and search "more" + a one-syllable adjective (e.g. "more fast"). Compare how many real hits it gets versus "faster". What does the number tell you?
  • Real reviews: Find two real gyms, running apps, or sports clubs near you online (Google Maps reviews work well). Read three reviews of each and collect every comparative or superlative you find.
  • Listening hunt: Watch two minutes of any English-language sports commentary (marathon, football, tennis) on YouTube. Commentators use superlatives constantly ("the fastest lap of the race so far") — try to write down three examples.
  • Word list expansion: Look up five more adjectives connected to sport and fitness that weren't taught this week (e.g. "flexible", "aggressive", "resilient") and write their comparative and superlative forms yourself.
  • Speaking challenge: Outside class, describe two sports you've tried to a friend or classmate using at least three comparatives, without writing anything down first.
