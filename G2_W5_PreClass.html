Imperatives · dates & ordinal numbers · punctuation in writing

1Context First: Read Before Any Rule

Read the café's training card, then the chat about Sofia's birthday. Just try to follow the story. We look at the grammar after.

Staff Training Card

Riverside Café — How to Make a Cappuccino

New staff, please read carefully!

  1. Add two spoons of coffee to the machine.
  2. Pour in the hot water.
  3. Heat the milk until it's warm.
  4. Stir gently, and pour the milk on top.

Don't forget to smile at every customer! Don't add sugar unless the customer asks for it. Always wash your hands before starting a shift.

Text Messages
Jack
How was your first shift?
Pablo
Good, I think! I made three cappuccinos and nobody complained 😄 By the way — when's Sofia's birthday exactly?
Jack
It's on the 21st of July. Should we celebrate at the café?
Pablo
Perfect! My shift finishes at 5pm on the 21st, so let's meet there after that.
Jack
Great — I'll order a coffee and a slice of cake for her. What's her 1st choice of cake, do you know?
Pablo
Chocolate, always! It's the 3rd time this month she's mentioned it.

Notice: The training card uses short instructions like "Add..." and "Don't forget..." with no subject like "I" or "you" — these are called imperatives. Jack and Pablo also use numbers like "21st", "1st" and "3rd" to talk about dates and order.

3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Imperatives

We use imperatives to give instructions, directions, recipes, or rules. There is no subject — the sentence starts directly with the verb.

How to make a cappuccino: instructions in order
Positive imperatives = base verb first. Negative imperatives = Don't + base verb.
FormExample
Positive: base verb firstAdd the coffee. Stir gently.
Negative: Don't + base verbDon't add sugar. Don't forget to smile.
Softer instructionsPlease wash your hands. Wash your hands, please.

⚠️ No Subject, No "-s"!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
You add the coffee.Add the coffee. (no subject needed)
Adds the coffee.Add the coffee. (no "-s" on imperatives)

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Does an imperative sentence have a subject like "I" or "you"? No.
How do we make an imperative negative? Don't + base verb.
Do we add "-s" to the verb in an imperative? No — always the base verb.

3.2 — Punctuation in Your Writing

From now on, punctuation is something to check every time you write — not a separate rule to learn once. Here's a quick reference.

Capital letters — start of every sentence, names (Pablo, Riverside Café), days and months (Monday, July)
Full stops (.) — at the end of a statement or an instruction
Question marks (?) — at the end of every question
Commas (,) — between items in a list, and in dates ("21st of July, 2026")
Apostrophes (') — in contractions (don't, it's) and possession (Sofia's birthday)
New line / list — for instructions, use a new line for each step (like the training card)

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do days and months need a capital letter? Yes — Monday, July, etc.
What punctuation mark shows "belongs to", as in "Sofia's birthday"? An apostrophe.

4Dates & Ordinal Numbers

Ordinal numbers (1st, 2nd, 3rd...) tell us the position or order of something — including the day in a date.

-st

1st (first)
21st (twenty-first)
31st (thirty-first)

-nd

2nd (second)
22nd (twenty-second)

-rd

3rd (third)
23rd (twenty-third)

-th

4th – 20th
24th – 30th
How to write itHow to say it
21st July / July 21st"the twenty-first of July" / "July the twenty-first"
3rd March"the third of March"
January February March April May June July August September October November December

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Does "2nd" end in -st, -nd, -rd or -th? -nd.
Does "15th" end in -st, -nd, -rd or -th? -th (most numbers use -th).

7Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "You add the coffee to the machine."
    Incorrect for an instruction — no subject needed: "Add the coffee to the machine."
  2. "Adds the milk slowly."
    Incorrect — no "-s" on imperatives: "Add the milk slowly."
  3. "Don't forget to smile."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Don't" + base verb is the correct negative imperative form. Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Her birthday is on the 2th of May."
    Incorrect ordinal ending — "2" needs "-nd": "Her birthday is on the 2nd of May."
  5. "my shift starts at 9am on monday."
    Incorrect punctuation — capital letters needed: "My shift starts at 9am on Monday."

🗣Speaking Practice

At the Riverside Café, people give instructions (First, press the button…) and talk about dates (on the third of May). This week your voice learns both. Voice memo app ready!

Give instructions

How to make my favourite drink

Think of a drink you make often — tea, coffee, anything. Explain how to make it in about 45 seconds, step by step, with imperatives and order words: "First, boil the water. Then, add… Next,… Finally, enjoy!"

How: Record it — even better, record it while you really make the drink. When you listen back, check: instructions start with the verb — "Boil the water," not "You boil." And each step gets its order word.
Dates challenge

Six dates from your life

Say six important dates aloud — with ordinal numbers, the café way: "My birthday is on the twelfth of March." Up to 60 seconds each:

  • Your birthday
  • A family member's birthday
  • A holiday you love
  • Today's date
  • The date of your next English class
  • One date you will never forget
How: One recording, timer on. When you listen back, check the endings: first, second, third, fourth… twelfth, twentieth. The "-th" sound needs your tongue between your teeth!
Shadowing drill

Copy the café dialogue

Choose 4 lines from this week's café dialogue — instructions or dates. Read them aloud three times:

  • Slowly and clearly;
  • at normal speed;
  • like a friendly barista on a busy morning.
How: Record the third time. When you listen back, check: a small pause after each order word — "First, | press the button." The pause makes instructions easy to follow.

Tips: clear steps, clear dates

  • Four order words are enough for any instructions: First, Then, Next, Finally. Learn them as a team.
  • Practise dates with your own calendar: every morning, say today's date aloud. Ten seconds, real progress.
  • The "-th" in fourth, fifth is hard in every language. Slow is fine — clear beats fast.
