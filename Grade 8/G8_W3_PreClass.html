ENGAGE

What would you change — if you could?

Before we start — a question about regret

Robert Frost's 1916 poem describes a traveller at a fork in a forest path. He chooses one road without knowing where the other leads — and imagines how he might feel about that choice much later.

This week's grammar is the grammar of that moment. Third conditionals, wish, and hedging language are tools for talking about things that didn't happen and what might have been.

Which of these would you most want to revisit if you could?

What you're going to read

You'll read a letter from a man named Elias writing to his estranged brother Marco, and a journal entry Marco wrote the same night he received it. Both are loaded with third conditionals, wish structures, and hedging phrases. Read for meaning and emotion first — grammar labels come after.

CONTEXT

Read first: Elias's letter

Elias hasn't spoken to his brother Marco in four years. He writes after their father's funeral. Before thinking about grammar: what does Elias most regret? What is he hoping for now?

Amber = Third Conditional. Forest green = Wish & Hope. Gold = Hedging language.

Personal LetterElias to Marco — 14 March

Dear Marco,

I know you didn't expect to hear from me. It seems like there's never a right time for this letter — but apparently it took losing Dad to make me write it.

It appears we both handled the last four years badly. I've been thinking about it for months. If I had called you after the argument, things would have been different. I know that now. If we hadn't let so much time pass, we might have seen Dad together before the end. That is the one I can't let go of.

I wish I had picked up the phone years ago. I've replayed that night in my head so many times. If I hadn't said what I said, you wouldn't have walked out. And if you hadn't walked out, none of this would have happened — but presumably neither of us was ready to hear what the other needed to say.

I hope we can try again. Not to pretend it didn't happen — but to find a way through it. I wish things were different between us right now, and it seems to me that we've both been waiting for the other person to move first.

So I'm moving first. Apparently that's what it takes.

Your brother,
Elias

Meaning check — no grammar terms yet

1. What specific event does Elias say he wishes he could change?

3. The word "presumably" softens a statement. What does it add — more certainty or less? Why might Elias use it here?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the amber phrases — find the pattern

Detective work: the grammar of "what might have been"

Did Elias call Marco? What time period does "If I had called you, things would have been different" describe?

No — he didn't call. The sentence describes a past event that didn't happen and imagines the outcome if it had. Both the condition and the result are imaginary past events.

Look at all four amber sentences. Can you find two parts in each — the "if" part and the "result"? What verb forms appear in each?

IF part: always had + past participle (past perfect). RESULT part: always would/might/could + have + past participle. This formula is fixed — every third conditional follows it.

Compare: "If it rained, I'd stay home" (2nd conditional) with "If I had called you, things would have been different" (3rd conditional). What is different about the time and the reality?

2nd = imaginary present/future (might happen). 3rd = imaginary PAST that definitely did NOT happen. The past is fixed — that's what makes the third conditional feel heavy with regret or relief.

STUDY

Third Conditional — the complete rule

1. Third Conditional

Imaginary past situations — things that didn't happen — and their imaginary results.

PartStructureFrom the letter
If-clauseif + subject + had + past participle"If I had called you…"
Result clausesubject + would have + past participle"…things would have been different."
Negative conditionif + hadn't + past participle"If we hadn't let so much time pass…"
Softer resultmight / could have instead of would have"We might have seen Dad together."

Three uses — same form, different emotional colour:

1Regret: something happened (or didn't) and you wish it had been different.
"If I hadn't said what I said, you wouldn't have walked out."Elias said something → regrets it → third conditional expresses that regret.
2Relief: something bad didn't happen and you're glad it didn't.
"If I hadn't left when I did, I would have missed the whole thing."Same structure — the speaker is relieved, not regretful.
3Blame or criticism: implies someone else made the wrong choice.
"If you had listened to me, this wouldn't have happened."Same form — the implication shifts responsibility to the other person.

⚠ The two most common errors:

✗If I would have called you, things would have been different.WRONG — "would have" cannot go in the if-clause. Only "had + past participle."
✗If I had called you, things were different.WRONG — result clause needs "would have been different," not simple past.
✓If we hadn't let so much time pass, we might have seen Dad together.Correct — "might have" is a valid softer alternative to "would have."

if + had/hadn't + PP → would / might / could + have + PP

Clause order can swap: "Things would have been different if I had called." — no comma when the if-clause comes second.

CONTEXT

Read next: Marco's journal

The same night he receives the letter, Marco writes in his journal. What does Marco wish were different? What does he hope for?

Forest green = Wish & Hope. Gold = Hedging language.

JournalMarco's journal — 14 March, late evening

I re-read the letter three times. It seems genuine — or apparently that's what I want to believe.

I wish I had handled things differently. That night, that argument — I wish I hadn't walked out. I've told myself for four years that I was right to. But sitting here reading Elias's words, it appears I've been telling myself a very comfortable story.

I wish things were simpler. I wish we were the kind of family that talked about things before they exploded. But we're not, and presumably we never were.

I hope he means it. I hope we can actually repair this — not just exchange polite letters for the next ten years. It seems to me that both of us have been grieving this relationship almost as much as we've been grieving Dad.

I wish I could say yes immediately. But I need time. Apparently even the first step is hard.

— M.

Meaning check

1. What "comfortable story" has Marco been telling himself?

2. What is the difference between Marco's wishes and his hopes — which are about the past, which about the present, which about the future?

3. Find a hedging phrase Marco uses when he isn't certain about something. What does it add?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the green and gold phrases — two sets of rules

Detective work: wish vs. hope, and hedging

Look at "I wish I had handled things differently" and "I wish things were simpler." Which is about the past? Which about the present? What verb forms follow wish in each?

"Wish I had handled" = past regret → wish + past perfect. "Wish things were simpler" = present impossible wish → wish + past simple. Wish always uses a tense one step back from reality.

Compare "I wish I could say yes" and "I hope we can actually repair this." Can both things happen? What's the key difference between wish and hope?

Wish = the speaker believes it's impossible or contrary-to-fact right now. Hope = the speaker thinks it could actually happen. Marco CAN'T say yes immediately (impossible) → wish + could. But repairing the relationship IS possible → hope + can.

Look at the gold phrases: it seems, apparently, it appears, presumably, it seems to me. What do they all signal about certainty? Why use them in a private journal?

All signal that the speaker is NOT fully certain — they soften a claim, making it provisional rather than absolute. Marco uses them because he's processing things he genuinely isn't sure about. In formal contexts, hedging also protects against being held to absolute statements.

STUDY

Wish & Hope — the complete system

2. Wish & Hope

Key rule: wish = impossible or contrary-to-fact. Hope = possible, speaker believes it can happen.

Wish + past simple → present impossible

  • The situation is NOT true now
  • Speaker wants it to be different

"I wish things were simpler." (They aren't.)
"I wish we lived closer." (We don't.)
"She wishes he understood." (He doesn't.)

Wish + past perfect → past regret

  • The action didn't happen in the past
  • Speaker regrets it

"I wish I had called." (I didn't.)
"I wish he hadn't left." (He did.)
"She wishes she had said something." (She didn't.)

Wish + could → impossible ability

  • The speaker is unable to do something
  • They want to be able to

"I wish I could say yes." (I can't.)
"He wishes he could turn back time." (Impossible.)
"She wishes she could explain." (She can't.)

Hope + present/future → genuinely possible

  • Speaker believes it can happen
  • They want it to

"I hope we can repair this." (Possible.)
"I hope he means it." (Plausible.)
"She hopes they will talk." (Could happen.)

⚠ Common errors:

✗I wish things are simpler.WRONG — wish does not take present simple. Use past simple: "I wish things were simpler."
✗I wish I would have called.WRONG — "would have" cannot follow wish for past regret. Use past perfect: "I wish I had called."
✓I wish I hadn't walked out.Correct past regret — wish + past perfect (hadn't + past participle).

⚠ Note on were: "I wish I were there" (subjunctive) is preferred in formal writing. "I wish I was" is widely accepted in speech. Both are understood.

STUDY

Hedging Language — a key B2+ feature

3. Hedging Language

Corpus data shows that B2+ speakers who sound too certain are often perceived as less sophisticated — or even blunt. Hedging signals intellectual honesty, caution, and social awareness. It appears in academic writing, journalism, professional emails, and everyday speech.

it seems (that)
Introduces an observation that appears to be true, but isn't stated as absolute fact. Very common in speech and writing.
"It seems like there's never a right time." / "It seems genuine."
it appears (that)
Slightly more formal than "it seems." Suggests the conclusion is based on evidence rather than feeling.
"It appears we both handled the last four years badly."
apparently
Introduces something that seems true from what you've observed or been told — without fully vouching for it. Can suggest mild irony or surprise.
"Apparently that's what it takes." / "Apparently I've been lying to myself."
presumably
States the most logical assumption without claiming certainty. Slightly formal; used in both speech and writing.
"Presumably neither of us was ready." / "Presumably he'll reply eventually."
it seems to me
Adds a personal perspective — makes clear this is one person's view, not an objective fact. Softer than "I think" in formal contexts.
"It seems to me that both of us have been grieving this relationship."

Position in the sentence:

HedgeSentence-initialMid-sentence
it seems / it appears"It seems we were both wrong."Less common mid-sentence
apparently"Apparently he never read the letter.""He apparently never read it."
presumably"Presumably she knew already.""She presumably knew already."
it seems to me"It seems to me that we've both changed."Always sentence-initial

Why this matters — compare:

✗You were wrong and you know it.Aggressive and certain — leaves no room for dialogue. Sounds blunt, possibly rude.
✓It seems to me that we may both have handled this badly.Softer, more honest, more open to dialogue. Note: "may have" adds a further layer of hedging.

SPEAKING PRACTICE

Regret, hope — spoken

Elias's letter says out loud what most people only think: what would have happened if things had gone differently. Saying it — fluently, with the grammar intact — is this week's spoken challenge. Voice memo app ready.

Timed monologue

The fork in the road

Set a timer for 2 minutes. Speak without stopping about a decision in your life that could have gone the other way — a course, a move, a job, a conversation you did or didn't have. What would have happened if you had chosen differently? Nothing too painful — pick something you can talk about comfortably.

Aim for at least three full Third Conditionals: "If I had taken that job, I would have moved to…"

How: Record it; don't stop to self-correct. When you listen back, check the machinery: if + had + past participle in one clause, would have + past participle in the other. The most common B2 slip is "if I would have" — hunt for it.
Opinion card

Agree or disagree?

"Regret is a useful emotion — people who say they have no regrets simply haven't learned anything."

Speak for 90 seconds. Use at least three items from this week's vocabulary (hindsight, closure, to dwell on, to come to terms with, a missed opportunity…) and soften at least two claims with hedging language from the Study section (it seems to me, perhaps, I tend to think).

How: One take, recorded. When you listen back, check the hedges: a B2 speaker who states everything as absolute fact sounds less convincing, not more. Did you leave room for the other side?
Shadowing drill

Reading Elias aloud

Go back to Elias's letter in the Context section and take four sentences that carry the most feeling — including at least one Third Conditional and one I wish sentence. Read them aloud three times:

  • Once slowly, every word deliberate;
  • once at normal reading-aloud pace;
  • once as if you were Elias — quiet, but sure of every word.
How: Record the third read. When you listen back, check: in natural speech, would have compresses to would've and had to 'd. If you can hear every full syllable, do a fourth read — softer and faster.

Technique: heavy grammar, light delivery

  • The Third Conditional is long. Native speakers survive it by compressing: "If I'd known, I would've called." Practise the compressed version until it's one smooth shape, not six words.
  • Wish + past for the present, wish + past perfect for the past. Before you speak, decide which one your regret is — the tense follows automatically.
  • Pausing before if-clauses is natural; pausing inside them isn't. Keep each clause in one breath.

Mixed conditionals — the next step

A mixed conditional combines a 3rd conditional if-clause (past) with a 2nd conditional result (present): "If I had taken that job, I would be living in Berlin now."
