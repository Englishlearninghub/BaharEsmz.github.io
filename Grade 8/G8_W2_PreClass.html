ENGAGE

Have you ever regretted something you posted online?

Before we start — a quick poll

Someone messages a friend at midnight. They'd had an argument earlier. They'd been stewing over it for hours. They'd already written a long apology — and then deleted it. Then they posted something cryptic on their public profile instead.

We've all seen (or done) something like this. When you look back on a social media mistake, what's the most common reason?

What this week is about

You're going to read a DM thread and a comment thread between two friends, Priya and Jade, whose friendship hit a wall after a social media incident. The grammar you'll study — the Past Perfect — is the tense of "before." It's the tense of backstory, context, and explanation. It answers the question: what had already happened before this moment?

Read both texts for meaning first. Everything in the grammar sections points back to sentences you've already read.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the rose phrases — what are they doing?

Detective work: the past behind the past

Look at: "I had already deleted it by the time you saw it." There are two past events here. Which one happened first — the deleting or the seeing? How does the sentence tell you this?

The deleting happened FIRST — that's what had already deleted signals. The seeing happened AFTER. "By the time" is a key phrase that explicitly orders two events in the past. Had deleted marks the earlier event.

Now look at: "People had already seen it. Kemal had already screenshotted it." These appear in Priya's message about the post. Is Priya describing things that happened before or after she sent this message — and before or after the deletion?

Both happened BEFORE the deletion — which itself happened BEFORE Priya's message. So we have three layers of time: (1) people seeing and screenshotting, (2) Jade deleting, (3) Priya messaging. The Past Perfect marks layer 1 as the furthest back.

Look at: "I hadn't meant to hurt you." and "You hadn't told me." These use the negative form. What is each person expressing? Is it an excuse, an explanation, or something else?

Both are explanations of context — things that HADN'T happened before the moment in question. Priya: "My intention wasn't to hurt you" (context for her action). Jade: "You hadn't warned me" (context explaining her reaction). The Past Perfect negative is especially powerful for establishing backstory and justifying a response.

STUDY

Past Perfect — the rule behind what you found

1. Past Perfect

You found the pattern in the DM thread. Here is the complete rule.

Form:

SubjectAuxiliaryPast participleExample from the DMs
all subjectshaddeleted, seen, tried…I had already deleted it.
Negativehad not / hadn'tthought, told, meant…I hadn't thought about that.
QuestionHad + subjectpast participleHad she seen it by then?

What it means — two main uses:

1 Sequencing past events: marks the earlier of two past actions.
I had already deleted it by the time you saw it. Delete = first. See = second. Past Perfect marks "delete" as the earlier event. Key signals: by the time, before, when, already, by then, after
2 Backstory and context: explains what had already happened to make sense of the present situation.
By the time you posted that story, we had been arguing for two weeks. The arguing was ongoing backstory — it explains WHY the post was so hurtful.

Past Perfect — ordering two past events

had + past participle = the earlier past eventSimple Past = the later past event

Common error to avoid:

✗ By the time Priya saw it, Jade deleted the post. WRONG — two Simple Pasts don't show which happened first. The meaning is ambiguous.
✓ By the time Priya saw it, Jade had already deleted the post. Correct — Past Perfect makes the sequence unambiguous.

⚠ Note: When the sequence is already clear from context words like before or after, the Past Perfect is optional but still natural. "She posted the story after they had argued" and "She posted the story after they argued" are both acceptable — but the Past Perfect version is more formal and more precise.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the indigo phrases — what follows each verb?

Detective work: what comes after the verb?

Look at these three verbs from the thread: suggest, avoid, consider. What do they all have in common about what comes immediately after them? Check: "suggest giving," "avoid sending," "consider asking."

They all take an -ing form (gerund) directly after them. You cannot say "suggest to give," "avoid to send," or "consider to ask" in standard English.

Now look at: "don't know whether to apologise or to give her space," "tried to reach out," "recommend calling." Which verb here takes to + infinitive, and which takes -ing? Is there one that could take both?

"Whether to apologise" = to + infinitive (after verbs of deliberation: know, decide, wonder). "Tried to reach out" = to + infinitive (try meaning "attempt"). "Recommend calling" = -ing. Note: "try" is interesting — "I tried calling" means I attempted it as an experiment; "I tried to call" means I attempted but may not have succeeded.

Finally: "I admit that I handled it badly," "I believe that a phone call would be more effective," "I've found that people appreciate knowing." What structure follows admit, believe, and find here?

All three are followed by a that-clause (a full sentence with its own subject and verb). This pattern is very common with verbs of thinking, perceiving, and communication — and it's essential for formal and written B2+ English.

STUDY

Verb Patterns — three types, clear rules

2. Verb Patterns

The verb that follows a main verb must be in a specific form. There is no universal rule — each verb must be learned with its pattern. But there ARE clear groups.

Verb + to + infinitive

  • want, need, decide, hope
  • refuse, agree, offer, promise
  • try (= attempt), manage
  • know, wonder (+ whether/what/how)

"I need to think about this."
"She decided to call him."
"I don't know whether to apologise."

Verb + -ing (gerund)

  • suggest, recommend, consider
  • avoid, keep, enjoy, mind
  • try (= experiment), practise
  • finish, stop, miss, deny

"I suggest giving her space."
"Avoid sending multiple messages."
"I've tried calling — no answer."

Verb + that-clause

  • admit, believe, think, know
  • notice, find, realise, feel
  • suggest*, recommend*
  • hope, expect, agree, say

"I admit that I handled it badly."
"I believe that a call would help."
"She found that space was needed."

Verb + object + to + infinitive

  • want, ask, tell, advise
  • invite, remind, encourage
  • expect, need, allow, warn

"I want you to know I didn't mean it."
"She asked him to wait."
"He advised her to call."

The try / remember / stop distinction — meaning changes with pattern:

Verb+ to + infinitive+ -ing
tryattempt (may not succeed): "I tried to call her."experiment with: "I tried calling — she didn't pick up."
rememberbefore the action: "Remember to call her."after the action: "I remember calling — she was cold."
stopstop in order to: "She stopped to read the message."stop the activity: "She stopped messaging him."

3. Adjective + Noun Collocations

Many adjective + noun combinations in English are fixed — the "right" adjective must be learned with its noun. Getting these wrong sounds unnatural even when the meaning is clear.

From the social media theme this week:

strong
opinion / argument / reaction / bond
close
friend / relationship / contact / call
heavy
traffic / workload / silence / criticism
mutual
friend / respect / agreement / benefit
genuine
apology / concern / effort / interest
direct
message / approach / question / contact
public
post / apology / statement / profile
online
presence / fallout / community / argument
deep
regret / understanding / connection / breath

Why "wrong" collocations sound odd:

✗ She has a big opinion about everything. → "strong opinion" — not "big." Size ≠ intensity in English.
✗ He sent a large criticism on her post. → "heavy criticism" or "strong criticism" — not "large."
✓ Priya and Jade had a strong bond before the incident. Natural — "strong bond" is a fixed collocation.

SPEAKING PRACTICE

Backstory — spoken

Priya and Jade's whole conflict is about backstory — what had already happened before each message was sent. Telling that kind of story out loud is a core B2 skill. Use your phone's voice memo app.

Retell task

Tell the whole story — in order and out of order

Re-read the DM thread and the comment thread. Then close them and retell the Priya–Jade story aloud from memory in about 90 seconds — but start from the moment Jade saw the post. That forces the Past Perfect: "By the time Jade saw it, Priya had already posted… she hadn't realised that…"

How: Record without looking at the text. When you listen back, count: your Past Perfect forms. Every "before" moment should have one. If your whole retell is in the Simple Past, do it again starting even later in the story.
Opinion card

Agree or disagree?

"Screenshotting a private conversation and showing it to someone else is always a betrayal — whatever the reason."

Speak for 90 seconds. Take a clear position, then defend it. Use at least three items from this week's vocabulary (to overshare, digital footprint, accountability, to reach out, passive-aggressive…) and at least one verb pattern from the Study section (regret doing, decide to do, accuse someone of doing).

How: Record it in one take. When you listen back, check: did you actually answer the statement, or drift into telling a story? B2 speakers hold a position; the story is only evidence.
Discussion questions

For a partner — or the microphone

Discuss with a partner, or record spoken answers (60–90 seconds each). Choose two:

  • Have you ever misread a message because you didn't know what had happened before it was sent? What had you missed?
  • Is it ever right to "ghost" someone instead of confronting them? Where's the line?
  • Priya deleted the post, but Jade had already seen it. Online, can anything really be undone?
  • Who should reach out first after a fight — the person who caused it, or the person who's least angry?
How: Voice memo, one question per recording. When you listen back, check: Did you give backstory with the Past Perfect where it belonged — "I thought she was ignoring me, but her phone had died"?

Technique: had — small word, heavy lifting

  • In natural speech, had shrinks to 'd: "She'd already seen it." Practise the contraction out loud — the full form sounds like reading.
  • One Past Perfect anchor is often enough to set the backstory; then Simple Past carries the rest. Don't chain five had dones in a row.
  • When you disagree in a discussion, concede first, then push: "Fair enough, but by that point she had already…"
