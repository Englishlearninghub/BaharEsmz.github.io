ENGAGE

What would you say — and how would you report it?

Before we start

You witness a car run a red light and clip a cyclist. The cyclist falls. The driver stops briefly, then drives away. A police officer arrives two minutes later and asks you what happened.

You say: "The driver ran the red light and hit the cyclist. He said something I couldn't hear, then drove off."

The officer writes in her report: "The witness stated that the driver had run the red light and had hit the cyclist. She added that the driver had said something she couldn't hear before driving off."

Which of these feels most true to how you'd handle this situation?

What you're going to read

You'll read a news article about a hit-and-run incident, and the police interview transcript with the key witness. Both are rich with reported speech and — in the interview — with cleft sentences used for emphasis. Read for meaning and sequence first, then look at how the language works.

Colour code: amber = Reported Speech. blue = Cleft Sentences. slate = Optional backshift (corpus note).

CONTEXT

Read first: the news article

Before thinking about grammar: What happened? Who reported it? What is still unknown? Note three facts and one gap in the story.

News ArticleRiverside Gazette — Local News

Cyclist injured in hit-and-run on Elm Street

By Rosa Ferreira, Staff Reporter · Tuesday, 11 March

A 34-year-old cyclist was taken to Riverside General Hospital on Monday night after being struck by a vehicle that failed to stop at the scene. The incident occurred at approximately 11:15 pm at the junction of Elm Street and Park Road.

A witness, Maya Osei, told reporters that the driver had run the red light at speed and had not appeared to brake before impact. She added that the driver had briefly stopped the vehicle, opened the window, and then driven away without assisting the victim.

Officer Diaz of Riverside Police confirmed that eyewitness statements had been taken and that the vehicle had been described as a dark-coloured saloon car. She stated that CCTV footage was being reviewed and that officers were appealing for anyone with dashcam footage to come forward.

The cyclist, who has not been named, was reported to be in a stable condition. A hospital spokesperson said that he had been treated for a broken collarbone and minor head injuries.

Maya Osei told this newspaper that she had been standing at the bus stop when she heard the collision. "What shocked me most," she said, "was that the driver slowed down, looked out, and then just drove away."

Anyone with information is asked to contact Riverside Police on 0800 555 111.

Meaning check — no grammar terms yet

1. At what time and location did the incident occur?

2. What did the driver do after the collision — according to Maya?

3. What two things is the police still trying to establish?

4. Maya's direct quote at the end uses "what shocked me most." What is she emphasising? Why does she start the sentence this way rather than saying "I was most shocked that the driver…"?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the amber phrases — find the reporting pattern

Detective work: turning direct speech into reported speech

Maya said: "The driver ran the red light at speed." The article reports: "…the driver had run the red light at speed." What changed — and what didn't?

The verb changed: "ran" (simple past) → "had run" (past perfect). The reporting verb "told reporters that" introduced the change. When you report what someone said in the past, verbs typically shift one step back in time — this is called backshift.

Officer Diaz said: "CCTV footage is being reviewed." The article says: "…CCTV footage was being reviewed." What happened to the tense? What other changes do you notice?

Present continuous "is being reviewed" → Past continuous "was being reviewed." One step back. Also note: pronouns may change (I → she, you → he/she) and time expressions may shift (today → that day, tomorrow → the following day).

Maya's direct quote says: "the driver slowed down, looked out, and then just drove away." This is inside a longer reported section. Does anything shift here? What does this tell you about how journalists use mixed reporting?

In the direct quote, the verbs stay as simple past — because it's direct speech (actual words, in quotation marks). Journalists often mix direct and indirect speech: exact words in quotes (direct), summaries in their own words (indirect/reported). Both are valid techniques.

STUDY

Reported Speech — Backshift Table

1. Reported Speech — Statement Backshift

When we report what someone said in the past, verbs shift one step back in time. This is called backshift.

Direct Speech (tense)→Reported Speech (tense)Example
Present simple→Past simple"He runs red lights." → She said he ran red lights.
Present continuous→Past continuous"CCTV is being reviewed." → She stated it was being reviewed.
Past simple→Past perfect"The driver ran the light." → She told us the driver had run the light.
Present perfect→Past perfect"Officers have arrived." → She confirmed officers had arrived.
will→would"We will appeal for witnesses." → She said they would appeal for witnesses.
can→could"I can see the number plate." → He said he could see the number plate.
may→might"The driver may be local." → Officers said the driver might be local.

Time and place expressions also shift:

Direct Speech→Reported Speech
now→then / at that moment
today→that day
yesterday→the day before / the previous day
tomorrow→the next day / the following day
here→there
this→that
ago→before / previously

Common reporting verbs:

say (that)tell + object (that)state (that) confirm (that)add (that)claim (that) warn (that)admit (that)deny (that) explain (that)report (that)announce (that)

⚠ Note: say does not take an object directly — "She said that…" ✓ / "She said me that…" ✗. Tell always needs an object — "She told officers that…" ✓ / "She told that…" ✗

STUDY

Reported Questions & Requests

2. Reported Questions & Requests

Questions and requests change structure — not just tense — when reported. The word order becomes statement order.

Yes/No Questions → whether / if

→Direct: "Did you see the number plate?"
Reported: She asked whether I had seen the number plate.No question mark. "Did" disappears — backshift only (see → had seen). Word order: subject + verb.
→Direct: "Was the driver alone?"
Reported: He asked if the driver had been alone.If / whether — both correct. "Was" backshifts to "had been."

Wh- Questions → wh-word + statement word order

→Direct: "Where did the car go?"
Reported: She asked where the car had gone.Wh-word kept. "Did go" → "had gone." No inversion (gone the car ✗ → the car had gone ✓).
→Direct: "What time did the incident occur?"
Reported: Officer Diaz asked what time the incident had occurred.Compound wh-phrase ("what time") stays together. Statement order follows.

Requests & Instructions → asked / told + to + infinitive

→Direct: "Please stay at the scene."
Reported: The officer asked Maya to stay at the scene.No tense change needed. The infinitive carries the meaning.
→Direct: "Don't leave before giving a statement."
Reported: She told witnesses not to leave before giving a statement.Negative: told + object + not + to + infinitive.

⚠ The word-order trap:

✗She asked where had the car gone.WRONG — keep statement word order: "where the car had gone" (subject before verb).
✓She asked where the car had gone.Correct — wh-word + subject + verb.
STUDY

Corpus Note — When Backshift Is Optional

3. Optional Backshift (corpus-informed)

This is a nuance missing from most textbooks but confirmed by corpus research. Backshift is NOT always required — it's optional when the information is still currently true, or in informal spoken English.

When backshift is optional:

"She told me she is a nurse."
←
Still true now — she's still a nurse. Backshift optional.
"He said the earth is round."
←
Universal/scientific fact — never backshifts.
"She said she lives nearby."
←
Informal spoken English — present tense kept for immediacy.
"He said he can help."
←
Still applicable now — "could" or "can" both acceptable.

When backshift IS required (formal written English, or when the situation no longer applies):

✓She told reporters the driver had run the red light.Formal written report. The running happened in the past — backshift appropriate and expected.
✓He claimed he hadn't seen the traffic light.Specific past event being reported formally. Backshift expected.

In this week's traffic/crime context: always use backshift in formal written reports and police statements. The optional-backshift rule applies more to everyday informal speech. Your exam will expect backshift unless specifically told otherwise.

Report each question or request. Pay attention to word order.

1."Did you see the colour of the car?" (officer / ask / Maya)
2."Where did the car go after the collision?" (reporter / ask)
3."Please wait here until we take your statement." (officer / ask / witnesses)

Exercise 3 · Error correction

Each sentence has one error. Find and fix it.

1.She said me that the driver had not stopped.
2.The officer asked where had the car gone.
3.He told that the road was clear at the time.
4.She said officers they should check the CCTV footage.
1.She told me that the driver had not stopped. / She said that the driver had not stopped."Said me" is the error — say never takes a direct object. "Said (that)…" or "told me (that)…"
2.The officer asked where the car had gone."Where had the car gone" = question inversion. Reported questions use statement order: where the car had gone.
3.He said that the road was clear. / He told officers that the road was clear."Told that" (without object) is the error. "Tell" always needs an object: "told officers / told us / told me."
4.She told officers they should check the CCTV footage. / She told officers to check the CCTV footage."Said officers" is wrong — "said" cannot take a personal object. Must be "told officers."

The blue-highlighted phrases are Cleft Sentences — look at how they structure emphasis differently from a plain statement.

Officer Diaz

Can you tell me in your own words what you observed last night?

Maya Osei

What I saw was a dark car accelerating through a red light. The cyclist had no chance. What happened next was the most disturbing part. The driver actually slowed down — I thought he was going to help. But then he just drove away.

Officer Diaz

Did you see the driver's face at any point?

Maya Osei

Not clearly. What I could make out was that he appeared to be wearing a dark jacket. What struck me was the way he looked directly at the cyclist and then looked away. He made a choice.

Officer Diaz

And the vehicle — any details you can give us?

Maya Osei

What I remember most clearly is the rear number plate — partial, ending in 7 or 9. And it was a saloon, dark blue or possibly black. What I don't know is whether there were any passengers.

Officer Diaz

Thank you, Ms Osei. This is very helpful. One more question — did anyone else witness the incident?

Maya Osei

There was a man walking a dog about fifty metres away. What I'm not sure of is whether he saw the same thing I did. He seemed shaken, though.

1. What detail about the driver's behaviour does Maya emphasise most — and how does her language show that it's the most important thing?

2. What partial information does Maya provide about the vehicle?

3. All of Maya's blue-highlighted sentences start with "What…". What kind of information follows the "what" part in each sentence?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the blue phrases — find the cleft pattern

Detective work: the What-clause for emphasis

Compare: "I saw a dark car accelerating through a red light." (plain) vs. "What I saw was a dark car accelerating through a red light." (cleft). Is the information different? What is different?

Same information — but the cleft version puts extra weight on "a dark car accelerating through a red light." The structure What + subject + verb + was + [focus] puts the most important part at the end, after "was." The listener's attention is drawn to what comes after "was."

Look at: "What happened next was the most disturbing part." Can you split this sentence into two parts — a "what" part and a "focus" part?

"What happened next" = the subject/topic. "was the most disturbing part" = the focus/emphasis. The cleft formula is: What + [clause] + was + [focus]. The focus (what you want to emphasise) always comes after "was."

Look at: "What I don't know is whether there were any passengers." Here the cleft emphasises a gap in knowledge, not a fact. Can you think of a situation where you'd use this kind of sentence?

What-clefts can emphasise uncertainty, missing information, or limits of knowledge — not just positive facts. "What I'm not sure of is whether…" / "What I can't confirm is…" This is very common in witness statements, academic hedging, and spoken English for emphasis.

STUDY

Cleft Sentences — What-clause for Emphasis

4. Cleft Sentences (What-clause)

A cleft sentence splits a plain statement into two clauses to create emphasis. The What-clause is the most common type in spoken English and witness/interview contexts.

The formula:

What + [subject + verb]  +  was/is  +  [the focus / emphasis]

Emphasising a noun phrase

Plain: I noticed the rear number plate.


Cleft: "What I noticed was the rear number plate."

Focus = the rear number plate (put last, after "was")

Emphasising an action / event

Plain: The driver's looking away disturbed me most.


Cleft: "What struck me was the way he looked away."

Focus = the way he looked away — put last for weight

Emphasising uncertainty / limits

Plain: I don't know if there were passengers.


Cleft: "What I don't know is whether there were any passengers."

Focus = a gap in knowledge — what-clause works here too

Emphasising importance / significance

Plain: The most disturbing thing was what happened next.


Cleft: "What happened next was the most disturbing part."

Focus = the most disturbing part — evaluation placed at end

Tense in the cleft:

ContextVerb in What-clauseLinking verbExample
Describing past eventsPast simple / past perfectwas"What I saw was a dark car."
Describing present statePresent simpleis"What concerns me is the lack of CCTV."
Describing what you rememberPast simpleis / was"What I remember most clearly is the number plate."

⚠ Common errors:

✗What I saw it was a dark car.WRONG — "it" is an extra subject. Remove it: "What I saw was a dark car."
✗What happened was the car it drove away.WRONG — "it" duplicates the subject again. The focus clause is a noun phrase, not another full clause with its own pronoun.
✓What I remember most clearly is the rear number plate.Correct — What + [I remember most clearly] + is + [the focus].

Exercise 7 · Reporting verb choice

Choose the most appropriate reporting verb. More than one may be acceptable — justify your choice.

said · confirmed · claimed · denied · admitted · alleged · stated

1.The driver __________ that he had not seen the cyclist at all.
2.He __________ running the red light when shown the dashcam footage.
3.He __________ having been at the scene — but witnesses contradicted this.

SPEAKING PRACTICE

Reporting it — out loud

A hit-and-run produces talk: witnesses tell police what they saw, reporters tell readers what witnesses said. Passing speech from one mouth to another is exactly this week's grammar — and it's much harder out loud than on paper. Voice memo app ready.

Roleplay scenario

The reporter and the eyewitness

Role A — Eyewitness: You were on Elm Street on Monday night. Describe what you saw in direct speech, with detail: the car, the cyclist, the driver's behaviour. Invent freely, but stay consistent.

Role B — Reporter (like Rosa Ferreira): Interview the witness — ask at least two reported questions afterwards when you summarise: "I asked her whether she had seen the driver. She told me the car had been travelling at speed…"

How: With a partner, or both roles yourself; record 3–4 minutes. Afterwards, Role B records a 60-second "news summary" of the interview entirely in reported speech. When you listen back, check: did questions lose their question order (I asked where he was, not where was he)?
Retell task

The radio bulletin

Re-read the Riverside Gazette article. Close it. Now deliver it as a 45-second radio news bulletin from memory — the key facts, what police said, what remains unknown. Reported speech will carry the middle: "Police said the driver had fled the scene… they appealed for witnesses to come forward."

How: Record it once, listen, then re-record tighter. When you listen back, check: a bulletin has no um — where you hesitated is where you need a fixed phrase (in a stable condition, appealed for witnesses).
Sentence challenge

Six terms, sixty seconds each

Use each of these items from this week's vocabulary in a spoken sentence about a news story you've actually seen or read. 60 seconds per item:

  • eyewitness
  • to come forward
  • dashcam footage
  • to flee the scene
  • allegedly
  • to press charges
How: One continuous recording, timer running. When you listen back, check: allegedly is the tricky one — it must sit next to the unproven claim, not the proven fact. Re-record any sentence where it drifted.

Technique: the backshift under pressure

  • Before you report a sentence aloud, take one silent second to shift the tense — says → said, has fled → had fled, will → would. One second of silence beats a wrong tense at full speed.
  • Reporting verbs are your gear system: said is neutral, claimed doubts, admitted concedes, insisted pushes. Vary them — five saids in a row is a flat bulletin.
  • Cleft sentences add spotlight when speaking: "What worries police most is the missing dashcam footage." Use one per bulletin, no more.

Grammar extension

It-clefts — a second type

You've studied What-clefts this week. There is a second type: It-cleft. "It was the number plate that Maya noticed." / "It was at 11:15 pm that the incident occurred."

  • Formula: It + was/is + [focus] + that/who/which + [rest of clause]
  • Rewrite three sentences from the news article as It-clefts.
  • Compare: when would you choose a What-cleft over an It-cleft? Bring one example of each to class.

Reported statementssay/tell/state + (that) + backshifted clauseVerbs shift one tense back; pronouns and time refs change"It's dark." → She said it was dark.
Reported yes/no Qask + if/whether + subject + verb (statement order)No question mark; "did" removed; word order inverts to statement"Did you see it?" → He asked if I had seen it.
Reported wh-Qask + wh-word + subject + verbWh-word kept; statement word order (no inversion)"Where did it go?" → She asked where it had gone.
Reported requestask/tell + object + (not) + to + infNo tense change; infinitive carries meaning"Please wait." → She asked us to wait.
Optional backshiftPresent tense may be keptWhen still true now, or in informal spoken English"She told me she is a nurse." (still true)
What-cleftWhat + [subject + verb] + was/is + [focus]Focus goes after "was/is"; no extra pronoun (not "What I saw it was")"What I saw was a dark car."
Cleft for gapsWhat + [I don't know / can't confirm] + is + whether/how/who…Works for uncertainty, not just facts"What I don't know is whether there were passengers."
