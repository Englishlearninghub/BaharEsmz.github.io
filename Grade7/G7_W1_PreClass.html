NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look back at the highlighted phrases

Detective work: imagining the unreal

Look at "If I became Prime Minister tomorrow, I would invest..." Is Amara actually likely to become Prime Minister tomorrow? What does that tell you about why the verb "became" is in a past form, even though she's talking about tomorrow?

No, it's a pure hypothetical, not a real future possibility. The past form ("became") signals unreality or distance from fact, not actual past time — this is the core idea behind the second conditional.

Look at Priya's sentence: "If voting were compulsory everywhere..." Most verbs here use the simple past form (became, had, earned), but "were" is used even with "voting" (singular). Have you seen "were" used with a singular subject before, and where?

This is the subjunctive "were," traditionally preferred after "if" with any subject in formal English ("if I were," "if she were"), even though "was" is now common informally. Both appear in real usage, but "were" is considered more correct in writing.

Compare "I would invest" and "I wouldn't make any huge changes." What does adding "n't" change about Diego's hypothetical plan?

Simply the polarity of the hypothetical result — "would" and "wouldn't" both describe imagined outcomes, just opposite ones. The grammar pattern itself doesn't change.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Wishing about now, then, and other people

Detective work: not all wishes are the same

Compare "I wish I had paid more attention" with "I wish I understood the local issues better." One describes a single past action, the other describes an ongoing state. Do both use a past form after "wish," even though their meanings are slightly different?

Yes — "had paid" (past perfect, regret about a past action) and "understood" (past simple, regret about a present/past state) both follow "wish" with a past-shifted form. The exact tense shifts depending on whether the regret is about the past or the present, but it always moves backward in time from the real situation.

Look at "I wish politicians would listen to young voters more." Is this wish about something amara_v wants to change about herself, or about someone else's behavior?

Someone else's behavior — "wish + would" is specifically used to express frustration about another person's (or group's) actions, often implying annoyance that they haven't changed already.

Now compare "I wish I would stop feeling like my vote doesn't matter" with "I wish there were a single trustworthy source." Both use slightly different patterns after "wish" — one is "would + verb," the other is "were." Based on what you just noticed about "would," does "I wish I would stop feeling..." sound slightly unusual to you, even though it's grammatically valid?

Yes, and that's intentional — "wish + would" about yourself is technically possible but sounds awkward to native speakers, since "would" is meant for other people's behavior. Most speakers would say "I wish I could stop feeling..." instead, which the Study section explains next.

STUDY

Now let's name it: wish

2. Wish + Past Simple (the most common pattern)

By far the most frequent way to express regret about the present in real English — learn this pattern first.

✓ I wish I understood the local issues better. Wishing the present situation were different — I don't understand them now. Found in: forum thread, riverthompson
✓ I wish I lived somewhere with a simpler voting system. Wishing my current, real situation were different. Found in: forum thread, diego_chen
✓ I wish I had paid more attention in my first election. Regret about a finished past action — uses past perfect, one step further back. Found in: forum thread, riverthompson

I didn't pay attention.I wish I had paid attention. (past perfect)
I can't vote in this election.I wish I could vote in this election. (could)

wish + past simple regret about the present

wish + past perfect regret about the past

wish + could regret about present ability/permission

2b. Wish + Would (secondary pattern: frustration with others)

A distinct, less frequent pattern — specifically for criticizing someone else's actions, never your own.

About yourself or a state

I wish I understood politics better.

I wish I could vote here.

Use past simple, past perfect, or could.

About someone else's behavior

I wish politicians would listen more.

I wish the news would stop exaggerating.

Use "would" — implies frustration they haven't changed.

✓ I wish politicians would listen to young voters more. Frustration about politicians' (someone else's) ongoing behavior. Found in: forum thread, amara_v
✗ I wish I would stop feeling like my vote doesn't matter. Unusual — "wish + would" about yourself sounds awkward to native speakers. More natural: I wish I could stop feeling like my vote doesn't matter.
PRONUNCIATION

The weak, fast "would"

"Would" almost disappears in fast speech

This is one of the biggest gaps between what learners expect to hear and what native speakers actually say.

In natural speech, "would" after a pronoun is almost always contracted and reduced to a quick "/d/" sound:

I'd invest in education. /aɪd ɪnˈvɛst/ — "would" reduces to just "/d/"

She'd change the system. /ʃiːd tʃeɪndʒ/ — almost merges into the pronoun

Learners who pronounce "would" fully and separately in every sentence ("I would invest," stressed evenly) tend to sound noticeably less fluent than learners who use the natural contraction "I'd invest." Try saying "If I had more time, I'd read more" out loud, blending "I'd" into one quick syllable.

Politics, systems & regret vocabulary

2. Political roles & titles

a ministernoun
a senior member of government responsible for a specific area (e.g. the Finance Minister, the Health Minister)
"I wish the Education Minister would spend a week in an actual classroom."
a prime ministernoun
the head of government in countries with a parliamentary system (e.g. the UK, India, Canada)
"If I were Prime Minister, I would make civic education compulsory."
a chancellornoun
the head of government in some countries (e.g. Germany, Austria); also the finance minister in the UK
"The Chancellor announced a new economic policy last week."
a vice presidentnoun
the second-highest official in a presidential system; takes over if the president cannot serve
"I wish the vice president had more influence on domestic policy."
a monarchnoun
a king or queen who rules a country, often by hereditary right
"In a constitutional monarchy, the monarch has mainly ceremonial powers."
a dictatornoun
a ruler who holds power without democratic accountability, often by force
"I wish there were international mechanisms to remove dictators from power."
a democratnoun
someone who believes in democracy as a system of government (lowercase d; separate from any specific party)
"He considers himself a democrat first and a party member second."
a republicannoun / adjective
someone who believes a country should be governed as a republic (without a monarch); note: also a specific US party name
"Many republicans argued that abolishing the monarchy would modernise the state."

3. Political systems

a monarchynoun
a system where a king or queen is the head of state; can be absolute or constitutional
"In a constitutional monarchy, the monarch's powers are limited by law."
a republicnoun
a system where the head of state is elected, not hereditary; most modern democracies are republics
"France became a republic after its revolution."
a dictatorshipnoun
a system in which one person or group holds absolute power, usually without free elections
"I wish the international community did more to challenge dictatorships."
democracynoun
a system where citizens vote to choose their leaders and influence decisions
"If more people participated, democracy would function much better."
capitalismnoun
an economic system where businesses and property are privately owned and operate for profit
"I wish capitalism were better regulated to protect workers and the environment."
socialismnoun
a system where key industries or resources are owned or regulated collectively, often by the state, to reduce inequality
"Supporters of socialism argue that it distributes wealth more fairly."
left-wingadjective
favouring social equality, workers' rights, public services, and redistribution of wealth
"Left-wing parties typically support higher taxes on the wealthy."
right-wingadjective
favouring free markets, limited government, traditional values, and lower taxes
"Right-wing governments often prioritise reducing public spending."

4. Political ideologies

conservatismnoun
a political ideology that values tradition, stability, free markets, and limited government intervention
"Conservatism generally opposes rapid social change."
a conservativenoun / adjective
someone who holds conservative beliefs; or an adjective describing cautious, tradition-respecting positions
"She is fiscally conservative but socially progressive."
liberalismnoun
a political ideology emphasising individual freedoms, civil rights, and democratic government
"Classical liberalism focuses on personal liberty rather than economic equality."
nationalismnoun
strong identification with one's own nation and its interests, sometimes to the exclusion of others
"Economic nationalism often leads to trade barriers between countries."
centristnoun / adjective
holding moderate political views; not strongly left-wing or right-wing
"I wish more centrist voices were represented in the debate."
progressiveadjective / noun
favouring social reform, equality, and modernisation; often associated with the political left
"Progressive policies on climate and education were central to the campaign."
radicaladjective
advocating thorough or fundamental change to existing systems; can apply to left or right
"If I were in charge, I would introduce some radical reforms to the tax system."
bipartisanadjective
involving or supported by both major parties; representing cross-party agreement
"I wish climate policy had broader bipartisan support."

A note on register

Many of these words are formal or academic in register. You wouldn’t use bipartisan or constituency in a casual text message, but you would in a discussion, article, or presentation.

Be careful with words that have a general meaning and a specific party name: democrat (lowercase) means someone who believes in democracy; Democrat (capital D) is a US party member. The same applies to republican / Republican.

Left-wing and right-wing are relative terms — what counts as “left” or “right” varies significantly by country and historical period.
