ENGAGE

Modal meaning sliders

Before any grammar: how strong is each modal?

Modal verbs express different degrees of certainty and obligation. For each sentence, drag the slider from weak/uncertain (left) to strong/certain (right). Reflect on why you placed it there — your reasoning matters more than the exact position.

She might be interested in the course — I’m not sure.

Weak / uncertainStrong / certain

 

You must submit your application by Friday. No exceptions.

Weak / uncertainStrong / certain

 

She must really like him — she keeps finding excuses to see him.

Weak / uncertainStrong / certain

 

You should talk to her — she probably feels the same.

Weak / uncertainStrong / certain

 

He can’t have forgotten — we spoke about it yesterday.

Weak / uncertainStrong / certain

 

What your sliders tell us: Modals carry different functions. The same word can mean different things: must can express obligation (sentences 2) or logical deduction (sentence 3). The five functions this week: possibility, obligation, permission, suggestion, ability.

For possibility, the typical strength order is: might < may < could < should < must (weakest → strongest certainty). Can't signals strong certainty that something is false. Bring your slider positions and reasoning to class.

CONTEXT

Read first: the advice column

Read this fictional advice column. Don’t analyse the modals yet — just answer: what are Yasmin’s two dilemmas, and what does Dr Fielding recommend for each?

Advice ColumnFutures — “The Dilemma”

Letter from Yasmin, age 23 · Response by Dr Rona Fielding

Dear Futures,
I have to make one of the biggest decisions of my life this month. I can apply to two universities — one local, which feels safe, and one abroad, which feels terrifying but exciting. My parents say I should stay close to home, but I might regret it if I don’t try. I can’t make up my mind.

To complicate things: there is someone in my study group who could be more than a friend. I’ve been taking notes in every lecture just to sit near him. He might feel the same — or he might not. My friend says I had better tell him before I leave, otherwise I’ll always wonder. But I could be completely wrong and things could get awkward. Do I take a risk? Do I have a conversation with him? — Yasmin

Dr Fielding responds:
Yasmin, both dilemmas feel enormous, but they can be separated. On the university question: you should apply to both and wait for results. You can always withdraw one offer. More importantly — you must not let fear make the decision for you. A feeling of “terrifying but exciting” is worth listening to; it may well be pointing you in the right direction.

On the romantic question: your friend is right that you ought to say something before you leave. You might be wrong about his feelings — or you might not. But avoiding the conversation because things could get awkward is a reason to delay, not to stay silent. Making progress in life usually requires discomfort first.

Quick meaning check

1. What is Yasmin’s practical dilemma about the university choice?

2. What does Dr Fielding say Yasmin must not let happen?

3. What reason does Dr Fielding give for speaking to the person in the study group?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Same form, different meanings

Detective work: which function is each modal serving?

Yasmin: “I have to make one of the biggest decisions of my life.” Dr Fielding: “You must not let fear make the decision for you.” Both are about obligation. But who is the authority in each case? Do have to and must feel the same?

Have to = external obligation (the university deadline is imposed by the system, not by Yasmin). Must in Dr Fielding’s response = speaker authority and strong personal recommendation. The rough rule: must = internal/speaker; have to = external rule or circumstance.

Must appears twice. In sentence 2 of the slider it means obligation. But the phrase “she must really like him” means something different. What function is must serving there?

Logical deduction from evidence — not obligation at all. The speaker is reasoning from observed behaviour to a confident conclusion. The same modal, a completely different function. Context is everything.

Find three uses of could in the letter. Do all three serve the same function?

“who could be more than a friend” / “I could be completely wrong” / “things could get awkward” — all three are possibility, but at different levels of speaker certainty. Could for possibility is always tentative.

2. Obligation & necessity

Must vs have to: who is the authority?

Both express obligation — the source of authority is what differs. Critical at B2, frequently confused.

ModalAuthorityExample
mustSpeaker imposes the obligationYou must read this — it’s essential. (my recommendation)
have toExternal rule or third partyI have to submit by Friday. (university rule)
must notStrong prohibition from speakerYou must not let fear decide for you.
don’t have toNo obligation (optional)You don’t have to decide today.
need toNecessity (neutral)She needs to consider all her options.
⚠Critical contrast: Must not = prohibition (forbidden). Don’t have to = no obligation (optional). These are opposite in meaning.You must not cheat. (= Forbidden.) You don’t have to take it today. (= Optional.)

3. Permission — register matters

Can / could / may: the same permission, different formality

All three grant or request permission, but at different levels of formality.

ModalRegisterExample

4. Suggestion & recommendation

Should / ought to / had better

All give advice — but had better carries an implicit warning the others do not.

ModalStrengthExampleNote
shouldNeutral adviceYou should apply to both.Most common; no implied consequence
ought toMoral/logical obligationYou ought to say something before you leave.Often implies a moral dimension

STUDY

5. Ability — general vs specific

Can / could / be able to / managed to

The B2 distinction: could = general past ability; was able to / managed to = specific achievement on one occasion.

ModalUseExample
canPresent / future abilityShe can apply to two universities this year.
couldGeneral past abilityAs a child, I could read French.
was/were able toSpecific past achievementShe was able to get an interview despite the late application.
managed toSpecific achievement (implies effort)He managed to finish the thesis in three months.
⚠Could vs was able to for specific events:✗ The roads were closed but he could find another route. (suggests general ability)
✓ The roads were closed but he was able to find another route. (specific achievement)

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Collocations — the right verb matters

Detective work: why these verbs?

The column contains: “can’t make up my mind”, “taking notes”, “take a risk”, “have a conversation”, “let fear make the decision”. Try substituting: “do a risk”, “make a conversation”, “have research”. Which work?

None of those substitutions work. Take a risk, have a conversation, do research — the verb choice is conventional, often arbitrary, and must be learned as a set phrase. This is what a collocation is: words that habitually appear together, where the “wrong” combination sounds unnatural to native speakers even if it’s grammatically possible.

6. Make / take / do / have + noun collocations

The four high-frequency collocation verbs

EVP corpus research identifies these as among the highest-frequency error sources at B2. The verb choice is not always predictable — it must be learned with each noun.

DIALOGUE

Hear it in conversation

A tutorial between Priya and her supervisor Dr Osei. Notice how modals and collocations appear together in natural academic speech.

DialogueA tutorial — Priya and Dr Osei
PR
Priya

I have to make a decision about my thesis topic by the end of this week. I’ve been doing research on both options but I still can’t choose.

DO
Dr Osei

You should take some notes on the pros and cons of each. That might make the choice clearer. Have you had any discussions with other faculty about either topic?

PR
Priya

I was able to have a brief conversation with Professor Singh last week. She said both are viable, but that option B might be harder to defend in the viva.

DO
Dr Osei

Professor Singh must know option B well — her whole career has been in that field. You had better make up your mind soon, though. The department has to confirm all thesis titles by Friday, and late submissions can’t be accepted.

PR
Priya

Understood. Can I send you a one-paragraph summary of my reasoning before I commit?

DO
Dr Osei

Of course you can. Send it by Thursday. And Priya — you’ve made a lot of progress this term. Whatever you choose, you can defend it well.

Dialogue analysis:

  • Have to (Priya) = external deadline set by the department. Must (Dr Osei) = deduction from evidence (her whole career).
  • Had better + base verb: “had better make up your mind” — implied warning about Friday's deadline.
  • Can’t be accepted: prohibition from external policy, not the speaker.
  • Was able to: specific achievement — Priya managed to have that conversation (one specific event).
  • Collocations embedded naturally: make a decision, do research, take notes, have discussions, have a conversation, make up your mind, make progress.
PRONUNCIATION

Modal reduction in fast speech

How modals sound in natural connected speech

Modals almost always reduce in fast speech. The can/can’t distinction is especially important for listening comprehension.

WrittenNatural spoken formKey point
should have/ˈʃʊdəv/ “shoulda”Never write “should of”
could have/ˈkʊdəv/ “coulda”Same pattern
might have/ˈmaɪtəv/ “mighta”Very fast in speech
had better/həd ˈbɛtə/ “’d better”had almost disappears
can vs can’tcan = /kən/ (weak, unstressed)   can’t = /kɑːnt/ (strong, stressed)Stress and vowel distinguish them

In fast speech, can reduces to /kən/ and is unstressed. Can’t is always stressed and uses the full /ɑː/ vowel. Listen for stress and vowel quality, not just the final /t/.

to hesitateverb
