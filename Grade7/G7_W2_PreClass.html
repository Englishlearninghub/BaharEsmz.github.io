Feature Fabric & Form Monthly — “Made to Last: Inside Thornfield Denim”

By Simone Araujo · Correspondent, sustainable fashion

At most denim brands, jeans are produced in large overseas factories and are shipped directly to retail stores within weeks of being ordered. At Thornfield, almost nothing works that way.

The raw cotton is grown on a co-operative farm in Portugal. Once harvested, it is cleaned, is spun into yarn, and is woven into denim using looms that date back to the 1960s. The fabric gets treated with natural indigo dye — a process that takes three days, because the dye gets absorbed more slowly at lower temperatures. “Patience,” says founder Marta Voss, “is literally built into every pair.”

The jeans themselves are cut by hand and are assembled by a team of twelve seamstresses in a converted farmhouse outside Porto. Each pair is inspected individually before it leaves the building. If a seam gets weakened or a button gets loose, the garment is returned to the workshop — not discarded.

It takes roughly six weeks from raw material to finished product. That’s about twelve times longer than the industry average. The price is reflected accordingly: a single pair starts at €280. However, the brand argues that the cost is justified by longevity. “These jeans are designed to last twenty years,” says Voss. “Fast fashion is made to be replaced. Ours are made to be kept.”

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Two kinds of passive — what do you notice?

Detective work: is + past participle vs. gets + past participle

The article uses two different passive patterns: coral-highlighted (is grown, is cut, is designed) and blue-highlighted (gets treated, gets absorbed, gets weakened). Both describe actions done to a subject — but do they feel exactly the same to you? Can you sense any difference in what they suggest about the situation?

The be-passive (is/was + past participle) is neutral and factual — it just says something happened or happens. The get-passive (get/got + past participle) often implies the event was unexpected, accidental, unplanned, or the result of an outside force. “The seam gets weakened” suggests it happens through wear and use, not intentional design.

In the sentence “The raw cotton is grown on a co-operative farm,” who actually does the growing? And in “the dye gets absorbed more slowly at lower temperatures,” who does the absorbing?

In the first sentence, farmers grow the cotton — but the sentence doesn’t name them. In the second, it’s a natural process — no human agent at all. This is one key reason writers choose the passive: the agent is already known, unimportant, or not human. The focus shifts entirely to the object of the action (the cotton, the dye).

At the end of the article, Marta Voss says: “Fast fashion is made to be replaced. Ours are made to be kept.” She uses the same structure twice. Why do you think she’s chosen passive here rather than “We make ours to be kept”?

Using the passive shifts the focus from the maker to the object (the jeans) and gives the statement a more universal, authoritative tone. It also creates a neat parallel contrast: “fast fashion is made to be replaced” vs “ours are made to be kept.” The passive is often chosen for rhetorical impact, not just because the agent is unknown.

STUDY

The passive voice: a complete guide

1. Active vs. passive: what actually changes?

This is your first formal encounter with the passive voice — but you have already been using it in English without realising it. Let’s make it explicit.

In an active sentence, the grammatical subject is the one doing the action:

Workers  weave  the fabric  on old looms.
■ Subject (doer)    ■ Verb    ■ Object (receiver)

In a passive sentence, the object of the action becomes the grammatical subject. The doer either disappears or moves to a "by" phrase:

The fabric  is woven  on old looms  (by workers).
■ New subject (was the object)    ■ be + past participle    ■ Agent (optional)

Active — focus on the doer

Marta Voss designed these jeans.

A seamstress inspects every pair.

The subject is the person performing the action. We know who did it and it matters.

Passive — focus on the result

These jeans were designed by Marta Voss.

Every pair is inspected.

The subject received the action. The focus shifts to the object. The agent is optional.

💡 An analogy: think of the passive as a camera angle change. Active = camera on the person doing the action. Passive = camera on the thing being done to. Same event, different focus.

2. When do we use “by + agent”?

The doer of the action can be included using by — but in most passive sentences, it is left out entirely.

Include "by + agent" when the doer is surprising, important, or new information:

✓ These jeans were designed by a team of three artisans. The agent is interesting — not the usual factory team. Worth mentioning.
✓ The dye was developed by a chemist in the 1940s. Specific, informative. The person is the interesting part.

Omit "by + agent" when the doer is obvious, unknown, or unimportant:

✓ Each garment is inspected before it leaves the factory. “By workers” is obvious — of course humans inspect it. No need to say it.
✓ My jacket was stolen at the market. We don’t know who did it — that’s precisely why the passive is used here.
✗ The cotton is grown in Portugal by farmers. Technically possible but “by farmers” adds no information — cotton is always grown by farmers. Omit it: The cotton is grown in Portugal.

Rule of thumb: If adding “by [someone]” would sound obvious or add nothing, leave it out. In real writing, about 80% of passive sentences have no "by" phrase at all.

3. Verbs that cannot be used in the passive

Only transitive verbs (verbs that take a direct object) can be made passive. Intransitive verbs — those that never take a direct object — cannot.

The test: can you ask “[verb] what?” or “[verb] whom?” and get an answer? If yes, it’s transitive and can be passive. If not, it’s intransitive and cannot.

✓ Transitive — CAN be passive

make → "make what?" → jeans → passive ✓

design → "design what?" → a coat → passive ✓

inspect → "inspect what?" → the garment → passive ✓

wash → "wash what?" → the fabric → passive ✓

✗ Intransitive — CANNOT be passive

arrive → "arrive what?" → ✗ no answer → no passive

happen → "happen what?" → ✗ no answer → no passive

sleep → "sleep what?" → ✗ no answer → no passive

exist → "exist what?" → ✗ no answer → no passive

✗ The conference was happened last week. “Happen” is intransitive — it never takes an object. You cannot say this. Correct: The conference happened last week. (Active only.)
✗ She was arrived at the store early. “Arrive” is intransitive. Correct: She arrived at the store early.
! Watch out: some verbs can be both transitive and intransitive depending on usage. “The door opened” (intransitive, no passive possible) vs “The door was opened by the manager” (transitive, passive works).

4. The be-passive across all tenses

The form is always be + past participle. Only the tense of be changes. The past participle itself never changes.

Tense Active form Passive form (be + pp) Clothing example
Present simple They make the jeans by hand. is / are + pp The jeans are made by hand.
Present continuous They are dyeing the fabric now. is / are being + pp The fabric is being dyed right now.
Past simple A seamstress stitched every hem. was / were + pp Every hem was stitched by a seamstress.
Past continuous Workers were weaving the cloth when we arrived. was / were being + pp The cloth was being woven when we arrived.
Present perfect The team has inspected every pair. has / have been + pp Every pair has been inspected.
Past perfect Someone had already cut the fabric before we arrived. had been + pp The fabric had been cut before we arrived.
Future (will) The factory will ship the order on Friday. will be + pp The order will be shipped on Friday.
Future (going to) They are going to launch the new collection soon. is / are going to be + pp The new collection is going to be launched soon.
Modal (can/could) You can wash this garment at 30°. can / could be + pp This garment can be washed at 30°.
Modal (should/must) You should store silk in a cool place. should / must be + pp Silk should be stored in a cool place.

Note: Past perfect continuous passive and future perfect passive exist but are very rare and not taught at B2. Focus on the ten forms above.

5. Common mistakes — and how to avoid them

These are the errors that appear most often in learner writing at B2 level. Read each one carefully before doing the exercises.

Wrong Correct Why
The jeans are make by hand. The jeans are made by hand. Use the past participle, never the base form.
The cotton is grew in Portugal. The cotton is grown in Portugal. Past tense ≠ past participle. grow → grew (past) → grown (pp).
It is been designed carefully. It has been designed carefully. Present perfect passive uses "has/have been", not "is been".
The fabric was being weaved. The fabric was being woven. Weave is irregular: weaved ✗, woven ✓.
She was arrived at the shop. She arrived at the shop. "Arrive" is intransitive — cannot be passive.
The dress is being make now. The dress is being made now. Present continuous passive: is/are + being + past participle.
The jacket will been delivered. The jacket will be delivered. After "will", use the base form of be: "will be", not "will been".

💡 Tip 1: the "pp test"

Before writing a passive verb, ask: is what I wrote a past participle? If it’s the past tense form instead (e.g. grew instead of grown), fix it immediately.

💡 Tip 2: learn irregulars in passive pairs

Learn: make→made, grow→grown, weave→woven, tear→torn, wear→worn, write→written. These are all frequent in passive contexts.

💡 Tip 3: "by" is usually not needed

If you’ve written “by people” or “by someone”, delete it. Only include “by” when the agent is surprising or specifically relevant.

💡 Tip 4: don’t overuse the passive

Using passive constantly makes writing feel cold and bureaucratic. Mix active and passive sentences. A good paragraph often has 1–2 passive verbs, not 5.

6. Listen in: a real conversation in a clothes shop

Notice how the passive appears naturally — nobody is “doing grammar.” They’re just talking. The passive is highlighted so you can spot it.

CU
Customer

Excuse me — do you know where this jacket was made? I can’t find the label.

ST
Staff member

That one is produced in Portugal, actually. The fabric is woven locally and then is cut and assembled in a small workshop. It’s designed here in the city, though.

CU
Customer

Oh, nice. What about this one? It got torn last time I tried it on — right along the seam. Was that just a one-off?

ST
Staff member

Unfortunately we were notified about a seam issue in that batch. All the affected garments have been recalled and are being replaced this week. Can I take your details?

CU
Customer

Yes, please. And will I be refunded automatically, or do I need to bring it back in?

Dialogue analysis — what to notice:

  • The customer asks a natural passive question: “where was this made?” — no agent mentioned because we don’t know who made it.
  • The staff member uses a chain of passive verbs (is produced / is woven / is cut and assembled) — typical of describing a manufacturing process.
  • The customer uses got torn (get-passive) for an accidental, unwanted event — not was torn, which would sound more formal.
  • The staff member uses present perfect passive (have been recalled) and present continuous passive (are being replaced) in the same sentence — two different passive tenses working together.
  • The customer asks “will I be refunded” — future passive question, very natural in customer service contexts.

7. When to use the passive: a summary

Now that you know the full form, here’s when to reach for it.

✓ Use passive when...

  • The agent is unknown: My bag was stolen.
  • The agent is obvious: The seam is stitched.
  • The result matters more than the doer: The fabric is woven on 1960s looms.
  • Formal / scientific / professional context: Participants were interviewed twice.
  • Deliberate stylistic effect: These jeans are made to be kept.

✗ Avoid passive when...

  • The verb is intransitive: ✗ She was arrived.
  • The agent matters: ✗ The painting was painted. → Who? By whom?
  • Active is clearer and more direct: ✗ The food was eaten by me → I ate the food.
  • You would overload the text with passive sentences back to back.
STUDY

The get-passive — more dynamic, more spoken

1b. The get-passive: get + past participle

Extremely common in spoken English and informal writing — often implies accident, change, or an outside force. According to COCA, it appears constantly in everyday speech but is rarely taught in EFL courses.

Be-passive — neutral statement

The seam is weakened over time.

Factual. Could happen by design or by chance.

Get-passive — dynamic / accidental

The seam gets weakened over time.

Suggests gradual wear, an outside process happening to the seam.

TenseFormExample
Present simpleget/gets + past participleSilk gets damaged easily.
Past simplegot + past participleMy jacket got torn on a nail.
Present perfecthas/have gotten + past participleThe button has gotten loose again.

Common get-passive situations:

Accidents: I got fired / she got hurt Changes: it got broken / he got promoted Natural processes: the dye got absorbed Informal/spoken register
✓ My coat got caught on the door and the sleeve ripped. Accidental, outside force — get-passive is perfect here.
✓ She got promoted after three years at the company. Change of state (positive here), very common in spoken English.
! Get-passive is NOT used in formal written reports or academic writing. Use be-passive in formal contexts: “The specimen was analysed”, not “got analysed”.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Contrast connectors — same job, different position

Detective work: where do contrast words go?

Look at: “However, I want to be honest” and “Despite the obvious quality, I found the sizing inconsistent.” Both express contrast with what came before. But however starts a new sentence while despite begins a subordinate clause. What grammatical difference follows each one?

However is an adverb — it connects ideas between sentences and is followed by a complete clause (subject + verb). Despite is a preposition — it introduces a noun phrase (not a full clause): “despite the obvious quality” = noun phrase. You cannot say “despite the quality is obvious” — use “although” instead for a full clause.

marta_k writes: “Whereas the fabric itself is excellent, the customer service left a lot to be desired.” Could you replace whereas with however here without any other changes? Why or why not?

No — whereas is a conjunction that connects two clauses in a single sentence, directly linking them in a before/after structure. However would require a full stop before it: “The fabric is excellent. However, the customer service...” Same contrast, different sentence structure.

STUDY

Now let’s name them: contrast connectors

2. Expressing contrast: the main connectors

Each connector works differently — the key is knowing what grammatical form follows each one.

howeveradverb
Starts a new sentence or clause. Followed by subject + verb. Usually has a comma after it.
The price is high. However, the quality justifies it.
although / even thoughconjunction
Joins two clauses in one sentence. Can go at the start or in the middle. Followed by subject + verb.
Although the jeans were expensive, they lasted for years.
despite / in spite ofpreposition
Followed by a noun or noun phrase (NOT a full clause). Can use despite the fact that + clause.
Despite the high price, sales have been strong.
whereas / whileconjunction
Directly compares two contrasting clauses. Often used for simultaneous contrast.
Whereas synthetic fabrics fade quickly, natural fibres last longer.
on the other handadverb phrase
Presents the second side of a balanced argument. Always refers back to a point already made.
Fast fashion is cheap. On the other hand, it creates enormous waste.
nevertheless / nonethelessadverb
Formal. Means “despite what was just said, this is still true.” Stronger than however.
The break-in period is long. Nevertheless, the fit is perfect once worn in.
✗ Despite the quality is high, the returns rate is low. Wrong — despite needs a noun phrase, not a clause. Fix: Despite the high quality, the returns rate is low. Or: Although the quality is high, the returns rate is low.

Fabrics, materials & fashion

1. Fabrics & materials

denimnoun

"This denim is woven on vintage looms."

📌 Passive connection: notice how many of these are used in passive descriptions

The fabric is printed with a floral design • The pattern is woven in • The polka dots are screen-printed • The houndstooth check was designed in Scotland. Describing patterns almost always uses the passive — the pattern is done to the fabric, not by the fabric.

4. A second dialogue: in the design studio

This conversation happens during a product development meeting. Notice: passive questions, passive with different tenses, and get-passive for accidents. The patterns vocabulary also appears naturally.

Dialogue 2 Thornfield Design Studio — New Collection Meeting
DA
Diego (Head of Design)

Right, let’s talk about the autumn line. The new flannel shirts — have they been approved by the production team yet?

PA
Priya (Production Manager)

The plain ones, yes. But the tartan versions haven’t been signed off yet. The colour palette still needs to be confirmed by the client.

DA
Diego

And what about the floral print blouses? Were those printed on the new sustainable base fabric, or the old polyester one?

PA
Priya

They were printed on the sustainable cotton, as agreed. Though I should mention — one of the sample runs got ruined when the ink got onto the wrong side. It was only three shirts, but we’re looking into why it happened.

DA
Diego

That’s frustrating. Will the process be reviewed before the full run starts?

PA
Priya

Yes — a new printing protocol is being developed right now. The polka dot and paisley blouses won’t be started until it has been tested and approved.

Dialogue 2 analysis — what to notice:

  • Passive questions: “Have they been approved?” / “Were those printed?” / “Will the process be reviewed?” — the passive is extremely common in professional meetings when checking the status of things.
  • Present perfect passive (have been approved / haven’t been signed off) describes whether actions are complete yet — very natural in project updates.
  • Get-passive for accidents: “one of the sample runs got ruined” — Diego uses get-passive for an unplanned, unfortunate event, while using be-passive for planned processes.
  • Present continuous passive: “a new protocol is being developed” — the action is happening right now, still in progress.
  • Pattern vocabulary in context: tartan, floral, polka dot, paisley — all appear as adjectives describing the products being made.

Be-passivebe + past participleNeutral, factual; all tenses; formal & informalThe fabric is woven by hand.
Get-passiveget + past participleAccidental, dynamic, change of state; mostly informal/spokenMy jacket got torn on a nail.
However / neverthelessadverb + comma + clauseContrast between sentencesIt is expensive. However, it lasts.
Although / even thoughconjunction + full clauseContrast within a sentenceAlthough it is expensive, it lasts.
Despite / in spite ofpreposition + noun phraseContrast with a noun, not a clauseDespite the price, it sells well.
Whereas / whileconjunction + clauseDirect side-by-side contrastWhereas cotton breathes, polyester doesn’t.

Before Session 1, make sure you can…

  • Explain the difference between active and passive voice, and when to choose each
  • Form the be-passive in at least four tenses (present simple, past simple, present perfect, future)
  • Explain why “by + agent” is usually omitted, and when to include it
  • Name two verbs that CANNOT be made passive, and explain why
  • Explain the difference between be-passive and get-passive, with one example of each
  • Use despite correctly (with a noun phrase, NOT a full clause)
  • Distinguish whereas (joins clauses) from however (starts a new sentence)
  • Put a list of three adjectives in the correct order before a noun
  • Name and describe at least six fabric patterns (e.g. floral, paisley, houndstooth, tartan…)
  • Pronounce woven, grown, torn correctly (monosyllabic past participles)
  • Have your 4–5 sentence clothing description ready to share
  • Bring at least one passive sentence from a real brand website to Session 1
