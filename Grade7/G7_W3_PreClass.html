Essay Elsewhere — “When Everything Changed”

By Amara Nwosu · Writer & educator, Lagos → Amsterdam

When I was growing up in Lagos, I used to fall asleep to street noise — vendors calling out, motorcycles, the sound of neighbours arguing across a courtyard. I never noticed it as a child. It was simply what evenings sounded like. Then I moved to Amsterdam at twenty-six, and the silence of my first apartment nearly drove me to distraction.

In the summers back home, my siblings and I would spend entire days at my grandmother’s house — she used to make pepper soup on Fridays, and we would crowd around the kitchen while she worked. The smell of it still does something to me when I encounter it now. That version of Fridays no longer exists.

People who move between countries often describe the same experience: the small rituals you used to take for granted become the things you grieve the most. I didn’t miss Lagos in a general way — I missed specific sounds, specific smells, specific routines I hadn’t realised I was attached to.

Adjusting to the Netherlands took longer than I expected. I was not used to the cold, obviously — but more than the cold, I wasn’t used to the directness. Dutch communication style felt blunt at first. I had to get used to people saying exactly what they meant without softening it. It took about two years before I stopped reading criticism into straightforward sentences.

I also had to get used to cycling everywhere. In Lagos I didn’t use to cycle at all — it wasn’t something adults in my neighbourhood did. In Amsterdam, the bicycle is infrastructure. Now I can’t imagine living without one. I’m completely used to it. But it took time, and I fell off twice.

That is the strange arithmetic of moving: you lose things, and you gain things, and eventually you get used to holding both. What I used to think of as home is still home. This place is also home. I am used to the contradiction now. I wasn’t always.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Three similar phrases — three very different meanings

Detective work: spot the differences

The essay contains: “I used to fall asleep to street noise,” “I wasn’t used to the directness,” and “I had to get used to saying exactly what they meant.” All three phrases contain the word “used.” But do they describe the same kind of experience? How does each one feel different?

They describe three completely different things: (1) a habit that existed in the past but no longer does; (2) a current state — something you are or aren't accustomed to right now; (3) a process — the gradual journey of becoming accustomed. Same word, three distinct grammatical and conceptual functions.

Amara also uses “would”: “my siblings and I would spend entire days at my grandmother’s house” and “we would crowd around the kitchen.” Could she have written “we used to crowd around the kitchen” instead? What about “she would make pepper soup on Fridays” — does that feel the same as “she used to make pepper soup”?

"We used to crowd" and "we would crowd" are both acceptable for a past repeated action. But "she used to make pepper soup" and "she would make pepper soup" are subtly different: used to can describe states (the pepper soup existed as a Friday tradition), while would strictly describes actions/habits. Would cannot describe a past state — "I would live in Lagos" sounds wrong for a general past fact. "I used to live in Lagos" is correct.

What grammatical form follows “be used to” and “get used to”? Look carefully at: “I wasn’t used to the cold,” “I’m used to it,” “I had to get used to cycling everywhere.” What comes directly after "to" in each case?

After "be used to" and "get used to", you use a noun ("the cold," "it") or a gerund ("cycling," "saying"). The "to" here is a preposition, NOT part of an infinitive. This is the most important thing to remember: "I am used to cycling" (✓) — never "I am used to cycle" (✗).

STUDY

1. Used to: past habits & states

Used to + base verb

Describes something that was true or happened regularly in the past, but is no longer true or no longer happens. Works for both habitual actions AND past states.

Past habit (action)

I used to fall asleep to street noise.

She used to make pepper soup on Fridays.

We used to spend summers at my grandmother’s.

These actions no longer happen regularly.

Past state (condition)

I used to live in Lagos.

He used to be very shy.

That place used to be a market.

These states no longer exist.

FormExample
Positive: used to + base verbI used to cycle to school.
Negative: didn’t use to + base verbI didn’t use to cycle anywhere.
Question: Did + subject + use to?Did you use to live here?

⚠ Spelling note: used to vs. use to

In positive sentences: used to (with d). In negatives and questions, after "did/didn't": use to (no d) — because the past tense is already carried by "did."

✗ I use to wake up early. Positive sentence needs "used to": I used to wake up early.
✗ I didn't used to cycle. After "didn't" drop the d: I didn't use to cycle.
STUDY

2. Would: past habits only

Would + base verb (for past habits)

Would describes repeated past actions — things done regularly in the past. It is more literary and nostalgic in feel than "used to." It CANNOT be used for past states.

✓ Would for repeated actions

We would spend Sundays at the market.

She would sing while cooking dinner.

He would always arrive ten minutes late.

All replaceable with "used to" without change of meaning.

✗ Would for past states (NOT allowed)

✗ I would live in Lagos. → ✓ I used to live in Lagos.

✗ She would be a teacher. → ✓ She used to be a teacher.

✗ They would know each other. → ✓ They used to know each other.

States require "used to" — not "would."

The test: if you can add “every day / every summer / whenever” and it makes sense, it is an action — both used to and would work. If the sentence describes a condition or state that existed over a period, only used to works.

✓ Every Friday, she would make pepper soup. (action — would ✓) Can also say: “she used to make pepper soup.” Both are correct. Found in: essay, paragraph 2
✓ That version of Fridays no longer exists. I used to take it for granted. (state) "Would take it for granted" is possible here too, but "used to" feels more natural for a general past truth.
STUDY

3. Be used to & get used to: present adjustment

Be used to doing / Get used to doing

These are NOT about the past. They are about your current relationship with something — whether you are accustomed to it (be used to) or the process of becoming accustomed (get used to). Both are followed by a gerund or noun — NOT a base verb.

Be used to + gerund/noun — STATE

I am used to cycling everywhere. (now comfortable with it)

She is used to the cold. (noun — accustomed to it)

We were used to working long hours. (past state of being accustomed)

Be used to describes a current (or past) state of familiarity. It can be in any tense.

Get used to + gerund/noun — PROCESS

I got used to cycling everywhere. (process, now complete)

She is getting used to the cold. (process, in progress)

You’ll get used to it. (noun — future process)

Get used to describes the process of becoming accustomed. Implies it took effort.

The most important rule

The "to" in be used to and get used to is a preposition, not part of an infinitive. This means the next verb must be a gerund (-ing), never a base verb.

✗ I am used to cycle to work. Wrong — “to” here is a preposition. Must use the gerund: I am used to cycling to work.
✗ I am getting used to wake up early. Same error: I am getting used to waking up early.
✗ I used to cycling to work as a child. This mixes the two patterns. "Used to" (past habit) takes a base verb: I used to cycle to work. "Be used to cycling" is a completely different structure.
STUDY

The three-way distinction at a glance

Used to + BASE VERB

used to do

Past habit or state that no longer exists. Neither the action nor the state is true anymore.

“I used to fall asleep to street noise.”
“She used to be very quiet.”

Be used to + GERUND / NOUN

am/is/are/was used to doing

A current (or past) state of being accustomed. You are familiar with something. No longer difficult.

“I’m used to cycling in the rain.”
“She wasn’t used to the directness.”

Get used to + GERUND / NOUN

get/got/getting used to doing

The process of becoming accustomed. Implies effort and time. Focuses on the transition, not the end state.

“I had to get used to the silence.”
“You’ll get used to it.”

Common confusion: “I used to cycling” — why it’s wrong

This is the single most frequent error with these structures at B2 level. Learners mix the two patterns:

Intended meaningWrongCorrect
Past habit (no longer true)I used to cycling to school.I used to cycle to school.
Currently accustomedI am used to cycle to work.I am used to cycling to work.
Becoming accustomedI got used to cycle there.I got used to cycling there.
Past stateI would live in Lagos.I used to live in Lagos.

DIALOGUE

Hear it in conversation

Two colleagues — one who recently moved cities, one who did so years ago — talk over lunch. Notice how naturally the three structures appear in ordinary speech.

Dialogue Two colleagues over lunch — talking about moving cities
TA
Tariq

How are you finding it? You’ve only been here three months, right?

LU
Luisa

Honestly, it’s good but hard. I’m still getting used to commuting by train. Back home I used to drive everywhere. The idea of standing on a platform at 7am was just not something I was used to.

TA
Tariq

I remember that feeling. I used to live about twenty minutes from work when I first arrived, and I would walk in. Then I moved and suddenly everything was a 45-minute tube journey.

LU
Luisa

How long did it take you to get used to it?

TA
Tariq

About a year, I’d say. Now I’m completely used to it — I actually use the commute to read. I didn’t use to read much before all this, so that’s been an unexpected upside.

LU
Luisa

That’s a good reframe. What about the people? The office culture here feels quite different to what I’m used to.

TA
Tariq

Yes, it can be. I used to work somewhere much more formal — we would always have a proper dress code, everyone wore suits. Here it’s much more relaxed. You’ll get used to that faster than the commute, I promise.

Dialogue analysis — what to notice:

  • Used to + base verb for past habits and states: “I used to drive,” “I used to live,” “I didn’t use to read.”
  • Would + base verb for repeated past actions alongside used to: “I would walk in,” “we would always have a dress code.”
  • Be used to + gerund/noun for current familiarity: “not something I was used to,” “what I’m used to,” “completely used to it.”
  • Get used to + gerund/noun for the ongoing process: “still getting used to commuting,” “get used to it,” “get used to that.”
  • Notice how Tariq uses would and used to in the same memory: “we would always have a dress code” (action, would ✓) describes the repeated habit, while “I used to work somewhere formal” (state, would ✗) describes where he worked.
PRONUNCIATION

How “used to” actually sounds

The /t/ at the end of “used” disappears

In natural, connected speech, “used to” sounds very different from how it’s spelled. The /d/ in “used” and the /t/ at the start of “to” merge into a single sound.

“used to” → /juːst tə/ sounds like "yoosta"

“I used to live there” → /aɪ juːstə lɪv ðeə/

Similarly, “get used to” in fast speech:

“get used to it” → /ɡɛt juːstə ɪt/ very fast: "gettyoosta-it"

⚠ Common mispronunciation

Learners often stress both words equally: /ˈjuːzd tuː/ — this sounds unnatural. “To” is almost never stressed and reduces to /tə/.

💡 Listening tip

When you hear “yoosta” in a podcast or film, recognise it as “used to.” It’s extremely common in spoken English and easy to miss if you’re listening for the spelled-out version.

VOCABULARY

Society & nostalgia vocabulary

1. Memory & nostalgia

nostalgianoun (uncountable)
a sentimental longing for a past period or place, often idealised
"There's a particular nostalgia for the neighbourhood I used to grow up in."
to reminisceverb
to think or talk about happy memories of the past
"We would spend hours reminiscing about our school days."
to take for grantedphrase
to fail to appreciate something because you are so used to having it
"I used to take the sense of community completely for granted."
bittersweetadjective
a feeling that is both happy and sad at the same time; used to describe nostalgic emotions
"Looking at old photos is always bittersweet — I miss it, but I'm glad I moved."
to grieveverb
to feel deep sorrow, often for something lost — not just a person but a place, time, or way of life
"It took years to realise I was grieving a version of my life I'd left behind."
a ritualnoun
a regular, often meaningful routine that gives structure or comfort to life
"Friday pepper soup was a ritual — we all used to look forward to it."

2. Society & change

to adaptverb
to change your behaviour or attitude to fit a new situation
"It took me longer to adapt than I expected."
to adjust (to)verb
to get used to a new situation; slightly more gradual than ‘adapt’
"I'm still adjusting to the pace of life here."
a generationnoun
a group of people born and living at approximately the same time
"My parents' generation used to stay in one city their whole lives."
communitynoun
a group of people living in the same place or with shared characteristics and a sense of belonging
"I used to have a real sense of community on my street."
anonymitynoun
the state of being unknown to others; not being recognised
"Moving to a city means getting used to anonymity — no one knows your name."
to uprootverb
to move yourself or someone else from an established place or way of life
"Being uprooted from a familiar place takes much longer to process than people expect."

3. Everyday life & habits

a commutenoun / verb
a regular journey between home and work; also the act of making that journey
"I'm getting used to commuting — I used to live five minutes from work."
a routinenoun
a regular sequence of actions that are followed as part of daily life
"Building a new routine is what helped me get used to living alone."
pace of lifephrase
the speed and intensity at which daily life is lived in a given place
"I used to find the pace of life in London exhausting."
upsidenoun (informal)
a positive aspect of something that is generally difficult or unwanted
"The upside of getting used to the long commute was discovering audiobooks."
