THE SCENE

Three documents. One promotion. Everything at stake.

The attaché has applied for a diplomatic posting.

Six weeks after the gala, the attaché has submitted an application for a full diplomatic posting — a significant career step. Three documents exist: the application letter they wrote, the interview panel’s internal notes, and the formal assessment report produced afterwards. Read all three carefully. Before any explanations, find what the highlighted structures have in common.

Cover letter Application for the Position of Third Secretary — Vienna Posting

Dear Members of the Selection Panel,

I am writing to apply for the position of Third Secretary at the British Embassy, Vienna. Not only do I possess the linguistic competencies required for this role, but I also bring three years of direct diplomatic experience gained at the highest levels of formal engagement.

Rarely have I encountered a professional environment that so closely aligns with my core values of rigour, discretion, and service. Only after years of careful preparation do I feel ready to take on the full responsibilities this posting demands.

Having worked directly alongside Ambassador Davies for two years, I have developed a thorough understanding of the complex diplomatic landscape in which this posting operates. Recognised by my peers for precision and reliability, I am confident in my ability to represent the United Kingdom’s interests effectively.

I would welcome the opportunity to discuss my application further at your convenience.

Yours faithfully,

A. Chen, Legal Attaché

Panel notes Interview Panel — Internal Notes (Confidential)

Candidate: A. Chen    Date: 14 March    Panel: Davies, Morrison, Weiss

Under no circumstances should a candidate of this calibre be dismissed without a full second-panel review. Not since 2019 have we seen an application demonstrating such consistent achievement across all criteria.

Asked about crisis management, the candidate demonstrated composure and analytical rigour well beyond the expected level for this grade. Noting the candidate’s particular strength in formal written communication, the panel recommends progression to the second stage.

Little do candidates realise that the informal section of the interview often reveals more about professional readiness than the structured questions. This candidate excelled in both.

Formal report Candidate Assessment Report — Third Secretary Selection

1. Introduction

This report presents the findings of the selection panel convened on 14 March. Its purpose is to assess the suitability of the shortlisted candidate for the position of Third Secretary, Vienna.

2. Findings

Drawing on observations from both the structured interview and written assessment, the panel concludes that the candidate meets all essential criteria. Presented with a complex hypothetical scenario, the candidate demonstrated exceptional judgement.

So impressive was the candidate’s performance that the panel reached unanimous agreement after a single session.

3. Recommendation

Only by investing in candidates of this calibre can the Service maintain its international standing. The panel recommends appointment to the posting with immediate effect.

Before reading any explanations — notice these things

1. The gold phrases all push something unusual to the front of the sentence. What happens to the verb and subject after them?

2. The teal phrases all begin with a verb form ending in -ed or -ing. They don’t have their own subject. Who is performing that action?

3. The report in Document 3 has a different structure from the letter in Document 1. What visual and organisational differences do you notice before reading the explanation?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

The gold phrases — something strange is happening

Work it out before reading the explanation

Look at: “Not only do I possess the linguistic competencies…” Normal word order would be: “I not only possess…” What exactly changed? Which words moved, and where?

The auxiliary verb do moved before the subject I. This is called subject-auxiliary inversion. The negative or limiting expression Not only is placed first, and the verb jumps before the subject — exactly like a question, but it’s a statement.

Look at: “Rarely have I encountered…” The same thing has happened. What is the auxiliary verb? What is unusual about this sentence in terms of where the meaning lands?

The auxiliary is have. It sits before the subject I. The inversion places the emphatic adverb Rarely at the front, which gives it maximum impact. The reader feels the weight of rarely before even reaching the rest of the sentence.

Now look at the register. All three documents are formal professional writing. Are any of these inverted sentences things you would say in casual conversation? What does that tell you about when inversion is used?

No — you would never say “Rarely have I seen such a good film!” in natural conversation. Inversion is almost exclusively a formal written English feature (COCA corpus data confirms this). It appears in essays, reports, formal letters, speeches, and academic writing. It is a deliberate stylistic choice for emphasis and formality.

STUDY

Inversion — emphasis through unusual word order

What is inversion?

Inversion means placing the auxiliary verb before the subject. In normal statements, we write subject + verb. In inversion, we write auxiliary + subject + main verb — exactly like a question, but functioning as a statement. The trigger is always a negative, limiting, or emphatic expression placed at the start of the sentence.

📌 Corpus note (COCA) Inversion is one of the clearest markers of formal written English. It appears in academic essays, formal letters, reports, parliamentary speeches, and literary prose — and almost never in natural spoken English. Use it in IELTS Writing Task 2, formal emails, and academic essays to signal C1 competence.
The core pattern
Negative/limiting expression + auxiliary + subject + main verb
Normal: “I have rarely encountered such dedication.”   Inverted: “Rarely have I encountered such dedication.”

The four main types

Type 1

Negative adverbs & adverbials

Triggers: never, rarely, seldom, hardly, scarcely, barely, no sooner, not once, at no time

“Rarely have I encountered a candidate of this calibre.”

← Normal: “I have rarely encountered…”

“Never before had the panel seen such consistent achievement.”

← Normal: “The panel had never before seen…”

“Hardly had the interview begun when the candidate distinguished herself.”

← Normal: “The interview had hardly begun when…”

Type 2

“Not only… but also” & restrictive “only”

Triggers: not only, only after, only when, only by, only then, only if

“Not only does she speak three languages, but she also has extensive field experience.”

← Normal: “She not only speaks three languages, but she also…”

“Only after years of preparation do I feel ready for this posting.”

← Normal: “I only feel ready for this posting after years of preparation.”

“Only by investing in such candidates can the Service maintain its standing.”

← Normal: “The Service can only maintain its standing by investing in such candidates.”

Type 3

“Under no circumstances” & other negative prepositional phrases

Triggers: under no circumstances, in no way, on no account, at no point, not since, not until

“Under no circumstances should this candidate be dismissed without a full review.”

← Normal: “This candidate should not be dismissed under any circumstances…”

“Not since 2019 have we seen such a strong application.”

← Normal: “We haven’t seen such a strong application since 2019.”

“On no account should personal considerations influence the panel’s decision.”

← Normal: “Personal considerations should not influence the panel’s decision on any account.”

Type 4

“So + adjective” & “Such + noun phrase”

Triggers: so + adj, such + noun, little, no sooner…than

“So impressive was the candidate’s performance that the panel reached unanimous agreement.”

← Normal: “The candidate’s performance was so impressive that…”

“Such was the quality of the application that it required no further discussion.”

← Normal: “The quality of the application was such that…”

“Little do candidates realise how much the informal section reveals.”

← Normal: “Candidates little realise / Candidates don’t realise how much…”

Emphatic do / does / did

Even without inversion, formal English uses auxiliary do/does/did for emphasis. This is different from the question form — it appears in statements and functions to intensify or contradict.

Emphatic do — intensify

“I do believe this candidate merits serious consideration.”

= I strongly/genuinely believe…

Emphatic did — contradict

“The panel did recommend her, despite the initial reservations.”

= pushing back against an expectation
Normal statementInverted / emphatic form
I have never seen such dedication.Never have I seen such dedication.
She not only manages but also mentors the team.Not only does she manage, but she also mentors the team.
The performance was so strong that we were unanimous.So strong was the performance that we were unanimous.
You should not leave under any circumstances.Under no circumstances should you leave.
The decision was only made after full deliberation.Only after full deliberation was the decision made.
I strongly agree with that assessment.I do agree with that assessment.
⚠ Common error — don’t invert without an auxiliary × “Never I have seen such dedication.” — Wrong: auxiliary must come immediately after the trigger, before the subject.
× “Rarely I encounter such precision.” — Wrong: you must supply do: “Rarely do I encounter…”
✓ After not only: invert only the first clause. The second clause (but also) stays in normal order.
NOTICE & DISCOVER

The teal phrases — clauses without a subject

Look back at the teal phrases in the three documents

“Having worked directly alongside Ambassador Davies for two years, I have developed…” — The phrase Having worked has no subject. Who did the working? How do you know?

The subject of the participle clause is always the same as the subject of the main clause — in this case, I. This is the golden rule: the implied subject of the participle must match the grammatical subject of the sentence it modifies.

“Recognised by my peers for precision, I am confident in my ability…” — This uses a past participle. Is the candidate doing the recognising, or being recognised? What does the past participle tell you about active vs. passive meaning?

The candidate is being recognised (passive meaning). Past participle clauses always carry passive meaning: the subject of the main clause is receiving the action. Present participle clauses (-ing) carry active meaning: the subject is performing the action.

Why do you think professional and academic writing uses participle clauses so frequently? What do they do to the rhythm and density of a sentence?

Participle clauses compress information. Instead of two separate sentences (“I worked alongside the Ambassador. As a result, I developed an understanding…”), the writer combines them into one elegant structure. This makes formal writing denser, more efficient, and more sophisticated — hallmarks of C1 prose.

STUDY

Participle clauses — compression and elegance

The golden rule

A participle clause has no subject of its own. The implied subject is always the grammatical subject of the main clause. Violating this rule produces a dangling participle — one of the most common C1 writing errors.

✓ Correct

“Having reviewed the file, the panel reached a decision.”

The panel reviewed the file. ✓ The panel is also the subject of the main clause.

✗ Dangling participle

“Having reviewed the file, a decision was reached.”

A decision cannot review a file. The passive main clause creates a mismatch.

The four types of participle clause

Present Participle — -ing

Active meaning. Subject performs the action simultaneously or just before the main clause.

“Working under significant pressure, she produced exceptional results.”

= She worked under pressure and (simultaneously) produced results.

“Noting the candidate’s strengths, the panel recommended progression.”

Past Participle — -ed / -en

Passive meaning. Subject receives the action described in the participle.

“Recognised for precision, she was considered an ideal candidate.”

= She was recognised for precision (passive), and she was considered ideal.

“Asked about crisis management, the candidate responded with composure.”

Perfect Participle — Having + past participle

Shows that the participle action is completed BEFORE the main clause action.

“Having worked alongside Davies for two years, I understand the role fully.”

= She first worked with Davies (completed), then she has this understanding.

“Having received the panel’s notes, the director approved the appointment.”

Negative Participle — Not + participle

Any of the above types can be negated by placing not before the participle.

“Not wishing to appear unprepared, she researched the role extensively.”

= She didn’t want to appear unprepared, so she researched…

“Not having been informed in advance, I could not prepare a full response.”

What do participle clauses replace?

In formal writing, they typically compress a causal, temporal, or conditional relationship into a single clause:

Full clause (less formal)Participle clause (more formal)
Because she had reviewed all the evidence, she was confident.Having reviewed all the evidence, she was confident.
When she was asked about the role, she described her experience clearly.Asked about the role, she described her experience clearly.
Since it drew on extensive research, the report was authoritative.Drawing on extensive research, the report was authoritative.
While she acknowledged the challenges, she remained optimistic.Acknowledging the challenges, she remained optimistic.
As she had not been briefed beforehand, she requested more time.Not having been briefed beforehand, she requested more time.
✓ C1 writing tip Using participle clauses at the start of sentences is one of the fastest ways to raise the sophistication of your formal writing. But always check the golden rule: the subject of the participle must match the subject of the main clause. Write the sentence, then ask: who is doing the action in the participle? Is it the same person/thing as the subject?
STUDY

Extended writing — essays & reports

Two genres, two purposes, two structures

The assessment report in Document 3 and the application letter in Document 1 are both formal documents — but they belong to different genres with different conventions. At C1, understanding genre is as important as understanding grammar. The right content in the wrong structure loses marks.

Genre

The Academic Essay

Purpose
To argue, analyse, discuss, or evaluate a position in response to a question. The writer must demonstrate critical thinking and use evidence to support a logical argument.
Introduction
Paraphrase the question → state your position (thesis) → briefly outline the structure. “This essay will argue that… It will first examine… before considering…”
Body paragraphs
PEEL structure: Point → Evidence or example → Explanation → Link back to the question. One main idea per paragraph. Topic sentence first.
Conclusion
Restate your thesis in different words → summarise the key points → end with a broader implication or recommendation. Never introduce new evidence here.
Tone & register
Impersonal, formal, objective. Avoid contractions. Use hedging where appropriate (it could be argued that, evidence suggests). No bullet points.
Key language
Furthermore, in contrast, it is argued that, this suggests, consequently, it would appear that, while it is true that… it is nevertheless the case that, to conclude…
Genre

The Formal Report

Purpose
To present findings objectively and make clear recommendations. Unlike an essay, a report is written for a specific audience who will act on its conclusions.
Structure
Numbered sections with headings: 1. Introduction / 2. Findings / 3. Conclusions / 4. Recommendations. Some reports add an Executive Summary at the top.
Headings
Clear, noun-phrase headings. Bold or underlined. They allow a busy reader to navigate without reading every word: “2. Key Findings”, “3. Analysis of Results”
Findings section
Present data and observations objectively. Use passive voice frequently: “It was found that…”, “Data indicates that…”. May use bullet points for lists.
Recommendations
Clear, direct, actionable. Use modal verbs: “It is recommended that…”, “The panel should…”, “It would be advisable to…”
Key differences from essay
Reports use headings; essays don’t. Reports have recommendations; essays have conclusions. Reports can use bullet points; essays never do. Reports are written for a decision-maker; essays for an examiner.

Language for contrast — connecting ideas in formal writing

Expressing contrast is central to both essays and reports. At C1, you need a wide range of contrast structures, not just “but.”

FunctionFormal structureExample
Simple contrasthowever / nevertheless / yet / nonetheless“The candidate performed well; however, some reservations remain.”
Concessionwhile / although / even though / despite + -ing“Although the interview was challenging, the candidate excelled.”
Unexpected contrastdespite / in spite of + noun/gerund“Despite limited preparation time, the performance was outstanding.”
Strong contrastwhereas / whilst / on the other hand“Whereas the first candidate hesitated, Chen responded immediately.”
Emphatic concessionMuch as / Granted that / Admittedly“Much as I admire the candidate’s record, further assessment is warranted.”

Key vocabulary in depth

aptitude
n — natural ability or talent for something
An innate capacity to learn or do something well.
“She showed a clear aptitude for negotiation from the outset.”
competency
n — a skill or quality required for a role
A specific, demonstrable ability needed to perform a job effectively.
“The role requires competency in written and spoken Arabic.”
deliberate
v — to consider carefully before deciding
To discuss and weigh up options, especially as a group.
“The panel deliberated for less than twenty minutes before agreeing.”
rigour
n — thoroughness and precision
The quality of being extremely thorough and careful.
“Her analytical rigour set her apart from the other candidates.”
discretion
n — careful judgement; confidentiality
The ability to make responsible decisions; keeping sensitive matters private.
“This posting requires absolute discretion at all times.”
calibre
n — quality or level of ability
The standard of ability or the quality of something.
“A candidate of this calibre rarely applies at this grade.”
convene
v — to come together formally
To assemble or arrange for a group to meet for a formal purpose.
“The selection panel convened on 14 March at 09:00.”
unanimous
adj — completely agreed
In full agreement; with no dissenting voices.
“The panel’s decision to recommend her was unanimous.”
commensurate
adj — proportional; corresponding in size or degree
Matching in scale or intensity; equivalent.
“The salary is commensurate with experience and qualifications.”

Exercise 1

True or False — test your instincts

Before studying, check what you already know. Click True or False — the reason is revealed after each answer.

Inversion (e.g. “Never have I seen…”) is common in everyday spoken English.

False. COCA corpus data confirms inversion is almost exclusively a formal written feature. Native speakers rarely use it in conversation.

In “Not only does she speak Arabic, but she also writes it fluently,” the second clause (but she also) stays in normal word order.

True. After “Not only…”, only the FIRST clause is inverted. The “but also” clause returns to normal subject-verb order.

In the sentence “Having reviewed the file, a decision was reached,” the grammar is correct because the passive construction is formal.

False. This is a dangling participle. A decision cannot review a file. The implied subject of “having reviewed” must match the subject of the main clause. Correct: “Having reviewed the file, the panel reached a decision.”

A formal report uses numbered headings; an academic essay does not use headings.

True. This is one of the most important genre distinctions. Reports have numbered section headings (Introduction, Findings, Recommendations). Essays are written in continuous prose with no headings.

Error clinic — spot the mistake

Each sentence contains one error. Find it and write the correct version in the box.

1.“Rarely I have encountered such dedication in a junior member of staff.”
2.“Having reviewed the applications, a shortlist was drawn up by the panel.”
