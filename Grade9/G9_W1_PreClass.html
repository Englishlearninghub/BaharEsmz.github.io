Where do you stand?

Before we start: three positions

This week's grammar — mixed conditionals — is not just a structural exercise. It is the language of hypothetical argument: what would be different now if something had happened differently in the past. This is the grammar of debate, regret, and counterfactual reasoning.

Before looking at any grammar, take a position on three genuinely controversial questions. There is no "correct" answer here. Your answer will shape your practice writing later.

Question 1: Mandatory childhood vaccination

Should childhood vaccination be legally required, with exemptions only for documented medical reasons?

Question 2: Conspiracy theories and social media

Should social media platforms be legally required to remove medically disproven health claims — even if this limits free speech?

Bring your reasoning to class — we open Session 1 by comparing positions.

Read first: two op-eds in response to each other

Before any grammar: What is each writer's core position? Where do they agree? Where do they most sharply disagree? What kind of evidence does each use?

Dr. Nadia Voss
Associate Professor of Public Health, University of Leiden · Published in The European Policy Review
FOR MANDATORY VACCINATION

Herd immunity is not a choice: the case for mandatory vaccination

The resurgence of measles across three European countries last spring was not inevitable. If governments had enforced vaccination requirements a decade ago, the current outbreaks would not be threatening immunocompromised children who cannot protect themselves. This is not a hypothetical exercise — it is arithmetic. Below a ninety-five percent vaccination threshold, herd immunity collapses.

Critics invoke individual liberty. But individual liberty, as I would argue, has never been absolute. You are not free to drive without a seatbelt in most jurisdictions precisely because your injury imposes costs on others. The evidence strongly suggests that vaccine hesitancy correlates more closely with misinformation exposure than with genuine philosophical conviction. Had social media platforms removed anti-vaccination content five years ago, vaccine uptake rates would be significantly higher today.

It is worth noting that countries with the most robust mandatory vaccination frameworks — Germany, France, Italy — have seen measles cases fall by over eighty percent since 2019. If the EU had implemented a unified vaccination directive in 2018, the public health landscape across member states would look dramatically different now. The data is not ambiguous. The political will has been.

Marcus Osei writes that mandates "trample parental rights." While it is true that parental autonomy is a genuine value, it is not a value that extends to placing other people's children at risk. We regulate food safety, building codes, and car seats. It is difficult to see why the bodies of unvaccinated children should be exempt from the same logic.

Marcus Osei
Science writer & editor, The Independent Health Monitor
AGAINST MANDATORY VACCINATION

Compulsion breeds resistance: why mandates backfire

Dr. Voss's argument, however carefully constructed, rests on a foundational error: that compulsion produces compliance. The epidemiological literature is more complicated. If France had not introduced its mandatory vaccination law in 2018, it is not certain that uptake would have been lower — in fact, some regional studies suggest mandates polarise communities that were previously ambivalent.

I do not dispute the fact that herd immunity is the goal. However, the claim that only mandates can achieve it is empirically contestable. Scandinavia — with no mandatory vaccination — maintains uptake above ninety-five percent through trusted public communication. If investment in health communication had matched investment in enforcement infrastructure, we might now have both higher uptake and greater public trust.

The conspiracy theory problem is real. But it does not follow that removing content solves it. Suppressed information migrates to harder-to-monitor platforms. Had platforms taken a transparency-first approach rather than removal, the vaccine-hesitant community would arguably be more reachable today. Censorship does not change minds; it changes venues.

What strikes me as most troubling in Dr. Voss's framing is the assumption that state power applied to bodies is analogous to state power applied to food labels. These are not equivalent. One governs things; the other governs people. If we had designed public health policy around persuasion rather than compulsion from the outset, the anti-vaccination movement would not have the martyrdom narrative it now feeds on.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Find the pattern in the red sentences

Look at the red sentences — what do they have in common?

Look at: "If governments had enforced vaccination requirements a decade ago, the current outbreaks would not be threatening immunocompromised children."
The if-clause is in the past — which specific tense? The main clause is in the present — which modal?

If-clause: past perfect (had enforced). Main clause: would not be + -ing (present conditional progressive). This mixes a past-time hypothesis with a present-time result — that is the "mixed" in mixed conditional.

Compare: "If France had not introduced its mandatory vaccination law, it is not certain that uptake would have been lower."
What is the time frame of the result here — is it past or present?

The result is also in the past: "would have been lower." This is a Type 3 conditional (standard): past hypothesis → past result. Not a mixed conditional. The key test: when is the result? Past → Type 3. Present/now → Mixed conditional.

Look at: "If investment in health communication had matched investment in enforcement infrastructure, we might now have both higher uptake and greater public trust."
What word tells you the result is in the present?

"Now" — "we might now have." The past action (if investment had matched) → a present situation (what we might have now). The presence of "now" or references to the current state of affairs is often the clearest signal that a conditional is mixed, not Type 3.

STUDY

Unreal Conditionals — Brief Review (Types 2 & 3)

Unreal Conditionals

These express situations that are contrary to reality — either present/future (Type 2) or past (Type 3). Both are needed to understand mixed conditionals.

Type 2 — Unreal Present / Future

Structure: If + past simple → would/could/might + infinitive

Meaning: An imaginary situation now or in the future that is not true or unlikely.

Example: "If mandatory vaccination were introduced tomorrow, many parents would resist it."

Note: Formal English uses were (not was) for all persons in the if-clause: "If I/she/he were…"

↔
Type 3 — Unreal Past

Structure: If + past perfect → would/could/might + have + past participle

Meaning: An imaginary situation in the past — something that didn't happen.

Example: "If France had not introduced the mandate, uptake might have been lower."

Note: Both clauses refer to the past. This is the standard "hypothetical about the past" form.

STUDY

Mixed Conditionals — Form, Meaning, and Register

Mixed Conditionals

A mixed conditional connects a hypothetical past condition with a present (or ongoing) result — or less commonly, a present condition with a past result. The key insight: the two time frames are different.

Type A (most common): Past condition → Present result

The most useful and frequent type. A different past would mean a different present.

PAST CONDITION → PRESENT RESULT
If-clause (past perfect)
Main clause (would/could/might + infinitive)
If + subject + had (not) done…
subject + would/could/might + (not) + be / have / do…
"If the EU had implemented a unified directive in 2018…"
"…the public health landscape would look dramatically different now."

Type B (less common): Present condition → Past result

A present state explains why something in the past happened differently. Less frequent, but important to recognise.

PRESENT CONDITION → PAST RESULT
If-clause (past simple — unreal present)
Main clause (would have + past participle)
If + subject + were / didn't + verb…
subject + would have + past participle…
"If I weren't a trained scientist…"
"…I would have believed the misinformation years ago."

All examples from the op-eds:

SentenceIf-clauseResultType
"If governments had enforced vaccination requirements a decade ago, the current outbreaks would not be threatening children."Past perfectPresent continuous (now)Mixed A
"Had social media platforms removed anti-vaccination content five years ago, vaccine uptake rates would be significantly higher today."Past perfect (inverted)Present state (today)Mixed A
"If investment in health communication had matched enforcement spending, we might now have both higher uptake and trust."Past perfectPresent state (now)Mixed A
"Had platforms taken a transparency-first approach, the vaccine-hesitant community would arguably be more reachable today."Past perfect (inverted)Present state (today)Mixed A

⚠ Corpus note — register:

Mixed conditionals are primarily a formal written feature. Corpus data (COCA, BNC) confirms they appear far more in written essays, opinion pieces, academic papers, and formal speeches than in everyday spoken conversation. In informal speech, speakers usually express the same meaning more simply:

formal"If the vaccine mandate had passed in 2018, we would now have herd immunity."Written op-ed or academic essay — mixed conditional appropriate.
informal"I mean, if they'd done it back in 2018, we'd probably be fine by now."Spoken conversation — contracted, simpler, no "now" required.

Use mixed conditionals when writing formal opinion pieces, essays, proposals, or delivering prepared speeches. In casual discussion, the distinction often disappears naturally.

Inverted form (formal writing only):

In formal written English, the if-clause can be inverted — the subject and auxiliary swap, and if is omitted:

✓Had governments enforced requirements earlier, the outbreaks would not be occurring now.Inversion: "Had + subject + past participle…" — only with had, were, should. Formal written register only.
✗Had I a doctor, I would have known sooner.WRONG modern English — inversion with "had" works only with "had been/done/etc." (perfect), not with simple "had."

Time signals that reveal mixed conditionals:

now · today · currently · at present still · no longer · already would be (not would have been) could/might + present infinitive

⚠ Common errors:

✗If the mandate would have passed, we would now have herd immunity.WRONG — no "would" in the if-clause. Use past perfect: "If the mandate had passed…"
✗If they had acted sooner, things would have been better now.MIXED — "would have been" is past result. For a present result: "would be better now." Small change, different meaning.
✓If they had acted sooner, things would be better now.Correct mixed conditional: past action → present state.

ADOPTION & REPRODUCTIVE RIGHTS PANEL

Excerpt: making the case for expanded adoption support

"The position I want to advance is not a simple one. I would argue that the most significant failure in reproductive policy over the past thirty years has not been in the domain of abortion law — it has been in the near-total collapse of adoption infrastructure in many Western countries.

If adoption had been made genuinely accessible — financially, legally, and emotionally — many of the women who sought abortions out of economic desperation would have had a real alternative. This is not to say that adoption could or should replace abortion rights. These are separate questions. Had robust post-adoption support systems been built in the 1990s, the framing of this debate would look quite different today.

What I find striking is that both sides of the abortion debate have largely ignored the adoption question. Pro-choice advocates have focused, rightly, on bodily autonomy. Pro-life advocates have focused on fetal personhood. Neither has built a serious policy framework for the children already born and in need of permanent families. It is difficult to escape the conclusion that adoption has been treated as a political afterthought by everyone.

If we had invested as much political energy in reforming adoption law as we have in litigating abortion rights over the past two decades, thousands of children currently in institutional care would now be in permanent homes. The evidence suggests that simplified, subsidised adoption processes — as piloted in the Netherlands and Denmark — result in significantly higher rates of adoption finalisation without reducing the safeguarding of children's interests."

STUDY

Arguing a Position — Language for Academic Opinion

Language for Arguing a Position

At C1 level, arguments are not just about what you say — but how you frame it. Corpus research shows that proficient academic writers use a repertoire of hedging, conceding, and positioning phrases that signal precision and intellectual honesty.

1. Stating your position

I would argue that… The position I want to advance is… My contention is that… It is my view that… I take the view that…

2. Hedging — softening a claim

Hedging signals that a claim is not absolute — it's based on evidence, not certainty. C1 writers hedge strategically, not out of weakness.

The evidence suggests that… It seems likely that… This appears to indicate… arguably · presumably · evidently in many / most cases

3. Conceding a point (then counter-arguing)

The concede-then-rebuttal move is one of the most powerful academic argument structures. You acknowledge the other side — then show why your position still holds.

ConcessionCounter-argument link
While it is true that……it does not follow that…
I do not dispute the fact that……however, the claim that X is empirically contestable.
This is not to say that……rather, the point is that…
Granted,……but this misses the broader picture…
It may well be that……nevertheless, the evidence indicates…

4. Introducing evidence or examples

It is worth noting that… The data indicates / suggests… As the case of [X] illustrates… This is borne out by… The evidence is unambiguous:

5. Drawing conclusions / challenging assumptions

It is difficult to escape the conclusion that… It is difficult to see why… What strikes me as troubling is… It does not follow that… The assumption that X is questionable because…

Combining argument language with mixed conditionals:

The most sophisticated academic arguments often embed a mixed conditional inside an argument structure phrase:

✓The evidence strongly suggests that, had adoption infrastructure been reformed in the 1990s, the current debate would look quite different today.Hedging phrase + inverted mixed conditional = high-level academic writing.
✓I would argue that if we had invested as heavily in persuasion as in compulsion, public trust in vaccines would be substantially higher now.Position statement + mixed conditional = thesis-level claim.

3.Type 2If-clause: past simple (invested — imaginary present). Result: present (would improve). Imaginary present → present result.
4.MixedInverted form (Had the EU introduced…). Result: present (would now have). Past condition → present result = Mixed A.
5.Mixed BIf-clause: past simple unreal present (weren't — she IS a researcher). Result: past (would never have questioned). Present state → past result = Mixed B.

Exercise 3 — Error correction

1.If the mandate had been introduced earlier, public trust would be higher now."would have been" → no "would" in the if-clause. Must be past perfect (had been introduced).
2.Had the research been published sooner, doctors would be better informed today."would have been" → this should be a Mixed conditional (past → present). Result = "would be" (now/today), not "would have been" (past).
3.If governments had taken action earlier, the situation would be different now."would been" is missing the auxiliary "be": "would be different." Classic modal error.
4.If she had been a stronger advocate in 2015, the policy would have passed."If she is" → present tense in the if-clause with a past result doesn't work. The hypothesis is in the past (2015), so: past perfect (had been). Both clauses past → Type 3.

Exercise 4 — Match the function

1.C — Concede + rebut"I do not dispute…" = concession; "however, the claim that… is contestable" = rebuttal.
2.E — Drawing a conclusion"It is difficult to escape the conclusion that…" is a fixed conclusion phrase.
3.D — Introducing evidence (also B)"The evidence suggests…" introduces a finding from data. It also hedges (B) — "suggests" not "proves." Both D and B are defensible; D is primary.
4.A — Stating a position"The position I want to advance is…" is a direct position statement.
5.B — Hedging"It seems likely that…" softens the claim — it's probable, not certain.

Exercise 7 — Collocation build

1.sparked / fuelled / stoked (all correct)All three collocate with "controversy." "Sparked" = started it; "fuelled" = intensified it; "stoked" = deliberately increased it.
2.advancing / defending"Advancing a position" = putting it forward for the first time. "Defending" = protecting it from objections. "Mounting" collocates with argument/case, not position.
3.rebutted / challenged"Rebutted" = directly argued against. "Challenged" = questioned. "Addressed" is weaker — it means dealt with, not necessarily opposed.
4.deeply / highly / bitterlyAll three work. "Bitterly controversial" implies more personal or painful division; "highly" is neutral; "deeply" suggests embedded disagreement.
