ENGAGE

Formal vs. informal: the same information, differently packaged

Before you start

Read these two versions of the same information. One is an informal summary. The other is from a formal policy document. They contain the same facts — but they sound completely different.

Version A (informal):
"They laid off over two million factory workers during the financial crisis. Everyone knows the government cut benefits, and it didn't really help anyone. The recession hit people in poorer regions the hardest — some areas still haven't recovered."

Version B (formal — from this week's policy brief):
"Over two million factory workers were made redundant during the financial crisis of 2008–09. The elimination of certain welfare provisions was widely contested. The disproportionate impact of economic contraction on peripheral regions has been extensively documented."

This week you will learn the two main tools that produce Version B: the passive voice (for objectivity, agent-removal, and focus) and nominalisation (converting verbs into nouns to create formal noun phrases).

CONTEXT

Read first: the GEPC policy brief

Before any grammar: What are the two main arguments in the brief? Where do Reyes and Okoro agree — and where does their framing differ? What policy does the brief ultimately recommend?

Global Employment Policy Centre · Policy Brief No. 47
Structural Unemployment and the Path to Poverty Eradication:
A Comparative Analysis
Authors: Dr. Sofia Reyes (ILO, Geneva) & James Okoro (Community Employment Initiative, Lagos) · Published 2024

Executive Summary

Structural unemployment is widely regarded as one of the most persistent barriers to poverty eradication in both developed and developing economies. The inadequacy of short-term stimulus measures in addressing long-term labour market displacement has been demonstrated repeatedly in post-crisis contexts. This brief argues that sustained investment in retraining infrastructure and community-based employment programmes represents the most evidence-based pathway to the reduction of structural unemployment.

1. The Scale of the Problem

Between 2008 and 2023, over forty million workers in OECD countries alone were displaced by a combination of automation, offshoring, and post-crisis austerity measures. In sub-Saharan Africa, the picture is more complex: formal employment figures are consistently understated because large portions of the workforce remain in the informal sector and are not captured in standard statistical frameworks.

The concentration of unemployment in specific demographic groups — young people, women, and workers in peripheral regions — has been extensively documented. In the aftermath of the 2020 pandemic, the rate of long-term unemployment among workers aged over fifty increased by twenty-three percent across the EU. These workers are at particular risk of permanent labour market exclusion once employment gaps exceed twenty-four months.

It should be noted that many of those who get laid off during economic downturns do not get reabsorbed into equivalent employment. Instead, they are pushed toward precarious, lower-wage positions that do not restore pre-displacement income levels. The erosion of household income following redundancy is compounded by the inadequacy of unemployment benefit systems in most lower-middle-income countries.

2. Policy Responses — What Has Been Tried

Active labour market policies (ALMPs) were introduced across most OECD economies following the 2008 crisis. However, the effectiveness of these interventions was limited by short funding cycles and weak coordination between national and regional authorities. The transition from manufacturing to service-sector employment was not adequately supported by retraining infrastructure in most affected regions.

In contrast, community-based employment models — such as those piloted in James Okoro's programme in Lagos State — demonstrate that the integration of skills training with local enterprise support can produce durable employment outcomes at significantly lower cost than centralised programmes. Workers who participate in such initiatives are not simply placed in jobs: they are equipped with transferable skills, are connected to peer networks, and are supported through the transition period.

3. Recommendations

The elimination of structural unemployment cannot be achieved through any single policy intervention. It requires coordinated action across education, social protection, and enterprise development. Specifically, we recommend that retraining programmes be designed with built-in flexibility to respond to sectoral shifts; that community-based models be scaled using public-private partnerships; and that the measurement of employment outcomes be broadened to capture informal sector participation.

The eradication of poverty in any meaningful sense requires that employment — not merely the absence of unemployment — be treated as the policy target. Good work, fairly rewarded, remains the most reliable mechanism by which individuals are lifted out of poverty.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the blue phrases — find the patterns

Two types of passive — notice the difference

Compare: "Workers were displaced by automation." vs. "Many of those who get laid off do not get reabsorbed."
Both are passive — but the verbs look different. What is the structural difference?

First sentence: be-passive — was/were + past participle. Second: get-passive — get + past participle. Both express that the subject receives the action. The get-passive often carries a different register connotation — see Study section 5.

Look at: "Active labour market policies were introduced across most OECD economies."
Who introduced the policies? Why is the agent not mentioned?

The agent (governments) is omitted because (a) it's obvious from context, and (b) the writer wants to focus on the policies themselves, not on who introduced them. This is one of the main functions of the passive in formal writing: to shift focus from the actor to the action or result.

Look at: "Retraining programmes be designed with built-in flexibility…"
This passive looks different — there's no is/are/was/were before "be designed." Why?

This is a passive subjunctive, used after "we recommend that…" In formal writing, "recommend/suggest/propose + that + subject + base form (without to)" is a pattern for formal recommendations. "Be designed" = bare infinitive passive — "we recommend that programmes be designed" = it should be designed. This is formal written English; spoken English often uses "should be designed" instead.

STUDY

Be-Passive — Full Review at C1

1. Be-Passive — Review

The standard passive: be (in any tense) + past participle. Used across all registers — but especially important in formal academic and policy writing.

Forms across tenses:

TenseActivePassive (be + p.p.)
Present simpleGovernments introduce policies.Policies are introduced by governments.
Present perfectResearchers have documented the gap.The gap has been documented extensively.
Past simpleAutomation displaced workers.Workers were displaced by automation.
Past perfectThey had designed the programme.The programme had been designed carefully.
Future (will)Experts will publish the data.The data will be published next year.
ModalAuthorities must expand access.Access must be expanded urgently.
InfinitiveWe need to reform the system.The system needs to be reformed.
Subjunctive (formal)We recommend that they design it flexibly.We recommend that it be designed flexibly.

Four main functions of the passive in formal writing:

FunctionWhyExample from the brief
Remove the agentAgent is obvious, unimportant, or politically sensitive"Workers were displaced by automation and austerity."
Shift focus to the objectThe result or patient is more important than who did it"The inadequacy of stimulus measures has been demonstrated repeatedly."
Objectivity / impersonalityAvoids "I/we" — maintains formal academic tone"It should be noted that…" / "The gap has been documented."
Continuity of topicKeeps the same subject across sentences without repetition"Workers… are not simply placed in jobs. They are equipped… are connected… are supported."

By + agent — when to include it:

Include agent when: it's surprising, important, or adds meaning "The programme was designed by a community of former redundant workers." Omit agent when: it's obvious, unimportant, or you want objectivity "Policies are introduced [by governments]." — obvious; agent adds nothing

⚠ Common errors at C1:

✗The policy was introduce by the ministry last year.WRONG — past participle needed: "was introduced."
✗Workers are being displaced since 2008.WRONG — present continuous passive (are being displaced) is for actions happening right now; "since" requires present perfect: "have been displaced since 2008."
✓Workers have been displaced from the formal sector since 2008.Present perfect passive + since = correct for ongoing situation starting at a past point.
STUDY

Get-Passive — Extended

2. Get-Passive

Get + past participle. Same passive meaning as be + past participle — but with important differences in register, connotation, and typical contexts.

Form: get (conjugated) + past participle

TenseStructureExample
Present simpleget/gets + p.p.Many workers get laid off in the first quarter.
Past simplegot + p.p.She got promoted despite the hiring freeze.
Present perfecthave/has gotten + p.p.Too many workers have gotten caught in the retraining gap.
Negativedon't/doesn't get + p.p.Workers often don't get compensated adequately.
QuestionDid + subject + get + p.p.?Did those workers get rehired?

Get-passive vs. Be-passive — the key differences:

Be-passiveGet-passive
RegisterAll registers — formal, neutral, informalInformal to neutral; uncommon in formal academic writing
ConnotationNeutral — just describes the situationOften implies the subject is affected negatively, or the event is unexpected/sudden
Agent focusFocuses on the action or the objectOften implies the subject had some involvement or the event was accidental
Typical contextsAcademic papers, policy briefs, newsConversation, news reports, personal narrative
Example"The workers were dismissed.""The workers got dismissed."

Why is the brief unusual in using get-passive?

The GEPC brief uses get-passive deliberately in section 1: "many of those who get laid off… do not get reabsorbed." In a formal policy document, this is a stylistic choice to foreground the human experience of displacement — to shift from abstract statistics to the felt reality of workers. It creates a brief register shift that draws the reader's attention before returning to formal language. This is an advanced rhetorical technique.

Corpus note: Corpus data (COCA, BNC) confirms that get-passive is significantly more frequent in spoken English. Common get-passive collocations to know:

get laid off · get made redundant · get fired · get promoted get paid · get hired · get replaced · get reabsorbed get caught (in) · get left behind · get overlooked get compensated · get supported · get connected

⚠ Register warning — get-passive in formal writing:

✗The policy was not effectively implemented and workers got ignored.WRONG register for formal writing — "were ignored" is more appropriate in a policy brief.
✓Workers got laid off at twice the usual rate — a statistic that formal displacement data consistently fails to capture.Acceptable: get-passive used deliberately to signal human impact before pivoting back to formal register.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the teal phrases — from verb to noun

Find the hidden verbs inside the teal noun phrases

Look at: "The inadequacy of short-term stimulus measures in addressing long-term labour market displacement has been demonstrated repeatedly."
Can you rewrite this using a verb instead of a noun phrase? Which version sounds more formal?

Verbal: "Short-term stimulus measures are inadequate for addressing long-term displacement, and this has been demonstrated repeatedly." — three clauses. Nominal: one noun phrase as subject. The nominal version is more dense, more formal, and packages the idea as a single given fact before moving on. This "packing" is a key feature of academic writing.

Look at: "The eradication of poverty in any meaningful sense requires that employment be treated as the policy target."
What verb is hidden inside "eradication"? What ending (-tion, -ment, -ance…) was added to create it?

"Eradicate" → "eradication" (-tion suffix). The verb "eradicate" has been turned into a noun — this is nominalisation. The noun phrase can then take articles (the), prepositions (of), and adjectives (meaningful), which gives it more argumentative weight than a simple verb.

Compare: "Unemployment increased by 23%" vs. "The increase in unemployment of 23% represents…"
What changes — grammatically and stylistically — when you nominalise?

Grammatically: the verb "increased" becomes the noun "increase" + article "the" + preposition "in" + noun "unemployment." Stylistically: the nominalised version allows you to comment ON the increase as a thing, rather than simply reporting it happening. "The increase in unemployment represents…" — the increase is the subject of the new sentence, allowing further analysis. This is why academic and formal writers nominalise: to make an event or process into a thing that can be evaluated, described, and argued about.

STUDY

Nominalisation — The C1 Writer's Tool

3. Nominalisation

Converting verbs (and sometimes adjectives) into nouns or noun phrases. A defining feature of academic, policy, and professional written English at C1 and above. Corpus research confirms it is one of the most significant differences between C1 and B2 learner writing.

Key nominalisation suffixes:

SuffixVerb/Adjective →NounExample in use
-tion / -sioneradicate · concentrate · eliminate · reduce · introduce · displaceeradication · concentration · elimination · reduction · introduction · displacement"The elimination of poverty requires sustained investment."
-mentemploy · measure · achieve · replace · commitemployment · measurement · achievement · replacement · commitment"The measurement of informal sector employment is complex."
-ance / -enceadequate → inadequate · comply · dependinadequacy · compliance · dependence"The inadequacy of benefit systems compounds the problem."
-ityflexible · effective · productive · stableflexibility · effectiveness · productivity · stability"The effectiveness of ALMPs was limited by short funding cycles."
-alpropose · approve · refuse · withdrawproposal · approval · refusal · withdrawal"The proposal to expand community programmes was approved."
Zero-derivationincrease · rise · fall · decline · shift · reportthe increase · the rise · the fall · the decline · a shift · a report"The rise in long-term unemployment was documented in 2022."

The full nominalisation pattern:

Nominalised noun phrases in formal writing follow a predictable structure:
[determiner] + [pre-modifier] + [noun] + [of-phrase or prepositional phrase]

Examples:

→ the rapid increase in unemployment = the + rapid (adj) + increase (nom.) + in unemployment (prep. phrase)

→ the inadequacy of current benefit systems = the + inadequacy (nom.) + of current benefit systems (of-phrase)

→ a significant reduction in working hours = a + significant (adj) + reduction (nom.) + in working hours (prep. phrase)

Verb → nominalisation — full transformation:

Verbal sentenceNominalised versionEffect
"Unemployment increased significantly.""The significant increase in unemployment…"Turns an event into a subject that can be analysed.
"They eliminated several welfare programmes.""The elimination of several welfare programmes…"Removes the agent (they); makes the action the subject.
"The system failed to support workers adequately.""The inadequacy of system support for workers…"Reframes failure as a property (inadequacy) — more neutral and analytical.
"Workers transitioned from manufacturing to services.""The transition from manufacturing to service-sector employment…"Packages a complex process as a single noun phrase.

⚠ When NOT to over-nominalise:

Academic writing can become impenetrable if nominalisation is overused — a phenomenon sometimes called "nominitis." At C1, the skill is not just to nominalise, but to know when to use a verb for clarity and when to use a nominal for density.

✗The implementation of the facilitation of the transition of workers towards the acquisition of new skills…WRONG — "nominitis." Stack of noun phrases with no clear verb. Unreadable.
✓The transition to new skills requires sustained support from employers and training providers.One nominalisation ("transition") + a clear main verb ("requires") + object. Formal but readable.

VOCABULARY

Unemployment & Poverty — The Full Word Set

Core vocabulary

structural unemploymentnoun phrase
/ˈstrʌktʃərəl ʌnɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
unemployment caused by a mismatch between workers' skills and the skills needed in the economy — not cyclical (short-term recession) but embedded in the structure of the labour market.
"Structural unemployment persists long after economic recovery because retraining takes time."
to be made redundantpassive phrase
/meɪd rɪˈdʌndənt/
to lose your job because your position no longer exists — usually due to company restructuring, automation, or downsizing. More formal than "be fired." Used for job loss that is not the worker's fault.
"Over two million factory workers were made redundant during the 2008 crisis."
labour marketnoun phrase
/ˈleɪbə ˈmɑːkɪt/
the supply and demand for employment — encompassing all workers, job seekers, employers, and the conditions under which labour is exchanged.
"Labour market participation rates fell sharply in the post-pandemic period."
austeritynoun
/ɒˈsterɪti/
government economic policy of reducing public spending, often during fiscal crises. Associated with cuts to welfare, public services, and employment programmes.
"Post-crisis austerity measures compounded the impact of displacement on vulnerable workers."
displacementnoun
/dɪsˈpleɪsmənt/
the forced removal of workers from their jobs — used technically in economics to describe job loss from automation, offshoring, or structural change.
"Labour market displacement has accelerated with the adoption of AI-assisted manufacturing."
precarious employmentnoun phrase
/prɪˈkeəriəs/
work that is insecure, poorly paid, and lacking in rights or protections — zero-hours contracts, gig work, and informal sector work are typical forms.
"Workers pushed from formal to precarious employment often cannot access redundancy or pension rights."
retrainingnoun
/riːˈtreɪnɪŋ/
the process of learning new skills to take a different job, usually after redundancy or sectoral change.
"The transition from manufacturing to service-sector employment was not adequately supported by retraining infrastructure."
eradicationnoun
/ɪˌrædɪˈkeɪʃən/
the complete elimination of something — used for poverty, disease, and other large-scale social problems. Implies thoroughness; stronger than "reduction."
"The eradication of extreme poverty is UN Sustainable Development Goal 1."

Extended vocabulary: policy & economics

active labour market policy (ALMP)noun phrase
/ˈæktɪv ˈleɪbə ˈmɑːkɪt ˈpɒlɪsi/
government interventions to help unemployed people find work — including job search support, retraining, wage subsidies, and direct job creation.
"ALMPs were introduced across most OECD economies following the 2008 crisis, with mixed results."
informal sectornoun phrase
/ɪnˈfɔːməl ˈsektə/
economic activity outside formal contracts, taxes, and legal protections — includes street trading, day labour, domestic work, and subsistence farming. Very large in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.
"Workers in the informal sector are not captured in standard unemployment statistics."
welfare provisionnoun phrase
/ˈwelfeə prəˈvɪʒən/
government-funded support for people without income — unemployment benefit, housing support, food assistance, etc.
"The inadequacy of welfare provision in lower-middle-income countries compounds the impact of displacement."
fiscal policynoun phrase
/ˈfɪskəl ˈpɒlɪsi/
government decisions about spending and taxation — key lever for affecting employment. Expansionary fiscal policy (more spending) can stimulate job creation; contractionary (cuts) can raise unemployment.
"Contractionary fiscal policy during a demand-side recession is likely to worsen unemployment."
offshoringnoun
/ˈɒfʃɔːrɪŋ/
moving business operations (especially manufacturing) to another country to reduce costs. A major driver of structural unemployment in advanced economies since the 1990s.
"A combination of automation and offshoring displaced manufacturing workers throughout the Midwest."
poverty trapnoun phrase
/ˈpɒvəti træp/
a situation where taking a job causes someone to lose benefits worth more than the wage — creating a disincentive to work. A well-documented failure of poorly designed welfare systems.
"Poorly calibrated taper rates create poverty traps that actively discourage employment."
gig economynoun phrase
/ɡɪɡ ɪˈkɒnəmi/
a labour market characterised by short-term contracts and freelance work, rather than permanent jobs. Workers in the gig economy often lack pension rights, sick pay, and job security.
"The growth of the gig economy has blurred the boundary between employment and self-employment."
upskillingnoun
/ˈʌpˌskɪlɪŋ/
learning additional or more advanced skills, usually while in employment. Distinct from retraining (which implies a complete career change).
"The integration of upskilling with enterprise support produced durable employment outcomes."

Exercise 1 — Transform to passive

1.Forty million workers were displaced by automation and offshoring between 2008 and 2023.Be-passive. Agent retained (by automation and offshoring) because it is key information — identifying the cause is the point of the sentence.
2.The concentration of unemployment in peripheral regions has been extensively documented.Present perfect passive. Agent omitted — "by researchers" is obvious and adds nothing. The nominalisation (concentration of unemployment) is preserved.
3.Many contract workers got fired / were fired before they could access redundancy payments.Both are grammatically correct. Get-passive is more appropriate if the register is informal or to foreground the sudden/negative human impact. Be-passive is more formal. Either is acceptable with justification.
4.We recommend that the measurement of employment outcomes be broadened.Subjunctive passive — no "should" in the that-clause. Note: "be broadened" not "should be broadened" or "is broadened." This is the formal recommendation structure.

Exercise 2 — Be- or get-passive?

1.B — be-passiveStandard formal be-passive. Appropriate for a policy brief.
2.G or G*"Get left behind" — get-passive. In a policy context, could be G* (deliberate register shift for rhetorical effect), or simply G (neutral informal). Both defensible.
3.G — get-passive"Got paid" and "were ignored" — mixed register. "Got paid" is informal. "Were ignored" is be-passive. For a formal policy brief, both should use be-passive: "were paid" and "were then ignored." The sentence would need rewriting for a formal document.
4.B — be-passive"Are not captured" — standard formal be-passive. Highly appropriate for a policy brief. "It should be noted that" is a classic formal distancing phrase.

Exercise 3 — Error correction

1.The retraining infrastructure was not adequately supported by government funding in most regions."adequate support" → "adequately supported" — the adverb (adequately) modifies the passive participle; the adjective form (adequate) cannot modify the verb.
2.Workers have been displaced from the formal sector since the automation wave began."displace" → "displaced" — past participle required in the passive construction (have been + p.p.).
3.We recommend that all employment outcomes be broadened to include informal sector data."should be broadened" → "be broadened" — in formal subjunctive after "recommend that," no modal is used. The bare infinitive passive is required.
4.The effectiveness of the interventions is / was limited by short funding cycles."are" → "is" — subject-verb agreement error. The head noun is "effectiveness" (singular), not "interventions." The prepositional phrase "of the interventions" does not change the number of the subject.

Exercise 4 — Nominalisation

1.The prevalence / widespread occurrence"Is widespread" → the prevalence of poverty / the widespread nature of poverty. "Prevalence" is the standard academic collocation for "how widespread something is."
2.increase"Increased" → the sharp increase in unemployment (zero-derivation: the verb "increase" becomes the noun "increase").
3.The government's failure to eliminate structural unemployment / The lack of progress in the elimination of…Several nominalisations possible. "Failed to eliminate" → failure to eliminate / the absence of elimination. Accept any clear formal nominalisation of the idea.
4.dependence"Depend on" → dependence on. "Workers' dependence on benefits during displacement."
5.integration"Integrates" → the integration of skills training with enterprise support. (-tion suffix)

Exercise 6 — Gap-fill

1.informal sectorThe informal sector is outside standard statistical frameworks.
2.structural unemploymentThe definition distinguishes structural (skills mismatch) from cyclical (recession-driven) unemployment.
3.precariousPrecarious work = insecure, lacking rights and protections.
4.austerityPost-crisis austerity = reduction in public spending during fiscal crisis.
5.eradicationEradication of poverty = UN SDG 1. Complete elimination, not mere reduction.
6.retraining / displacementRetraining programmes reduce displacement (or long-term unemployment). Both words in the sentence must be from the vocabulary set.

Exercise 7 — Collocation + nominalisation

1.The exclusion of workers from the labour market / labour market exclusion"Were excluded from the labour market" → the exclusion of workers from the labour market. Key collocation: "labour market exclusion."
2.The inadequacy of welfare provision"Was inadequate" → the inadequacy of welfare provision. (-acy suffix from "inadequate")
3.The displacement of workers by automation"Were displaced" → the displacement of workers by automation. (-ment suffix)
4.The transition from manufacturing to service-sector employment"Transitioned from manufacturing to services" → the transition from manufacturing to service-sector employment. (zero-derivation)
