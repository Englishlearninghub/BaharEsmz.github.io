ENGAGE

Before we start: where do you stand?

Two quick positions

Astrology is this week's theme — and it's a genuinely interesting case study for language learning, because making predictions is at the heart of astrological discourse. Astrologers predict. Sceptics critique. Both use the same grammar.

Before looking at any language: what's your position? Choose one for each question.

1. Do you think astrology has any genuine predictive value?

2. Have you ever acted on a prediction — horoscope, tarot, fortune-teller — and had it feel accurate?

Bring your answers to Session 1 — we open the class by comparing positions. Neither answer is more intelligent than the other; both are linguistically productive.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the violet phrases — what do they have in common?

Three questions about the violet phrases

Look at: "We will be feeling its effects for approximately three weeks."
Compare: "We will feel its effects." Which version sounds more certain, and which more like a gradual ongoing process?

"Will feel" is a simple future prediction — it could be a single event or a state. "Will be feeling" is the future progressive — it foregrounds the activity as ongoing and in-progress over time. In the astrology context, Rafael is describing a process that will be happening across three weeks, not a single moment.

Look at: "Venus would be moving into Capricorn in December."
This is reported speech — the original prediction was future progressive. What does the future progressive add here that a simple future wouldn't?

A planetary transit is a gradual movement — it unfolds over time, it doesn't "happen" in a single moment. The future progressive ("will be moving") is more accurate than "will move" because it frames the transit as an in-progress action rather than a single completed event. This is a key function: describing future situations in progress, not just future events.

Yuna says: "She was already going to be meeting lots of people in December."
This isn't will + be + -ing — it's going to + be + -ing. Is this the same structure? What does "already going to be" mean?

"Going to be meeting" is the future progressive with "going to" instead of "will" — same progressive structure, different future marker. "Going to" here signals that the event was already planned or predictable before Priya's reading. Yuna's point: Priya would have been meeting people anyway — the stars didn't create the situation, they just gave it a frame.

STUDY

Future Progressive — Form, Meaning, and When to Use It

Future Progressive

Two forms — both describe an action that will be in progress at or around a future point. The key contrast is with the simple future.

Forms:

FormStructureExample
will be + -ingsubject + will be + verb-ing"Mercury will be moving through Scorpio for the next three weeks."
going to be + -ingsubject + am/is/are going to be + verb-ing"We are going to be feeling the effects well into November."
Negativesubject + won't be + verb-ing"Astrology won't be making falsifiable claims anytime soon."
QuestionWill + subject + be + verb-ing?"Will you be attending the astrology conference next month?"

Five main uses — all from the podcast context:

#UseExampleWhy not simple future?
1In-progress future
An action in progress at a specific future time		"At midnight, Mercury will be entering Scorpio.""Will enter" = simple event. "Will be entering" = the transition is in motion at that moment.
2Gradual process / extended period
Describes something unfolding over time		"We will be feeling its effects for three weeks.""Will feel" sounds like a single future moment. "Will be feeling" = ongoing, spread across time.
3Planned / expected future event
Something already arranged or predictable		"She was going to be meeting lots of people in December anyway.""Going to be meeting" emphasises the arrangement was already in place before any horoscope.
4Polite enquiry about plans
Asking about future arrangements without presupposing		"Will you be coming to next week's recording?""Will you come?" sounds like a request or invitation. "Will you be coming?" is softer — just asking about plans.
5Simultaneous future actions
Two things happening at the same future time		"While Rafael will be reading the chart, Yuna will be checking the statistics."Simple future here would sound like two separate completed events, not simultaneous processes.

Future progressive vs. simple future — the key contrast:

will + inf."Mercury will enter Scorpio on Thursday."Simple event — completed at a point in time. Like a switch being flipped.
will be + -ing"Mercury will be moving through Scorpio for the next three weeks."Gradual in-progress process — Mercury is in motion throughout the period.
will + inf."I will meet you at the conference."Simple future decision or prediction — a single meeting event.
will be + -ing"I will be attending the conference all week."In-progress — I'll be there throughout the week, not just for one moment.

⚠ Stative verbs — no progressive:

As with other progressive tenses, stative verbs (know, believe, understand, contain, seem) cannot normally form the future progressive:

✗I will be believing in astrology more after this episode.WRONG — "believe" is stative. Use: "I will believe in astrology more after this episode."
✓I will be reading about astrology more after this episode.OK — "read" is dynamic (an activity), so progressive is fine.

⚠ Common errors:

✗Mercury will moving through Scorpio next week.WRONG — "be" is missing. Will + be + -ing: "will be moving."
✗What will you be doing yesterday at this time?WRONG — future progressive cannot refer to the past. Use past progressive: "What were you doing yesterday at this time?"

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the gold phrases — how do they function?

Find the function of each gold phrase

Look at: "In terms of communication, travel, and technology — these are the domains where you need to be careful."
What does "in terms of" do here? Could you replace it with a simpler word?

"In terms of" introduces the domain or dimension being discussed — "regarding" or "concerning" could replace it, but "in terms of" is more formal and allows a longer list to follow naturally. It tells the reader: "I'm going to specify the area we're talking about." It focuses the scope of what follows.

Look at: "As a result of the reading, she was open to connection."
What relationship between ideas does "as a result of" signal? Is it cause or effect — and which side of that relationship comes in the phrase?

"As a result of" signals a cause-effect relationship — the phrase introduces the CAUSE ("the reading"), and the main clause describes the EFFECT ("she was open to connection"). It's the formal equivalent of "because of" — but more academic and appropriate for written registers.

Look at: "With regard to confirmation bias — I think this is the key concept here."
Compare: "In terms of confirmation bias…" — are these interchangeable? What's the nuance?

Both introduce a topic or domain. "With regard to" is slightly more formal and more commonly used at the start of a new point or to signal a topic shift. "In terms of" tends to be used when specifying a dimension or angle of evaluation. In practice, they overlap significantly — the most important thing is knowing both and using them to replace "about" or "concerning" in formal speech or writing.

STUDY

Prepositional Phrase Collocations — The C1 Fluency Markers

Prepositional Phrase Collocations

Corpus research confirms that the most significant gap between B2 and C1 learner writing is not grammar errors — it is the absence of multi-word prepositional collocations. These phrases are fluency markers that signal academic register, organisation of ideas, and discourse sophistication.

in terms of
Regarding a specific dimension, aspect, or area of evaluation

"In terms of predictive accuracy, astrology has no empirical basis."

"In terms of psychological benefit, the reading helped Priya feel more attentive."

"How does this differ, in terms of methodology, from cold reading?"

in terms of + noun phrase / noun clause
with regard to
Concerning a particular topic or issue — used to introduce or shift to a new point

"With regard to the mechanism — I think you're asking the wrong question."

"With regard to confirmation bias, this is the key psychological concept."

"With regard to responsible practice, the best astrologers are explicit."

with regard to + noun phrase (no article omission)
as a result of
Because of / due to — introduces a cause. The effect follows in the main clause.

"As a result of the reading, she was open to connection."

"As a result of confirmation bias, accurate predictions are remembered more."

"As a result of this transit, a period of reflection will follow."

as a result of + noun phrase → [comma] + main clause (effect)

Extended set — four more high-frequency C1 phrases:

in the context of
Against the background of; taking into account the situation of

"In the context of rising anxiety rates, astrology's appeal is understandable."

in the context of + noun phrase
on the basis of
Using something as evidence or reason for a decision

"No major life decision should be made on the basis of a horoscope."

on the basis of + noun phrase
in the light of
Taking into account new information or evidence

"In the light of the evidence, we should reconsider the claim."

in the light of / in light of + noun phrase
in addition to
As well as; besides — adds a further point

"In addition to confirmation bias, the Barnum effect explains astrology's appeal."

in addition to + noun phrase / verb-ing

The Barnum effect — a key concept for this week:

The Barnum effect (also called the Forer effect) is the psychological phenomenon where people rate general personality statements as highly accurate descriptions of themselves, without realising the same statement applies to almost everyone. Horoscopes depend on this: phrases like "You sometimes feel that others don't fully appreciate you" are true of almost all humans, yet feel remarkably personal.

"In terms of psychological mechanisms, the Barnum effect and confirmation bias together explain most of astrology's perceived accuracy."

⚠ Common errors with prepositional phrases:

✗In terms to the mechanism, I disagree.WRONG — "in terms of" not "in terms to."
✗With regard of the evidence, the claim is weak.WRONG — "with regard to" not "with regard of."
✗As a result, she opened up more. As a result of, she met him.WRONG — "as a result of" must be followed by a noun phrase. "As a result" (without "of") is used as a sentence adverbial, not before a noun phrase.
✓As a result of the reading, she met him. / She met him; as a result, she changed her mind about astrology.Correct: "as a result of" + noun phrase; "as a result" + comma as standalone connector.

VOCABULARY

Astrology & Superstition — The Full Word Set

Core vocabulary: astrology & belief

horoscopenoun
/ˈhɒrəskəʊp/
a forecast of a person's future, based on the positions of the stars and planets at the time of their birth. Usually presented as a sign-based prediction (Aries, Taurus, etc.).
"Rafael checks his horoscope every morning — Yuna considers this a charming but irrational habit."
natal chartnoun phrase
/ˈneɪtəl tʃɑːt/
a map of where all the planets were positioned at the exact moment of a person's birth. The core document of astrological interpretation — far more detailed than a sun sign horoscope.
"Priya had her natal chart read professionally — it described her Venus placement and its implications."
transitnoun / verb
/ˈtrænzɪt/
the movement of a planet through a sign of the zodiac, or past a point on an individual's natal chart. Astrologers interpret transits as symbolically significant periods.
"Venus will be transiting Capricorn throughout December — an auspicious period for Libra."
retrogradeadj / noun
/ˈretrəɡreɪd/
the apparent backward movement of a planet as observed from Earth (due to orbital mechanics). In astrology, Mercury retrograde is associated with communication and travel disruption.
"Mercury retrograde is a real astronomical phenomenon — the astrological interpretation of its effects is not."
astrologernoun
/əˈstrɒlədʒə/
a person who studies or practises astrology — interpreting planetary positions to make predictions or provide guidance. Distinct from an astronomer (who studies the physical universe scientifically).
"The astrologer told Priya that her Venus placement suggested a strong capacity for deep commitment."
adherentnoun / adj
/ədˈhɪərənt/
a person who supports or follows a particular belief, cause, or philosophy. Used for both religious and non-religious belief systems.
"Rafael describes himself as a former strict adherent who now takes a more symbolic approach."
confirmation biasnoun phrase
/ˌkɒnfəˈmeɪʃən ˈbaɪəs/
the psychological tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information in a way that confirms one's existing beliefs. A key concept in understanding why people find horoscopes convincing.
"Confirmation bias means we remember the accurate predictions and forget the many that were wrong."
falsifiableadj
/ˈfɔːlsɪfaɪəbəl/
capable of being tested and potentially proven false. Karl Popper's criterion for scientific claims — a claim is scientific only if it could, in principle, be disproven. Astrology's claims are widely considered not falsifiable.
"Rafael concedes that astrology makes no falsifiable claims — and that this matters."

Extended vocabulary: superstition & belief systems

superstitionnoun
/ˌsuːpəˈstɪʃən/
a belief or practice based on the idea that events can be influenced by specific rituals, omens, or magical thinking, without any rational basis.
"Touching wood, avoiding the number thirteen, and reading horoscopes are all forms of superstition."
the Barnum effectnoun phrase
/ˈbɑːnəm ɪˈfekt/
the tendency for people to accept vague, general statements as specifically accurate descriptions of themselves. Also called the Forer effect. Central to understanding horoscope credibility.
"The Barnum effect explains why 'You have a great need for others to like you' feels personally revealing."
sceptic / skepticnoun
/ˈskeptɪk/
a person who questions beliefs that are accepted without sufficient evidence. In the context of astrology: someone who demands empirical proof before accepting astrological claims.
"Yuna self-identifies as a sceptic — but by the end of the episode, she concedes 'slightly.'"
zodiacnoun
/ˈzəʊdiæk/
a band of sky along which the sun, moon, and planets appear to travel. Divided into twelve signs, each named after a constellation. The basis of Western astrology.
"The twelve signs of the zodiac correspond to the twelve months — Aries through Pisces."
celestialadj
/sɪˈlestiəl/
relating to the sky or outer space — stars, planets, comets. Also used metaphorically to mean heavenly or divine.
"Celestial bodies — planets and stars — move in patterns that astrologers claim are symbolically meaningful."
mysticaladj
/ˈmɪstɪkəl/
relating to a belief in forces or realities beyond human understanding or scientific explanation. Often used positively (a sense of wonder) and critically (lacking evidence).
"Rafael's mystical framing of planetary transits coexists, surprisingly, with his scientific background."
cognitive dissonancenoun phrase
/ˈkɒɡnɪtɪv ˈdɪsənəns/
the psychological discomfort caused by holding two contradictory beliefs simultaneously. Rafael — a former astrophysicist who follows astrology — is a case study in cognitive dissonance.
"Rafael's position produces cognitive dissonance: he knows astrology isn't scientific, yet finds it meaningful."
self-fulfilling prophecynoun phrase
/selfˈfʊlfɪlɪŋ ˈprɒfɪsi/
a prediction that directly or indirectly causes itself to become true, because of the belief in the prediction changing the believer's behaviour.
"Priya's horoscope may have been a self-fulfilling prophecy — believing she would meet someone made her more open."

Extended vocabulary II: pseudoscience & scientific literacy

These terms give you the precise language to analyse why astrology, homeopathy, crystal healing, and similar belief systems are considered pseudoscientific — and to do so with nuance, not dismissiveness.

pseudosciencenoun
/ˈsjuːdəʊˌsaɪəns/
a set of beliefs or practices that claim to be scientific but lack the evidence, methodology, or falsifiability required by actual science. The prefix pseudo- (Greek: false) signals the imitation without the substance. Examples: astrology, homeopathy, phrenology, crystal healing, conversion therapy.
"Astrology is frequently cited as a textbook example of pseudoscience — not because its practitioners are dishonest, but because its claims are not empirically testable."
anecdotal evidencenoun phrase
/ˌænɪkˈdəʊtəl ˈevɪdəns/
evidence based on personal stories, individual cases, or informal observations rather than systematic data collection. Compelling as narrative but scientifically weak — it cannot establish causation and is subject to confirmation bias and selective memory.
"Rafael's argument rests partly on anecdotal evidence — the accounts of his clients — which Yuna notes cannot substitute for controlled studies."
placebo effectnoun phrase
/pləˈsiːbəʊ ɪˈfekt/
a measurable, real improvement in a person's condition caused by their belief in a treatment — rather than the treatment itself. Crucial in evaluating pseudoscientific therapies: people feel better after crystal healing, but is the crystal causing it, or the belief and attention?
"The placebo effect is real and documented — which makes it genuinely difficult to dismiss practices like homeopathy on the grounds that 'nothing happens,' because something does happen."
empiricismnoun
/ɪmˈpɪrɪsɪzəm/
the philosophical position that knowledge comes primarily from sensory experience and observation — the foundation of the scientific method. An empiricist demands observable, replicable evidence before accepting a claim as knowledge.
"Yuna's empiricism leads her to demand peer-reviewed data — a standard that no astrological tradition has yet met."
peer reviewnoun phrase
/pɪə rɪˈvjuː/
the process by which scientific research is evaluated by other experts in the same field before publication, to verify its methodology and conclusions. The gold standard for scientific credibility — and the threshold that pseudoscience typically cannot clear.
"In terms of peer review, no rigorous double-blind study has found astrology to predict personality or events above chance level."
correlation ≠ causationmaxim
/ˌkɒrəˈleɪʃən ˌkɔːzˈeɪʃən/
the principle that two things occurring together (a correlation) does not mean one causes the other. A foundational concept in statistics and critical thinking. Astrological predictions that seem accurate often reflect correlation at best — the patterns people notice may share a third cause, or be coincidental.
"Yuna's core methodological objection is that correlation does not equal causation: even if people born in March do share certain traits, that doesn't mean Mars caused them."
cherry-pickingnoun / verb phrase
/ˈtʃeri pɪkɪŋ/
selectively choosing data or evidence that supports a conclusion while ignoring contradictory data. A common criticism of pseudoscientific claims and anecdotal reasoning — you count the hits and forget the misses.
"As a result of cherry-picking confirming instances, believers in astrology build a compelling but statistically misleading case for its accuracy."
extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidencemaxim (Sagan)
 
a principle associated with Carl Sagan stating that the more unusual or counter-intuitive a claim, the more robust the evidence required before it should be accepted. Widely used in sceptical discourse about pseudoscience and the paranormal.
"With regard to the claim that Mercury's position affects contract negotiations, Yuna invokes the Sagan standard: extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence."
homeopathynoun
/ˌhəʊmiˈɒpəθi/
a system of alternative medicine based on the belief that a substance that causes symptoms in healthy people can cure similar symptoms in sick people, when diluted to extreme concentrations. Considered pseudoscientific by mainstream medicine; randomised controlled trials consistently find it no more effective than placebo.
"Homeopathy is perhaps the most extensively studied pseudoscience — and the gap between its popularity and its evidence base is striking."
apophenianoun
/ˌæpəˈfiːniə/
the tendency to perceive meaningful connections or patterns in unrelated things — sometimes called patternicity. Thought to underlie many forms of magical thinking, conspiracy theories, and belief in astrology: the human brain is so good at finding patterns that it finds them even in random noise.
"Rafael's pattern-noticing could be reframed as apophenia — the brain's tendency to find signal in noise, which is adaptive in evolution but misleading in epistemology."
occam's razornoun phrase
/ˈɒkəmz ˈreɪzə/
the principle that among competing explanations, the one requiring the fewest assumptions should be preferred. Named after 14th-century philosopher William of Ockham. Applied to astrology: the simplest explanation for horoscope accuracy is the Barnum effect and confirmation bias — no planetary mechanism required.
"In light of Occam's razor, Yuna argues that the psychological explanation for astrology's appeal is far more parsimonious than any causal claim involving celestial mechanics."
double-blind trialnoun phrase
/ˈdʌbəl blaɪnd ˈtraɪəl/
a scientific experiment in which neither the participants nor the researchers know who is receiving a real treatment versus a placebo — eliminating researcher and participant bias. The methodological gold standard for testing medical and psychological claims.
"By virtue of the double-blind design, the study eliminated both expectation bias and researcher confirmation effects — and still found no astrological effect."

Answer Key

Exercises 5, 7, and Putting It Together are open-ended — review in class.

Exercise 1 — Will be -ing or will + infinitive?

1.will move (simple event) OR will be moving (if emphasising the transit as a gradual process)Both are grammatically possible. "Will move into Capricorn" = moment of entry. "Will be moving into Capricorn" = transition in progress. In astrology, the progressive is common because transits are gradual.
2.will be keepingOngoing precaution during the retrograde period — progressive emphasises the continuous action across time, not a single act of backing up.
3.will be travellingIn-progress at a specific future moment (the new moon). At that point in time, the travel will be happening — not completed.
4.will understandStative verb — "understand" cannot take progressive aspect. Use simple future: "will understand."
5.Will you be appearingPolite enquiry about plans. "Will you appear?" sounds like a direct request or invitation; "Will you be appearing?" is softer — merely asking about arrangements.

Exercise 2 — Complete the prediction

1a.will be formingGradual astronomical process over a fortnight — progressive appropriate.
1b.will be reconsideringOngoing mental process, spread across time — progressive appropriate.
2.will be navigating"Navigate a period" = extended process throughout the lunar cycle. Progressive.
3a.will be transitingVenus transiting = gradual movement through a house — in-progress across a period.
3b.won't be focusing / will not be focusingNegative future progressive. Note: "won't be focus" is wrong — needs -ing.
3c.will be drawnPassive future progressive — "attention will be drawn" (be-passive, future progressive form).

Exercise 3 — Error correction

1.The stars will be moving into a rare configuration on the winter solstice."will moving" → "will be moving." Missing "be" in the future progressive structure.
2.At exactly midnight on New Year's Day, Jupiter will be entering Aquarius."will be enter" → "will be entering." After "will be," the verb must be in -ing form.
3.I will know my birth chart by heart after this course."will be knowing" → "will know." "Know" is a stative verb — no progressive form.
4.Will you believe in astrology differently after reading Yuna's sceptic column?"will you be believe" → "will you believe." "Believe" is stative — no progressive. Also: "be believe" is grammatically impossible regardless.

Exercise 4 — Prepositional phrase gap-fill

1.In the light ofNew evidence (a new study) prompts reconsideration — "in the light of" introduces new information that changes the picture.
2.In terms ofEmpirical validity is the specific dimension being evaluated — "in terms of" introduces the frame of assessment.
3.In the context of / As a result ofBoth work: "in the context of the Barnum effect" = against the background of that phenomenon; "as a result of" = because of. Both defensible. "In the context of" is slightly more precise here.
4.With regard toIntroducing a topic for discussion — "with regard to responsible practice" shifts to the question of what astrologers should do.
5.as a result of"As a result of the reading" — the reading is the cause; the attitudinal change is the effect in the main clause.
6.on the basis of"On the basis of an astrological reading alone" — the reading is the grounds being warned against. "On the basis of" = using as evidence or justification for a decision.

Exercise 6 — Vocabulary gap-fill

1.falsifiablePopper's criterion: a claim is scientific only if it could be disproven.
2.natal chartMore detailed than a horoscope — maps all planet positions at birth.
3.Barnum effectGeneral statements feel personally specific — the mechanism behind horoscope credibility.
4.self-fulfilling prophecyBelief in the prediction changes behaviour → outcome matches prediction.
5a.sceptic / skepticYuna demands evidence before accepting any claim.
5b.retrogradeMercury retrograde = the apparent backward movement of Mercury.
6.confirmation biasWe remember hits, forget misses — makes vague systems seem accurate.

Exercise 6b — Pseudoscience vocabulary

1.cherry-pickingCounting confirming instances (helpful readings) while ignoring disconfirming ones (unhelpful ones). A core flaw in anecdotal reasoning.
2.placebo effectThe benefit is real — but it's caused by belief and attention, not the crystal itself. Important nuance: placebo effect doesn't make a practice scientific.
3.correlation ≠ causationBirth-month personality correlations might exist for many reasons — school year effects, vitamin D, parental age — with no planetary mechanism needed.
4.apopheniaThe brain finds patterns in random noise — adaptive for survival, misleading for epistemology. Explains why planetary timing seems meaningful even to trained scientists like Rafael.
5.Occam's razorThe simpler explanation (Barnum effect + confirmation bias) should be preferred over the more complex one (celestial causal mechanism). Parsimony as a methodological principle.
6.double-blind trialNeither participants nor researchers know who received the real treatment — eliminates expectation bias on both sides. The gold standard that pseudoscientific claims typically cannot meet.
7.pseudoscienceImitates science in form (predictions, patterns, categories) but fails the methodological tests: falsifiability, replication, peer review, controlled study.
8.anecdotal evidenceIndividual client testimonials — compelling as narrative, but cannot establish causation and subject to cherry-picking, Barnum effect, and confirmation bias simultaneously.
