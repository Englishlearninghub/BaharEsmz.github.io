ENGAGE

Who would do this — and why?

Before you read: think about a lie that spread

Think of a time you encountered a false story — online, in the news, or in everyday life. Before any grammar, consider:

1. How did you first come across it? Did it look credible? What made it seem true?

2. When you found out it was false — how did you reason about who might have created it and why? What language did you use when explaining your reasoning to someone else?

3. Is there a difference between an honest mistake (saying something false you believe to be true) and deliberately lying? Where is the line?

Keep these questions in mind as you read the podcast transcript below.

Background · The story the podcast investigates
CEO Marcus Hale Resigns Amid Fraud Allegations — Later Found to Be Fabricated

Fictional account · For educational purposes

In November 2024, a set of financial documents — later proven to be fabricated — began circulating on a fringe investment forum called DeepDig. The documents appeared to show that Marcus Hale, CEO of mid-sized technology firm Hale Technologies, had diverted company funds to a personal account. Within 24 hours, the story had been picked up by three mainstream financial news outlets, none of which had independently verified the documents' authenticity. Hale resigned on the second day, citing the need to "protect the company." On day four, forensic document analysts at two independent institutions confirmed the documents were forgeries. By then, the damage was done.

"The story moved faster than the truth could follow — and that is precisely the design of disinformation." — Dr. Camila Reyes, Media Studies, University of Lisbon
The Northgate Files · Episode 31 · "The Paper Trail That Wasn't" · Edited transcript
Keiko Watanabe

So let's start where we always start — with what we actually know versus what we're inferring. The documents first appeared on DeepDig on a Tuesday evening. Someone must have uploaded them carefully — the metadata was scrubbed, the account was two days old, and the formatting was professional enough to pass a first glance. This can't have been accidental. Whoever did this knew exactly what they were doing.

Dmitri Solov

I'd push back slightly. The account being two days old — that might have been a coincidence. I don't want us to assume coordination where there might have been just one person acting alone. Neither the metadata scrubbing nor the professional formatting necessarily implies a group. A single skilled person could have done all of this.

Keiko Watanabe

Fair. But I want to talk about the outlets that picked it up. Three financial news sites ran the story within 24 hours. Their editors must have known — or at least suspected — that something this explosive required independent corroboration. And yet, neither the documents nor the source were verified by any of the three newsrooms before publication. They can't have conducted any meaningful fact-check in that time frame. It's simply not possible.

Dmitri Solov

And this is where it gets uncomfortable. At some point, the editors had to bite the bullet and make a decision under competitive pressure. Every outlet was watching every other outlet. If you wait, you get scooped. The industry has been sitting on the fence about platform accountability for years — and cases like this are the direct consequence. The Hale story might have been avoided if a single editor had asked for one more day.

Keiko Watanabe

And Hale himself — he must have been terrified. To resign that quickly, he can't have felt that staying was a viable option. The board might have pressured him. His lawyers might have advised him that resignation would limit legal exposure. We don't know. But whoever designed this story understood how institutions respond to scandal — the resignation was almost certainly anticipated by the people who planted the disinformation.

Dmitri Solov

This podcast has, in a small way, broken the ice on what had become an uncomfortable industry conversation. The question nobody wanted to ask publicly is: do financial journalists understand what disinformation looks like well enough to recognise it? After the Hale case, people are finally asking it. But the reforms — they're going to need somebody to bite the bullet and push for mandatory fact-checking protocols. And so far, neither the regulator nor the industry bodies have shown any appetite for that.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the red phrases — fact or inference?

Perfect modals of deduction — noticing questions

Compare: "Someone must have uploaded them carefully." vs "The board might have pressured him."
Both are past inferences. What is the difference in certainty?

"Must have uploaded carefully" = Keiko is very confident — the evidence (scrubbed metadata, professional formatting, new account) strongly points to deliberate action. The only logical conclusion. / "Might have pressured" = Keiko is speculating — no direct evidence, only a plausible possibility. So: must have = near-certain logical deduction. Might have = possible but unconfirmed.

Look at: "They can't have conducted any meaningful fact-check in that time frame. It's simply not possible."
What is "can't have" doing — and how does it differ from Week 4's "couldn't have"?

"Can't have conducted" = Keiko deduces that this is logically impossible based on the evidence (24 hours, what a real fact-check requires). It is a logical conclusion, not a complaint about a missed opportunity. Compare to Week 4: "couldn't have" (regret/advice strand) = it wasn't possible for them to do it given their resources — neutral past inability. "Can't have" (deduction strand) = based on the evidence, I conclude it didn't happen. Same structure, different meaning — the modal strand changes everything.

Compare Week 4: "The government should have issued warnings earlier" with this week: "Someone must have uploaded this carefully." Both use modal + have + past participle. What is the fundamental difference?

Week 4 (regret/advice): SHOULD HAVE = the right thing didn't happen — this is a criticism. The speaker makes a moral/advisory judgement about what SHOULD have occurred. / Week 5 (deduction): MUST HAVE = I'm reasoning from evidence — this is what I believe happened. The speaker makes an EPISTEMIC claim (a claim about knowledge/certainty). / Same structure, completely different function — understanding the modal STRAND, not just the form, is the C1 key.

STUDY

Must have / Might have / Can't have — Deduction

Perfect Modals of Deduction (Speculation)

The same form as Week 4 (modal + have + past participle) — but a completely different purpose. This week: the speaker reasons from evidence to a conclusion about the past. This is the epistemic use of perfect modals.

Modalmust havemight havecan't have
Deduction meaningNear-certain positive deduction — evidence strongly points to this. "I'm almost sure it happened."Tentative deduction — a possibility among others. "It may have happened — I'm not sure."Near-certain negative deduction — evidence makes this impossible. "I'm almost sure it DIDN'T happen."
Evidence typeStrong, specific evidence pointing to one conclusionPartial or ambiguous evidence; multiple possibilities remainEvidence that rules something out; logical impossibility given circumstances
Certainty level~90% certain — "the only logical explanation"~40–60% — "possible but not established"~90% certain it DID NOT happen
Example (transcript)"Someone must have uploaded them carefully — the metadata was scrubbed.""The board might have pressured him to resign early.""They can't have conducted any meaningful fact-check in 24 hours."
UK/US notemust have = standard in both BrE and AmEcould have = common AmE alternative for deduction possibilitycan't have = BrE preference; couldn't have = AmE alternative for negative deduction

Form — all three:

FormStructureExample
Affirmativemust / might + have + past participle"Hale must have been told before the board meeting."
Negativecan't / couldn't + have + past participle"They can't have verified the source in 24 hours."
Passivemust / might / can't + have + been + past participle"The story must have been planted by someone with inside knowledge."
Negative of mightmight not have + past participle"The editor might not have realised the documents were forged."
Progressivemust / might / can't + have + been + -ing"Someone must have been monitoring financial news cycles carefully."

Common C1 errors:

✗They must have conduct a fact-check before publishing.WRONG — past participle required: "must have conducted." Never base form after "have."
✗Someone must uploaded the documents to cause damage.WRONG — missing "have": must HAVE uploaded. Structure: modal + HAVE + past participle.
✓The person who created the fake documents must have had detailed knowledge of how corporate financial records are formatted — the forgery was sophisticated.CORRECT — strong deduction from specific evidence (sophistication → detailed prior knowledge). Must have had = past tense of "have" as a main verb.
✓Keiko can't have spoken to Hale's lawyers — she would have mentioned it in the episode.CORRECT — negative deduction from absence of evidence. "She would have mentioned it" = the reasoning that makes "can't have spoken" logical.
CONTRAST

Week 4 vs Week 5 — Same Form, Different Meaning

The Two Strands of Perfect Modals

This is the most important C1 insight about modal grammar. The form is identical — the modal strand changes everything.

SentenceWeek 4: Regret / AdviceWeek 5: Deduction / Inference
should have"The editor should have verified the source."
= It was the right thing to do — but it didn't happen. Criticism of a failure.		(should have does NOT appear in the deduction strand — it is exclusively regret/advice)
might have"The retraction might have come sooner if they had been more cautious."
= Unrealised possibility — it was possible but didn't happen.		"The board might have pressured him to resign."
= Tentative deduction — it may have happened, based on partial evidence.
must have(must have rarely appears in the pure regret/advice strand)"Someone must have uploaded these carefully — the metadata was scrubbed."
= Near-certain deduction from evidence.
can't / couldn't have"The journalist couldn't have known — they had no access to the data."
= Neutral past inability — it wasn't possible (no blame implied).		"They can't have conducted a fact-check in 24 hours."
= Strong negative deduction — logically impossible given the time constraint.

How to identify the strand: Ask — is the speaker making a moral/advisory judgement about what should have happened (regret/advice)? Or are they reasoning from evidence to a conclusion about what did or didn't happen (deduction)? The grammatical form is identical — the speaker's intention and the discourse context make the difference.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the gold phrases — literal or figurative?

Idiomatic collocations — noticing questions

Look at: "At some point, the editors had to bite the bullet and make a decision under competitive pressure."
What does this mean literally? What does it mean in context?

Literally: to bite down on a bullet — historically, soldiers bit bullets during surgery to endure pain without anaesthetic. Figuratively: to accept a painful or unpleasant situation and act decisively, despite the difficulty or cost. Here: the editors had to make a hard, potentially costly choice (publishing without full verification) rather than wait and risk being scooped. The "pain" = professional and reputational risk. The "bullet" = the decision itself.

Look at: "The industry has been sitting on the fence about platform accountability for years."
What does this imply about the industry's position — and is Dmitri's tone positive or critical?

Literally: sitting on a fence between two properties — not committing to either side. Figuratively: refusing to commit to a position on a controversial issue; deliberately staying neutral to avoid conflict or consequences. Here: the industry has avoided taking a clear stance on whether social media platforms should be held responsible for hosting disinformation. Dmitri's tone is critical — "fence-sitting" is generally negative in English, implying cowardice or evasion of responsibility.

Look at: "This podcast has, in a small way, broken the ice on what had become an uncomfortable industry conversation."
What does Dmitri mean — and why does he qualify it with "in a small way"?

Literally: break the ice covering a frozen waterway — to clear a path. Figuratively: to do or say something that makes it easier to begin a difficult or awkward conversation; to overcome initial social or professional hesitation. Dmitri is saying the podcast started a conversation that the industry was too uncomfortable to have openly. "In a small way" is a hedge — modesty and accuracy: one podcast doesn't solve the problem, it only opens the conversation.

STUDY

Idiomatic Collocations

Bite the bullet · Sit on the fence · Break the ice

Corpus data (COCA) shows the main gap at C1 is not grammar but collocation and idiom. These three are high-frequency in journalistic, professional, and political discourse — and all three appear naturally in this week's context.

bite the bullet
/baɪt ðə ˈbʊlɪt/
Origin: military surgery (pre-anaesthetic)
To accept a painful or unpleasant situation and act decisively, despite the difficulty. Used when a hard choice can no longer be avoided.
Register: neutral to formal. Very high COCA frequency. Journalism, politics, business. Bite the bullet and [verb] is the most common pattern.
"At some point, the editors had to bite the bullet and make a decision." | "The regulator will eventually have to bite the bullet and impose mandatory standards." | "She finally bit the bullet and retracted the story."
sit on the fence
/sɪt ɒn ðə fens/
Origin: literal fence between two properties
To deliberately avoid committing to a position on a controversial issue; to stay neutral when a decision is expected. Usually carries a negative connotation — implies cowardice or evasion.
Register: neutral to slightly informal. Political and journalistic commentary. Noun: fence-sitter / fence-sitting.
"The industry has been sitting on the fence about platform accountability for years." | "You can't sit on the fence on this — a decision is required." | "His fence-sitting damaged his credibility with both sides."
break the ice
/breɪk ðə aɪs/
Origin: icebreaker ships clearing frozen waterways
To say or do something that makes a difficult, awkward, or formal situation easier to begin; to initiate a previously avoided conversation.
Register: neutral to slightly informal. Social, professional, political. Noun: icebreaker (activity / first question). Adj: ice-breaking.
"This podcast has broken the ice on an uncomfortable conversation." | "The moderator used a short video to break the ice before the difficult discussion." | "Nobody raised it first — someone had to break the ice."

Key collocation patterns — grammatical behaviour:

IdiomCommon patternsAvoid
bite the bullethave to / need to / finally / eventually bite the bullet; bit the bullet (past); bite the bullet and [verb]✗ "bite a bullet" (always "the") ✗ "bited" (irregular: bite → bit → bitten)
sit on the fencekeep / stop sitting on the fence; fence-sitting (noun); fence-sitter (person); can't afford to sit on the fence✗ "sit on a fence" ✗ "sitting in the fence"
break the icebreak the ice on [topic]; finally break the ice; icebreaker (n.); break the ice between [people]✗ "break an ice" ✗ "breaking ice" (needs "the")

Connected expressions (expanding your repertoire):

grasp the nettle (BrE) ≈ bite the bullet take the plunge = commit to a difficult decision fence-sitter / fence-sitting (noun forms) come off the fence = finally take a position icebreaker = activity / question to start a conversation open a can of worms = start a complicated difficult discussion

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Teal phrases — neither…nor in a new context

Neither…nor — review with new examples

Find both uses of neither…nor in the transcript. For each, what are the two things being excluded — and why does the speaker choose this structure rather than "and…not"?

"Neither the metadata scrubbing nor the professional formatting necessarily implies a group." — Dmitri is pushing back: both pieces of evidence could be achieved by one skilled person. Neither one alone, nor both together, proves coordination. / "Neither the documents nor the source were verified by any of the three newsrooms." — Keiko emphasises: two things that should have been verified (documents, source) — and neither was. Neither…nor makes this more emphatic and formal than "the documents and the source were not verified."

Look at: "Neither the regulator nor the industry bodies have shown any appetite for that."
What is the verb form — and which subject does it agree with? Why?

"Have" is plural. The proximity rule: verb agrees with the nearer subject. "Industry bodies" (plural) is closer to the verb than "the regulator" (singular) → "have." If reversed: "Neither the industry bodies nor the regulator has shown…" — "has" agrees with "the regulator" (singular, now nearer).

REVIEW

Neither…nor — applied to media analysis

Neither…nor — focused review

You studied neither…nor in depth in Week 4. This week's context gives you a new domain to use it in — media analysis and investigative journalism. The key rules remain: exclusion · proximity agreement · parallel structure · no double negatives.

RuleCorrectIncorrect (and why)
Meaning: excludes both"Neither the editor nor the fact-checker caught it."N/A — the meaning itself is correct
Proximity agreement"Neither the journalists nor the editor was available." (editor = singular → was)✗ "Neither the journalists nor the editor were available." (ignores proximity)
Parallel structure"Neither verifying the source nor checking the metadata was done." (gerund + gerund)✗ "Neither verifying the source nor the metadata was checked." (gerund ≠ passive clause)
No double negative"Neither the outlet nor the platform responded."✗ "Neither the outlet nor the platform didn't respond." (double negative = wrong)

New C1 uses in this week's context:

✓"Neither the speed of the story's spread nor the sophistication of the forgery was consistent with an amateur operation — the commission concluded it was coordinated."Complex noun phrases as subjects. "Was" agrees with "the sophistication" (singular, nearer). High analytical register.
✓"Neither sitting on the fence nor biting the bullet is a sustainable long-term strategy — the industry must adopt clear standards."Idioms as subjects within neither…nor. "Is" agrees with "biting the bullet" (singular, nearer). Two grammar points from this week combining in one sentence.
VOCABULARY

Lying & Disinformation

Core vocabulary: the anatomy of a lie

misinformationnoun
/ˌmɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
False or inaccurate information — regardless of intent. The person spreading it may genuinely believe it. Compare to disinformation (which requires deliberate intent). The mis- prefix = wrong, not necessarily intentional.
"The three outlets spread misinformation — not disinformation — because they believed the documents were genuine."
disinformationnoun
/dɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
False information deliberately created and spread to deceive. The intent to mislead is definitional — without it, it's misinformation. Disinformation is a tool of political, financial, and corporate manipulation.
"Whoever forged the Hale documents was engaged in disinformation — the deception was systematic and intentional."
fabricationnoun
/ˌfæbrɪˈkeɪʃən/
Something invented with the intention of deceiving — entirely made up (unlike a distortion, which is based in fact but twisted). Verb: to fabricate. Adjective: fabricated.
"The forensic analysis confirmed the financial documents were a fabrication — not based on any real data."
deepfakenoun
/ˈdiːpfeɪk/
AI-generated synthetic media — video, audio, or images — depicting a real person saying or doing something they never said or did. Named from "deep learning" + "fake." Increasingly powerful and difficult to detect.
"The viral video of Hale 'confessing' was later identified as a deepfake — lip movements didn't match the audio perfectly."
propagandanoun
/ˌprɒpəˈɡændə/
Information — often biased or misleading — used to promote a political cause or viewpoint. Propaganda doesn't have to be false (unlike disinformation) — it can be selective truth. Intent is to influence, not to inform.
"State-sponsored propaganda relies on the same psychological mechanisms as disinformation: repetition, emotional appeal, and confirmation bias."
gaslightingnoun / verb
/ˈɡæzlaɪtɪŋ/
Psychological manipulation in which the perpetrator causes the target to question their own memory or perception of reality. In media contexts: when institutions deny documented events and insist the affected person is mistaken. From the 1944 film Gaslight.
"Hale's initial statement — 'there is no story here, only rumour' — was seen by some as gaslighting a public that had already read the documents."
astroturfingnoun
/ˈæstrəʊtɜːfɪŋ/
Masking the true origin of a campaign, making it appear to be a spontaneous grassroots initiative when it is professionally organised and funded. Named after AstroTurf (artificial grass) — fake grassroots.
"The flood of supportive comments under the DeepDig post in the first two hours suggested astroturfing — no organic post generates that pattern."
echo chambernoun phrase
/ˈekəʊ ˌtʃeɪmbə/
A social or media environment in which people are exposed only to information that reinforces existing beliefs — because algorithms or social networks filter out contradictory views. Echo chambers amplify disinformation because false information goes unchallenged.
"DeepDig functioned as an echo chamber — its users were already predisposed to believe executives were corrupt."

VOCABULARY

Media Literacy

Extended vocabulary: how to evaluate information

fact-checkingnoun / verb
/ˈfækt ˌtʃekɪŋ/
Verifying factual claims before publication or broadcast — cross-referencing primary sources, documents, and expert testimony. Fact-check (verb). Fact-checker (person). Distinct from editorial judgement (opinion-based decisions).
"None of the three newsrooms had conducted a meaningful fact-check of the documents before publication."
primary sourcenoun phrase
/ˈpraɪmeri sɔːs/
Original, first-hand evidence — a document, recording, or testimony that comes directly from the source rather than through an intermediary. Compare to secondary source (someone reporting what a primary source said).
"In the Hale case, the documents were treated as primary sources — without anyone verifying their provenance or authenticity."
corroborationnoun
/kəˌrɒbəˈreɪʃən/
Confirmation of a claim from a second, independent source — the traditional gold standard of investigative journalism. Verb: to corroborate. Adjective: corroborated / uncorroborated.
"The most glaring failure was the absence of corroboration — no outlet checked whether any independent source confirmed the documents."
editorial independencenoun phrase
/ˌedɪˈtɔːriəl ɪndɪˈpendəns/
The principle that journalistic decisions should be made by editors on the basis of journalistic standards, free from political, commercial, or personal pressure. Opposite: editorial capture.
"The competitive pressure to publish first directly compromised editorial independence — business interests overrode journalistic standards."
clickbaitnoun
/ˈklɪkbeɪt/
Online content designed to attract clicks through sensational or misleading headlines rather than to inform. The incentive structure of digital advertising rewards clicks, not accuracy.
"A fabricated story about a tech CEO accused of fraud is perfect clickbait — it has every element that generates high engagement: wealth, crime, and betrayal."
dog whistlenoun
/ˈdɒɡ wɪsəl/
A political or rhetorical message with an overt meaning for a general audience but a coded message for a specific target group. Named after the fact that dogs hear frequencies humans cannot — allows a communicator to signal positions while maintaining plausible deniability.
"Some analysts argued the story was a dog whistle — framed as fraud news for general audiences but understood in certain circles as an attack on Hale's political associations."
platform accountabilitynoun phrase
/ˈplætfɔːm əˌkaʊntəˈbɪlɪti/
The principle that social media platforms bear responsibility for the content they host — particularly harmful or false content. The central debate: should platforms be neutral conduits (like telephone companies) or publishers (responsible for what they distribute)?
"The industry has been sitting on the fence about platform accountability — and the Hale case shows why neutrality is no longer defensible."
post-truthadjective / noun
/ˌpəʊst ˈtruːθ/
Relating to circumstances in which objective facts are less influential in shaping opinion than appeals to emotion and personal belief. Oxford Dictionaries Word of the Year 2016. A post-truth environment normalises the irrelevance of the distinction between truth and falsehood.
"Disinformation campaigns thrive in a post-truth environment — where the goal is not to persuade people of a specific lie, but to make truth itself feel contested."

Answer Key

Exercises 2, 5, 7, and Putting It Together are open-ended — model answers below. Bring all open-ended work to class.

Exercise 1 — Choose the modal

1.must haveStrong deduction: exact font + margin specifications = specific enough evidence to conclude near-certainty of insider access. The only logical explanation.
2.can't haveNegative deduction from logical impossibility: rendering time makes solo creation in 12 hours impossible. "Can't have worked alone" = I deduce this is impossible.
3.might haveTentative deduction: "not been confirmed" = partial evidence, possible among several explanations. Might have = one possibility, not the only one.
4.must haveStrong deduction: confirmed testimony (told within 30 min + immediate lawyer call) = near-certain prior knowledge before the board meeting.
5.might haveTentative deduction: "can't rule it out" explicitly signals we must not use must have or can't have. The ambiguity requires the hedged form.

Exercise 2 — Model answers

1.Model"The forger must have had access to Hale Technologies' internal document templates — possibly as a current or former employee." / "They must have studied the formatting in detail over a significant period."
2.Model"The editors can't have conducted a serious fact-check in under 24 hours — corroboration alone typically requires 48–72 hours for a story of this complexity." / "No meaningful fact-check can have been completed in that timeframe."
3.Model"The story might have been deliberately seeded across multiple platforms simultaneously to prevent any single outlet from being identified as the origin." / "The pattern of amplification suggests the story might have been coordinated by a team with professional experience."

Exercise 3 — Deduction or regret?

1.R (Regret)"Should have waited" = criticism of what didn't happen; it was the right thing to do. Moral/advisory judgement. Exclusively in the regret/advice strand.
2.D (Deduction)"Must have felt enormous pressure" = reasoning from evidence (speed of resignation, significance of the decision). Speaker deduces Hale's emotional state from observable behaviour.
3.R — borderline"Could have caught" = Week 4 unrealised possibility / regret. They had the capacity to catch it but didn't — criticism of a missed opportunity. NOTE: in a deduction context, "could have caught" would mean "it was possible they caught it" — the strand depends entirely on whether the speaker is criticising (R) or inferring (D).
4.D (Deduction)"Can't have been created" = strong negative deduction from evidence. Speaker reasons to a conclusion: sophistication → insider knowledge required → outsider impossible.
5.D (Deduction)"Might have been watching" = tentative past speculation — inferring a prior behaviour pattern from the timing and sophistication of the attack. No moral judgement — purely epistemic.

Exercise 4 — Idiom gap-fill

1.bite the bulletDifficult, unavoidable action — introducing mandatory regulation despite industry resistance. "Finally bite the bullet" is the standard collocational pattern here.
2.sit on the fenceRefusing to commit — "all options needed further study" = diplomatic non-answer. "Continued to sit on the fence" emphasises ongoing evasion.
3.broken the icePresent perfect past participle after "has." The Hale case initiated a previously avoided conversation — a one-time event with present relevance.
4.sit on the fence"Can no longer afford to sit on the fence" = the situation has become urgent enough that neutrality is no longer viable.
5.broke the iceSimple past — the act of asking the editor directly was the specific moment the ice broke. A completed action, not an ongoing state.

Exercise 6 — Vocabulary

1.astroturfingOrganised paid accounts creating the appearance of organic grassroots support = the definition of astroturfing. Not "propaganda" (which lacks the fake-grassroots element).
2.misinformationNot disinformation: the journalist believed the study was valid. Absent intent → misinformation, not disinformation.
3a.fabricationThe documents themselves (entirely invented false records) = a fabrication.
3b.disinformationThe broader campaign (creating and planting the fabrications) = disinformation — deliberate, systematic deception.
4.corroboration"Independent corroboration" = a second independent source confirming the claim. The journalistic gold standard absent here.
5.post-truthA post-truth environment = emotional resonance and ideological consistency matter more than factual accuracy. Defined by the diminishing influence of objective facts on public opinion.
