The Climbing Wall: Can You Reach It?

Can / can't / could · polite requests · body parts & ability vocabulary

📍 This week's story: Tom and Maya are having so much fun that they decide to try the Riverside Community Centre's "Taster Day" together. First stop: the indoor climbing wall. Neither of them has climbed before, so they need to talk about what they can and can't do — and ask each other, and the instructor, for help. Read what happens at the wall before we name any grammar.

1Context First: Read Before Any Rule

Read the Taster Day poster, then the conversation between Tom, Maya and their instructor at the climbing wall. Just try to understand what is happening — the grammar comes later.

Poster

Riverside Community Centre — Taster Day!

Saturday, all activities free for first-time visitors

Come and try something new this Saturday. No experience needed — our instructors will help you every step of the way.

  • Climbing Wall — You can try all levels, from beginner to advanced. Comfortable shoes required.
  • Dance Studio — Beginners can join the 11 a.m. class; no partner needed.

Conversation
Instructor
OK, Maya, put your foot on that yellow hold. Can you reach it?
Maya
Yes, I can! But I can't reach the next one — my arms are too short!
Instructor
No problem. Could you try the hold on the left instead?
Maya
OK... yes, that's better!
Tom
Maya, could you pass me the chalk bag, please? My hands are sweating.
Maya
Sure! Here you go.
Tom
Thanks. I can climb quite well, actually — I used to do it a lot as a teenager.
Instructor
Great! Tom, could you help Maya find her next foothold? She can't quite see it from there.

Notice: "can" and "can't" talk about ability — what someone is able or unable to do. "Could" is used differently here — it makes a request sound more polite. All three words appear naturally, side by side, in this one short conversation.

3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Can / Can't: Form & Use (Ability)

FormExample
subject + can + verb (no "to")I can climb quite well.
subject + can't + verbI can't reach the next hold.
question: Can + subject + verb?Can you reach it?

We use can to say that someone has the ability to do something, and can't to say they do not have that ability. The form never changes — no "-s" for he/she/it, and never add "to" after can/can't.

⚠️ Important: Modal Verbs Never Change Form

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
She cans climb well.She can climb well.
I can to reach it.I can reach it. (no "to")

3.2 — Could: A Polite Way to Ask

In the conversation, "could" is not about ability or the past — it is simply a more polite way of asking someone to do something. Compare these two ways of asking the same thing:

Direct requestMore polite request
Can you pass me the chalk bag?Could you pass me the chalk bag, please?
Can you help me?Could you help me, please?
The politeness scale: Can → Could
Both are correct and common. "Could" simply sounds softer and more polite — useful with an instructor, a stranger, or someone older than you.
UseExample
Polite requestCould you help Maya find her foothold?
Ability (present)I can climb quite well.
Inability (present)She can't quite see it from there.

You may also see "could" used for past ability (e.g. "When I was a teenager, I could climb trees easily"). This is a different use from the polite request above — we will look at this more closely in a later grade. For now, focus on could as a polite request word.

6Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "She cans climb very well."
    Incorrect — modal verbs never take "-s": "She can climb very well."
  2. "I can to reach the top."
    Incorrect — never add "to" after can/can't: "I can reach the top."
  3. "Could you help me with this knot, please?"
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Could" here is a polite request, correctly formed with the subject after it. Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "You can help me?"
    Incorrect word order for a question — the modal comes before the subject: "Can you help me?"
  5. "He can't to see the hold from there."
    Incorrect — no "to" after can't: "He can't see the hold from there."

8Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

Use these tasks to notice this week's language in real English before your next class.

  • Body idioms: Search online for 5 English idioms that use body parts (for example, "cost an arm and a leg", "break a leg", "keep an eye on"). Write down what each one means.
  • Politeness check: Watch a short customer-service video in English (a hotel check-in, a café order). Count how many times staff use "could" instead of "can" — what does that tell you about formal service language?
  • Real taster days: Search for a real community centre, gym, or sports club near you online. Find an activity you could try, and write 2 sentences about what you can and can't currently do in that activity.
  • Listening hunt: Watch a 1-2 minute video of a climbing, dance, or sports lesson in English. Note down two examples of "can/can't" for ability and one polite request with "could".
  • Speaking challenge: Practise asking a friend or family member for something using "could" instead of your usual way of asking. Notice how it changes the tone.
