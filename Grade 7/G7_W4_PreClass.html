Fix the festival announcement

Before any grammar: something is wrong

The text below is a fictional festival announcement that has been deliberately written with five errors — mostly in the relative clauses and connective language. Click each underlined phrase you think is wrong, then click Reveal all fixes to see the corrections. Don’t worry if you’re not sure yet — this is a discovery task, not a test.

Harrowfield Festival — Volunteer Briefing

Welcome to Harrowfield Summer Festival, that is one of the largest outdoor events in the region. You are volunteering this weekend as part of a team who's members have all been trained in crowd management.

The main stage, that was built only three years ago, is supposed to open at 6pm. There will be a safety briefing for volunteers, which it will be held in Tent B at 5pm. The festival organisers, which have arranged events like this for over a decade, ask that all team members arrive by 4:30.

Food vendors are supposed to keep their areas clean at all times. As a result, any vendor whose area is found untidy will receive a formal warning.

Click the underlined phrases you think are wrong.

Error 1: “that is one of the largest” → “which is one of the largest” — non-defining relative clauses use which, never that.

Error 2: “who’s members” → “whose members” — who’s = who is; possessive relative pronoun is whose.

Error 3: “that was built” → “which was built” — same rule: non-defining clauses need which.

Error 4: “which it will be held” → “which will be held” — the relative pronoun is already the subject; don’t add a second subject pronoun.

Error 5: “which have arranged” → “who have arranged” — use who for people, which for things.

What this tells us

The five errors above cover the key confusions in relative clauses at B2 level: that vs which in non-defining clauses, who’s vs whose, who vs which for people vs things, and the doubled-subject trap. These are exactly what you’ll study this week.

By Clara Voss · Arts correspondent

Harrowfield Summer Festival, which celebrated its twentieth anniversary this year, drew a record crowd of 45,000 visitors over three days. The event, which was founded by two local musicians in a field outside the town, has grown into one of the most anticipated weekends in the regional calendar.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Four types of clause — what do you notice?

Detective work: commas and clauses

Compare: “a main stage that can accommodate up to 12,000 spectators” vs “The event, which was founded by two musicians, has grown into…” Both are relative clauses, but only one has commas. What is different about what each clause does in the sentence? Could you remove either clause without losing the core meaning?

The first clause (that can accommodate) tells you which stage — without it, you don't know which one. It is essential and cannot be removed. The second clause (which was founded) adds extra information about something already identified. Remove it and the sentence still works: "The event has grown into…" The commas signal the clause is optional extra information. This is the defining/non-defining distinction.

The article uses three different relative pronouns: that, which, who, and whose. Look at what each one refers to. Can you identify the pattern for when each pronoun is used?

Who refers to people. Which refers to things, organisations, or events. That can refer to both — but only in defining clauses (no commas). Whose shows possession — it replaces "his/her/its/their" in the clause and can refer to people or things.

The director says: “All vendors were supposed to be briefed by noon.” The vendor says: “I was supposed to receive a written copy.” Neither briefing happened. What does supposed to communicate here that should or was expected to doesn’t fully capture?

Supposed to implies a specific obligation or arrangement that was planned but not fulfilled. "Was supposed to" always implies something didn't happen — there's a sense of failure or unmet expectation. "Should have" also implies this, but "was supposed to" specifically references a pre-existing plan or agreement, not just general moral duty.

STUDY

1. Defining relative clauses

Defining relative clauses: essential information

A defining clause identifies which person or thing is being talked about. Remove it and the sentence loses its precise meaning. No commas.

Defining — no commas

The stage that can hold 12,000 people opens at 6pm.

Remove the clause: “The stage opens at 6pm.” — Which stage? We don’t know.

The vendor who was found in violation was asked to leave.

The clause identifies which vendor specifically.

Non-defining — always has commas

The festival, which celebrated its twentieth year, drew 45,000 visitors.

Remove: “The festival drew 45,000 visitors.” — still makes complete sense.

Ama Osei, who founded the event, took responsibility.

Ama is already named — the clause adds extra, non-essential detail.

Refers toDefining pronounExample
A personwho / thatThe volunteer who arrived late missed the briefing.
A thing / event / organisationwhich / thatThe tent that collapsed has been removed.
A placewhereThe field where the market is held can hold 500 stalls.
A timewhenFriday when the gates open is usually the quietest day.

Object relative clauses: the pronoun can be omitted

When the relative pronoun is the object of the clause (not the subject), it can be dropped in informal English:

✓ The singer that the crowd loved most was the last act. (pronoun = object, can drop) → "The singer the crowd loved most" is also correct.
✓ The singer who performed last received a standing ovation. (pronoun = subject, cannot drop) → "The singer performed last" loses the relative clause entirely — wrong.
STUDY

2. Non-defining relative clauses

Non-defining relative clauses: extra information

Adds background information about something already identified. Always enclosed in commas. Never uses that — only who, which, whose, where, when.

Always use commas Never use that Use who (people) / which (things) Can refer to a whole preceding clause
✓ The headliner, who played for ninety minutes, was the highlight of the festival. Non-defining — the headliner is already identified. Commas essential. Found in: article, paragraph 2
✓ The festival sold out in three hours, which surprised even the organisers. Which can refer to the entire previous clause, not just a noun.
✗ The festival, that celebrates its twentieth year, drew record crowds. Non-defining clauses never use that. Correct: which celebrates its twentieth year.
✗ Ama Osei, who it took responsibility, apologised publicly. Don’t add a second subject pronoun (it). The relative pronoun who is already the subject.
STUDY

3. Whose — the possessive relative

Whose: showing possession in relative clauses

Whose is the possessive relative pronoun — it replaces his / her / its / their in the relative clause. It can refer to people and things. It is one of the most avoided and most misused structures at B2 level.

Two sentences → one with whose

The headliner performed a long set. Her album is at number one.

→ The headliner, whose album is at number one, performed a long set.

The vendor was asked to leave. His stall was near the east entrance.

→ The vendor whose stall was near the east entrance was asked to leave.

⚠ whose vs who’s

Whose = possessive relative pronoun

The team whose equipment failed was not at fault.

Who’s = who is (contraction)

The volunteer who’s running late needs to call ahead.

These sound identical in speech — context and grammar decide the spelling.

Whose for things (often overlooked)

✓ The council, whose transport committee met last week, will publish a report. Whose refers to the council (an organisation/thing), not a person. This is perfectly correct.
✓ The tent, whose roof had been damaged in the storm, had to be closed. Whose replaces its — referring to the tent. Correct and natural.
! The tent of which the roof had been damaged had to be closed. This is the formal/written alternative to whose for things. You may see it in formal documents but whose is more common and preferred in most B2 writing.
STUDY

4. Supposed to: obligation, expectation & failure

Supposed to: three uses

Supposed to is one of the most useful and natural ways to express obligation, expectation, or the fact that something was planned but didn’t happen. It often implies a degree of responsibility or accountability.

UseFormWhat it meansExample
Current obligation/expectation is/are supposed to + base verb There is a rule, plan, or expectation that this should happen Vendors are supposed to keep their areas clean.
Prohibition is/are not supposed to + base verb It is against the rules or expectation Volunteers are not supposed to handle cash directly.
Past failure / unmet plan was/were supposed to + base verb Something was planned or expected but did NOT happen I was supposed to receive the checklist. (But I didn’t.)
✓ The gates were supposed to open at 5pm, but there was a delay. Past plan not fulfilled — the gates didn’t open at 5pm. Found in: article, paragraph 3 (adapted)
✓ You are not supposed to bring glass bottles into the festival site. Clear prohibition — it’s against the rules.

Supposed to vs Should: the key difference

Should expresses the speaker’s opinion about what is right or ideal. Supposed to refers to an external rule, plan, or arrangement — what was agreed, expected, or required.

You should arrive early = it would be a good idea (my opinion). You are supposed to arrive by 4:30 = the organisers require it (external rule).

CONTEXT

Read next: the incident report

This is a fictional internal incident report from the festival. Read for meaning: what were the two distinct problems that led to the incident, and who was responsible for each?

HARROWFIELD FESTIVAL — Internal Incident Report Ref: HF-2024-08 · Confidential

Incident summary: A crowd barrier in the east zone collapsed at approximately 21:40 on Saturday. Three attendees sustained minor injuries. No hospitalisations occurred.

Contributing factors: The barriers, whose structural integrity had not been assessed since the previous festival, were found to be below the required load standard. As a result, the weight of the crowd in the east zone exceeded what the barriers were rated for.

The safety officer who was responsible for equipment checks confirmed that an inspection was scheduled for Friday morning. On the other hand, documentary evidence suggests the inspection was recorded as complete at a time when the officer was confirmed to be off-site. This represents a serious record-keeping failure.

The barriers that were removed after the incident will not be used at future events. Replacements, which have already been ordered from a certified supplier, will arrive within six weeks. In contrast to the previous procurement process, all new equipment will require a third-party inspection certificate before installation.

The festival team whose communication with contractors was found to be inadequate has been asked to review its protocols. Consequently, a new line of reporting has been established between the safety officer and the festival director. All future equipment suppliers are supposed to provide a full structural report no later than four weeks before the event. Suppliers are not supposed to begin installation until the report has been reviewed and signed off.

Quick meaning check

1. What was wrong with the barriers, according to the report?

2. What is suspicious about the inspection record?

3. What new rule applies to all future equipment suppliers?

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Discourse connectors — cause, contrast, and consequence

Detective work: what job does each connector do?

The report contains: “the barriers were below standard. As a result, the crowd weight exceeded the rating.” And: “a new line of reporting has been established. Consequently, all suppliers must provide a structural report.” Both connect two ideas. Are they expressing the same relationship? Could you swap them without any change in meaning?

Yes — as a result and consequently both express consequence (cause → effect). They are largely interchangeable in meaning, though consequently is slightly more formal. Both come at the start of the new sentence and are followed by a comma.

The report also says: “On the other hand, evidence suggests the inspection was false.” And: “In contrast to the previous procurement process, all new equipment will require certification.” These both express contrast — but what grammatical difference follows each one?

On the other hand is followed by a full clause (subject + verb). It presents a second perspective or counterpoint. In contrast can also be followed by a full clause — but it frequently introduces a prepositional phrase (In contrast to the previous process) before the main clause. The key difference is that in contrast to directly compares two specific things, while on the other hand simply shifts to a second viewpoint.

STUDY

5. Discourse coherence devices

Linking ideas across sentences and paragraphs

These connectors signal the logical relationship between ideas. They are essential in formal writing — corpus research confirms B2 learners who use them correctly are rated as significantly more coherent writers.

as a resultconsequence
Starts a new sentence or clause. Signals that what follows is caused by what came before.
The inspection was missed. As a result, safety was compromised.
consequentlyconsequence (formal)
Slightly more formal than as a result. Often used in reports, official documents, and academic writing.
The record was falsified. Consequently, the officer has been suspended.
on the other handcontrast / counterpoint
Presents a second, contrasting perspective or argument. Always followed by a full clause.
Visitor numbers were up. On the other hand, traffic problems have worsened.
in contrast (to)contrast / comparison
Directly compares two specific things. Can be followed by a noun phrase (in contrast to last year) or a clause.
In contrast to last year’s event, this festival was tightly managed.

Common placement errors

✗ The festival was successful, as a result the organisers were pleased. These connectors join separate sentences, not clauses within one sentence. Fix: The festival was successful. As a result, the organisers were pleased. Or use so within a single sentence.
✗ In contrast, last year's event was chaotic. (when no specific contrast has been established) In contrast must refer to something specific that came before. Make the comparison explicit: This year was smoothly run. In contrast, last year’s event was chaotic.

How they fit together

Think of these four devices in two pairs: as a result / consequently = cause-and-effect; on the other hand / in contrast (to) = contrast. Knowing which pair to reach for — and then choosing between the two in each pair based on register — is what moves writing from B1 to B2.

DIALOGUE

Hear it in conversation

Two festival volunteers talk at the end of their shift. Notice how naturally relative clauses, whose, supposed to, and discourse connectors appear in everyday speech.

Dialogue Two volunteers after their shift — Harrowfield Festival
SA
Sara

What a day. Did you hear about the barrier thing?

MI
Miguel

Yeah, one of the guys in my zone who was working near the east entrance told me. Apparently the barriers, which weren’t checked properly, gave way. Three people got hurt.

SA
Sara

That’s awful. The safety officer whose job it was to check those is going to be in serious trouble. He was supposed to sign off the whole inspection by noon.

MI
Miguel

To be fair, he was supposed to have a full team for that, but two people didn’t show up. On the other hand, that doesn’t explain how the inspection got recorded as complete when he was apparently off-site.

SA
Sara

Exactly. As a result of all this, they’ve already announced changes for next year. Suppliers are supposed to send a structural report four weeks before the event now.

MI
Miguel

That’s a good change. The people who make these decisions really are not supposed to wait until something goes wrong before they act. In contrast to the way they handled last year’s issues, though, at least they’re being transparent about it now.

Dialogue analysis — what to notice:

  • Defining RC: “the guy who was working near the entrance” — identifies which person (no commas).
  • Non-defining RC: “the barriers, which weren’t checked, gave way” — commas, adds information about barriers already identified.
  • Whose: “the officer whose job it was to check” — possessive, replaces “his job.”
  • Supposed to appears in three forms: past failure (was supposed to sign off), obligation (are supposed to send), prohibition (are not supposed to wait).
  • Discourse connectors in natural speech: on the other hand introduces a counterpoint; as a result connects cause to effect; in contrast to directly compares this year to last.
PRONUNCIATION

Reducing relative clauses in speech

Spoken English: two reduction patterns

In fast connected speech, relative clauses often sound very different from their written form. Two patterns are worth knowing.

Written formNatural spoken formWhat reduces
the barrier that was checkedthe barrier checked /ðə ˈbæriə tʃɛkt/Reduced relative: that was dropped entirely
the person who is responsiblethe person responsibleAdjective phrase replaces relative clause
supposed to/səˈpəʊstə/ — “suh-pohz-tuh”to reduces to /tə/; final /d/ in “supposed” merges with /t/

The third pattern is the most important: learners often say /səˈpəʊzd tuː/ (both syllables full) — but native speakers say /səˈpəʊstə/ in normal pace. Practise the reduced form.

SPEAKING PRACTICE

Who, which, whose — spoken

After the incident at Harrowfield, everyone had to be precise: the barrier that failed, the vendor whose stall was damaged, the crew who were supposed to check it. Relative clauses are how speech points at exactly the right thing. Voice memo app ready.

Roleplay scenario

The investigator and the organiser

Role A — Safety investigator: Interview the festival organiser about the incident. Every question should pin something down with a relative clause: "Who was the contractor that installed the barrier? The staff who were supposed to inspect it — where were they?"

Role B — Organiser: Answer carefully. Use supposed to for what should have happened: "The checks were supposed to be done on Friday… the crew whose job that was had been reassigned."

How: With a partner, or both roles yourself; record 3–4 minutes. When you listen back, check: who for people, which/that for things, whose for possession — and no doubled pronoun ("the man who he saw" is the error to hunt).
Retell task

The incident, in one minute

Re-read the incident report in the Context section. Close it. Retell what happened at Harrowfield in 60 seconds, from memory, for someone who wasn't there. You'll need relative clauses to identify people and things without names — and this week's coherence devices (as a result, meanwhile, following this) to keep the sequence clear.

How: Record once, listen, re-record tighter. When you listen back, check: could a stranger follow who did what? If any sentence needs a second listen, it needs a relative clause or a connector.
Sentence challenge

Six terms, sixty seconds each

Use each of these items from this week's vocabulary in a spoken sentence — about Harrowfield or any real event you've attended. 60 seconds per item:

  • a vendor
  • crowd management
  • to sustain an injury
  • negligence
  • a protocol
  • to comply (with)
How: One continuous recording, timer on. When you listen back, check: at least two sentences should contain a relative clause — if none do, redo two: "the protocol that everyone ignored…"

Technique: the spoken relative clause

  • In speech, defining clauses run straight on with no pause: "the barrier-that-collapsed" is one breath. Non-defining clauses get a real pause where the commas are: "The headliner — who arrived late anyway — refused to comment." The pause IS the comma.
  • Supposed to is pronounced /səˈpoʊstə/ — the d vanishes. Say it five times fast.
  • Dropping the pronoun is natural where grammar allows: "the stall (that) he runs". Try both versions aloud and keep the one that flows.
