1Context First: Two People, One Morning

Read Priya's diary entry. Then read the short interview with Marcus. Both texts talk about the same race morning. First, just focus on the meaning — what was happening, and what happened. We will look at the grammar after that.

Diary Entry

Sunday, 7:40 a.m. — Race Day

Priya's personal diary

I got to the start line at 7 a.m. The sun was rising slowly over the river, and hundreds of runners were stretching and chatting nervously. Some volunteers were still setting up the water stations when the announcer suddenly shouted "Two minutes!"

At 7:15, the gun fired and everyone started running. I lost sight of Marcus almost immediately, but I found a good spot near kilometre 15 to watch. While I was waiting there, it started to drizzle, so I put my hood up. A few minutes later, I saw Elena running past — she looked strong, although she was clearly tired.

The best moment was at the finish line. While the crowd was cheering as loudly as possible, Marcus suddenly appeared around the corner and crossed the line in 1:28:40! However, the real surprise came twenty minutes later, when Sam finished third because he had paced himself perfectly the whole way.

Interview Transcript
City Sports Reporter
Congratulations, Marcus! Talk us through the race. What was going through your mind at the final kilometre?
Marcus
Honestly, my legs were burning, but I just kept telling myself to hold the pace. When I saw the finish line, I couldn't believe it was finally there.
City Sports Reporter
What was happening around you during the race — did the weather affect you?
Marcus
It started raining lightly around kilometre 15, while I was running through the park. It actually helped — I was overheating a little before that.
City Sports Reporter
Is this your best half-marathon time?
Marcus
Yes — I've never run this distance so fast before. This is actually the first time I've broken 1:30.

Notice: Priya's diary is written, so it feels calm and personal. The interview is spoken, so it feels more informal. But both texts mix two things: actions that were "in progress", and actions that just happened. That difference is this week's main grammar point.

3Grammar Focus: Describing the Past

3.1 — Past Progressive: Form

SubjectFormExample
I / he / she / itwas + verb-ingI was waiting near kilometre 15.
you / we / theywere + verb-ingThe runners were stretching before the race.
negativewasn't / weren't + verb-ingShe wasn't watching when Marcus crossed the line.
questionWas/Were + subject + verb-ing?Were you waiting long?

3.2 — Past Progressive vs. Simple Past: Use

We use the past progressive for an action that was already in progress. It is like the "background" of the story. We use the simple past for a shorter, finished action — one that happens in the middle of that background, or just a simple list of finished actions. The three timelines below show the three patterns you need this week.

① A short action interrupts a longer, ongoing one
While the crowd was cheering, Marcus crossed the line. — the bar shows the action already "in the middle of happening"; the dot shows the short action that interrupts it.
② Two long actions happening at the same time
Both bars are past progressive — two backgrounds overlapping at the same time, with no interruption.
③ A simple sequence of finished actions (no progressive needed)
Each dot is a short, complete action. They simply happen one after another — simple past for all of them.

Pattern: While/As + past progressive, + simple past. Or: Simple past + while + past progressive. Both orders are fine. Just remember: "while" usually goes with the longer, background action.

UseExample
Two long actions happening at the same timeWhile Priya was watching the race, volunteers were handing out water.
A short action interrupting a longer oneIt started to rain while Marcus was running through the park.
Simple sequence of finished actions (no progressive needed)The gun fired and everyone started running.

⚠️ Common Error at This Level

Learners often use the simple past for BOTH actions. This loses the sense of "background vs. interruption."

❌ Less accurate✅ More accurate
While I waited, it started to rain.While I was waiting, it started to rain.
While the crowd cheered, Marcus crossed the line.While the crowd was cheering, Marcus crossed the line.

Rule of thumb: after "while", use the past progressive for the longer background action. You can use the simple past too, but it sounds much less natural. Real English almost always uses the progressive form here.

3.3 — A First Look at the Present Perfect (Preview Only)

You will study the present perfect fully next week. For now, just notice its form and its basic meaning. This tense connects the past to now.

FormExample from the interviewBasic idea
have/has + past participle"I've never run this distance so fast before."An experience at some point up to now — not a specific finished moment.
have/has + past participle"This is the first time I've broken 1:30."Something true up to this moment, connected to now.
④ Simple past (one fixed point) vs. present perfect (connected to now)
The simple past dot sits alone at one exact moment in the past. The present perfect bar has no fixed starting point — it stretches up to, and touches, NOW.

This week, just try to notice this difference. Full rules and practice come in Week 6.

4Using "The" Correctly

The diary entry uses "the" constantly: the start line, the river, the water stations, the gun, the crowd, the finish line. Here's why.

RuleExample
Something already mentioned / known to both peopleA gun fired. The gun was very loud.
Something unique / only one exists in the situationThe sun was rising. The finish line was ahead.
Superlatives (recycled from Week 4)The best moment was at the finish line.
Specific group in this situationThe crowd was cheering. (this specific crowd, not crowds in general)

Now compare this with NO article. We drop "the" for general or plural ideas: "Runners often feel nervous before a race" means runners in general. But "The runners at the start line looked nervous" means this specific group we already know about.

5Linkers for Ideas

These six linkers are the most common ones in real English at this level. They all appear in Priya's diary entry. Go back and find each one before you study the table below.

LinkerJobPositionExample
andadds informationjoins two clausesThe gun fired and everyone started running.
butcontrastjoins two clausesShe looked strong, but she was clearly tired.
becausegives a reasonjoins two clauses (either order)Sam finished third because he paced himself perfectly.
sogives a resultjoins two clausesIt started to drizzle, so I put my hood up.
howevercontrast (more formal)starts a new sentence + commaMarcus was exhausted. However, he kept going.
althoughcontrast/concessioncan start the sentence or go in the middleAlthough it was raining, the runners kept going. / She finished, although she was tired.

Key distinction: "but" and "however" have almost the same meaning (contrast). But they work differently. "But" joins two clauses in ONE sentence, with a comma before it. "However" usually starts its OWN new sentence, with a comma after it. Mixing these two up is one of the most common writing errors at this level.

8Error Clinic

Read each sentence below. Some have errors from this week's grammar. Fix them. But one sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "While I waited near kilometre 15, it started to drizzle."
    Incorrect — the background action needs the past progressive: "While I was waiting near kilometre 15, it started to drizzle."
  2. "Marcus was tired, however he kept running until the end."
    Incorrect punctuation/structure — "however" needs to start its own sentence with a comma after it: "Marcus was tired. However, he kept running until the end." (Or use "but" instead, joined in one sentence: "Marcus was tired, but he kept running.")
  3. "While the volunteers were setting up the water stations, the announcer shouted 'Two minutes!'"
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. The background action ("were setting up") is correctly in the past progressive, and the shorter interrupting action ("shouted") is correctly in the simple past. Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Sam finished third because he was pacing himself perfectly the whole way."
    Less natural — "paced himself" describes the whole race as a completed, overall action, so simple past fits better here: "Sam finished third because he paced himself perfectly the whole way." (Past progressive isn't wrong grammar, but native-speaker usage strongly favours simple past for a whole completed activity like this.)
  5. "Although it was raining, the runners kept going."
    Also correct. "Although" correctly starts the sentence, followed by a comma after the first clause. A second trap: not every sentence in an error clinic needs a correction.
