Notice the Pattern

Before we give these patterns a grammar name, let's find them ourselves. This helps you remember the rule much better than just reading it.

Step 1: Scroll back up to Section 1 and re-read Daniel's blog post. Step 2: Come back here and read questions A and B. Step 3: Try to answer them yourself before clicking "Check your noticing."

A. Look at the bold words in Daniel's blog: went, got, came, took, made, saw, thought, told, knew, said. All of these are Simple Past — but do any of them end in -ed? Compare them to last week's words like "walked" and "guided."

B. Now look at the market dialogue: "How much would you like?" and "It's made locally." Which words are past, and which are actually still present? Is the vendor's dialogue happening now or in the past?

A. None of the bold words end in -ed. Each one changes in its own unique way: go→went, get→got, come→came, take→took, make→made, see→saw, think→thought, tell→told, know→knew, say→said. These are called irregular verbs, and you simply have to learn each one individually — there's no spelling rule to help you.

B. The market dialogue is happening now, in the present — that's why it uses present forms. Daniel's blog is written afterwards, describing what already happened, which is why it uses the past. Recognising this difference is important: not everything you read is automatically in the past.

Well done — you've just found a real (and tricky!) grammar pattern by yourself, with no rules given to you first. Section 3 now introduces the ten most important irregular verbs, and Section 4 compares them to the regular verbs you met last week.

Grammar Focus — Simple Past of Irregular Verbs

The ten most frequent irregular verbs in English, in frequency order

Irregular verbs don't add -ed. Instead, each one has its own past form that you simply need to memorise. The good news: a small number of irregular verbs cover most of what you'll ever need to say. Here are the ten most common ones in real English, starting with the most frequent.

3.0 The Top 10 Irregular Verbs

Base FormSimple PastExample from the story
saysaidSomebody said it was the best weekend.
gowentWe finally went on our countryside trip.
getgotWe got some last-minute snacks.
comecameMei's colleague came by early.
taketookWe took the scenic route.
knowknewI knew immediately it was a great day.
seesawWe saw beautiful vineyards.
thinkthoughtI thought the drive would take longer.
makemadeJulia made sure we didn't get lost.
telltoldJulia told us a funny story.

Corpus note: why this order?

These ten verbs are sequenced by real frequency in spoken and written English (COCA data). Learner corpora show that studying irregular verbs in a random order produces far more errors than studying the most useful ones first. Master these ten before moving on to less common irregular verbs.

3.1 Form — irregular verbs still follow the same three patterns

FormStructureExample
Positivesubject + irregular past formWe went to the countryside.
Negativesubject + didn't + base formWe didn't go by train.
QuestionDid + subject + base form?Did you go by car?

The most important rule on this page

✗ We didn't went by train.

✓ We didn't go by train. (after didn't/did, ALWAYS use the base form — even for irregular verbs)

This is true for every verb in English, regular or irregular: the irregular past form (went, saw, made...) is only used in positive statements. Negatives and questions always go back to the base form.

Grammar Focus — Regular vs. Irregular Past

Recycling from Week 2 — Simple Past of regular verbs (walked, guided, asked...)

Last week, you learned that most English verbs are regular: they simply add -ed. This week, you've met ten common irregular verbs. Real English sentences constantly mix both types together — so being able to recognise which is which matters more than any single rule.

RegularIrregular
Ruleadd -ed (with spelling changes)no rule — each verb is different and must be memorised
Examplewalk → walked, guide → guidedgo → went, see → saw
Negative / Questiondidn't/did + base form (walk)didn't/did + base form (go) — exactly the same rule!

The good news

The negative and question forms work exactly the same way for both regular and irregular verbs: didn't/did + base form, always. The only real difference between regular and irregular verbs is the positive form.

4.1 A sentence with both types together

From Daniel's blog: "We took the scenic route, and Julia made sure we didn't get lost." vs. last week's "We walked along the canal, and Julia guided us confidently." Both sentences are 100% Simple Past — they just use different verb types.

1. We walked (regular) to the market, then took (irregular) the bus home.
2. Julia told (irregular) us a story, and we all laughed (regular).
3. Sofia asked (regular) the vendor a question, and he gave (irregular — not one of today's ten, but a bonus example) her a good price.
4. I thought (irregular) about the trip all week, and I packed (regular) my bag on Friday night.

Vocabulary — Shopping

Every word below appeared in Section 1. Read the example from the story before the definition — try to guess the meaning first.

marketˈmɑːkɪtn
"We got some last-minute snacks from the market near Julia's flat."
A place, often outdoors, where different sellers offer food or goods, usually cheaper and fresher than shops.
Collocates: a farmers' market, go to the market, market stall
receiptrɪˈsiːtn
The Naschmarkt receipt shows exactly what the group bought and how much it cost.
A piece of paper that proves you paid for something, showing the items and prices.
Collocates: keep the receipt, ask for a receipt, a paper/digital receipt
vendorˈvendərn
The vendor told Sofia the cheese was made locally.
A person who sells things, especially at a market or from a stall.
Collocates: a market vendor, a street vendor
How much is / are...?phrase
"Excuse me, how much is the cheese?"
The standard question for asking the price of something in a shop or market.
Pattern: How much is + singular noun? / How much are + plural noun?
We'll take itphrase
"Perfect, we'll take it."
A common way to confirm that you want to buy something.
Also: I'll have this one. / I'll get this. / We'll go with this one.
a sliceslaɪsn
"Would you like a slice or the whole one?"
A thin, flat piece cut from something larger, like bread, cake, or cheese.
Collocates: a slice of cake/bread/cheese, cut into slices

Vocabulary — Cars & Borrowing

These words came from the WhatsApp messages in Section 1.

borrowˈbɒrəʊv
"I can ask her if we can borrow it for the day."
To take and use something that belongs to someone else, planning to give it back.
Collocates: borrow something from someone, borrow a car/book/money
lendlendv
Mei's colleague didn't just allow it — she effectively lent them her car for the day.
To give something to someone for a period of time, expecting it back — the opposite direction of "borrow."
Collocates: lend something to someone, lend a hand (idiom = help)
colleagueˈkɒliːɡn
"My colleague has a car."
A person you work with (reviewed from Week 1 — useful again here).
Collocates: a close colleague, ask a colleague a favour
drop offphrasal verb
"Mei's colleague came by early to drop off the car keys."
To deliver something (or someone) to a place, briefly, before leaving again.
Collocates: drop off the keys, drop someone off (at a place)
the scenic routeˈsiːnɪkn phrase
"We took the scenic route along the Danube."
A longer but more beautiful way of travelling somewhere, chosen for the view rather than speed.
Collocates: take the scenic route, a scenic drive/view
keyskiːzn, plural
Mei's colleague dropped off the car keys before the trip.
The small metal objects used to start a car, or to lock and unlock something.
Collocates: the car keys, hand over the keys, lose your keys

Practice — Fix the Errors

Five sentences below. One of them is already correct — don't "fix" a sentence that has nothing wrong with it. Write your corrections on paper first, then click "Reveal corrections" to check all five at once.

1. We goed to the countryside on Saturday.

2. Did you saw the vineyards?

3. Julia told us a funny story.

4. Mei's colleague didn't wanted any money for the car.

5. Could I lend your car this weekend?

1. We went to the countryside. ("goed" doesn't exist — go is irregular: go/went)
2. Did you see the vineyards? (base form after "did", not "saw")
3. ✅ Correct as written.
4. Mei's colleague didn't want any money for the car. (base form after "didn't", not "wanted")
5. Could I borrow your car this weekend? (the speaker wants to take/use it — that's "borrow", not "lend", which is the opposite direction)

Skills — Listening: Julia's Voice Message

Transcript-based listening task — read aloud with a partner or record yourselves, then answer.

Step 1: Read (or listen to) the message once, just for the general idea. Step 2: Read the three questions below. Step 3: Read the message a second time, looking for the answers. Step 4: Click "Reveal" to check each one.

From: Julia → To: the "New in Vienna" group — Sunday, 20:10

"Hey everyone, just a quick thank-you message. I really enjoyed today — I think it was one of the best trips we've had so far. I already messaged my colleague's friend, and apparently there's a great little vineyard we didn't visit this time — we should go back next month. Also, I got some extra cheese from the market this morning because the one from yesterday was so good, and I already ate half of it! One more thing: my colleague said we can borrow her car again next time, so we don't need to worry about that. Sleep well, everyone!"

Listening check 1: What did Julia learn about a vineyard?

She learned there's a great little vineyard they didn't visit this time, and they should go back next month.

Listening check 2: What did Julia buy again, and why?

She got more cheese from the market because yesterday's cheese was so good she already ate half of it.

Listening check 3: What did Julia's colleague say about the car?

She said they can borrow her car again next time, so they don't need to worry about transport.
