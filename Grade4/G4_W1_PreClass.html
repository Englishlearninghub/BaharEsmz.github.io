Notice the Pattern

Before we give these patterns a grammar name, let's find them ourselves. This helps you remember the rule much better than just reading it.

Step 1: Scroll back up to Section 1 and re-read the dialogue at Café Josef. Step 2: Come back here and read questions A and B. Step 3: Try to answer them yourself — in your head or on paper — before clicking "Check your noticing."

A. Simple Present. Find these sentences: "Sofia works as a graphic designer." "Daniel doesn't work on Sundays." "Do you always bring cake, Julia?" Notice how the verb changes with he/she (works, doesn't work) but not with I/you/we/they. Notice how questions and negatives use a helper word.

B. Object Pronouns. Find: "I met her last month." "I baked this cake for us." "Thanks for organising all of this for us." Who do these small words replace? Are they before or after the verb/preposition?

A. With he/she/it, the verb adds -s (works, teaches, goes). With I/you/we/they, the verb stays in its base form (work, teach, go). Negatives and questions use do/does + base verb — never two changed verbs in the same sentence (never "does works").

B. "Her" replaces "Mei" (the object of "met"). "Us" replaces "Sofia, Daniel, and Mei" (the object of "for"). Object pronouns always come after the verb or after a preposition like for, with, to — never in the subject position.

Well done — you've just found two real grammar patterns by yourself, with no rules given to you first. That's the method we use every week: notice first, name it second. Sections 3 and 4 now explain each pattern fully, using the exact same examples you just looked at.

Grammar Focus — Simple Present

Consolidation & Extension — you already know the basics; here we make them solid and extend them

You noticed this pattern yourself in Section 2. Now let's look at it properly, rule by rule, with clear examples.

3.0 Form — the three patterns

FormStructureExample
PositiveI / you / we / they + base verb
he / she / it + verb + -s		I work in an office.  ·  Sofia works as a designer.
Negativesubject + don't / doesn't + base verbDaniel doesn't work on Sundays.
QuestionDo / Does + subject + base verb?Does Mei enjoy her job?

3.1 Third person -s — spelling rules

Read the table below slowly. For each row, cover the "Example" column and try to guess it first.

RuleExample
Most verbs: add -swork → works
Verbs ending -s, -sh, -ch, -x, -o: add -esteach → teaches, go → goes, do → does
Consonant + y: change y → iesstudy → studies, carry → carries
Vowel + y: just add -splay → plays, enjoy → enjoys
Irregularhave → has

3.2 What is the Simple Present used for?

All four uses below come directly from this week's story — look for the character's name in each example.

  • Routines and habits: "I usually cook every day." "She often brings cake."
  • Facts and general truths: "Vienna has a lot of cafés." "Nurses work in hospitals."
  • Jobs and what people do: "Sofia works as a graphic designer." "Julia teaches German."
  • Schedules and timetables: "The meetup starts at 7 pm every Wednesday."

3.3 Frequency adverbs — how often?

Position rule

Frequency adverbs go before the main verb, but after the verb "to be":
"I usually cook every day." (before the main verb "cook")
"She is always busy on Fridays." (after "is")

The table below goes from "always true" to "never true." Use it to describe your own routine honestly — not everything is "always" or "never."

AdverbApproximate frequency
always100%
usually / normally~80%
often~60%
sometimes~40%
rarely / hardly ever~10%
never0%

3.4 A common learner error

3.4 A common learner error

Don't double the change

✗ She doesn't works on Sundays.

✓ She doesn't work on Sundays. (the -s moves to "doesn't"; the main verb stays base form)

✗ Does she works as a nurse?

✓ Does she work as a nurse?

4.1 Where do object pronouns go?

PositionExample
After a verbJulia met her last month. (her = Mei, the object of "met")
After a preposition
(for, with, to, about)		She baked a cake for us.  ·  Daniel goes hiking with them.

Subject vs. object — don't mix them up

✗ Julia met she last month.

✓ Julia met her last month.

✗ Thanks for organising this for we.

✓ Thanks for organising this for us.

4.2 Object pronouns in everyday introductions

Object pronouns are essential when you introduce people or talk about your social circle:

  • "This is Daniel. I met him at the meetup."
  • "Sofia and Mei are friends now — I see them every Wednesday."
  • "Julia helps us practise English."

1. This is Sofia. I met her two months ago.
2. Daniel invited us to go hiking with him next weekend.
3. Julia bakes a cake every week — everyone loves it.
4. Sofia and Daniel are nice; I really enjoy talking to them.

Vocabulary — Jobs & Work

Every word below appeared in Section 1. Read the example from the story before the definition — try to guess the meaning first.

graphic designerˌɡræfɪk dɪˈzaɪnərn phrase
"I work as a graphic designer for a small studio here in Vienna."
A person whose job is to create visual designs — logos, posters, websites — usually using computer software.
Collocates: work as a graphic designer, a freelance graphic designer
chefʃefn
"I work as a chef at a restaurant near the Naschmarkt."
A professional cook, especially someone who is skilled and often in charge of a kitchen.
Collocates: head chef, pastry chef, work as a chef
nursenɜːsn
"I work as a nurse at the general hospital."
A person trained to take care of sick or injured people, usually in a hospital or clinic.
Collocates: work as a nurse, a nursing shift, a night nurse
teacherˈtiːtʃərn
"I teach German at a language school," says Julia — she is a language teacher.
A person whose job is to help others learn a subject or skill, usually in a school or classroom.
Collocates: teach a subject, a language teacher, a primary/secondary school teacher
work asv phrase
"Sofia works as a graphic designer." "Julia teaches German" — but "Daniel works as a chef."
Used to say what job or role someone has.
Pattern: work as + a/an + job title (never "work as chef" without the article)
work forv phrase
Sofia works for a small studio.
Used to say which company or person employs you.
Pattern: work for + company/person — compare "work at" (a place) and "work in" (an industry/field)
colleagueˈkɒliːɡn
Mei has several colleagues at the hospital who work the same shifts.
A person you work with, usually at the same company or in the same profession.
Collocates: a close colleague, colleagues at work
shiftʃɪftn
Mei often works night shifts at the hospital.
A fixed period of working time, especially in jobs where people work in different time blocks (morning, night).
Collocates: a night shift, work shifts, a 12-hour shift
freelancerˈfriːlɑːnsərn
Sofia isn't a freelancer yet, but she'd like to become one and work for herself one day.
A person who works for different companies or clients, instead of being employed by one company.
Collocates: work as a freelancer, freelance work, go freelance
part-time / full-timeadj
Julia teaches German part-time; she also has another job on weekends.
Part-time = working fewer hours than a standard working week. Full-time = working the standard number of hours.
Collocates: a part-time job, work full-time, full-time hours

5.2 Talking About Employment Status

Not everyone has a traditional job — these words let you describe your situation accurately.

Employment Status

employed (have a job) · self-employed (work for yourself, not a company) · unemployed (do not have a job at the moment) · retired (stopped working, usually because of age) · a stay-at-home parent (look after children instead of having a paid job) · a student (studying, not working full-time) · between jobs (a polite way of saying "unemployed right now")

More Job & Work Words to Know

workplace (n) · job title (n phrase) · career (n) · employer / employee (n) · apply for a job (v phrase) · earn a salary (v phrase) · take a day off (v phrase) · be in charge of (v phrase) · retire (v)

Language for Talking About Jobs — What to Say

SituationUseful phrases
Asking about someone's jobWhat do you do? / What's your job? / Who do you work for? / What do you do for a living? / What line of work are you in?
Talking about your own jobI work as a... / I work for... / I'm a... (+ job title) / I'm self-employed. / I'm currently between jobs.
Talking about your scheduleI usually work from 9 to 5. / I often work weekends. / I never work on Sundays.
Talking about how you feel about itI really enjoy my job. / It's a busy job. / It can be stressful sometimes.

Vocabulary — Hobbies & Free Time

Notice the different verb patterns: go + -ing, do + noun, play + sport.

hikingˈhaɪkɪŋn, gerund
"I love hiking in the mountains." / "He goes hiking whenever he has a free weekend."
Walking for a long time in nature, especially in hills or mountains, as a leisure activity.
Collocates: go hiking, a hiking trail, hiking boots
yogaˈjəʊɡən, uncountable
"In my free time, I do yoga."
A system of physical exercises combined with breathing and relaxation, originally from India.
Collocates: do yoga, a yoga class, practise yoga
photographyfəˈtɒɡrəfin, uncountable
"She takes photos of the city — she never leaves home without her camera."
The activity or hobby of taking pictures with a camera.
Collocates: enjoy photography, take up photography, a photography hobby
bakingˈbeɪkɪŋn, gerund
"She loves baking, and she often brings cake to the meetup."
Making bread, cakes, or other food in an oven, usually as a hobby or a job.
Collocates: do some baking, a baking hobby, home-baked (bread/cake)
paintingˈpeɪntɪŋn, gerund
"She does yoga, and she paints on weekends."
Creating pictures using paint, as an art form or hobby.
Collocates: enjoy painting, a painting class, paint a picture
free timen phrase
"In my free time, I do yoga." "I take photos when I'm not working."
Time when you are not working or studying, and can do what you enjoy.
Collocates: in your free time, have some free time, spend your free time

Skills — Listening: Sofia's Voice Message

Transcript-based listening task — read aloud with a partner or record yourselves, then answer.

Step 1: Read (or listen to) the message once, just for the general idea. Step 2: Read the three questions below. Step 3: Read the message a second time, looking for the answers. Step 4: Click "Reveal" to check each one.

From: Sofia → To: the "New in Vienna" group — Thursday, 09:15

"Hi everyone! Thanks for a great evening yesterday. I just wanted to say — I really enjoyed meeting Daniel and Mei. Daniel, if you ever want company on one of your hikes, let me know — I'd love to join you sometime. And Mei, your photos are amazing! Do you ever do photography walks with other people? I'd be interested. Anyway, I don't usually make new friends this quickly, so thank you all. See you next Wednesday!"

Listening check 1: What does Sofia want to do with Daniel?

She would like to join him on one of his hikes sometime.

Listening check 2: What does Sofia ask Mei about?

She asks if Mei ever does photography walks with other people, because she'd be interested in joining.

Listening check 3: How does Sofia usually feel about making new friends, based on what she says?

She says she doesn't usually make new friends this quickly, showing that this group feels special to her.
