Notice Bramwell Professional Institute — Term 2 Update

Dear candidates,

It has been a busy term at Bramwell! We have made a few important changes to our course policies, and we want to explain them clearly.

First, the good news: candidates have never been allowed to choose their own seminar groups before, but starting this month, every cohort will have flexible scheduling. Dr. Whitfield announced this change at the staff briefing last Tuesday, and the response from learners has been very positive.

Second, a reminder about punctuality. As always: if a candidate arrives after the 15-minute grace period, the exam room is locked, and an invigilator records the absence on file. This has been our policy for many years and it will not change.

Third, the device policy. We know this is the rule candidates ask about most. We have already explained this twice this year, but to be completely clear: phones and smartwatches must stay in bags during all assessed sessions. We have had three confiscations so far this term — please review the handbook if you're unsure.

Finally, congratulations to the Tuesday evening cohort, who achieved a 94% pass rate last term. The Director of Studies has never seen a cohort improve so quickly.

Kind regards,
The Bramwell Institute Office

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look back at the coral-highlighted phrases

Detective work: find the pattern yourself before we name it

Look at "has been," "have made," "have never been allowed," "have already explained," "have had," "has never seen." None of these phrases tell us exactly when something happened. What do they have in common instead?

They all connect something from the past to right now — a total, a result, or an experience that's still relevant today. None of them name a specific date or time.

Now look at "announced... last Tuesday" and "raised... last week." These DO have a time word. What's different about how they're built compared to the phrases above?

They use a simple one-word past form (announced, raised) with no "have/has", and they're anchored to one specific, finished moment in time.

Finally, look at "if a student arrives... the gate is locked... a member of staff walks." This describes school policy, not a story about last Tuesday. What tense is being used, and why do you think that is?

Present simple in both halves — because this is a general rule that's always true, not a one-time event.

STUDY

Now let's name what you found: Past Simple vs. Present Perfect

1. Past Simple vs. Present Perfect

You already noticed the pattern in the notice. Here's the rule behind it.

✓ Past Simple: Dr. Whitfield announced this change last Tuesday. A finished action at a specific, known time. We know exactly when. Found in: notice, paragraph 2
✓ Present Perfect: We have made a few important changes. A connection between past and now — we care about the result or the total, not exactly when. Found in: notice, paragraph 1

Past Simple finished time word (yesterday, last Tuesday, in 2019)

NOTICE & DISCOVER

jordan.t asked the right question — let's answer it

Detective work: two kinds of "if"

Look at mira.k's sentence: "If a phone rings in class, the teacher takes it." Is this describing something that happens once, or a permanent rule? What two tenses are used — and are they the same tense or different?

A permanent rule, true every time. Both halves use present simple: "rings" and "takes." Same tense in both parts.

Now look at deepa_p's sentence: "If they catch you a second time this term, you will lose phone privileges." Is this a permanent rule, or one possible thing that might happen this term only? Which word signals the future result?

One possible specific event, limited to "this term." The word "will" in the second half signals a predicted future result, not a general truth.

So: one structure uses present simple + present simple, and the other uses present simple + will. Which one is the "always true" rule, and which one is the "maybe, in the future" rule?

Present simple + present simple = always true (zero conditional). Present simple + will = one possible future result (first conditional).

STUDY

Now let's name it: Zero Conditional vs. First Conditional

2. Zero Conditional (review) & First Conditional (new)

You found this pattern yourself in the forum thread — here's the full rule.

TypeFormMeaningFound in the forum
Zero
(review)		 If + present simple, + present simple Always true / general rule If a phone rings in class, the teacher takes it.
First
(new)		 If + present simple, + will + base verb A real, possible future result If they catch you a second time, you will lose phone privileges.

Zero conditional — true at every point, no timeline needed

always true, every single time — past, present, and future

First conditional — one possible branch in the future

now
possible future
"you will lose..."

Notice the difference: the zero conditional describes something that always happens (a fact, a rule). The first conditional predicts one specific future result if a condition is met.

PRONUNCIATION

How this sounds in real speech

The "if" clause and the result clause each get their own intonation

Imagine deepa_p saying her sentence out loud, not just typing it.

In spoken English, the if-clause rises slightly, and the result clause falls — like asking and then answering your own question.

Try saying this out loud, raising your pitch on the underlined part:

If they catch you again, you'll lose your phone for weeks. /ɪf ðeɪ kætʃ juː/

This rise-fall pattern is one of the clearest signals in spoken English that a sentence has two connected parts — condition, then consequence. Native speakers use this pattern automatically, even in casual chat like the forum above.

VOCABULARY

School rules & consequences: full word set

Collocations with "rule" (from the texts above)

Both texts used several different verbs with the word "rule." Mixing these up is one of the most common errors at this level.

break a rule follow a rule bend a rule (informal, slightly relax it) enforce a rule set a rule change/update a rule do a rule

We never say "do a rule" in English, even though it might feel logical to translate it that way.

Cultural research

Compare school rules in two countries

Choose two countries (one can be your own). Research one real school rule from each country that surprised you — it could be about uniforms, technology, discipline, or daily schedule.

  • Write 3–4 sentences for each country. Try to use at least one Present Perfect sentence ("This school has never allowed...") and one conditional ("If a student does X, then...").
  • Be ready to briefly present your findings to a partner or small group next session.
Try searching: "[country name] school rules" Try: news articles, school websites, or student forums in English

Why this matters

Looking things up yourself, rather than waiting for a teacher to give you a list, is one of the most important skills for becoming a genuinely independent English user. The goal isn't to find a "correct" answer — it's to practise noticing language patterns on your own, the same way you did earlier with the notice and forum thread.
