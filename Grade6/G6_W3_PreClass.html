ENGAGE

What's an assumption people make about your country?

Before we start — true or false?

Airports are full of half-remembered "facts" about other cultures — some true, some pure stereotype. Guess which of these are real before you read on.

In Japan, it's considered normal to nap in public, even on the train.

True! It's called "inemuri" and is widely tolerated as a sign of working hard.

In Germany, jaywalking is legal as long as no children are nearby.

False! Jaywalking is against the law everywhere in Germany, regardless of who's nearby.

In many Middle Eastern countries, it's polite to accept tea or coffee even if you don't want it.

True! Refusing hospitality outright can be seen as impolite in many of these cultures.

In most of South America, arriving exactly on time to a social event is expected and required.

False! In much of South America, arriving somewhat later than the stated time is common and socially fine.

Notice how easy it is to be confident about a "fact" that's actually a stereotype — that's exactly what this week's theme explores.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look back at the highlighted phrases

Detective work: how much, and how necessary?

Look at the coral words: "all passengers," "most travelers," "some passengers," "all visitors," "most" (short-stay tourists). These all come before nouns and describe a quantity or proportion. Put them in order, from describing the smallest group to the largest group.

some (a part, not all) → most (a large majority, but not literally everyone) → all (100%, with zero exceptions).

Now look at the blue words: "must," "should," "may," "can," "don't have to," "might need to," "don't need to." The text uses "must" for the security scanner rule and "should" for the two-hour arrival guideline. Both feel like advice on the surface — so what's actually different about how strongly each one is enforced?

"Must" signals a rule with no real choice (security will stop you otherwise). "Should" signals strong advice, but you technically could ignore it and just risk missing your flight — nobody will physically stop you for arriving later.

Look at "don't have to" and "don't need to" versus "must." If "must" means something is required, what do you think "don't have to" means? Is it the opposite of "must," or the opposite of "must not"?

"Don't have to" means it's optional — not the opposite of "must" (forbidden), but simply "no obligation either way." That's a common confusion point at this level.

STUDY

Now let's name it: determiners and modal verbs

1. Determiners: some, most, all

You already sequenced these yourself — here's the rule behind the pattern.

✓ All passengers must remove laptops and liquids. 100% of the group, with zero exceptions. Found in: notice board, paragraph 1
✓ Most travelers should arrive two hours early. A large majority — true for nearly everyone, but not a universal claim. Found in: notice board, paragraph 2
✓ Some passengers can use the priority lane. An unspecified part of the group — more than zero, less than all. Found in: notice board, paragraph 3

Quantity scale: from a part of the group to the whole group

(a part)

all
(100%, no exceptions)

some + plural noun an unspecified part of a group

most + plural noun nearly all, but allows for exceptions

all + plural noun the entire group, no exceptions

✗ All of passengers must remove laptops. Wrong — "all" goes directly before the plural noun with no "of": All passengers must remove laptops. ("All of the passengers" is only correct with "the" added.)

1b. General group or specific group? The "of the" test

This is the single most common error with these determiners — and it's a meaning difference, not just a grammar rule.

General — no "of the"

Some passengers prefer window seats.

Talking about passengers in general, anywhere, any flight — not one identifiable group.

Specific — needs "of the"

Some of the passengers on this flight are connecting to Rome.

A particular, already-identified group: specifically the passengers on this one flight.

General — no "of the"

Most travelers carry a passport.

A general truth about travelers as a category.

Specific — needs "of the"

Most of the travelers in this queue look frustrated.

A specific, visible group: the travelers in this particular queue, right now.

determiner + plural noun general / indefinite — any members of that category, anywhere

determiner + of the / of my / of her... + plural noun specific — a particular, identifiable group you could point to

TestGeneral (no "of the")Specific (with "of the")
Could you point to the exact group?No — it's a category, not a set of individualsYes — a known, identifiable set of people
Typical patternsome / most / all + nounsome / most / all + of the/my/her/these + noun
ExampleAll airports have security checks.All of the airports in this country use the same system.
✗ Some the passengers on this flight are connecting to Rome. Wrong — you can't drop "of" when you keep "the": it's either "some passengers" (general) or "some of the passengers" (specific), never "some the passengers."
✗ Most of travelers carry a passport. Wrong if you mean travelers in general — without "the" (or "my/her/these"), drop "of" entirely: Most travelers carry a passport. Only add "of" once there's a specific, identified group: "most of the travelers I met."

2. Modal Verbs: obligation, permission, and possibility

The notice board used six different modals — each one signals a different strength of rule.

ModalStrengthExample from the notice

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Politer, longer, and strangely word-order-reversed

Detective work: building the indirect question

Compare: direct "Where is the exit?" vs. indirect "...if she could tell me where the exit was." In the direct question, "is" comes before "the exit." In the indirect version, what order are "the exit" and "was" in?

"The exit was" — subject before verb, just like a normal statement. The question word + inverted verb order disappears once it's embedded inside a longer, politer question.

Now compare: direct "Do I need to declare this?" vs. indirect "...whether I needed to declare..." There's no question word like "where" or "why" in the direct version — it's a yes/no question. What word does the indirect version use to introduce it instead?

"Whether" (or "if") — used specifically for yes/no questions that don't have their own question word.

Look at ravi_h's sentence: "if they knew what time the next train left." This has two layers — an embedded yes/no question ("if they knew") AND an embedded wh-question ("what time...left") inside it. Does the second part also follow subject-before-verb order?

Yes — "the next train left," not "left the next train." Every embedded question inside an indirect question keeps statement word order, no matter how many layers deep.

STUDY

Now let's name it: indirect questions

3. Indirect Questions

A more polite, often longer way to ask something — you found the two key rules yourself in the forum above.

Direct question

Where is the exit?

Indirect question

Could you tell me where the exit is?

Direct question

Do I need to declare this?

Indirect question

I was wondering whether I need to declare this.

Rule 1 subject before verb in the embedded question (no inversion)

Rule 2 yes/no questions need "if" or "whether" to introduce them

Rule 3 wh-questions keep their question word (where/why/what time), but lose the inversion

Polite introduction phraseTypical use
Could you tell me...?asking a stranger for information politely
I was wondering...softer, less direct than asking outright
Do you know...?casual but still more polite than a bare question
Would you mind telling me...?extra polite, useful in formal settings
✗ Could you tell me where is the exit? Wrong — this keeps the direct-question word order inside the indirect frame. Correct: Could you tell me where the exit is?
PRONUNCIATION

Softer questions sound softer, too

Indirect questions usually have a smoother, slower rhythm

The politeness isn't only in the words — it's in the delivery too.

A blunt direct question can sound rushed or even rude if said quickly with a sharp falling tone: "Where's the exit?" said fast and flat can sound impatient.

Try saying the indirect version slowly, with a gentle, rounded intonation — almost like you're being careful not to impose:

Could you possibly tell me where the exit is? /kʊd juː/ soft, unhurried

Notice the extra words ("possibly," "I was wondering") aren't just grammar padding — they buy you time to sound calm and unhurried, which is a big part of why indirect questions feel more polite in English.

VOCABULARY

Airport vocabulary: from kerb to carousel

Why this section exists

The notice board and forum thread this week used accurate airport language — but only the tip of the iceberg. Real airport conversations cover check-in counters, security lanes, gate announcements, and arrivals halls. This section walks you through the full journey, from arriving at the airport to collecting your bag.

1. Check-in — arriving at the airport

check inverb (phrasal)
To register your arrival at the airport, confirm your seat, and drop your bag before a flight. You check in at a desk or using a self-service kiosk.
"I need to check in at least 90 minutes before departure." / "You can check in online 24 hours before your flight."
check-in desk / counternoun phrase
The physical counter in the airport where airline staff process your bags, verify your documents, and print or confirm your boarding pass. Different from a self-service kiosk.
"The check-in desk for flight BA407 is at counter 14." / "There was a long queue at the check-in counter."
boarding passnoun phrase
The document (paper or digital) that gives you the right to board a specific flight. It shows your name, flight number, seat number, and gate. You cannot board without it.
"Can I see your boarding pass, please?" / "I have my boarding pass on my phone — is that okay?"
baggage allowancenoun phrase
The maximum weight and/or number of bags you are permitted to take on a flight without paying extra. Exceeding it means paying an overweight or extra-bag fee.
"My baggage allowance is 23 kg for the hold and 10 kg for hand luggage." / "Budget airlines have a very strict baggage allowance."
scale (at check-in)noun
The weighing machine at the check-in counter where your suitcase or bag is weighed to check it is within the baggage allowance. Also called "luggage scales."
"Put your suitcase on the scale, please." / "My bag was 2 kg over the limit when I put it on the scale."
overweight (luggage)adjective / noun
Luggage that exceeds the weight limit set by the airline. You must pay an overweight fee or rearrange items before your bag is accepted.
"I'm afraid your bag is overweight by 3.4 kg." / "The overweight fee was €60 — more than I expected."
conveyor belt (at check-in)noun phrase
The moving belt at the check-in counter that carries your checked bag from the desk to the loading area. You place your bag on it after the agent attaches the luggage tag.
"She put her suitcase on the conveyor belt and watched it disappear." / "The bag tag gets scanned before it goes onto the conveyor belt."
carry-on / hand luggagenoun phrase
Bags small enough to take onto the plane with you, stored in the overhead compartment or under the seat. Carry-on is more common in American English; hand luggage in British English.
"All passengers must ensure their carry-on fits in the overhead bin." / "You're allowed one piece of hand luggage and one personal item."

2. Security — the scanner lane

security (lane / checkpoint)noun phrase
The area in an airport where passengers and their bags are screened for prohibited items. You go through security (not "do" security). A compulsory step for all passengers.
"All passengers must go through security before reaching the gate area." / "Security was very slow because of the morning rush."
metal detectornoun phrase
The walk-through frame at the security checkpoint that beeps if you have metal objects (keys, belt, coins, etc.) on your body. You walk through it after placing your belongings in a tray.
"Please remove your belt before walking through the metal detector." / "The metal detector beeped — I'd forgotten my keys in my jacket pocket."
body scannernoun phrase
A more advanced scanning machine (often replacing or used alongside the metal detector) that creates a 3D image of your body to detect concealed items without physical contact.
"Some passengers may be directed to the body scanner instead of the metal detector." / "The body scanner is faster than a manual pat-down."
security traynoun phrase
The plastic tray provided at the security checkpoint, into which you place all loose items — phone, keys, belt, watch, laptop, liquids bag — before they go through the X-ray machine.
"Please place your laptop flat in a separate security tray." / "Don't forget to collect all your items from the tray on the other side."
X-ray machinenoun phrase
The screening machine at security that passes your bag through a tunnel using X-rays, allowing screeners to see inside without opening it. Your carry-on always goes through the X-ray.
"Put your bag on the belt — it'll go through the X-ray machine." / "The X-ray showed something suspicious in his carry-on."
conveyor belt (at security)noun phrase
The same term applies to the moving belt at the security checkpoint. You place your tray on it; it moves through the X-ray machine and emerges on the other side for you to collect.
"Put your tray on the conveyor belt and walk through the detector." / "The conveyor belt was moving so slowly the queue barely moved."
liquids bagnoun phrase
The small, clear, resealable plastic bag required by most airports for carrying liquids, aerosols, and gels in your carry-on. Each container must hold no more than 100 ml.
"All liquids must be in a clear liquids bag no larger than one litre." / "I had to throw away my water bottle because it wasn't in the liquids bag."
pat-downnoun phrase
A manual physical check by a security officer, who uses their hands to feel for concealed items. It may be required if the metal detector or body scanner triggers an alert.
"The alarm went off, so a security officer did a pat-down." / "You can request a same-gender officer for a pat-down."

3. Gate & boarding — the departure lounge

departure loungenoun phrase
The area of the airport beyond security, where passengers wait for their flights. It contains shops, restaurants, gates, and seating areas. You reach it after clearing security.
"There are several cafés in the departure lounge." / "Most passengers spend 30–40 minutes in the departure lounge before boarding."
boarding call / final callnoun phrase
An announcement over the PA system telling passengers to go to a specific gate to board their flight. A final call means the gate is about to close — if you miss it, you miss your flight.
"This is the final boarding call for passengers on flight TK452." / "We heard the boarding call and rushed to Gate 22."
overhead bin / compartmentnoun phrase
The storage space above the seats in the cabin of an aircraft, where passengers place their carry-on bags during the flight. Bins fill up quickly on busy flights.
"Please ensure your bag fits in the overhead bin." / "All the overhead bins were full by the time I boarded."
aisle seat / window seat / middle seatnoun phrases
The three seat positions in a standard 3-seat row on a plane. Window = next to the window. Aisle (pronounced /aɪl/) = next to the walkway. Middle = between the two — generally the least preferred.
"Could I have an aisle seat, please — I prefer to get up easily." / "I ended up in the middle seat on a six-hour flight."

4. On the plane

red-eye (flight)noun / adjective
An overnight flight that departs late at night and arrives early in the morning, leaving passengers with red, tired eyes from lack of sleep. Common on long-haul transatlantic or domestic US routes.
"I took the red-eye from New York — landed at 6 a.m. exhausted." / "A red-eye saves you a hotel night, but you arrive tired."
turbulencenoun (uncountable)
Irregular air movement that causes the aircraft to shake or bump during flight. It is caused by changes in air pressure, storms, or jet streams. Unpleasant but rarely dangerous.
"The pilot warned us about turbulence over the mountains." / "Some turbulence is normal — it doesn't mean the flight is in danger."
bumpy (flight / ride)adjective
An informal way to describe a flight with a lot of turbulence. More conversational than "turbulent." Often used in everyday speech but not in official announcements.
"It was a pretty bumpy flight over the Alps." / "The landing was smooth, but the approach was bumpy."
fasten your seatbeltverb phrase
To click your seatbelt into place and tighten it. The instruction passengers hear during takeoff, landing, and whenever the seatbelt sign is switched on (during turbulence).
"All passengers must fasten their seatbelts during takeoff." / "The seatbelt sign came on — please fasten your seatbelt."
tray tablenoun phrase
The folding table attached to the back of the seat in front of you, used for meals, laptops, or books during the flight. Must be stowed (put away) for takeoff and landing.
"Please fold and stow your tray table before landing." / "He spilled his coffee all over the tray table during the turbulence."
jet lagnoun (uncountable)
The physical tiredness, disorientation, and disrupted sleep caused by flying across multiple time zones. Your body clock is still on the time of your departure city.
"I had terrible jet lag after flying from London to Tokyo." / "Some passengers find it helps to adjust to the local time immediately on arrival."

5. Arrivals — baggage reclaim & customs

baggage reclaim / baggage claimnoun phrase
The area in the arrivals hall where passengers collect their checked bags after landing. Baggage reclaim is more common in British English; baggage claim in American English.
"Could you tell me where the baggage reclaim is?" / "I waited 25 minutes at baggage claim before my suitcase appeared."
carouselnoun
The large rotating belt or platform at baggage reclaim that carries passengers' checked bags around in a loop until they are collected. Each flight is assigned a different carousel number.
"Bags for flight LH301 are on carousel 4." / "I stood at the wrong carousel for ten minutes before realising."
customs officernoun phrase
An official who checks passengers and their goods as they enter a country, ensuring customs laws are followed. They can stop and question any passenger, open bags, and demand declarations.
"The customs officer asked me to open my suitcase." / "Could you tell me what happens if a customs officer stops me?"
green channel / red channelnoun phrase
At customs, the green channel is for passengers with nothing to declare. The red channel is for those carrying goods over the duty-free limit who must make a declaration and possibly pay tax.
"If you have nothing to declare, use the green channel." / "She went through the red channel because she had purchased items over the limit."

Easy to confuse — precision distinctions

Don't confuse…DistinctionExample
boarding pass vs ticket A ticket is your proof of purchase and your right to travel. A boarding pass is issued at check-in and is what you show to actually board. You can have a ticket without a boarding pass — but not vice versa. "I have my ticket confirmation on my email, but I still need to check in to get my boarding pass."
hand luggage vs checked baggage Hand luggage (carry-on) travels with you in the cabin. Checked baggage goes into the hold (cargo area) of the aircraft — you don't see it again until the carousel at arrivals. "My laptop is in my hand luggage, but my clothes are in my checked bag."
red-eye vs overnight flight A red-eye is specifically a flight that departs late at night and arrives early morning. An overnight flight is any flight during which you sleep — even one that departs at 10 p.m. and lands at 8 a.m. "The 11 p.m. departure is technically a red-eye — it gets in at 5 a.m."
turbulence vs bumpy Turbulence is the technical/official noun (used in announcements and reports). Bumpy is the informal adjective (used in conversation). Same phenomenon, different register. Official: "We are currently experiencing moderate turbulence." / Casual: "That was such a bumpy flight!"
check in (verb) vs check-in (noun/adj) Two words as a verb: "I need to check in." Hyphenated as a noun or modifier: "the check-in desk," "the check-in time." "I forgot to check in online, so I had to queue at the check-in counter."
conveyor belt (security) vs conveyor belt (check-in) vs carousel (arrivals) Same physical mechanism — a moving belt — but the name and location differ. At security, it's always "conveyor belt." At check-in, also "conveyor belt." At arrivals, the rotating ring that delivers bags is specifically a "carousel." "I put my tray on the conveyor belt at security, then watched my suitcase disappear onto the conveyor belt at check-in — and found it on carousel 5 at the other end."

Notice & discover — look back at the dialogue

Count how many times the agent uses a modal verb. For each one, decide: is it obligation, advice, permission, or possibility? Start with Priya's turn 7 and the gate agent's turns 15, 17, and 19.

Turn 7: "must keep" (obligation) · "might check" (possibility) · "should be fine" (confident advice). Turn 15: "should still have enough time" (advice/probability). Turn 17: "can check" (permission/ability) · "might be able to tell" (possibility). Turn 19: "may be some turbulence" (uncertain possibility) · "make sure you fasten" (instruction). Notice how much work these modals do — even a short airport conversation contains at least ten.

Farid asks four indirect questions across the dialogue. Find them all. For each one, identify the direct question he's turning into a polite indirect one.

(1) Turn 6: "Could you tell me if I need to weigh my carry-on?" ← direct: "Do I need to weigh my carry-on?" (2) Turn 10: "I was wondering whether I need to take my laptop out at security." ← direct: "Do I need to take my laptop out?" (3) Turn 12: "Could you tell me where the security lane is?" ← direct: "Where is the security lane?" (4) Turn 14: "I was wondering whether the delay is going to affect my connection." ← direct: "Is the delay going to affect my connection?" All four use the correct word order: subject before verb, no inversion.

Find three uses of all / most / some in the dialogue. For each one, decide: is it making a general statement, or referring to a specific identifiable group?

Turn 5: "Most passengers find it easier to just take out a few things" — general statement about passengers in general, not a specific group. / Turn 7: "All passengers must keep hand luggage under 10 kg" — general rule applying to all passengers as a category. / Turn 17: "Some passengers with tight connections are also allowed to exit first" — general category (passengers with tight connections), not pointing to specific individuals. All three use the noun directly (no "of the") because they are making general rules and observations, not pointing to specific people in front of them.

The PA announcement says: "Most passengers should remain in the departure lounge." Why "most" rather than "all"? And why "should" rather than "must"?

"Most" rather than "all" — because some passengers (like Farid, who goes to speak to a gate agent) have a legitimate reason to approach the desk. The announcement is polite; it can't really enforce everyone staying seated. / "Should" rather than "must" — because the announcement is a recommendation, not a legal obligation. The airport cannot force you to sit down. "Must" would suggest there's a rule with consequences for breaking it. This is the same must/should distinction you discovered in the notice board.
