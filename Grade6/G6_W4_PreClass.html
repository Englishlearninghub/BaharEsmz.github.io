Advice column "Ask Maya" — Friendship Compatibility

Dear Maya, my closest friend and I are total opposites — she's outgoing, I'm reserved — and lately I've been wondering if our friendship can really last. Is compatibility about being similar, or something else?

Maya's answer: Opposite personalities can absolutely work. Think about your friend's strengths and your own — an outgoing person often brings out a quieter friend's confidence, while a reserved person gives an outgoing friend space to slow down. That balance is often a relationship's greatest strength, not a weakness.

What actually predicts whether a friendship's foundation is solid isn't shared personality traits at all — it's shared values. Ask yourself: do you trust her judgment? Does she respect yours? My sister's husband and I are nothing alike, yet he's become one of my closest confidants, because we both value honesty above comfort.

One last thought: don't confuse a quiet friend's silence for disinterest. My best friend's way of showing she cares looks completely different from mine, and learning to read that difference, rather than judging it, transformed our entire friendship.

— Maya

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look back at the highlighted phrases

Detective work: who owns what?

Look at the coral words: "our," "your friend's," "your own," "her," "yours," "my best friend's." Some of these have an apostrophe and "s" attached to a noun, others are standalone words with no noun attached at all. Sort them into those two groups.

Apostrophe + noun: "your friend's," "my best friend's." Standalone words: "our," "your own," "her," "yours." The second group are possessive pronouns/determiners; they never take an apostrophe.

Compare "your friend's strengths" with "yours" in "does she respect yours?" Both show possession, but one is followed directly by a noun and one stands completely alone. What do you think "yours" actually means here, in full?

"Yours" = "your judgment" — it replaces the noun entirely so it isn't repeated. This is exactly like "mine," "his," "hers," "ours," "theirs."

Look at the blue phrase "a relationship's greatest strength" and the purple phrase "my sister's husband." Both use apostrophe-s on a singular noun. Now imagine the noun were plural instead — "my two sisters" — where do you think the apostrophe would move?

After the "s": "my two sisters' husbands" — the apostrophe moves to the end of the existing plural "-s," rather than adding a new one.

STUDY

Now let's name it: possessive forms

1. Apostrophe-S vs. Possessive Pronouns

Two different ways to show ownership — you already sorted them yourself above.

✓ Singular noun + 's: my best friend's way of showing she cares One friend, so apostrophe before the "s." Found in: advice column, paragraph 4
✓ Plural noun ending in -s + ': my two sisters' husbands More than one sister, so the apostrophe goes after the existing "s," with no extra "s" added.
✓ Possessive pronoun (stands alone): Does she respect yours? Replaces "your judgment" entirely — never takes an apostrophe. Found in: advice column, paragraph 3
Possessive determiner (+ noun)Possessive pronoun (stands alone)
my friendmine
your judgmentyours
her silencehers
our friendshipours
their valuestheirs

singular noun add 's (the dog's owner)

plural noun ending in -s add only ' (the dogs' owner)

irregular plural (no -s) add 's as normal (the children's toys)

possessive pronoun never an apostrophe (it's hers, not her's)

✗ This book is her's. Wrong — possessive pronouns never take an apostrophe: This book is hers.
✗ Its a great friendship. Classic confusion — "it's" (with apostrophe) means "it is." The possessive form has no apostrophe: Its foundation is solid.

1b. The "double possessive": a friend of mine

A third pattern, used specifically to introduce one of several — very common in spoken English about relationships.

Plain possessive

My friend called me yesterday.

Sounds like your only friend, or at least a specific one the listener should already know.

Double possessive

A friend of mine called me yesterday.

Clearly just one of several friends — new information, not someone already known to the listener.

PatternExampleUse it when...
a/an + noun + of + possessive pronouna colleague of hers, a cousin of oursIntroducing one member of a group, for the first time
this/that + noun + of + possessive pronounthat habit of his, this idea of yoursOften adds emotional color (affection, irritation, admiration)
✓ a friend of mine is getting married next month. Equivalent in meaning to "one of my friends," but more natural in spoken introductions.
✓ That stubbornness of his drives me crazy, but I love him for it. The double possessive here adds a warm, slightly exasperated tone — common when describing a familiar trait.
✗ A friend of me is getting married. Wrong — the second half must be a possessive pronoun (mine/yours/his/hers/ours/theirs), never an object pronoun: a friend of mine.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Two words, one idea

Detective work: building a compound word

Look at: brother-in-law, best friend, go-to, soulmate, grandparents. Some have a hyphen, some are written as two separate words, and one is written as a single fused word. Without checking a dictionary, can you guess which spelling rule is most reliable for a new word you're unsure about?

There isn't a fully reliable rule — compound spelling is genuinely inconsistent in English and changes over time (many fused words like "soulmate" started as two words). When unsure, a dictionary check is the only sure way.

Look at "best friend" (two separate words) and "soulmate" (one fused word). Both describe a similarly close relationship. Does the spelling style change the meaning at all, or just the convention?

Just the convention — meaning isn't affected by spacing or hyphenation. This is exactly why compounds are a vocabulary-memorization challenge rather than a grammar rule to apply.

"Brother-in-law" and "go-to" both use hyphens. What part of speech is "go-to" functioning as in "is she still your go-to person"?

An adjective, describing "person" — even though "go" and "to" are normally a verb and preposition. Compounding can shift a phrase into an entirely different word class.

Now let's name it: compound words

2. Compound Words

Two (or more) words combined to express a single idea — with three possible spelling styles.

brother + in + law = brother-in-law
soul + mate = soulmate
best + friend = best friend
StyleExampleNotes
Open (two words)best friend, close friendMost common for newer or descriptive compounds
Hyphenatedbrother-in-law, go-toCommon for family terms and compound adjectives
Closed (one word)soulmate, grandparentOften older, well-established compounds

noun + noun best friend, soulmate, grandparent

adjective + noun close-knit (family), best man

verb/preposition combos go-to, get-together

✗ He is my brotherinlaw. Wrong — "brother-in-law" always keeps its hyphens; it never fuses into one word. When in doubt about a specific compound's spelling, check a dictionary rather than guessing.
PRONUNCIATION

Stress tells you it's one idea, not two

Compound nouns usually stress the FIRST word

This is one of the most useful, underused pronunciation patterns in English.

Compare these two phrases, which are spelled almost the same but mean something completely different:

GREENhouse /ˈɡriːn.haʊs/ — a glass building for plants

green HOUSE /ɡriːn ˈhaʊs/ — a house that is painted green

The compound noun ("greenhouse," one idea, a specific type of structure) stresses the first part. The adjective + noun phrase ("green house," just a house that happens to be green) stresses the noun instead, as usual. Try saying "BEST friend" out loud — the stress on "best" signals it's a fixed relationship term, not just any friend who happens to be good.

Compound adjectives usually stress the SECOND word

This is the opposite pattern from compound nouns — worth contrasting directly.

Many personality adjectives in English are themselves compounds — and they typically stress the second part, not the first:

close-KNIT /ˌkloʊs ˈnɪt/ — tightly bonded (family, group)

easyGOING /ˌiːzi ˈɡoʊɪŋ/ — relaxed, hard to upset

good-LOOKING /ˌɡʊd ˈlʊkɪŋ/ — physically attractive

Compare that to compound nouns like BESTfriend or GREENhouse, which stress the first part instead. A rough rule of thumb: compound nouns → stress first; compound adjectives → stress second. It's not airtight, but it will get you the right rhythm most of the time, and a wrong stress pattern here is one of the most common ways learners sound less fluent than they actually are.

VOCABULARY & COLLOCATIONS

Personality, relationships & your first collocations

3. Make or Do? Your first collocation pair

This is the beginning of a new strand you'll build on for the rest of the course: certain verbs simply "go with" certain nouns, with no logical reason — you just have to learn the pair. Make and do are the most common confusion at this level.

MAKE

  • make an effort
  • make a promise
  • make friends
  • make time (for someone)
  • make a decision

DO

  • do a favor
  • do your best
  • do some thinking
  • do the dishes
  • do well (in a relationship)

Rough pattern (not a strict rule): "make" often relates to creating or producing something new; "do" often relates to performing an activity or task. The pattern breaks down often enough that memorizing common pairs is more reliable than applying logic.

4. Informal phrases for romantic relationships

These phrasal verbs and idioms are everyday, informal English — the kind you'd hear in conversation or in a TV show, not in a formal letter. They tend to follow a rough real-life order, from first noticing someone to a long-term commitment.

1
to flirt (with someone)
to behave playfully in a way that signals romantic interest
2
to hit on someone
to approach someone directly with romantic/sexual interest
3
to ask someone out
to invite someone on a date
4
to fall for someone
to start developing strong romantic feelings
5
to fall in love
to reach a deep, established state of love
6
to settle down
to commit to a stable, long-term relationship

Two more worth knowing: to have a crush on someone (a strong but often short-lived infatuation, common among teenagers but used at any age) and to break up (to end a romantic relationship) — the realistic opposite ending to this timeline.

Why this matters

Collocations and compound spelling are exactly the kind of thing no grammar rule can fully predict — native speakers learn them through repeated exposure, not logic. Building the habit of checking a dictionary or collocation tool now, rather than guessing, is what will keep your vocabulary accurate as it grows.
