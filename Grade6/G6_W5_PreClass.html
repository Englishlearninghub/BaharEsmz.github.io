IT notice Northgate Systems — Scheduled Maintenance Notice

To all staff: Please be advised that our project management platform will undergo scheduled maintenance this Friday from 22:00 to 02:00. During this window, users will be unable to log in to the system.

Before the maintenance window begins, all staff are required to back up any unsaved work and log out of active sessions. The IT department will carry out the necessary updates remotely; no action is required from staff beyond saving and logging out.

Once the update has been completed, the system will come back online automatically, and staff will be able to log in as normal from Saturday morning. If the platform does not come back online by 08:00, please turn off your device, wait two minutes, and try again before contacting the helpdesk.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look back at the highlighted phrases

Detective work: verb plus small word

Look at: log in, back up, log out, carry out, come back, turn off, find out. Each one is a verb plus one or two short words like "in," "up," "out," "off," "back." Do you think "log in" means the same thing as "log" alone?

No — "log" alone usually means to record something (like a ship's log), while "log in" means to access a system with credentials. The small word completely changes the meaning; this combination is called a phrasal verb.

Compare "log in" and "log out" — same verb, opposite particle. What do you notice about how the particle alone seems to carry the opposite meaning?

The particles "in" and "out" create an opposite pair, just like "turn on" / "turn off." Many phrasal verbs come in such opposite pairs — learning them together can help you remember both at once.

This whole notice is written in a formal register (passive voice, "please be advised," no contractions). Do you think phrasal verbs are only used informally, or can they appear in formal writing too?

Phrasal verbs absolutely appear in formal writing — "carry out," "log in," and "come back" are all completely natural here. What changes between registers is usually the surrounding grammar (passive voice, no contractions, no slang) rather than avoiding phrasal verbs altogether.

STUDY

Now let's name it: phrasal verbs

1. What is a phrasal verb?

A verb plus one or more particles (small words like up, out, on, off, back) that together create a meaning often very different from the verb alone.

✓ find out = to discover information Not the literal meaning of "find" + "out" combined — the phrase has its own fixed meaning. Found in: IT notice, paragraph 4
✓ come back = to return A simple, extremely common phrasal verb — one of the most frequent in everyday English. Found in: IT notice, paragraph 3

According to corpus research on contemporary English (the Corpus of Contemporary American English, or COCA), phrasal verbs built on a small set of common verbs and particles account for a disproportionate share of everyday English. The eight verbs you'll focus on this week — find out, come back, go on, turn on/off, pick up, give up, look up, carry on — are consistently identified in this research as among the most frequent phrasal verbs in English, which is exactly why they're worth mastering early.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

-ing or "to"? It can change the meaning

Detective work: remember, stop, try

Maya writes "i remember turning it off last night." Does this mean she has a memory of the action of turning it off, or that she didn't forget to do it as a future task?

It means she has a memory of the action itself — she can picture herself doing it. "Remember + -ing" looks back at a memory of something already done.

Maya says "i stopped checking notifications." Did she pause her phone-checking in order to do something else, or did she completely quit checking notifications?

She completely quit the activity of checking notifications. "Stop + -ing" means ending an activity entirely — compare this to "stop to do something," which means pausing one activity in order to do a different one.

Jordan says "did you try to turn it off... properly." Is "try to turn it off" describing an experiment to see if something works, or an enjoyable activity Maya is sampling?

An experiment or attempt to solve a problem. "Try + to-infinitive" means making an effort at something difficult or uncertain — compare this to "try + -ing," which means testing out an option just to see what happens, with less at stake.

STUDY

Now let's name it: gerunds vs. infinitives

2. Verbs that change meaning: remember, stop, try

Most verbs just pick one pattern (gerund or infinitive) and stick with it. These three are different — the pattern you choose changes what you mean.

Verb+ -ing (gerund)+ to (infinitive)
remember looking back at a memory of something already done
I remember turning it off. (I have a memory of doing it)		 not forgetting a future obligation
Remember to turn it off! (don't forget to do it)
stop ending an activity completely
She stopped checking her phone. (she no longer checks it)		 pausing one activity to do another
She stopped to check her phone. (she paused to look at it)
try testing something out, low stakes
Try restarting it. (just see what happens)		 making an effort at something difficult
Try to restart it. (it might not work, but attempt it)

remember + -ing

I remember turning it off.

A memory of an action that already happened.

remember + to

Remember to turn it off.

A reminder about a future obligation, not yet done.

remember/stop/try + -ing the activity itself, often already happened

remember/stop/try + to a future obligation, a pause, or a difficult attempt

✗ I stopped to use my phone so much this year. If the meaning is "I quit the habit entirely," this needs the gerund: I stopped using my phone so much this year. "Stopped to use" would mean she paused something else in order to use her phone.
✗ Did you remember to turn it off last night? (when you actually want to ask about her memory of the action) If you're asking whether she has a memory of doing it (not whether she forgot a task), the gerund is needed: Do you remember turning it off last night?
PRONUNCIATION

Where does the stress go in a phrasal verb?

Stress the particle, not just the verb

This is a small detail that makes a big difference to how natural you sound.

In most two-word phrasal verbs, the particle carries equal or greater stress than the verb itself — this is different from many learners' instinct to stress only the main verb.

I'll look it UP. /lʊk ɪt ˈʌp/ — both syllables carry real stress

Just carry ON. /ˈkæri ɒn/ — "on" is not swallowed or reduced

Compare this to a normal preposition, which is usually unstressed and quick: "look at the screen" reduces "at" almost to nothing, but "look up" (search for) keeps "up" clear and stressed. If a listener can't tell whether you stressed the particle, they may not register that you used a phrasal verb at all.

VOCABULARY EXPANSION

Technology, slang & speaking formally vs. informally

3. Formal vs. informal: the same idea, two registers

Technology is a great place to notice register shifts, because the same situation gets described very differently depending on who you're talking to.

Formal (IT notice, workplace email)

  • Please be advised that...
  • carry out (scheduled) maintenance
  • the system will resume normal operation
  • we apologise for any inconvenience
  • no action is required

Informal (texting a friend)

  • ugh, it's acting up again
  • did you try turning it off and on again?
  • it's being so glitchy rn
  • my bad, lol
  • no worries, np
