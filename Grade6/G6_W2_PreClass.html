NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look back at the highlighted phrases

Detective work: three ways to talk about the future

Look at the coral phrases: "are going to launch," "is going to become," "are going to remove." These all describe decisions the bank has already made, with a clear plan behind them. What form comes right after "going to" in every case?

The base form of the verb (launch, become, remove) — "going to" + base verb describes a decided plan or intention.

Now look at the blue phrases: "is changing," "is running." One refers to something happening this Friday; the other refers to something happening "this time next week." Both use the same -ing structure as the present continuous you already know for "now." Why do you think this same form can describe the future too?

Because both uses share the same core meaning: something fixed, scheduled, or already arranged — whether that's happening right now or at a confirmed point in the future.

Finally, look at the purple phrases: "will probably rise," "will complete." The text explicitly says the contactless limit decision "isn't final yet." What does that tell you about when we choose "will" instead of "going to"?

"Will" is for predictions, guesses, and things that are not yet a fixed plan — the opposite of the decided, arranged feeling of "going to" and the present continuous.

STUDY

Now let's name it: three future forms, three jobs

1. Going to vs. Present Continuous vs. Will

All three talk about the future — but they answer different questions about how certain or fixed the event is.

✓ Going to + base verb: We are going to remove the old budgeting tool. A decision or intention already made, with no fixed schedule attached yet. Found in: app notice, paragraph 3
✓ Present Continuous: The login screen is changing this Friday. A fixed arrangement with a specific time or date already confirmed — like an appointment in a diary. Found in: app notice, paragraph 2
✓ Will + base verb: Contactless limits will probably rise to £200. A prediction, guess, or opinion about the future — not yet a confirmed plan. Found in: app notice, paragraph 4

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Those short question phrases have a name

Detective work: building the tag

Look at: "That's going to affect my pass, isn't it?" The main sentence is positive ("That's going to affect"). Is the little question at the end positive or negative?

Negative ("isn't it"). A positive statement gets a negative tag.

Now look at: "You're not going to lose anything there, are you?" This time the main sentence is negative ("You're not going to lose"). What happens to the tag?

It flips to positive ("are you"). Negative statement, positive tag — the two parts always have opposite polarity.

Look closely at the verb in the tag each time: "isn't it" matches "that's" (is), "are you" matches "you're" (are), "haven't you" matches "you've" (have), "won't it" matches "will replace" (will). What's the pattern connecting the main verb to the verb used in the tag?

The tag always repeats the auxiliary verb (or modal) from the main sentence — it doesn't introduce a new verb. If the main sentence has no obvious auxiliary (e.g. a simple present/past verb), the tag uses do/does/did instead.

STUDY

Now let's name it: tag questions

2. Tag Questions

A short question added to the end of a statement, used to check information or invite agreement — you found the pattern yourself in the chat above.

Main sentenceTagRule
That's going to affect my pass,isn't it?positive statement → negative tag
You're not going to lose anything,are you?negative statement → positive tag
The app will replace them,won't it?repeat the modal (will → won't)
You've already tested it,haven't you?repeat the auxiliary (have → haven't)
She works at the bank, doesn't she? (no auxiliary in the main clause → use do/does/did)
They didn't update the app, did they? (past simple, negative → positive tag with did)

Rule 1 positive statement, negative tag — and vice versa

Rule 2 the tag always repeats the auxiliary/modal verb already in the sentence

Rule 3 no auxiliary verb present? Borrow do/does/did

✗ You like coffee, do you like it? Wrong — the tag must be short, just the auxiliary + pronoun: You like coffee, don't you?
PRONUNCIATION

Two completely different meanings, same words

Rising tag = a real question. Falling tag = you're already sure.

The intonation of a tag question changes its entire meaning — this is one of the most important pronunciation features in spoken English.

Say this with your voice rising at the end, like a genuine question (you really don't know the answer):

You haven't used the new app yet, have you? ↗ /hæv juː/ rising

Now say the same words with your voice falling, like a comment you're confident about (you're just confirming what you already believe):

You haven't used the new app yet, have you? ↘ /hæv juː/ falling

Rosa's tag questions in the chat above ("isn't it?", "haven't you?") were mostly falling — she was fairly confident, just checking. A rising tag on the same words would sound much more uncertain, almost worried.

VOCABULARY

Financial vocabulary: beyond the basics

Why this matters

The bank app notice and the dialogue below use everyday grammar — but the content words around money are what make you sound natural in real banking situations. This section gives you the vocabulary you'll actually need: at the counter, on a call, or reading a statement.

Paying: the different ways

When you buy something, especially a large purchase, you have choices about how to pay. These terms appear constantly in bank letters, credit-card apps, and conversations at the counter.

interest ratenoun phrase
The percentage a bank charges you for borrowing money, or pays you for saving. Usually shown as an annual percentage (e.g. 19.9% APR).
"What's the interest rate on this credit card?" / "The interest rate on your savings account is 4.5% per year."
pay in fullverb phrase
To pay the entire amount at once, with nothing left owing. If you pay a credit-card bill in full each month, you pay no interest.
"I always pay my credit card in full by the due date." / "You can avoid interest charges by paying the balance in full."
pay in instalmentsverb phrase
To pay a large amount in smaller regular payments spread over time. The total cost is often higher than paying in full because of interest.
"She's paying for the laptop in 12 monthly instalments." / "You can pay in instalments, but there's an extra charge."
minimum paymentnoun phrase
The smallest amount you must pay on a credit card each month to avoid a penalty fee. Paying only the minimum means you keep paying interest on the rest.
"The minimum payment on this card is £25 or 1% of the balance, whichever is higher." / "Warning: making only the minimum payment means it will take years to clear your debt."
outstanding balancenoun phrase
The total amount of money you still owe — what's left unpaid on a loan, credit card, or bill.
"Your outstanding balance is £842.50." / "The interest is calculated on your outstanding balance each day."
credit limitnoun phrase
The maximum amount of money a bank will let you borrow on a credit card or overdraft. Spending beyond it triggers penalty fees.
"Your credit limit has been increased to £3,000." / "I went over my credit limit last month and was charged a fee."
due datenoun phrase
The deadline by which a payment must be received. Missing the due date usually means a late-payment fee and a mark on your credit record.
"Your payment is due on the 15th of each month." / "Set a reminder before the due date so you don't forget."
penalty fee / late feenoun phrase
A charge added to your account when you miss a payment, go over your credit limit, or break a condition of your account.
"There's a £12 penalty fee for missing a payment." / "I was charged a late fee because the payment arrived one day after the due date."

Managing an account: key terms

direct debitnoun phrase
An instruction to your bank to let another organisation (e.g. a utility company or gym) take agreed amounts from your account automatically. The amount can vary.
"My rent goes out by direct debit on the 1st of every month." / "You can set up a direct debit to pay your electricity bill automatically."
standing ordernoun phrase
An instruction you give your bank to pay a fixed amount to someone at regular intervals. Unlike a direct debit, you control it, not the company receiving the money.
"I have a standing order for £200 a month to my savings account." / "She cancelled the standing order when she moved out."
bank statementnoun phrase
An official record (paper or digital) of all transactions in your account over a period — usually one month. It shows deposits, withdrawals, direct debits, and your closing balance.
"I noticed an unfamiliar charge on my bank statement." / "Can you send me a paper statement for the last three months?"
transactionnoun
Any movement of money in or out of an account — a purchase, transfer, withdrawal, or deposit. The plural "transactions" is very common in banking contexts.
"I can see the transaction was processed at 14:32." / "There are three transactions I don't recognise on this statement."
APR (Annual Percentage Rate)noun / acronym
The true yearly cost of borrowing, including interest and fees, expressed as a percentage. Used to compare credit cards and loans fairly.
"This credit card has a 21.9% APR." / "Always compare the APR, not just the monthly payment, when choosing a loan."
to dispute a chargeverb phrase
To formally tell your bank that you don't recognise or agree with a payment on your account, and to ask them to investigate.
"I'd like to dispute a charge I don't recognise." / "You have 60 days to dispute a transaction under consumer protection rules."
loan repaymentnoun phrase
A regular payment made to pay back a loan — usually monthly. Each payment reduces your outstanding balance and covers some of the interest.
"My monthly loan repayment is £350." / "If you miss a loan repayment, contact the bank immediately."
to be in the red / in the blackidiom
In the red = your account has a negative balance (you owe money). In the black = your balance is positive. Both come from old accounting when red ink meant debt.
"My account is £200 in the red after that payment." / "Finally — after three months, the account is back in the black."

Easy to confuse — precision distinctions

These pairs are common sources of error, even at B1–B2 level:

Don't confuse…DistinctionExample
interest rate vs APR The interest rate is the basic borrowing rate. The APR includes fees too — it's always higher and gives a fairer comparison. "The interest rate is 18% but the APR is 21.9% because of the annual fee."
direct debit vs standing order With a direct debit, the company requests the money (amount may vary). With a standing order, you instruct the bank (fixed amount). "My gym takes a direct debit — the amount changed when I upgraded my membership. My savings transfer is a standing order — always exactly £150."
pay in full vs minimum payment Paying in full clears the debt — no interest charged. Paying only the minimum keeps the debt alive and interest accumulates on the rest. "If you only make the minimum payment of £25, you'll pay £18 in interest this month — and still owe £817."
outstanding balance vs credit limit Outstanding balance = what you currently owe. Credit limit = the maximum you're allowed to borrow. The difference is your available credit. "My credit limit is £2,000 and my outstanding balance is £650 — so I have £1,350 of available credit."

Collocations: financial language in action

These are the most natural verb + noun combinations for money and banking. Learning them as fixed pairs is faster than building them word by word.

We say "make a payment" — not "do a payment." Interest is something you "pay" or "charge" or "accrue" — not something you "make."

Notice & discover — now look back at the dialogue

James says: "What I'm going to do is raise a formal dispute." / "I'm also going to cancel that direct debit."
Why does he use "going to" here rather than "will"? What does it signal to Maya?
"Going to" signals a decision he has just made and is committed to — it reassures Maya that these are concrete actions he will take right now, not vague promises. "Will" would sound more uncertain and less committed in this context.
James uses two tag questions: "you've been paying in full every month, haven't you?" and "It's worth considering, isn't it?"
Are these the same type of tag question? Is he checking a fact — or making a gentle suggestion?
The first ("haven't you") is a genuine information check — James is looking at her account and confirming what he sees. The second ("isn't it") is a soft sales suggestion — inviting Maya to agree with him rather than genuinely asking. In a bank setting, this is a common polite technique to recommend a product without being pushy. The intonation would fall on both — they're confident, not uncertain.
Maya says: "the dispute on the £134 — I'll hear back within ten days, won't I?"
She's just been told this. So why is she using a tag question at all?
She's confirming what she understood — a common conversational function of tag questions. This is not uncertainty; she's recapping to make sure she hasn't misunderstood something important. With falling intonation, it means: "I'm pretty sure that's right — just checking." This is extremely natural in service encounters.
Find three different future forms in James's turn 7 (starting "It's possible…"). What does each one express?
(1) "I'm going to raise a formal dispute" — going to: decided plan, immediate intention. (2) "The investigation will take up to ten business days" — will: a factual future prediction (standard bank policy, not a personal plan). (3) "the money will be refunded" — will: a confident prediction / promise about a future outcome. (4) "I'm also going to cancel that direct debit" — going to again: another immediate commitment. Notice how "going to" is used for James's own actions (his plan), while "will" is used for general outcomes and policies.
