2Notice the Language (Before Any Rule)

You've just read how Elena and Diego introduced themselves on their first day. Before we name any grammar rules, let's look closely at the exact words they used — noticing the pattern yourself makes the rule much easier to remember later.

Look again at the highlighted words in Sam, Elena and Diego's conversation. Talk to a partner about these questions.

Q1. Find every "I'm" in the conversation. "I'm" is short for two words. Which two words?
Q2. Sam asks "How are you?" and "Where are you from?" Which word — "am", "is", or "are" — goes with "you"?
Q3. Find "I've got three children" and "I've got two brothers". What do you think "I've got" means?
Q4. Elena is a woman's name, and Diego is a man's name. In the conversation, do we ever use "he" or "she"? Why do you think not, yet?

Talk about your answers before you go to Section 3.

3Grammar Focus

You noticed that Elena and Diego both said "I'm...", and that Sam asked "Are you...?" Now let's turn those patterns into clear rules — ones you can use to talk about yourself, or anyone else, not just Elena and Diego.

3.1 — The Verb "To Be"

"To be" is the most important verb in English. It changes depending on who we are talking about.

Am, is, or are?
"I am Elena." "You are Diego." "She is happy." "We are students."
PositiveNegativeQuestion
I am (I'm) tired.I am not (I'm not) tired.Am I late?
You are (you're) happy.You aren't happy.Are you happy?
She is (she's) from Spain.She isn't from Spain.Is she from Spain?

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we say "I is" or "I am"? I am.
Do we say "you is" or "you are"? You are.
Do we say "she are" or "she is"? She is.

3.2 — Have Got

Elena and Diego didn't only talk about names and countries — they also talked about their families: "I've got three children," "I've got two brothers." We use "have got" to talk about family, possessions, and things we have.

SubjectFormExample
I / you / we / they've gotI've got three children.
he / she / it's gotShe's got two brothers.
negativehaven't/hasn't gotI haven't got a sister.
questionHave/Has...got?Have you got children?

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we say "I's got" or "I've got"? I've got.
Do we say "he've got" or "he's got"? He's got.

7Error Clinic

Even careful learners make small mistakes with "to be" and "have got" when they're new to English. Here are five sentences a student might write — read each one. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "I are from Poland."
    Incorrect — "I" needs "am": "I am from Poland."
  2. "She have got two brothers."
    Incorrect — "she" needs "has got": "She has got two brothers."
  3. "He is happy."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "He" correctly takes "is". Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "You is tired."
    Incorrect — "you" needs "are": "You are tired."
  5. "I've got three children."
    Also correct. "I've got" correctly matches "I". A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

8Your Turn: Writing Task

Now it's your turn to do exactly what Elena and Diego did on their first day — introduce yourself in writing.

Write 3–4 short sentences (about 20–30 words) about yourself. Say your name, where you're from, your nationality, and one feeling. Use "I'm" and "I've got" if you can.

0 words
Self-check before you submit: Did you write "I'm" (not "I is")? Did you use a capital letter at the start of your name and country?

🗣Speaking Practice

This week you say hello, say your name, and say where you are from — out loud. Use the voice memo app on your phone. Speaking to your phone is easy practice!

My introduction

30 seconds: this is me

Say your introduction. Four sentences:

  • "Hello! My name is…"
  • "I'm from…" (your country)
  • "I live in…" (your city)
  • "Nice to meet you!"
How: Record it. Listen. Record it again — a little faster and with a smile (you can hear a smile!). Check: I'm = "I am". Say it as ONE word: "I'm from…"
Roleplay: first meeting

Hello, what's your name?

Person A asks: "Hi! What's your name? Where are you from?"

Person B answers: "I'm… I'm from… And you?"

Then change! Person B asks, Person A answers.

How: With a partner — or play both people yourself with two voices. Record 1–2 minutes. Check: "And you?" — two small words that keep every conversation alive. Did you use them?
Shadowing drill

Copy the classroom phrases

Find the Classroom Language section in this unit. Choose 4 phrases (for example: "Can you repeat that, please?" — "How do you spell it?"). Read each one aloud three times: slowly → normal → friendly and natural.

How: Record the third time. Check: questions go UP at the end — "Can you repeat that? ↗" These phrases are your tools for EVERY class. Learn them with your voice, not only your eyes.

Tips for your first English words

  • Say your introduction every day in front of the mirror. 30 seconds. After one week, it comes out by itself.
  • Speak slowly. Slow and clear is GOOD English.
  • Practise spelling your name aloud: "M-A-R-I-A." People will ask you — be ready!

9Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

You've learned a lot this week — from Elena and Diego's first conversation to writing your own introduction! Great learners keep noticing English outside of class too. Try one or more of these tasks before your next lesson.

  • Flag hunt: Find 3 more country flags online. Write the country name and the nationality word for each.
  • Count around you: Count 10 things you can see right now, out loud, in English.
  • Mirror practice: Look in a mirror and say 3 sentences about yourself using "I'm" and "I've got".
  • Listening hunt: Watch a 1-minute video of someone introducing themselves in English. Listen for "I'm" and "I've got".
