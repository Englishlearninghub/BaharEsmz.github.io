1Context First: Read Before Any Rule

Read the conversation about the class schedule. Just try to understand. We look at the grammar after.

Class Schedule
Sam
OK, everyone! Our class is on Mondays and Wednesdays, at 7 o'clock.
Elena
Great! What about your other classes, Diego?
Diego
My work starts at half past eight, every day. It's busy!
Sam
OK, open your books, everyone! I need two pens and three books for the group table.
Elena
Diego, can you give me a pen, please?
Diego
Sure! Here — this is my pen. I'll give it to you now.
Sam
Thanks! And Yuki, can you give them to us — the books on your table?
Yuki
Of course! Here you go.

Notice: Sam, Elena, Diego and Yuki use "my/your/our" to talk about things that belong to them. Words like "pens" and "books" have an extra "-s" because there's more than one. Small words like "me", "you", "them" and "us" come after verbs.

2Notice the Language (Before Any Rule)

You've just read how Sam, Elena, Diego and Yuki talked about their schedule and shared classroom things. Before we name any grammar rules, let's look closely at the exact words they used — this will make the rules much easier to understand.

Look again at the highlighted words. Talk to a partner about these questions.

Q1. Find "pens" and "books". What extra letter do you see at the end? What does this letter tell you?
Q2. Find "our class", "my work", "your books". Who does "our" belong to? Who does "my" belong to?
Q3. Find "give me a pen", "give it to you", "give them to us". These small words (me, you, them, us) come after the verb "give". Are these the same as "I", "you", "they", "we"?

Talk about your answers before you go to Section 3.

3Grammar Focus

You noticed the extra "-s" on "pens" and "books", and that "my/your/our" show who things belong to. Now let's turn those patterns into clear rules you can use with any object, not just Sam's classroom things.

3.1 — Plural Nouns

RuleExample
Most nouns: + -spen → pens, book → books
Noun ends in -s, -sh, -ch, -x: + -esbox → boxes, watch → watches
Irregular plurals (no rule, just learn them)child → children, man → men, woman → women

🔎 Check Your Understanding

What's the plural of "pen"? Pens.
What's the plural of "child"? Children (irregular).

3.2 — Possessive Adjectives

Sam said "our class," Elena asked about "your other classes," and Diego talked about "my work." These small words show who something belongs to.

SubjectPossessive adjectiveExample
ImyMy pen is blue.
youyourIs this your book?
hehisThis is his bag.
sheherThat's her pen.
ititsThe class has its own room.
weourOur class is on Monday.
theytheirThese are their books.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Which word means "belongs to me"? My.
Which word means "belongs to us"? Our.

3.3 — Object Pronouns

Elena asked Diego to "give me a pen," and Sam asked Yuki to "give them to us." Object pronouns come after a verb (like "give", "help", "call").

Subject pronounObject pronounExample
ImeGive it to me.
youyouI'll give it to you.
hehimCall him, please.
sheherHelp her, please.
weusGive them to us.
theythemGive them to Yuki.

⚠️ Don't Confuse Subject and Object!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
Give it to I.Give it to me.
Call he, please.Call him, please.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we say "give it to I" or "give it to me"? Give it to me.
What's the object pronoun for "he"? Him.

4Telling the Time

Remember how Sam said "our class is on Mondays and Wednesdays, at 7 o'clock," and Diego said his work starts "at half past eight"? Let's look closely at exactly how to say and understand times like these — and a few more useful ones too.

4.1 — The Four Key Times

Every hour has four important "landmark" times. Once you know these four, you can build almost any time.

O'clock, quarter past, half past, quarter to
Notice: after half past, we start counting down to the next hour — "quarter to 4", not "quarter past half".

🔎 Check Your Understanding

"Quarter" means how many minutes? 15 minutes.
Is 3:45 called "quarter to 4" or "quarter to 3"? Quarter to 4 — we look forward to the next hour.

4.2 — Reference List: Minutes Past & To

For times that aren't o'clock, quarter, or half, we use "past" (after the hour) or "to" (before the next hour). Keep this table as a reference.

TimeWe say
3:05five past 3
3:10ten past 3
3:15quarter past 3
3:20twenty past 3
3:25twenty-five past 3
3:30half past 3
3:35twenty-five to 4
3:40twenty to 4
3:45quarter to 4
3:50ten to 4
3:55five to 4

Tip: up to and including 30 minutes, we count "past" the hour. After 30 minutes, we count "to" the next hour.

4.3 — Asking and Answering

QuestionAnswer
What time is it?It's quarter past 3.
What time does the class start?It starts at 7 o'clock.
What time does it finish?It finishes at half past 5.

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we say "It's" or "It has" before a time? It's (It is).
Do we say "at 7 o'clock" or "in 7 o'clock"? At 7 o'clock.

4.4 — Morning, Afternoon, Evening, Night

English usually doesn't use 13:00, 14:00, etc. in speech — instead, we add a part of the day.

🌅 morning (before 12pm) ☀️ afternoon (12pm–6pm) 🌆 evening (6pm–bedtime) 🌙 night (bedtime–early morning)
24-hourWe say
7:007 o'clock in the morning
14:30half past 2 in the afternoon
19:15quarter past 7 in the evening
23:0011 o'clock at night

Note: with "night" we say "at night", not "in the night".

7Error Clinic

Even careful learners mix up plurals and object pronouns when they're new to English. Here are five sentences a student might write — read each one. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "Give it to I."
    Incorrect — after a verb, use the object pronoun: "Give it to me."
  2. "I need two pen."
    Incorrect — "two" needs a plural noun: "I need two pens."
  3. "This is my pen."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "My" correctly shows it belongs to "I". Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Call he, please."
    Incorrect — use the object pronoun: "Call him, please."
  5. "Our class is on Monday."
    Also correct. "Our" and the capital letter on "Monday" are both correct. A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

🗣Speaking Practice

What time is it? What's this? How many? This week your voice learns times, things, and plurals. Voice memo app ready!

Time challenge

Six times, out loud

Say these times from YOUR day — full sentences: "I get up at seven o'clock. My class is at half past six." Up to 60 seconds each:

  • When you get up
  • When you have lunch
  • When your English class starts
  • When you have dinner
  • When you go to bed
  • The time RIGHT NOW (look at a clock!)
How: One recording, timer on. Check: "It's seven o'clock. It's half past two." The time always starts with It's…
Roleplay: this and that

What's this? What are those?

Walk around your room. Point at things near you and far from you:

  • Near: "This is my phone. These are my keys."
  • Far: "That is my bag. Those are my books."

Say at least eight sentences — four near, four far.

How: Really point! Your body helps your brain. Record it. Check the pairs: this → these (near), that → those (far).
Sentence challenge

Plurals: one and many

Say one sentence for each pair — count real things around you! Up to 60 seconds each:

  • a pen → pens
  • a book → books
  • a bag → bags
  • a phone → phones
  • a chair → chairs
  • a key → keys
How: One recording, timer on. Check the -s sound: books = "s", pens/bags = "z". Small sound, big difference — listen for it!

Tips: times and things

  • Say the time aloud every time you look at a clock this week. Ten times a day, free practice.
  • Touch and talk: touching real things while naming them builds strong memory.
  • Numbers first! If big times are hard, practise 1–12 aloud until they are automatic.

9Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

You've learned a lot this week — from Sam's class schedule to writing about your own! Great learners keep noticing English outside of class too. Try one or more of these tasks before your next lesson.

  • Schedule hunt: Write down your real weekly schedule (school, work, gym) using days and times.
  • Plural hunt: Look around you and name 5 objects using their plural forms (e.g. "three chairs").
  • Clock practice: Look at a clock or your phone 3 times today, and say the time out loud in English.
  • Speaking challenge: Ask a friend or classmate to give you an object, using "Can you give it to me, please?"
