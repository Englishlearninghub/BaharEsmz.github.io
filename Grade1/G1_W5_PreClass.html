Our Goodbye Party: Making Conversation

1Context First: Read Before Any Rule

Read how Sam, Elena, Diego and Yuki plan their goodbye party, then what happens at the party itself. Just try to understand. We look at the grammar after.

Planning the Party
Sam
This is our last class! Let's have a small party. What shall we bring?
Elena
I can bring some cake! Where shall we have the party?
Sam
Here, in the classroom. When does everyone finish work on Friday?
Diego
I finish at 5. Who else is coming?
Sam
Just the four of us! Why don't we start at half past six?
Yuki
Perfect! How many people is that... four! I'll bring drinks.
At the Party
Diego
Hi everyone! Good evening!
Elena
Hello, Diego! How are you today?
Diego
I'm great, thanks! Can I have some cake, please?
Elena
Of course! Here you go.
Sam
Yuki, can you pass the drinks, please?
Yuki
Sure, no problem!
Sam
Well, it's getting late. It was nice to meet all of you this term!
Elena
Goodbye, everyone! Take care, and see you soon!
Diego
Bye, all! Thank you for a wonderful term!

Notice: Sam, Elena, Diego and Yuki use question words like "what", "where", "when", "who", "why" and "how" to plan the party. They use "can" to ask for things politely, and lots of different greetings and farewells to open and close the conversation.

2Notice the Language (Before Any Rule)

You've just read how the class planned — and celebrated — their goodbye party. Before we name any grammar rules, let's look closely at the exact words they used — this will make the rules much easier to understand.

Q1. Find "What shall we bring?", "Where shall we have the party?", "When does everyone finish?", "Who else is coming?", "Why don't we start at half past six?" and "How many people is that?" Each question starts with a different question word. What kind of answer does each one need (a place, a time, a person, a reason, a number)?
Q2. Find "Can I have some cake, please?" and "Can you pass the drinks, please?" Who is asking for something for themselves? Who is asking someone else to do something?
Q3. Find every greeting and farewell in the party conversation (hi, hello, good evening, nice to meet you, goodbye, take care, see you soon, bye). Which ones do we say at the start of a conversation? Which ones do we say at the end?

Talk about your answers before you go to Section 3.

3Grammar Focus

You noticed that each question word — what, where, when, who, why, how — asks for a different kind of answer, and that "can" changes meaning depending on who it's for. Now let's turn those patterns into clear rules you can use in any conversation.

3.1 — Wh- Questions

Question words go at the very start of a question, before the verb.

What
a thing
What's your name?
Where
a place
Where do you live?
When
a time
When is the party?
Who
a person
Who is coming?
Why
a reason
Why are you late?
How
a way/manner
How are you?

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Which question word asks about a place? Where.
Which question word asks about a person? Who.
Does the question word go at the start or end of a question? Start.

Here is a fuller reference list of question words, including some useful combinations with "how".

3.2 — Can for Requests

Diego asked "Can I have some cake?" for himself, and Sam asked "Can you pass the drinks?" to ask Yuki to do something. Same word, two different jobs.

UseFormExample
Asking for something for yourselfCan I + verb...?Can I have some cake, please?
Asking someone else to do somethingCan you + verb...?Can you pass the drinks, please?
Positive replySure! / Of course! / Yes, here you go.—
Polite negative replySorry, I can't. / I'm afraid not.—

⚠️ Don't Forget "Please"!

❌ Less Polite✅ More Polite
Can I have cake.Can I have some cake, please?
Can you pass the drinks.Can you pass the drinks, please?

🔎 Check Your Understanding

Do we say "Can I" or "Can you" when we want something for ourselves? Can I.
Do we say "Can I" or "Can you" when we ask someone else to do something? Can you.

6Error Clinic

Even careful learners mix up question words and "can I/you" when they're new to English. Here are five sentences a student might write — read each one. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "Who is the party?"
    Incorrect — asking about a place needs "where": "Where is the party?"
  2. "Can you have some cake, please?" (asking for yourself)
    Incorrect — asking for yourself needs "Can I": "Can I have some cake, please?"
  3. "Can you pass the drinks, please?"
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. Asking someone else to do something correctly uses "Can you". Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "What time the party start?"
    Incorrect — missing "does": "What time does the party start?"
  5. "Good night! Nice to meet you at the party!" (said when arriving)
    Incorrect greeting — "good night" is only for bedtime: "Good evening! Nice to meet you at the party!"

🗣Speaking Practice

It's the goodbye party! This week your voice learns the music of greetings, small talk, and goodbyes — the sentences you will use for the rest of your English life. Voice memo app ready!

Roleplay: at the party

Hello → talk → goodbye

Play a small party conversation, start to finish:

  • Hello: "Hi! How are you?" — "I'm great, thanks! And you?"
  • Talk: ask two questions you know: "Where are you from? What do you do? Do you like the music?"
  • Goodbye: "It was nice talking to you! Take care — see you soon!"
How: With a partner, or both people yourself. Record 2 minutes. Check: a real conversation has three parts — hello, talk, goodbye. Did yours have all three?
Shadowing drill

The greetings melody

Choose 5 greetings from this week's list ("Good morning!" — "Nice to meet you!" — "Take care!"). Say each one aloud three times: slowly → normal → warm and real, like you mean it.

How: Record the third time. Check the melody: greetings go UP and smile — "Good MORning! ↗" A flat greeting sounds sad. Warm voice, warm welcome.
My little speech

30 seconds: thank you and goodbye

Imagine: it's the last day of this course. Make a tiny goodbye speech — 30 seconds:

  • "Thank you, everyone!"
  • One thing you learned: "Now I can talk about my job!"
  • "Good luck — and see you soon!"
How: Record it — then listen to your very first recording from Week 1. Hear the difference? That's five weeks of your voice growing. Keep both recordings!

Tips: keep speaking after the party

  • Greet someone in English every day — a friend, your mirror, your phone. Greetings are muscles: use them.
  • Learn goodbye pairs: "See you soon!" — "Take care!" One says it, one answers it.
  • Your 30-second introduction + your 30-second goodbye = your first real English conversation. You have both now!

8Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

You've learned a lot this week — from planning a party to writing your own! Great learners keep noticing English outside of class too. Try one or more of these tasks before your next lesson.

  • Question hunt: Write 5 questions about a classmate using different question words (what/where/when/who/why/how).
  • Request practice: Practise asking for 3 things politely using "Can I have...?" out loud, at home or in a shop.
  • Greeting hunt: Watch a short video in English. Listen for 3 greetings or farewells and write them down.
  • Speaking challenge: Greet a friend or family member appropriately for the time of day, and say goodbye politely when you leave.
