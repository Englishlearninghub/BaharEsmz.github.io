ENGAGE

How do you describe feeling unwell in English?

Before we start — a quick scenario

Imagine this: a colleague looks exhausted on a Monday morning. You ask "Are you okay?" and she says: "Yeah, I've been having headaches."

That sentence is doing something very specific. It's not saying "I have a headache right now." It's not saying "I had headaches in 2019." It's saying something about a period of time leading up to this moment — an ongoing, repeated experience.

Which of these situations would you most want to describe using that kind of language?

What we're doing this week

You're going to read two documents: a patient consultation record from a GP's surgery and a transcript of a pharmacy counter conversation. Both contain the same people — Maya and her pharmacist, Reza. Read for meaning first. Grammar labels come after. Everything in the grammar sections will point back at sentences you've already read.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the coral phrases — find the pattern

Detective work: figure it out before we name it

Look at the coral phrases: "have been waking up," "have been taking," "have been coughing," "has been feeling," "has been sleeping," "has been experiencing." All of them describe health symptoms. What do they have in common about time — do they describe something that happened once, something finished, or something that started before now and has been continuing?

They all describe an activity that started some time before now and has been happening repeatedly or continuously up to the moment of the consultation. None of them are finished — that's why Dr. Ferrara is still treating Maya.

Compare these two sentences from the notes. What's the difference in what they mean?
a) "I have woken up every night."   b) "I have been waking up every night."

Both are grammatically correct at this level. (a) emphasises the count or result — how many times it happened. (b) emphasises the activity itself as an ongoing process — the experience of it happening again and again. In a medical context, (b) sounds more natural because we care about the ongoing experience, not just the number.

Now notice what's NOT in the notes: you won't find "I have been knowing something," "she has been needing the prescription," or "I have been wanting to feel better." Why do you think those verbs can't take this form?

Know, need, want, and similar verbs describe states — how you permanently or semi-permanently feel or think — not activities you actively perform. You can't "be in the process of knowing" something. This is the key restriction, and we'll study it in detail in the next section.

STUDY

Present Perfect Progressive — the rule behind what you found

1. Present Perfect Progressive

You already noticed the pattern in Dr. Ferrara's notes. Here is the full rule.

Form:

SubjectAuxiliaryMain verb (-ing)Example from the notes
I / you / we / theyhave beenwaking, taking, coughing…I have been waking up every night.
he / she / it / Mayahas beenfeeling, sleeping, experiencing…She has been feeling run-down since February.

What it means — three uses:

1 Duration: an activity that started in the past and is still continuing now.
She has been sleeping fewer than five hours a night. She started doing this in the past and is still doing it. It isn't over. Key time signals: since, for, all week, lately, recently
2 Recent continuous activity with visible result: explains why something is the way it is now.
"You look exhausted." — "I know. I've been working 12-hour days." The explanation (working) has been happening continuously; the result (exhaustion) is visible now.
3 Repeated activity up to now: something happening again and again, not just once.
I have been waking up every night this week. Not a single event — a repeated pattern leading up to today.

⚠ The most important restriction: stative verbs

This is the most commonly made mistake at B2+ and the key corpus teaching point for this week.

Some verbs describe states — mental states, emotions, senses, or possession. These verbs cannot take the progressive form in standard English — not in the present progressive, past progressive, or the present perfect progressive.

❌ Stative — NOT used in progressive forms

  • Thinking/knowing: know, believe, understand, think (= opinion), mean, remember, forget
  • Feelings/emotions: love, hate, like, prefer, want, need, wish
  • Senses (involuntary): see, hear, smell, taste, feel (= seem)
  • Possession/being: have (= possess), own, belong, seem, appear, contain

✓ Active/dynamic — CAN be used in progressive

  • Physical actions: run, walk, work, sleep, wake, breathe, cough
  • Medical activities: take (medicine), experience, feel (= physically feel), suffer, recover
  • Mental actions (deliberate): think (= consider carefully), look (= search), listen
✗ I have been knowing about this allergy for years. WRONG — "know" is stative. → Correct: I have known about this allergy for years.
✗ She has been needing the spray since March. WRONG — "need" is stative. → Correct: She has needed the spray since March.
✓ She has been taking the antihistamines. CORRECT — "take" is an action/activity. She is actively performing the action of taking medicine.
✓ Maya has been experiencing chest tightness. CORRECT — "experience" is used as a dynamic verb (an active process of going through something).

Activity verb + been + -ing = correctStative verb + been + -ing = always wrong in standard English

⚠ Note: some verbs can be both stative and dynamic, but with different meanings. "I think she's right" (stative — opinion) vs. "I've been thinking about your advice" (dynamic — deliberate mental process). Context decides.

Present Perfect vs. Present Perfect Progressive — when to choose which

Both describe a connection between past and present. The difference is emphasis.

TenseEmphasisExampleLikely context
Present Perfect
have/has + past participle		 Completion, result, count, or experience "I have taken three courses of antibiotics." Focused on how many / whether it's done
Pres. Perfect Progressive
have/has been + -ing		 Duration, repetition, ongoing process "I have been taking antihistamines for weeks." Focused on the ongoing activity itself

With stative verbs, only Present Perfect is possible: "I have known Dr. Ferrara for five years" — never "I have been knowing."

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the teal connectors — what is each one doing?

Detective work: while vs. even though

Look at Reza's sentence: "While this spray works differently from your antihistamines, it's very safe." He's making two points about the same medication at the same time. Is he saying the two ideas are opposite/surprising, or is he saying they happen simultaneously / in parallel?

Simultaneously / in parallel — the spray working differently AND being safe are both just true at the same time. "While" here is like saying "at the same time as / alongside the fact that." It doesn't mean they're surprising together; they just coexist.

Now look at Maya's sentence: "Even though I've been feeling this bad for two months, is a week really long enough?" Is she making two parallel points, or is she highlighting a contrast — something surprising or unexpected?

A surprising contrast — she's saying "I've suffered for two months, so it seems strange/surprising that one week of treatment would fix it." Even though always introduces something that makes the main clause feel unexpected or counter-intuitive.

Look at both together: "While the antihistamines deal with symptoms, this spray targets inflammation." and "Even though I've been taking them correctly, they haven't helped." Can you identify a rule about which connector shows "two things happening in parallel" and which shows "a result that seems to contradict the situation"?

While = two things that are true at the same time (can also contrast, but the contrast is not surprising — just two coexisting facts). Even though = one thing that you'd expect to prevent or change the other, but doesn't. The main clause is surprising given what the even-though clause says.

STUDY

While & Even Though — the rule behind the contrast

2. While & Even Though

Both are subordinating conjunctions — they connect a subordinate clause to a main clause. The difference is what kind of relationship they create.

ConnectorRelationshipFeel of the sentenceFrom the conversation
while Simultaneous / parallel (or mild contrast) "These two things are both true at the same time" — not surprising, just coexisting While the antihistamines deal with symptoms, this spray targets inflammation.
even though Concession (strong contrast) "This is true despite what you'd expect" — the result is surprising given the condition Even though I've been taking them correctly, they haven't helped much.

Position in the sentence:

✓ Connector clause first (comma required after the subordinate clause):
While the spray works slowly, it's very effective long-term. Connector clause → comma → main clause
✓ Main clause first (no comma):
The spray is very effective long-term while working slowly. Main clause → connector clause, no comma needed

Common confusion: "while" has two meanings

1 While = at the same time (temporal):
While you're using the spray every morning, keep taking the antihistamine in the evening. Both things happen during the same time period.
2 While = although / parallel contrast (concessive):
While this spray works differently, it is safe for long-term use. Two facts that both exist — not temporally, but as parallel truths. Less strong contrast than "even though."

The contrast spectrum — from weakest to strongest:

while
(parallel / mild)
although
(contrast)
though
(informal contrast)

Exercise 3 — While or Even Though?

1.while / even thoughBoth work but with different meanings: "while" = parallel facts (they coexist); "even though" = surprising contrast (you'd expect a slow spray to be less good, but it isn't).
2.even though / althoughStrong concession — following the prescription should lead to improvement, but it didn't.
3.whileParallel contrast — two simultaneous truths about the two medications. "Even though" doesn't fit as well here because neither fact is surprising given the other.
4.even though / althoughConcession — being busy would typically prevent someone from giving good advice. The clear advice is surprising.

Exercise 5 — Collocation matching

1.prescribe → a medicationYou prescribe medicine; you don't "give" or "make" a medication in formal medical English.
2.suffer from → a chronic condition"Suffer from" collocates with conditions and illnesses, not symptoms (which you "experience" or "have").
3.experience → a flare-up"Experience" is the natural verb for symptoms and episodes.
4.run → some tests"Run" a test is the standard medical/professional collocation — not "do" or "make."

2.chronicLong-term, recurring — the opposite of acute.
3.dosageThe prescribed amount — not "dose" (which is one instance) or "prescription" (which is the document/medicine).
4.flare-upA sudden worsening of a recurring condition.
5.side effectsAlways plural in standard usage ("side effects," not "a side effect" when listing more than one).
