The park at 2 am

A man is found unconscious in Riverside Park at 2 am. He had been lying there for some time before anyone noticed. There is one witness — a local named Theo, who had been walking his dog.

Detective Okafor asks Theo what he saw. Theo is nervous, not entirely sure what he observed, and his spoken account is hesitant, hedged, and full of vague expressions.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the yellow phrases

Detective work: the yellow phrases

All the yellow phrases share the same structure. What is it?

They all follow: had been + verb+ing. This is the Past Perfect Progressive (also called Past Perfect Continuous).

"The subject had been lying there for 45 minutes before Theo found him." — Which event came first, and which came second?

The man lying there came first (the ongoing background action). Theo finding him came second (the reference point). The PPP shows the action that was already in progress when the second event happened.

"The footpath lighting had been malfunctioning for several days." — Does this emphasise the single moment of malfunction, or the duration of an ongoing situation?

Duration — the PPP stresses that this wasn't a one-off event; it had been going on continuously for days. This is the core difference from the Past Perfect simple: PPP = duration/continuity of an activity up to a point in the past.

STUDY

Past Perfect Progressive — Form & Use

1. Past Perfect Progressive (PPP)

Also called Past Perfect Continuous. Shows an ongoing or repeated activity that was in progress up to a past reference point — emphasising duration or continuity.

Form:

Subject  +  had been  +  verb + -ing
FormExample
PositiveThe man had been lying there for 45 minutes.
NegativeTheo hadn't been expecting to find anyone in the park.
QuestionHad the vehicle been idling there for long?
Short answerYes, it had. / No, it hadn't.

Signal words: often used with for, since, all night/day/morning, how long

for 45 minutessince midnightall evening for several dayshow long had he been…?

Three key uses:

1Ongoing activity up to a past point
He had been working the night shift for three months when the incident occurred.Action in progress up to that reference point. Stresses continuous effort / duration.
2Cause of a past result
His hands were cold — he had been lying on the damp path for nearly an hour.The PPP explains why the past result came about. The evidence points back to the ongoing action.
3Repeated action in the past
The dog had been pulling towards the east path for several minutes before Theo let him lead.Not a single pull — repeated, persistent tugging. PPP captures the repeated, habitual quality.
STUDY

PPP vs Past Perfect — the key distinction

2. PPP vs Past Perfect Simple — when to choose

Both refer to events before another past event. The difference is emphasis: duration & continuity (PPP) vs completion (Past Perfect).

Past Perfect SimplePast Perfect Progressive
FocusCompletion of an actionDuration / continuity of an activity
Asks"Did it finish?""How long was it going on?"
Stative verbs?Yes — know, believe, own, wantNo — stative verbs don't use -ing
Result visible?Sometimes impliedOften explains a present/past result

Compare these pairs — same event, different emphasis:

PPBy the time paramedics arrived, the subject had lain there for nearly an hour.Focus: the completed duration — it's now over. More formal.
PPPThe subject had been lying there for nearly an hour when paramedics arrived.Focus: the ongoing nature of the action — it was still happening when they arrived.
PPThe dog had pulled Theo towards the path once — then he let him go.One completed action, no duration implied.
PPPThe dog had been pulling Theo towards the path for several minutes.Repeated/sustained action — the pulling kept happening.

⚠ Stative verbs — these do NOT use PPP:

✗Detective Okafor had been knowing about the lighting fault for days.WRONG — "know" is stative. Use Past Perfect: "had known."
✓Detective Okafor had known about the lighting fault for days.Stative verbs (know, believe, want, own, seem, contain) use Past Perfect, not PPP.

NOTICE & DISCOVER

Look at the blue and green phrases

Detective work: hedging and like

Theo says: "Rex was being kind of weird." What does "kind of" add that just "weird" wouldn't?

"Kind of" hedges the adjective — Theo isn't fully committing to the word "weird." It signals: approximately weird / somewhat weird / I'm not certain "weird" is exactly right. This is vague language: speakers use it to avoid sounding too confident about something they're not sure of, or to soften a statement.

Theo says: "There was stuff around him — a bag and things." What is Theo doing with "stuff" and "things"?

He can't or doesn't want to name the specific items. "Stuff" and "things" are vague approximators — they signal that a more exact list exists but Theo either doesn't know it or doesn't feel it's important to specify. Very common in spoken English; less common in formal written English.

Theo says: "I was like, 'Rex, come on'" AND "I was like, a while?" — are these two uses of "like" doing the same job?

No — two different jobs. "I was like, 'Rex, come on'" = quotative like — introducing what someone said or thought (a very common spoken English feature). "It was like, a while?" = approximator like — meaning "approximately" or "about." Both are common in informal speech; neither is appropriate in formal writing.

STUDY

Vague Language — types, function, register

3. Vague Language — what it is and why it exists

COCA spoken corpus data confirms: kind of, sort of, like, stuff, things, and whatever are among the most frequent words in spoken English. Learning vague language is not learning to be imprecise — it is learning to sound natural.

Why do speakers use vague language?

They genuinely don't know They don't want to over-commit They're being polite / indirect They're managing their certainty They're summarising rather than listing They want to sound less formal

The main types:

TypeExamplesWhat it does
Approximators
(hedging adjectives / adverbs)		kind of, sort of, like, a bit, roughly, prettySoftens or approximates a description. "Kind of dark." "Sort of urgent."
General extenders
(catch-all nouns)		stuff, things, everything, peopleStands in for items the speaker can't or doesn't want to name: "a bag and stuff."
Set completers
(end-of-list signals)		or whatever, or something, etc., and thatSignals a list is incomplete or the rest is obvious: "asleep or whatever."
Discourse markers
(structuring / stalling)		like, sort of, kind of, you knowBuys time while thinking, or signals transition: "And I was like, walking…"

STUDY

Using like in speech — four functions

4. Like in spoken English — four functions

The word like does far more in spoken English than "similar to." Understanding its functions is key to sounding natural and to understanding native speakers.

#FunctionExample from TheoWhat it signals
1Preposition / comparison
(traditional meaning)		"It smelled like the park usually does."Similar to. Standard meaning — fine in all registers.
2Approximator
(hedging)		"It was like, a while ago."Approximately / around / about. Softens a number or description. Very frequent in spoken English.
3Quotative
(introducing speech/thought)		"I was like, 'Rex, come on.'"
"I was like, why is this car here?"		Introduces what someone said, thought, or felt. Can replace "said" or "thought" in informal speech. Very frequent in young adults' speech but spreading across age groups.
4Discourse marker / filler
(structuring / hesitation)		"And I was, like, walking Rex…"Signals the speaker is thinking, keeps the floor, or introduces a new piece of information. Not meaningful in itself — functions like "um" with more purpose.

Register note: Functions 2, 3, and 4 are informal spoken features. They appear naturally in casual speech and are widely understood — but should not appear in formal written English (academic essays, legal documents, journalism). Understanding them is essential for listening comprehension.

Pronunciation note: In natural speech, like as a filler/approximator is typically unstressed and quick — /lɪk/ rather than /laɪk/. Listen for this when you watch English-language TV.

Grammar extension

Past Perfect Progressive vs Present Perfect Progressive

You've studied PPP (had been + -ing). There's a parallel tense: Present Perfect Progressive (have/has been + -ing). Compare these two:

Exercise 1 — PPP form

1.had been walkingPositive PPP. Ongoing action: 20 minutes on the south path before finding the man.
2.had been idlingPositive PPP. Sustained: engine running, not moving, for 10+ minutes.
3.had been malfunctioningPositive PPP. Several days = duration up to reference point (night of incident).

5.hadn't been planningNegative PPP. The planning was NOT in progress — he didn't intend to stay.

Exercise 2 — PPP or Past Perfect?

1.had been lying (PPP)Duration — still in progress when paramedics arrived. PPP is clearly better.
2.had read (PP)Completed action counted ("twice") before a next event. PP = completion.
3.had known (PP)"Know" is stative — never uses PPP. Past Perfect only.
4.had been waiting (PPP)Duration explicitly signalled ("over an hour"). PPP emphasises the ongoing wait.
5.had written (PP)Count ("three pages") signals a measurable completed result. PP for completion.

Exercise 3 — Error correction

1.The suspect had known about the park's blind spot for months."Know" is stative — cannot use "had been knowing." Past Perfect: "had known."
2.The dog had been pulling at the lead for minutes when it finally broke free."Has been pulling" = Present Perfect Progressive — wrong tense. Must be past: "had been pulling."
3.The lighting had been malfunctioning since the storm last Tuesday.Missing "had" — "been malfunctioning" alone is not a complete verb phrase. Needs "had been."
4.Had the vehicle been idling there when the second witness arrived?"Had idled" = Past Perfect (one completed event) — but the question asks about an ongoing process. PPP question: "Had…been idling?"

Exercise 4 — Like function

1.approximator"Like, two in the morning" = approximately 2 am. Hedging a time.
2.quotative"She was like, 'I don't even know'" = introduces what someone said/thought.
3.discourse marker"The park was dark, like, I couldn't see" — "like" here is a structuring filler introducing an elaboration.
4.comparison (preposition)"Like an arrow" = traditional preposition: in the manner of, similar to.
5.quotative"I was like, should I call the police" = introduces an internal thought / reported inner speech.

Exercise 6 — Vague language gap-fill
