Planning a Trip: Where Were You, Where Are You Going?

Simple past of "be" · "going to" for future plans (preview) · Me neither/too

📍 This week's story: Tom and Maya had such a good time cooking together that they've decided to plan a weekend trip. Before choosing where to go, they're swapping stories about places they've been before — and starting to make real plans for the weekend ahead. Read their conversation carefully before any grammar is named.

★What You'll Master This Week

VocabularyWeather, travel, and hometown words.

1Context First: Read Before Any Rule

Read Tom and Maya's planning chat. Just try to understand what they're talking about — past trips, or future plans? We'll look at the grammar afterwards.

Text Messages
Tom
So, where should we go this weekend? I was in Portugal two years ago — the weather was amazing, really sunny and warm.
Maya
Nice! I've never been to Portugal. I was in Scotland last spring, but honestly the weather was terrible — it was rainy and cold the whole week!
Tom
Ha, I hate the cold. Me too! Actually, let's just stay somewhere closer. My hometown has a nice coast, about two hours away.
Maya
I don't really know your hometown at all. Me neither... wait, I mean, tell me more! 😅
Tom
Ha! OK. So — we're going to drive down on Saturday morning. I'm going to book a small guesthouse near the beach tonight.
Maya
Perfect! I'm going to pack my camera — I want to take lots of photos. What's the weather forecast like for Saturday?
Tom
Sunny, apparently! Great, so we're going to have perfect beach weather.
Hometown Profile

Tom's Hometown: Riverside-on-Sea

From the Riverside Community Centre website

Riverside-on-Sea is a small coastal town, about two hours from the city. It was originally a fishing village, and the harbour was the centre of the town for over 100 years. Today, the beaches and seafood restaurants are the main attraction. Summers here are usually warm and sunny, while winters are mild but rainy.

Notice: Tom and Maya use "was/were" to talk about trips that already happened, and "going to" to talk about plans they've already decided on for the weekend. "Me too" and "me neither" let them agree quickly without repeating the whole sentence.

3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Simple Past of "Be": Form

SubjectFormExample
I / he / she / itwasI was in Portugal two years ago.
you / we / theywereYou were right about the weather.
negativewasn't / weren'tThe weather wasn't great in Scotland.
questionWas/Were + subject...?Were you in Scotland last spring?
One timeline, two grammar points: past "be" and future "going to"
"Was/were" points backward from now; "going to" points forward from a decision already made now.

3.2 — "Going To" for Future Plans (Preview)

We use am/is/are + going to + verb to talk about something we have already decided to do. You will study this fully, alongside "will", in a later grade — for now, just learn to recognise and use this basic pattern.

FormExample
I'm going to + verbI'm going to book a guesthouse tonight.
He/She/It's going to + verbIt's going to be sunny on Saturday.
We/You/They're going to + verbWe're going to drive down on Saturday.
negativeI'm not going to bring my laptop.
questionAre you going to pack a camera?

⚠️ Don't Forget the Verb "Be"!

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
I going to book a guesthouse.I'm going to book a guesthouse.
She going to visit her hometown.She's going to visit her hometown.

"Going to" is not a verb by itself — it always needs am/is/are in front of it, just like any other "-ing" form.

4Me Too / Me Neither

In spoken English, we often agree with someone without repeating their whole sentence. The choice between "me too" and "me neither" depends on whether the original sentence was positive or negative.

Positive statement → Me too!"I love the beach." "Me too!"
"I'm going to bring a camera." "Me too!"
Negative statement → Me neither!"I don't like the cold." "Me neither!"
"I've never been to Portugal." "Me neither!"

This is why Maya nearly made a mistake in the chat — she started to say "me neither" after her own negative sentence, catching herself because "me neither" is for agreeing with someone else's negative sentence, not repeating your own.

7Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "I were in Spain last year."
    Incorrect — "I" always takes "was": "I was in Spain last year."
  2. "We was at the beach yesterday."
    Incorrect — "we" always takes "were": "We were at the beach yesterday."
  3. "She going to visit her hometown next month."
    Incorrect — missing "is": "She's going to visit her hometown next month."
  4. "I wasn't in London last winter, but he was."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Wasn't" and "was" are both correctly matched to their subjects. Resist the urge to change anything.
  5. "I love travelling." "Me neither!"
    Incorrect — the first sentence is positive, so the reply should be "Me too!" ("Me neither" is only for agreeing with negative sentences.)

🗣Speaking Practice

Maya and Tom are planning a trip — talking about where they were before, and where they're going to go next. Past and future, in one conversation. Voice memo app ready!

Timed monologue

One minute: then and next

Set a timer for 1 minute. Two parts:

  • Part 1 — your last trip: "Last summer I was in… the weather was… the people were…"
  • Part 2 — your next trip: "Next time, I'm going to visit… I'm going to try…"
How: Record without stopping. When you listen back, check the pair: was with I/he/she/it, were with you/we/they. And every future plan gets going to + verb.
Me too / Me neither game

Agree out loud

Say these five sentences aloud — and after each one, answer honestly with Me too! or Me neither! or Really? Not me.

  • "I was tired yesterday."
  • "I wasn't at home last Saturday."
  • "I'm going to travel this year."
  • "I can't pack a suitcase quickly."
  • "I love looking at other people's holiday photos."

Then make three sentences of your own for a partner (or your future self) to answer.

How: Best with a partner — alone, record both parts. When you listen back, check: Me too answers a positive sentence, Me neither answers a negative one. Quick answers, no thinking pause — that's the game!
Sentence challenge

Six travel words, one sentence each

Say one sentence with each word — about a real or dream trip. Up to 60 seconds per word:

  • a passport
  • luggage
  • accommodation
  • a souvenir
  • sightseeing
  • an itinerary
How: One recording, timer on. When you listen back, check: luggage has no plural — "my luggage IS heavy," never "luggages."

Tips: past and future in one breath

  • The trick word is the time word. Start with it, and the grammar follows: "Last year… was. Next month… going to."
  • Going to often sounds like gonna: "I'm gonna visit…" You'll hear it everywhere now.
  • Me too / Me neither are the two fastest ways to keep any conversation alive. Use one today.

9Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

Use these tasks to notice this week's language in real English before your next class.

  • Real weather check: Search online for this weekend's weather forecast in a city you'd like to visit. Write 2 sentences using "going to" about what the weather is going to be like.
  • Travel vlog hunt: Watch a 1-2 minute travel vlog in English. Note down 2 examples of "was/were" (about the trip so far) and 2 examples of "going to" (about upcoming plans in the video).
  • Hometown facts: Look up 3 interesting facts about your own hometown or a city you love, and write them using "was" (for history) and "is" (for today).
  • Corpus check: Search "going to" vs "will" on a corpus website like English-Corpora.org. Which is more common in spoken English? Why do you think that might be?
  • Speaking challenge: Tell a friend or classmate about a past trip and a future plan, and try to get a natural "me too" or "me neither" response from them.
