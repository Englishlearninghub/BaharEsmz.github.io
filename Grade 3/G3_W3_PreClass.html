Good at Cooking? Let's Go Shopping First

Gerunds after prepositions · containers · grocery shopping vocabulary

📍 This week's story: After the climbing wall, Tom invites Maya over to cook dinner together. Before they can cook anything, they need to plan the menu and go shopping. Read their planning chat and their shopping list before we name any grammar — both are full of exactly the language you need this week.

3Grammar Focus: Gerunds After Prepositions

A gerund is the "-ing" form of a verb used like a noun (cooking, swimming, writing). This week's rule is simple but important:

The pattern: preposition + gerund (never "to")
Whenever a verb comes directly after a preposition, it must be a gerund (-ing form) — this never changes, in any tense.

3.1 — Common Adjective/Verb + Preposition + Gerund Combinations

ExpressionExample
good/bad atI'm good at cooking, but bad at baking.
interested inShe's interested in trying new recipes.
keen onAre you keen on cooking something spicy?
excited aboutI'm excited about trying a new sauce.
tired ofI'm tired of eating the same meals.
worried aboutHe's worried about burning the garlic bread.
instead ofInstead of buying sauce, let's make our own.
before / afterAfter writing the list, we went shopping.

⚠️ The #1 Error at This Level

Learners very often use the base verb or the infinitive ("to + verb") after a preposition, because that's the pattern used after many other verbs. But after a preposition, only the gerund is correct.

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
I'm good at cook.I'm good at cooking.
She's interested in to try new food.She's interested in trying new food.
Instead of buy sauce, we made our own.Instead of buying sauce, we made our own.

Rule of thumb: if you can identify the word right before the verb as a preposition (at, in, on, about, of, before, after, instead of, without...), the verb after it must end in "-ing". This rule doesn't change for different tenses — "I was good at cooking" and "I will be good at cooking" both still use "cooking", never "to cook".

4Containers: Talking About Quantities

English doesn't usually say "two rices" or "three oils" — instead, we use a container word + of + the food. This pattern appeared throughout the shopping list in Section 1.

7Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "I'm good at cook pasta."
    Incorrect — after a preposition, use the gerund: "I'm good at cooking pasta."
  2. "She's interested in to try new food."
    Incorrect — never "to" after a preposition: "She's interested in trying new food."
  3. "After writing the list, we went to the supermarket."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "After" is a preposition here, correctly followed by the gerund "writing". Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "Instead of buy sauce, let's make our own."
    Incorrect — "instead of" is a preposition phrase: "Instead of buying sauce, let's make our own."
  5. "We need two bags of rice and three jars of honey."
    Also correct. The plural "-s" is correctly on the container words (bags, jars), not on the food itself. A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

🗣Speaking Practice

Cooking together means talking together: what to buy, how much, and what you're good at making. This week's speaking mixes shopping words, containers, and little -ing verbs after prepositions. Voice memo app ready!

Roleplay scenario

The shopping list

Role A: You and a friend are cooking dinner together tonight. Read your list aloud and ask what you need: "We need a loaf of bread, a carton of milk… how much cheese do we need?"

Role B: You're at the shop. Answer with containers and amounts: "Get two bottles of water and a bag of carrots. We don't need any butter."

How: With a partner, or both roles yourself; record 2 minutes. When you listen back, check: every food word has its container or amount partner — a loaf OF bread, a piece OF cheese. Don't lose the of!
Timed monologue

One minute: me and cooking

Set a timer for 1 minute. Talk about you and food — and use this week's special pattern, preposition + -ing, at least three times: "I'm good at making… I'm interested in trying… I'm tired of eating… before cooking, I always…"

How: Record without stopping. When you listen back, hunt for the classic slip: "good at cook" / "interested in to try." After a preposition, the verb always wears -ing.
Sentence challenge

Six containers, one sentence each

Say one sentence with each container — real things from your own kitchen. Up to 60 seconds per item:

  • a loaf of…
  • a carton of…
  • a bottle of…
  • a bag of…
  • a slice of…
  • a jar of…
How: One recording, timer on. Walk to your kitchen and say what you really see! When you listen back, check: the little of is almost silent in real speech — "a cup o' tea."

Tips: kitchen English

  • Learn container + food as one word: a-loaf-of-bread. Never just "loaf".
  • The pattern good at / interested in / tired of + -ing works for EVERYTHING, not just cooking. Three shapes, endless sentences.
  • Cook with English once this week: say each step aloud while you do it. "Now I'm cutting the onions… after washing the rice…" Free practice, real memory.

9Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

Use these tasks to notice this week's language in real English before your next class.

  • Recipe hunt: Find a simple recipe online in English. Count how many container words (a cup of, a pinch of, a tablespoon of...) it uses — there are more than the ones taught this week!
  • Preposition + gerund collection: Over the next few days, collect 5 more examples of "preposition + gerund" from anything you read or hear in English (social media, articles, conversations).
  • Store vocabulary: Look up the names of 3 real grocery stores or supermarket chains near you (or famous ones). Visit their websites and see what container words they use in their online product descriptions.
  • Listening hunt: Watch a 1-2 minute cooking video in English. Note down every container word used (a cup of, a spoonful of, a handful of...).
  • Speaking challenge: Tell a friend or classmate three things you're good at, interested in, or tired of — using the target grammar naturally, without writing it down first.
