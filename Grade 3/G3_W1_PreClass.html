Who Is Tom? Meeting a Stranger at the Airport

Present progressive · order of adjectives · clothes & appearance vocabulary

📍 This week's story: Maya is going to meet Tom for the very first time. They only know each other from messages online. Tom is already at the airport, so he sends Maya a message to describe himself — what he is wearing, and what he looks like — so Maya can find him in the crowd. Read the messages carefully. Can you guess which person is Tom before Maya does?

SkillsReading messages and a poster, listening, pronunciation, describing pictures.

Lost & Found — Have You Seen This Bag?

City Airport — Customer Service Desk

2Notice the Language (Before Any Rule)

Look again at the highlighted words. Talk to a partner about these questions.

Q1. Look at "I'm wearing", "I'm carrying", "I'm standing". What two little words do you see in every one of these? What comes after them?
Q2. Is Tom describing something that is always true about him, or something that is true right now, at this exact moment?
Q3. Look at "a small, blue, cotton backpack". There are three adjectives before the noun "backpack". Which one describes the size? Which one describes the colour? Which one describes the material (what it is made of)?
Q4. Now look at "an old, black, leather wallet" and "a nice, new, silver watch". Do you see the same order: size/age, then colour, then material? Could you say "a leather, blue, small backpack" instead? Does it sound strange?

3Grammar Focus

3.1 — Present Progressive: Form

SubjectFormExample
Iam + verb-ingI'm wearing a red jacket.
he / she / itis + verb-ingHe's standing near the coffee shop.
you / we / theyare + verb-ingWe're waiting at the airport.
negativeam/is/are + not + verb-ingShe's not wearing a hat today.
questionAm/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing?Are you wearing glasses?

3.2 — Present Progressive: Use

We use the present progressive to talk about something happening right now, at this exact moment — including what someone is wearing, carrying, or doing at this moment.

Present progressive: an action happening around NOW
The bar starts a little before now and continues a little after now — it is true at this exact moment, not necessarily forever.

⚠️ Important: Some Verbs Are Not Usually Progressive

Some verbs describe a state, not an action — like have (when it means "possess"), like, want, know, need. We do not normally add "-ing" to these verbs.

❌ Incorrect✅ Correct
I'm having a black backpack.I have a black backpack.
I'm knowing his name.I know his name.

But be careful: "wearing", "carrying", and "standing" ARE action verbs, so they ARE fine in the progressive: "I'm wearing glasses" ✅, "I'm carrying a bag" ✅.

3.3 — The Order of Adjectives

When we use more than one adjective before a noun, the adjectives usually go in a fixed order. At this level, the four most useful categories are:

The adjective order "train"
Opinion always comes first, then size, then colour, then material — right before the noun.
CategoryQuestion it answersExamples
1. OpinionWhat do you think of it?nice, lovely, beautiful, ugly
2. SizeHow big is it?small, big, large, tiny
3. ColourWhat colour is it?red, blue, black, yellow
4. MaterialWhat is it made of?cotton, leather, silver, wool

There are more categories used at higher levels (age, shape, origin, purpose), but these four will cover almost everything you need to describe clothes, bags, and everyday objects at this level. You can explore the complete order in Section 9 (Explore More).

7Error Clinic

Read each sentence. Some have mistakes — fix them. One sentence is already correct — do not "fix" it if it isn't broken.

  1. "I'm having a black backpack."
    Incorrect — "have" (meaning possess) is a state, not an action: "I have a black backpack."
  2. "She's wearing a blue, small, cotton jacket."
    Incorrect adjective order — size comes before colour: "She's wearing a small, blue, cotton jacket."
  3. "Tom is standing near the coffee shop."
    This sentence is already correct — no error here. "Standing" is an action happening right now, correctly in the present progressive. Resist the urge to change anything.
  4. "I'm knowing his name because he told me on the phone."
    Incorrect — "know" is a state verb, not normally used in the progressive: "I know his name because he told me on the phone."
  5. "He is carrying a big, red suitcase."
    Also correct. "Carrying" is an action verb (fine in the progressive), and the adjective order (size before colour) is correct. A second trap — not every sentence needs a correction.

🗣Speaking Practice

Maya found Tom by describing him: what he's wearing, what he looks like. Now you describe people — out loud. You only need your phone's voice memo app.

Describe a person

One minute: someone you know

Set a timer for 1 minute. Describe one person you know well — or a person in a photo on your phone. Say what they look like and what they are wearing today (or in the photo): "She has long dark hair. She's wearing a blue jacket and white trainers…"

How: Record it. Don't stop to fix mistakes. When you listen back, check the two verbs: has for hair and eyes, is wearing for clothes. Both are your friends this week!
Roleplay: Guess who

Find my person

Role A: Look around you (or at a group photo). Choose one person secretly. Describe them: "This person is tall. He's wearing glasses and a green T-shirt…"

Role B: Ask yes/no questions and guess: "Is he wearing a cap? Does she have short hair? Is it…?"

How: Play with a partner — or describe a famous person and let a friend guess later from your recording. When you listen back, check: Is he wearing…? (now) but Does she have…? (always).
Sentence challenge

Six words, one sentence each

Say one sentence with each word — about yourself or people near you. Up to 60 seconds per word:

  • a jacket
  • jeans
  • glasses
  • a backpack
  • tall / short
  • curly / straight (hair)
How: One recording, timer on. When you listen back, check: jeans and glasses are always plural — "my jeans ARE blue."

Tips: describing people

  • Describe from big to small: height → hair → clothes → one special thing (glasses, a cap). Your listener builds the picture step by step.
  • Wearing loses its g in fast speech: "wearin' a jacket." You don't have to say it that way — but now you'll hear it.
  • Practise with your own mirror: describe yourself every morning in three sentences. Thirty seconds a day builds real fluency.

9Explore More: Build Your Own Independence

Use these tasks to notice this week's language in real English before your next class.

  • Full adjective order: Search online for "order of adjectives in English" and find the complete list (opinion, size, age, shape, colour, origin, material, purpose). Try to write one example sentence using four categories together.
  • People-watching: Next time you are in a public place (a café, the street, public transport), silently describe three people to yourself using the present progressive: "That man is wearing...", "That woman is carrying...".
  • Photo challenge: Choose a photo of a group of people (your own photos, or any photo online). Describe one person in the photo in writing without saying who — can a friend or classmate guess who you mean?
  • Listening hunt: Watch any 1–2 minute video in English where someone describes what they are wearing (a "what I'm wearing today" video works well). Note down three present progressive sentences.
  • State verbs check: Make a list of five more verbs that are NOT usually used in the progressive (for example: believe, love, understand, prefer, own). Check your list is correct by searching online.
